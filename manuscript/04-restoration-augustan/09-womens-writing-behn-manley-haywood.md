## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Every writer in this Part has needed some means of financial support — royal office, political patronage, church income, or, increasingly, the open market itself. For the three women this closing chapter studies, the available options were considerably narrower. Women could not attend Oxford or Cambridge, could not vote or hold public office, and, once married, lost most independent legal and financial standing under a legal doctrine called **coverture**, which folded a married woman's property and legal identity into her husband's. Widowhood, separation, or simply never marrying often left a woman with very few respectable ways to support herself at all.

Professional authorship, for exactly the reasons this Part has already traced — the growth of the periodical and novel markets (Chapters 4 and 7), the decline of strict pre-publication censorship after 1695 (Chapter 4) — turned out to be one of the few paths open to a woman with talent, ambition, and no other reliable income. All three writers in this chapter reached that path through real financial precarity: Aphra Behn returned from government espionage work heavily in debt and apparently unpaid by the Crown; Delarivier Manley was drawn into a bigamous marriage by a cousin who was already secretly married, a scandal that left her socially and financially exposed; and Eliza Haywood's early biography, including the exact nature of a reported marriage to a clergyman, remains genuinely disputed among scholars, though her own writing repeatedly returns to women left without reliable financial or social protection. Writing was not an easy or comfortable solution to these circumstances — as this chapter shows, all three women faced attacks on their personal reputation that their male contemporaries, writing about similar material, rarely faced in the same way — but it was, for each of them, a viable one.

## Literary Movements

This chapter introduces two genres largely dominated by women writers, and largely left out of the "rise of the novel" narrative you studied through Ian Watt's thesis in Chapter 7. The first is **secret history** (sometimes called scandal fiction): narratives that fictionalize real, contemporary political and social scandal, thinly disguising real public figures behind invented names and exotic settings, in a mode literary historians now call a **roman à clef** — a novel whose fictional characters are meant to be decoded as real people. Delarivier Manley, discussed fully below, is this genre's most important English practitioner. The second is **amatory fiction**: shorter prose narratives centered on passionate romantic and sexual intrigue, exploring desire, seduction, and their social and emotional consequences with a directness and psychological attention that later, more moralized fiction (including much of Richardson's work, from Chapter 7) would soften or restrain. Behn's *Love-Letters Between a Nobleman and His Sister* and the early fiction of Eliza Haywood, both discussed below, are central examples.

Later readers and critics have grouped Behn, Manley, and Haywood together — sometimes under the phrase "the fair triumvirate of wit," used by contemporaries and later writers to credit the three as a connected literary lineage — recognizing that each built directly on the commercial and reputational ground the writer before her had cleared. It's worth returning, one more time, to Chapter 7's discussion of Ian Watt's account of the novel's origins: Watt's focus on Defoe, Richardson, and Fielding leaves secret history and amatory fiction almost entirely out of the story, even though both genres were commercially successful, widely read, and directly formative for the novel's development of sustained interiority, romantic plotting, and serialized popular publication. This chapter, in effect, supplies the half of that origin story Chapter 7 could only gesture toward.

## Author Profiles

**Aphra Behn** (c. 1640–1689) is a figure this book has already introduced, in Chapter 2, as the Restoration stage's most successful woman dramatist and the first Englishwoman to earn her living principally by writing. Very little about her early life is documented with certainty, but she appears to have traveled to the English colony of Surinam around 1663–1664, an experience that later supplied the setting for her most enduring prose work, and she worked, not especially successfully or profitably, as a government spy in Antwerp during the Second Anglo-Dutch War in 1666, under the code name "Astrea," which she later reused as a literary pen name. Beyond the roughly nineteen plays that made her the Restoration stage's most prolific woman playwright, Behn also wrote important early prose fiction: *Love-Letters Between a Nobleman and His Sister* (1684–1687), an epistolary narrative blending real political scandal (loosely based on the Monmouth Rebellion, which you may recall from Dryden's *Absalom and Achitophel* in Chapter 3) with romantic intrigue, and *Oroonoko* (1688), discussed fully below. Behn died in 1689 and was buried in Westminster Abbey, though not in the Poets' Corner reserved for most literary figures. Two and a half centuries later, Virginia Woolf's *A Room of One's Own* (1929) urged that "all women together ought to let flowers fall upon the tomb of Aphra Behn," crediting her directly with having "earned them the right to speak their minds" — a judgment this chapter's whole account of Manley and Haywood, writing in the professional space Behn helped open, bears out.

**Delarivier Manley** (c. 1663–1724) built her career on a genre distinctly her own: fictionalized, thinly veiled attacks on real contemporary political and social figures. After the humiliation of her bigamous marriage in 1688, and following an early stage tragedy, *The Royal Mischief* (1696), Manley turned to prose satire aimed squarely at the Whig political establishment, beginning with *The Secret History of Queen Zarah and the Zarazians* (1705), which attacked Sarah Churchill, Duchess of Marlborough, under a barely disguised fictional name. Her most notorious work, *The New Atalantis* (1709) — its full title runs *Secret Memoirs and Manners of Several Persons of Quality, of Both Sexes, From the New Atalantis, an Island in the Mediterranean* — extended this technique across a whole fictional island populated by recognizable, unflattering portraits of real Whig politicians and society figures, and caused a genuine public sensation, leading to Manley's arrest on charges of libel. She was eventually released without conviction, reportedly because she insisted the work was pure invention and refused to implicate any source or collaborator who might have supplied her political information. In 1711, Manley succeeded Jonathan Swift as editor of the Tory party paper *The Examiner*, a direct professional continuation of the political journalism you studied in Chapter 5.

**Eliza Haywood** (c. 1693–1756) was, by the sheer volume and popularity of her output, one of the most commercially successful novelists of the entire early eighteenth century — a fact modern readers often find surprising, given how thoroughly her reputation was overshadowed by Defoe, Richardson, and Fielding until twentieth-century feminist scholarship recovered her importance. Her early amatory fiction, especially *Love in Excess* (1719–1720), was published the same year as Defoe's *Robinson Crusoe* and became one of the best-selling works of fiction of its decade. She was also a playwright, an actress, and, in the 1740s, a periodical essayist: *The Female Spectator* (1744–1746), discussed below, was the first English periodical written by a woman explicitly for a female readership. Haywood parodied Richardson's *Pamela* in her own *Anti-Pamela* (1741), published the same year as Fielding's better-remembered *Shamela* — evidence of just how widely *Pamela*'s ending provoked skepticism across the literary marketplace, from writers of very different backgrounds and genders alike. Her late novel, *The History of Miss Betsy Thoughtless* (1751), moves toward a calmer, more morally reflective mode of fiction tracking a young woman's social education and eventual maturity, anticipating the sentimental and domestic fiction you will meet at the start of Part V.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Oroonoko* (1688).** Presented partly as an eyewitness account by its female narrator, the novel tells the tragic story of an enslaved African prince transported to Surinam, and it is often cited, alongside the fiction discussed in Chapter 7, as an important forerunner of the English novel. Critics read it as a genuinely complicated text: it treats Oroonoko with real sympathy and nobility, and has sometimes been claimed as an early antislavery work, but it does not challenge slavery as an institution outright, and its narrator's own position within the colonial system it describes remains, at best, uneasy rather than fully critical.
- ***Love-Letters Between a Nobleman and His Sister* (1684–1687).** Blending real political scandal with romantic and erotic intrigue, told through an exchange of letters, this work anticipates Richardson's epistolary technique (Chapter 7) by more than half a century, while working in a far franker, more overtly sexual register than Richardson's later domestic fiction would allow itself.
- ***The New Atalantis* (1709).** Manley's fictional island setting lets her attack real Whig figures under a veil of invention thin enough for contemporary readers to see through easily, while giving her just enough legal deniability to avoid conviction when prosecuted. The work is a vivid demonstration of how fiction, in this period, could function as a genuinely dangerous form of political journalism — closely related to, but formally distinct from, the periodical political writing of Swift and Addison and Steele in Chapters 4 and 5.
- ***Love in Excess* (1719–1720).** Haywood's early amatory fiction traces its characters' romantic desires and misjudgments with a directness that later, more moralized eighteenth-century fiction would increasingly restrain, and its enormous popularity — rivaling *Robinson Crusoe*'s in its own year — is a useful reminder that Defoe's more famous novel was only one part of a much broader, more varied fiction market.
- ***The Female Spectator* (1744–1746).** Written entirely by Haywood under a female narrating persona, and explicitly modeled on Addison and Steele's *Spectator* from Chapter 4, this periodical addressed a female readership's own concerns — courtship, education, reading, household management — from a woman's own perspective, transforming a genre Chapter 4 introduced as (with rare exceptions) a male-authored form into one a woman could use to speak directly to other women.
- ***The History of Miss Betsy Thoughtless* (1751).** Haywood's late masterpiece follows its title character through a long process of social misjudgment and eventual maturation, in a calmer, more reflective register than her earlier amatory fiction. Many critics treat it as a direct bridge toward the novel of sensibility and the female Bildungsroman tradition you will encounter at the start of Part V.

## Critical Perspectives

- **What did Woolf mean by crediting Behn with "the right to speak their minds"?** Woolf's tribute in *A Room of One's Own* treats Behn not just as a talented individual writer but as the person who proved, commercially and publicly, that a woman could support herself through frank, professional authorship — opening a path Manley and Haywood, in their very different ways, both walked. Reading the three writers together, as this chapter does, makes that inherited lineage concrete rather than merely symbolic.
- **Why were these writers judged by different standards than their male contemporaries?** Behn's, Manley's, and Haywood's frank treatment of desire and scandal was routinely used, by contemporary critics, as evidence of the writers' own personal immorality — a charge rarely leveled with the same intensity at male writers covering comparable material, including Wycherley's and Congreve's comedy of manners in Chapter 2. This double standard shaped both these writers' contemporary reputations and their long marginalization in literary history afterward.
- **Does Ian Watt's account of the novel's origins hold up once secret history and amatory fiction are included?** As Chapter 7 already noted, Watt's *Rise of the Novel* focuses almost entirely on Defoe, Richardson, and Fielding. Once you weigh Manley's and Haywood's commercial success and formal innovation — political roman à clef, sustained amatory interiority, women-authored periodical writing — against that narrower account, the novel's early history looks considerably more various, and considerably less exclusively male, than the traditional narrative suggests.
- **What does Manley's prosecution reveal about fiction's relationship to political reality?** *The New Atalantis* case shows contemporary readers and authorities alike treating "obvious fiction" and "real political accusation" as genuinely blurred categories — a tension that made secret history simultaneously a powerful weapon and a serious personal risk for the women who wrote it.

## Chapter Summary

Barred from university, public office, and most professions, and legally absorbed into their husbands' identities under coverture once married, Aphra Behn, Delarivier Manley, and Eliza Haywood each turned to professional authorship out of real financial necessity, and each built a genuinely successful career doing so. Behn, already met as a dramatist in Chapter 2, also produced *Oroonoko* (1688) and the epistolary *Love-Letters* (1684–1687), and Virginia Woolf later credited her with winning women "the right to speak their minds." Manley's secret histories, especially *The New Atalantis* (1709), fictionalized real political scandal so effectively that she was arrested for libel, and she later succeeded Swift himself as editor of *The Examiner*. Haywood's amatory fiction rivaled *Robinson Crusoe*'s popularity in 1719, and her *Female Spectator* (1744–1746) turned Addison and Steele's periodical form, from Chapter 4, into a vehicle for women's own voices — while her late novel *Miss Betsy Thoughtless* (1751) points directly toward the sentimental fiction that opens Part V.

That closes Part IV. Across nine chapters, you've traced English literature from the restored monarchy and the founding of the Royal Society, through Restoration drama and Dryden's satire, the periodical essay, Swift's and Pope's satiric mastery, the early novel's rise in several competing forms, Samuel Johnson's consolidation of English criticism and lexicography, and, in this chapter, the women writers whose careers ran alongside — and, in Behn's case, helped make possible — every other development this Part has covered. Part V picks up the story in the second half of the eighteenth century, as sensibility, sentiment, and a new, more emotionally expressive literary mode begin to displace Augustan reason and wit, carrying several of this Part's own writers' late tendencies — Haywood's reformed fiction chief among them — into a new literary era.

## Glossary

- **Coverture** — the legal doctrine under which a married woman's property and legal identity were absorbed into her husband's, sharply limiting married women's independent financial and legal standing.
- **Secret history (scandal fiction)** — a genre of fiction that thinly fictionalizes real contemporary political and social scandal, associated above all with Delarivier Manley.
- **Roman à clef** — a novel whose fictional characters and events are meant to be decoded as real people and events.
- **Amatory fiction** — prose fiction centered on passionate romantic and sexual intrigue, exploring desire and its consequences with unusual directness.
