## Historical and Socio-Political Context

One small legal change made an enormous amount of this chapter possible. Since 1662, English printing had operated under a Licensing Act requiring government approval before anything could be published — a form of pre-publication censorship. In 1695, Parliament simply let that Act expire rather than renew it, not out of any grand commitment to press freedom, but largely out of practical dissatisfaction with how it had been enforced. The effect, intended or not, was dramatic: within a decade, English print culture exploded in volume, and newspapers, pamphlets, and — the subject of this chapter — periodical essays became genuinely everyday reading for the first time.

They had somewhere to be read, too. London's coffeehouses, which had first appeared in the 1650s and multiplied steadily after the Restoration you studied in Chapter 1, had become the era's characteristic social space: places where men of the merchant, professional, and gentry classes gathered to talk, read the news aloud to one another, and argue about politics, trade, and literature over a drink that (unlike the tavern's ale) kept its drinkers alert rather than drunk. A periodical essay was written, in a very real sense, to be read in exactly this setting — short enough to finish over one cup of coffee, engaging enough to spark the room's conversation afterward.

This chapter's two central figures, Joseph Addison and Richard Steele, were lifelong friends who met as schoolboys at Charterhouse School in London and went on, separately, to Oxford. Both were Whigs — supporters of the political faction that had backed the Glorious Revolution and religious toleration for Protestant Dissenters, which you met briefly in Chapter 3 — and both, at different points, held government office. But their periodicals were deliberately written to reach past party lines, toward a broad, newly literate, newly prosperous readership hungry for entertaining, morally serious, and socially useful reading — a readership this chapter's second half will show you how they actually built.

## Literary Movements

The **periodical essay** is this chapter's genre: a short prose essay, published at regular intervals (daily or several times a week) in a dedicated single-essay paper, addressing manners, morality, taste, and everyday social life in a conversational, often gently humorous voice. It did not appear from nowhere. You already met one important ancestor in Part III, Chapter 10: Francis Bacon's *Essays*, brief, aphoristic prose reflections on subjects like truth, ambition, and friendship. What Addison and Steele add to that inheritance is regularity (a new issue on a fixed schedule, building a habit of reading) and a fictional framing device that turns the essay into something closer to a character-driven performance than a simple statement of opinion.

Steele's *The Tatler* (1709–1711) borrowed its narrating persona, "Isaac Bickerstaff," from a joke already famous among London readers: in 1708, Jonathan Swift — whom you will study fully in Chapter 5 — had published, under that invented name, a mock astrological prediction that a real, well-known almanac-maker named John Partridge would die on a specific date, then followed up by cheerfully "confirming" Partridge's death regardless of the man's very public, very alive protests. Steele's *Tatler* adopted "Bickerstaff" as a genial, socially connected observer of London life, reporting gossip, opinion, and moral commentary as if from a specific, recognizable character rather than an anonymous editorial voice.

*The Spectator* (1711–1712), which Addison and Steele developed together after *The Tatler* closed, refined this device further and became the single most influential periodical of the century. Its narrator, "Mr. Spectator," is a silent, observant gentleman who belongs to an imagined social circle called the Spectator Club — including Sir Roger de Coverley, a good-natured, old-fashioned country landowner; Sir Andrew Freeport, a hardheaded London merchant; and several other recurring types drawn from different corners of English society. Addison stated the paper's ambition directly, in its tenth issue: to bring philosophy "out of closets and libraries, schools and colleges, to dwell in clubs and assemblies, at tea-tables and in coffee-houses," and "to enliven morality with wit, and to temper wit with morality." Notice how deliberately that second phrase positions *The Spectator* against the Restoration comedy of manners you studied in Chapter 2: where a play like *The Man of Mode* let wit and immorality coexist without apology, Addison and Steele's project explicitly wants wit to serve moral improvement instead. This is part of the broader shift toward more moralized, "polite" culture that Jeremy Collier's 1698 attack, discussed in Chapter 2, had already helped set in motion.

## Author Profiles

**Joseph Addison** (1672–1719), educated at Charterhouse and then Queen's and Magdalen Colleges, Oxford, was a Whig politician as well as a writer, eventually rising to Secretary of State in 1717. His literary reputation, though, rests almost entirely on his periodical essays, above all his contributions to *The Spectator*, and on his hugely popular political tragedy *Cato* (1713), admired by readers across the political spectrum for its portrayal of Roman republican virtue under tyranny. Addison's prose style — clear, balanced, and even-tempered — became a widely imitated model of "correct" English writing for generations of readers and schoolchildren after his death, in a way not unlike the Royal Society's plain-style ideal you met in Chapter 1, applied now to moral and literary rather than scientific subjects.

**Richard Steele** (1672–1729) met Addison as a schoolboy at Charterhouse and remained his closest literary collaborator for the rest of his life, though the two men's temperaments differed considerably: Steele was warmer, more impulsive, and more directly sentimental than the cooler, more polished Addison. Steele founded both *The Tatler* (1709) and, jointly with Addison, *The Spectator* (1711), and went on, after *The Spectator*'s original run ended, to launch *The Guardian* (1713). Beyond periodical writing, Steele also wrote sentimental comedy for the stage, including *The Conscious Lovers* (1722), which deliberately reversed the amoral wit of Restoration comedy (Chapter 2) in favor of virtuous, emotionally earnest characters — a theatrical style historians now call "sentimental comedy," and a direct sign of the same broader cultural shift *The Spectator* itself represents.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***The Tatler* (1709–1711).** Published under the "Isaac Bickerstaff" persona and appearing three times a week, *The Tatler* mixed genuine news, gossip, moral reflection, and light satire, and it established the basic periodical-essay format — a short, single-topic essay, signed with a fictional identity — that *The Spectator* would refine. Steele began it alone but was soon joined by Addison, whose contributions grew steadily more central as the paper matured.
- ***The Spectator* (1711–1712, revived briefly 1714).** Published daily and reaching, by Addison's own estimate, as many as 60,000 readers in London through coffeehouse copies shared and read aloud among many people per issue, *The Spectator* is this period's single most successful periodical. Its Spectator Club device let Addison and Steele explore social types — the old-fashioned landed gentleman, the ambitious merchant, the urban rake — through recurring, developing characters rather than one-off essays, giving the paper a narrative continuity closer to fiction than to journalism.
- **The Sir Roger de Coverley papers.** Across dozens of *Spectator* issues, Addison and Steele built up a genuinely affectionate, gently comic portrait of Sir Roger, an old Tory country squire whose habits, opinions, and small eccentricities recur and develop from issue to issue. Many literary historians treat these papers as an important step toward the sustained, psychologically consistent characterization the English novel would develop fully in Defoe, Richardson, and Fielding — writers you will meet in Chapter 7.
- **Addison's Saturday papers on *Paradise Lost* (*Spectator*, January–May 1712).** In a remarkable sequence of eighteen essays, Addison undertook the first sustained, serious critical study of Milton's epic (Part III, Chapter 12), judging it systematically against Aristotle's classical criteria for epic poetry and arguing forcefully for its greatness alongside Homer and Virgil. This was not simply admiring commentary: Addison's papers played a major role in cementing *Paradise Lost*'s status as a canonical English classic, at a moment when Milton's own political history as a defender of the regicide republic (Part III, Chapter 11) still made him a genuinely controversial figure for some readers.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was coffeehouse culture as inclusive as it sounds?** Coffeehouses are often described as an early, relatively open forum for public discussion across social ranks, and in some respects they were — but they were also, with rare exceptions, spaces for men only. Women read and were written about in *The Tatler* and *The Spectator* extensively, and some, like the pseudonymous "Mrs. Crackenthorpe" of the rival paper *The Female Tatler* (1709–1710), wrote periodical essays of their own — a detail worth holding onto ahead of Chapter 9's fuller discussion of women's writing in this period. But the coffeehouse itself, as a physical institution, largely excluded the very readers whose manners its essays so often discussed.
- **Reform project or middle-class entertainment?** Addison's stated ambition — bringing philosophy out of "closets and libraries" into everyday social life — can be read as genuine moral reform, an attempt to civilize a reading public still shadowed by the sexual frankness Collier had attacked in Chapter 2. It can equally be read as a shrewd commercial strategy: entertainment respectable enough for a rapidly growing, status-conscious middle-class readership to consume without embarrassment. The two readings are not mutually exclusive, and most modern critics hold both at once.
- **Did Addison's Milton criticism shape the poem's reputation, or just reflect it?** Addison's Saturday papers are often credited with single-handedly securing *Paradise Lost*'s canonical status. Scholars caution against overstating this: the poem already had serious admirers before 1712. But there is little dispute that Addison's systematic, widely read defense reached a far larger audience than earlier praise had, and gave that admiration a shared critical vocabulary — a good example of how criticism can consolidate a reputation even when it doesn't single-handedly create one.
- **How much do Sir Roger and the Spectator Club anticipate the novel?** The question of exactly where "the English novel" begins is contested (you'll see the debate continue in Chapter 7), but Addison and Steele's recurring, gradually developed characters are a genuine and often-cited step toward it — proof that English readers were already developing an appetite for continuous, evolving fictional personalities well before Defoe's *Robinson Crusoe* (1719) is conventionally cited as the novel's starting point.

## Chapter Summary

The 1695 lapse of the Licensing Act and the rapid growth of London's coffeehouse culture created the conditions for a new literary form, the periodical essay, which Richard Steele launched with *The Tatler* (1709) under the borrowed "Isaac Bickerstaff" persona and which he and Joseph Addison perfected together in *The Spectator* (1711–1712). Through the fictional Spectator Club, especially the beloved figure of Sir Roger de Coverley, Addison and Steele developed a sustained, evolving style of character sketch that anticipates the English novel you will meet in Chapter 7, while Addison's eighteen Saturday papers on Milton's *Paradise Lost* (Part III, Chapter 12) helped secure that poem's status as an English classic. Their explicit project — to "enliven morality with wit, and to temper wit with morality" — deliberately positioned the periodical essay against the amoral comedy of manners you studied in Chapter 2, part of a broader early eighteenth-century turn toward "polite," reform-minded culture that continues through the rest of Part IV, from Swift's satire in Chapter 5 to Pope's criticism in Chapter 6.

## Glossary

- **Licensing Act** — a law requiring government approval before publication, in force in England from 1662 until it lapsed in 1695.
- **Periodical essay** — a short prose essay published at regular intervals in a dedicated paper, typically addressing manners, morality, and everyday social life.
- **Coffeehouse culture** — the social world of London's coffeehouses, which flourished after the Restoration as venues for reading, conversation, and debate.
- **Persona** — a fictional narrating identity (such as "Isaac Bickerstaff" or "Mr. Spectator") adopted by an essayist, distinct from the author's own voice.
- **Politeness** — an early eighteenth-century social and literary ideal combining refined manners, moral seriousness, and sociable wit, promoted by *The Spectator* as an alternative to both aristocratic excess and Puritan austerity.
- **Stamp Act (1712)** — a tax on newspapers and periodicals that raised their price and contributed to changes in how they were published.
