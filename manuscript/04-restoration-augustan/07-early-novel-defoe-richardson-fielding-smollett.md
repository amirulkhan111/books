## Historical and Socio-Political Context

You have now watched this Part's writers build careers on royal office (Dryden, Chapter 3), Whig politics (Addison and Steele, Chapter 4), the Church of Ireland (Swift, Chapter 5), and, with Pope in Chapter 6, on the open market itself, through subscription publishing. This chapter's four novelists represent that market-based path taken even further: none of them held court office, and their fiction was written to be sold, in growing numbers, to an expanding readership of merchants, professionals, and literate households who could now afford books and had the leisure time to read them.

Several conditions had to align for this to happen. Literacy was rising steadily across the period, especially in London and among the middle ranks of society. Printing costs were falling. And circulating libraries — businesses that lent books out for a small fee rather than requiring readers to buy them outright — were beginning to put fiction within reach of readers who could never have afforded a personal library. One further, more specific event shapes this chapter directly: in 1737, Parliament passed a new Licensing Act aimed specifically at the theatre (distinct from the general printing Licensing Act of 1662 you met in Chapter 4, which had already lapsed by this point), giving a royal official called the Lord Chamberlain the power to approve or ban any play before it could be performed. Henry Fielding, one of this chapter's four novelists, had built his early career writing sharp political satire for the London stage — satire so effective against Robert Walpole's government that many historians consider it a direct provocation for the 1737 Act. Once the Act passed, Fielding's playwriting career was effectively over, and he turned to prose fiction instead, a redirection that this chapter's story would look very different without.

Daniel Defoe, the earliest of the four writers, came from a different world again: a Presbyterian Dissenting family, part of the Nonconformist community you first met in Chapter 1's discussion of the Clarendon Code, still legally and socially disadvantaged well after the Restoration. Defoe's own restless career as a merchant, pamphleteer, and sometime government spy — working, at different points, for both Whig and Tory administrations, including for Robert Harley, the same statesman who patronized Swift in Chapter 5 — gave him an unusually wide, streetwise knowledge of English commercial and criminal life, which shows up directly in the fiction discussed below.

## Literary Movements

This chapter uses a word you've been reading throughout Part IV without a formal definition: the **novel**. For our purposes, treat it as an extended prose narrative, generally realistic in setting and detail, following the developing lives and inner experiences of specific, individual characters — as opposed to the older prose romance tradition, which favored idealized aristocratic heroes, marvelous adventures, and elevated, often improbable plots. Where exactly "the novel" begins in English is genuinely contested. The influential critic Ian Watt, in his 1957 study *The Rise of the Novel*, argued that Defoe, Richardson, and Fielding together invented something new: a fiction built on **formal realism** — circumstantial, specific, everyday detail (exact dates, prices, place names) designed to create a powerful illusion that the reader is encountering real, verified experience rather than invented romance. Later critics have pushed back on Watt's account for treating the novel as a uniquely English, uniquely Protestant, uniquely middle-class invention: they point to Aphra Behn's *Oroonoko* (1688), which you already met in Chapter 2, to the long European romance and picaresque traditions, and to spiritual autobiographies like John Bunyan's *The Pilgrim's Progress* (1678) as evidence that the ground for the novel had been prepared from multiple directions well before 1719. Both views matter: Watt correctly identifies something genuinely new in the specific *combination* of techniques these four writers develop, even if none of the individual ingredients was entirely without precedent.

Each of this chapter's four writers, in fact, solves the basic problem of "how do you make invented events feel real" in a different way. Defoe presents his fiction as **found documents** — real first-person confessions, journals, or memoirs that he claims merely to have edited, packed with the exact, practical detail of formal realism. Richardson invents the **epistolary novel**: a story told entirely through the letters its characters write to one another, which lets the reader watch feelings and decisions form in real time, a technique Richardson himself called **"writing to the moment."** Fielding, by contrast, embraces open, self-conscious fictionality: his narrator openly addresses the reader, comments on the art of fiction itself, and organizes his novels according to what he calls, in his preface to *Joseph Andrews*, a **"comic epic in prose"** — a form modeled explicitly on the Spanish novelist Miguel de Cervantes's *Don Quixote*, treating human vanity and hypocrisy, rather than heroic action, as comedy's true subject. Smollett, finally, revives an older Continental tradition, the **picaresque** — episodic narratives following a resourceful, roguish protagonist (a *picaro*) through a loosely connected string of adventures, often satirical and physically rough in tone — and, like Fielding, explicitly draws on Cervantes as a model.

## Author Profiles

**Daniel Defoe** (c. 1660–1731) spent decades as a merchant, political pamphleteer, and government intelligence agent before turning to long fiction relatively late in life. His journalism made him formidable and controversial in equal measure: *The Shortest-Way with the Dissenters* (1702), a sustained ironic pamphlet written in the voice of an extremist demanding the violent suppression of Dissenters like Defoe's own family, was taken literally by many readers, and once its irony was exposed, Defoe was prosecuted, fined, and put in the pillory. *Robinson Crusoe* (1719), his most famous work, presents itself as a genuine castaway's memoir, drawing partly on the real story of the sailor Alexander Selkirk, and combines a spiritual narrative of sin, isolation, and providential redemption with an unusually detailed, almost accountant-like record of practical survival — inventory-taking, tool-making, farming — that later critics have read as an early literary portrait of the resourceful, self-reliant economic individual. *Moll Flanders* (1722) applies the same first-person "true confession" technique to a female protagonist who survives poverty, multiple marriages, and a career as a thief through sheer resourcefulness, framed by a moralizing preface that sits in genuine tension with how sympathetically the narrative itself often treats her.

**Samuel Richardson** (1689–1761) worked for decades as a London printer before writing his first novel, almost by accident, in his early fifties: a publisher had commissioned him to write a model letter-writing guide for young people, and the project grew into *Pamela, or Virtue Rewarded* (1740), the epistolary story of a servant girl who resists her employer's repeated attempts at seduction and assault and is eventually rewarded with his genuine, reformed love and marriage. The novel was immensely popular and immediately controversial — many readers found its ending, in which resisted seduction is rewarded with upward marriage, more mercenary calculation than moral virtue, a suspicion Fielding's parody *Shamela* (1741) turned into open mockery. Richardson's second novel, *Clarissa, or the History of a Young Lady* (1747–1748), one of the longest novels in the English language, abandons *Pamela*'s comic resolution entirely: its heroine, pressured into an unwanted marriage by her own family, is deceived and ultimately raped by the charming, predatory Robert Lovelace, and dies a slow, spiritually dignified death after refusing, against enormous social pressure, to marry the man who violated her. *Clarissa* is widely regarded as Richardson's masterpiece and one of the great tragedies of English prose fiction.

**Henry Fielding** (1707–1754), unlike Defoe or Richardson, came from a gentry background, and began his career as a successful and politically dangerous playwright of stage satire aimed at Robert Walpole's government — work that, as explained above, the 1737 theatrical Licensing Act effectively ended. Fielding's turn to prose fiction began as parody: *Shamela* (1741) mocked *Pamela* directly, and its planned sequel, imagining Pamela's brother Joseph resisting seduction by his own employer, grew far beyond parody into *Joseph Andrews* (1742), Fielding's first full "comic epic in prose." *Tom Jones* (1749), his masterpiece, follows a foundling of mysterious parentage through an intricate, expansive plot and a broad panorama of English social life, narrated by a famously intrusive, witty narrator who regularly pauses the story to discuss the craft of fiction itself. Fielding later became a London magistrate and, with his half-brother John Fielding, helped establish the Bow Street Runners, an early organized police force — a second career whose close knowledge of crime and the courts informs his later, darker novel *Amelia* (1751).

**Tobias Smollett** (1721–1771), a Scottish writer trained as a naval surgeon, served at the disastrous 1741 Battle of Cartagena de Indias during the War of Jenkins' Ear, an experience that directly shapes the brutal, satirical naval scenes of his first novel, *Roderick Random* (1748). Smollett's fiction generally follows the picaresque model described above — looser, more episodic, and often rougher in its comedy and violence than Fielding's carefully architected plots — across *Roderick Random*, *Peregrine Pickle* (1751), and his final and most admired novel, *The Expedition of Humphry Clinker* (1771), an epistolary comic novel narrated through the letters of several different characters touring Britain together, whose contrasting perspectives on the very same events generate much of the book's comedy.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Robinson Crusoe* (1719).** Read as both spiritual autobiography and practical survival manual, the novel's central tension lies in how it fuses religious providence (Crusoe's isolation as divine punishment and eventual redemption) with an almost obsessively detailed economic self-sufficiency. Its treatment of Crusoe's relationship with Friday, the man he rescues and effectively enslaves and "civilizes," has become a central focus of modern postcolonial criticism, which reads the novel as an early and influential document of European colonial ideology as much as an adventure story.
- ***Moll Flanders* (1722).** The gap between the novel's moralizing preface, which frames Moll's story as a cautionary warning, and its actual narrative voice, which often treats her resourcefulness and survival with real sympathy, has made this novel a key test case for how far Defoe's own moral judgment can be separated from his narrator's.
- ***Pamela* (1740).** The controversy this novel triggered — was Pamela's resistance genuine virtue, or a calculated strategy for social advancement? — is arguably as important to literary history as the novel itself, because it produced an entire pamphlet war and, more lastingly, Fielding's career-launching parody.
- ***Clarissa* (1747–1748).** Unlike *Pamela*'s comic resolution, *Clarissa* refuses to reward its heroine's virtue with a happy marriage; her death instead becomes a kind of moral victory, since she preserves her own integrity even at the cost of her life. The novel's epistolary form, with letters flying between Clarissa, Lovelace, and their respective confidants, lets Richardson show you a rape's psychological aftermath, and the surrounding social pressure to "resolve" it through marriage, entirely through the characters' own competing, unreliable self-accounts.
- ***Joseph Andrews* (1742) and *Tom Jones* (1749).** Both novels announce, through their self-aware narrators, that comedy's proper target is human vanity and hypocrisy rather than physical misfortune — a theory Fielding draws explicitly from Cervantes. *Tom Jones*'s tightly engineered plot, in which a seemingly minor detail from early in the novel turns out, hundreds of pages later, to resolve the mystery of Tom's parentage, is often cited as a landmark of novelistic plot construction.
- ***Roderick Random* (1748) and *The Expedition of Humphry Clinker* (1771).** Smollett's picaresque structure trades Fielding's careful architecture for episodic momentum and satirical range, particularly effective in *Roderick Random*'s unsparing depiction of naval brutality. *Humphry Clinker*'s multiple letter-writing narrators, each describing the same journey through Britain from a distinct and often comically limited point of view, show the epistolary form, invented for Richardson's tragic purposes, working equally well for sustained social comedy.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Did the English novel really begin here?** Ian Watt's *The Rise of the Novel* remains the starting point for this debate, but most scholars today treat his account as too narrow — too focused on English, Protestant, middle-class experience, and too quick to treat Behn's earlier fiction and the Continental romance and picaresque traditions as mere background rather than genuine ancestors. The safest position is that this chapter's four writers didn't invent fiction from nothing, but did converge, in a short span of decades, on a powerful and lastingly influential set of techniques for making invented lives feel real.
- **Is Defoe's formal realism a truth-telling technique, or a rhetorical trick?** *Robinson Crusoe*'s detailed inventories and *Moll Flanders*'s circumstantial confessions are often praised for their vivid concreteness, but it's worth remembering that this concreteness is itself a carefully engineered literary effect, not a guarantee of accuracy — Defoe was, after all, a professional propagandist skilled at making invented arguments sound like plain fact.
- **Virtue or hypocrisy: how should we read Pamela?** The eighteenth century's own readers split sharply on this question, and the split still structures how critics discuss the novel: is Pamela a genuine moral heroine, or does the novel's reward structure quietly teach that chastity is a bargaining chip for social advancement? Fielding's *Shamela* takes the second position to satirical extremes, but even readers who find *Pamela* sincere generally admit the novel's ending sits uneasily with its own moral claims.
- **Richardson's "heart" versus Fielding's "action."** Samuel Johnson, whom you'll meet fully in Chapter 8, is reported to have said that anyone reading Richardson for the plot would hang themselves from impatience — praising instead Richardson's minute exploration of feeling and moral struggle, while finding Fielding's brisker, more externally plotted comic fiction shallower by comparison. Most modern critics resist ranking the two so firmly, treating "interior, epistolary intensity" and "external, ironic comic panorama" as two equally serious, equally influential answers to the same new problem: how should extended prose fiction represent a human life?

## Chapter Summary

Rising literacy, falling printing costs, and circulating libraries created a genuinely new mass readership for prose fiction in the first half of the eighteenth century, and four writers, working in four distinct technical modes, met that readership with what critics now call the early English novel. Defoe's *Robinson Crusoe* (1719) and *Moll Flanders* (1722) present fiction as authentic first-person confession, built on the detailed "formal realism" Ian Watt identified as the novel's defining innovation. Richardson's epistolary *Pamela* (1740) and *Clarissa* (1747–1748) use letters and "writing to the moment" to dramatize interior feeling and moral crisis, while Fielding, redirected from playwriting by the 1737 theatrical Licensing Act, developed a self-aware "comic epic in prose" in *Joseph Andrews* (1742) and *Tom Jones* (1749), modeled on Cervantes. Smollett's picaresque *Roderick Random* (1748) and *Humphry Clinker* (1771) extended the form's satirical and episodic possibilities. Later scholarship has complicated Watt's tidy account of the novel's "rise," pointing back to Aphra Behn (Chapter 2) and earlier prose traditions — but the convergence of techniques these four writers achieved in a few short decades shaped the form English fiction would follow for the next two centuries, carrying directly into the sentimental novel you'll meet in Part V.

## Glossary

- **Novel** — an extended prose narrative, generally realistic in setting and detail, centered on the developing lives and inner experience of individual characters.
- **Formal realism** — Ian Watt's term for the technique of building fictional credibility through detailed, specific, everyday circumstantial detail.
- **Epistolary novel** — a novel told entirely through the letters its characters write to one another.
- **"Writing to the moment"** — Richardson's term for narrating events and emotions as they happen, through letters written in the heat of the experience rather than in retrospect.
- **Comic epic in prose** — Fielding's own term, from his preface to *Joseph Andrews*, for a new kind of extended comic fiction modeled on epic structure and on Cervantes, treating human vanity and hypocrisy as its proper subject.
- **Picaresque** — an episodic narrative tradition following a resourceful, roguish protagonist (a *picaro*) through a loosely connected series of adventures.
