## Historical and Socio-Political Context

In Chapter 2, you met John Dryden as Restoration drama's leading figure, the master of the heroic play. This chapter follows him past the theatre, into the two areas of writing where his lasting reputation actually rests: satirical and religious poetry, and literary criticism. To understand both, you need one more piece of political background beyond what Chapter 1 already gave you.

Charles II had no legitimate son. His heir under the normal rules of succession was his younger brother James, Duke of York — and James had converted to Roman Catholicism, a fact that deeply alarmed a Protestant nation still only two decades removed from the religious and political chaos of the civil wars. In 1678, a former clergyman named Titus Oates fabricated a conspiracy story known as the Popish Plot, claiming Catholics were plotting to assassinate Charles II and place James on the throne by force. Though the plot was invented, the panic it caused was real, and it fed directly into the **Exclusion Crisis** of 1679–1681: a sustained political effort, led by the Earl of Shaftesbury and his allied faction (the beginnings of what we now call the Whig party), to pass a law excluding James from the succession altogether, in favor of Charles's illegitimate but Protestant son, the Duke of Monmouth. Charles resisted fiercely, dissolved Parliament rather than allow the exclusion bill to pass, and ultimately won this political battle — but not before it had produced deep public division and some of the greatest political poetry in the English language, written by Dryden in Charles's defense.

Dryden's own religious life tracked this turbulence closely. He had been raised in a Puritan household, praised Oliver Cromwell in verse on Cromwell's death in 1658, then just as readily celebrated the Restoration of Charles II in 1660 — a pivot some critics have called opportunistic and others have read as a genuine, principled preference for stability and order over ideology, whichever regime happened to provide it. After Charles's death in 1685, his brother did succeed as James II, and Dryden, remarkably, converted to Roman Catholicism that same year. When James was deposed only three years later, in the Glorious Revolution of 1688, and replaced by the Protestant William III and Mary II, Dryden — now a Catholic loyal to a deposed king — lost the royal offices and income he had held for two decades. He spent his final years writing not from royal favor but for his living, chiefly through translation.

## Literary Movements

This chapter introduces a term you will meet repeatedly for the rest of Part IV: **Augustan**. English writers of this period admired the Roman poets Virgil, Horace, and Juvenal, who wrote under the emperor Augustus in a period Romans themselves saw as one of order, stability, and cultural achievement after decades of civil war. Restoration and early eighteenth-century writers, having just lived through their own civil wars and their own restoration of order, self-consciously modeled their poetry — its reason, its decorum, its preference for balanced form over wild invention — on these Roman models, and later literary historians named the whole period after the analogy. Dryden is the writer who does more than anyone else to establish this Augustan style in English.

The heroic couplet, which you already met in Chapter 2 as the verse form of heroic tragedy, is central here too — but in a completely different register. On stage, Dryden used the couplet for elevated, serious speech. In his satirical poetry, he uses the very same form for precision, wit, and controlled aggression: each couplet works almost like a closed argument, building to a sharp, quotable final rhyme. This chapter's two major poems, *Absalom and Achitophel* and *Mac Flecknoe*, both use the couplet this way, but through two different satirical strategies.

*Absalom and Achitophel* works through **allegory**: it retells the Exclusion Crisis as a story from the biblical Second Book of Samuel, in which King David's son Absalom rebels against his father, egged on by the treacherous counselor Achitophel. Dryden's readers would have recognized immediately that "David" stood for Charles II, "Absalom" for the Duke of Monmouth, and "Achitophel" for the Earl of Shaftesbury — a technique that let Dryden attack living political figures with the moral authority of scripture behind him, while giving his poem a dignity ordinary topical satire wouldn't have had. *Mac Flecknoe*, by contrast, works through **mock-heroic** (sometimes called mock-epic): it borrows the grand machinery of epic poetry — solemn diction, a formal coronation scene, echoes of Virgil — and applies all of it to a deliberately trivial and personal subject, the "crowning" of the poet Thomas Shadwell as heir to a kingdom of bad writing. You should hold onto this device carefully, because Alexander Pope will build an entire epic-length satire, *The Dunciad*, out of exactly this same mock-heroic strategy, and you'll study it directly in Chapter 6.

## Author Profiles

**John Dryden** (1631–1700) was educated at Westminster School under the famously strict headmaster Richard Busby, then at Trinity College, Cambridge. His career, as the historical background above shows you, moved through nearly every major political and religious realignment of his century: from a Puritan household and an elegy for Cromwell, to celebratory Restoration poetry and the office of Poet Laureate (from 1668) and Historiographer Royal (from 1670), to a late conversion to Roman Catholicism that cost him both offices when James II was deposed in 1688. Across all of this, Dryden also worked continuously as a literary critic — his essay *Of Dramatick Poesie* (1668), written as a dialogue between four speakers debating ancient versus modern drama and English versus French tragedy, is widely regarded as the first substantial work of English literary criticism in the modern sense, and the eighteenth-century critic and biographer Samuel Johnson later called Dryden "the father of English criticism." In his final years, stripped of royal income, Dryden turned to translation to earn his living, producing a landmark English *Aeneid* (1697) and, in his last major work, *Fables Ancient and Modern* (1700), a collection of translations and adaptations from Ovid, Boccaccio, Homer — and Chaucer, whose *Canterbury Tales* (Part II, Chapter 3) Dryden helped reintroduce to a contemporary readership nearly three centuries after Chaucer wrote them. He died in 1700 and was buried in Westminster Abbey, close to Chaucer's own grave.

**Thomas Shadwell** (c. 1642–1692) was a dramatist and poet who became the target of *Mac Flecknoe*'s satire, cast as the dull, untalented heir to the "kingdom" of bad poetry. The two men had once been on reasonably friendly terms — they disagreed mainly over literary and political questions, Shadwell favoring the older dramatist Ben Jonson's style of comedy and, eventually, siding with the Whig opposition Dryden's *Absalom and Achitophel* had attacked. The story does not end with Dryden's mockery, though: after the Glorious Revolution of 1688, it was Shadwell, a committed Protestant with the right political sympathies for the new regime, who was appointed Poet Laureate in Dryden's place — a genuinely ironic turn of literary history that later critics have rarely resisted pointing out.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Annus Mirabilis* (1667).** The title means "year of wonders," and the poem covers two of the events of 1666 you already know from Chapter 1: England's naval war with the Dutch, and the Great Fire of London. Dryden treats both as trials England survives and, in the Fire's case, is even renewed by — an early example of his lifelong instinct to read recent history as evidence of underlying national order and resilience, whatever chaos it looks like on the surface.
- ***Of Dramatick Poesie* (1668).** Structured as a dialogue among four gentlemen debating the merits of ancient versus modern drama, and French versus English tragedy, this essay does something genuinely new: it takes literary criticism seriously as a form of reasoned argument rather than casual opinion or a poet's personal preface. Notice that Dryden lets multiple, unresolved positions coexist in the dialogue rather than simply declaring a winner — a formal choice that mirrors the "balanced critical presentation" you have seen this book itself aim for whenever scholarly opinion is genuinely divided.
- ***Absalom and Achitophel* (1681).** As explained above, this poem retells the Exclusion Crisis as the biblical rebellion of Absalom against King David. Its most famous passage is its devastating portrait of Achitophel/Shaftesbury as "a fiery soul, which working out its way, / Fretted the pigmy body to decay" — a couplet that manages to be an insult, a psychological character study, and a piece of political argument all at once, which is exactly the kind of compression Dryden's couplet style specializes in.
- ***Mac Flecknoe* (1682, written c. 1676).** Here Dryden imagines the recently deceased bad poet Richard Flecknoe formally crowning Thomas Shadwell as his successor, "Mac Flecknoe" (son of Flecknoe), ruler over the realm of Nonsense. Every device of solemn epic — invocation, procession, coronation speech — is deployed in service of an entirely personal literary grudge, and the gap between the poem's grand manner and its trivial occasion is the whole joke. As you'll see in Chapter 6, Pope studied this poem closely.
- ***Religio Laici* (1682) and *The Hind and the Panther* (1687).** These two poems track Dryden's religious journey directly. *Religio Laici* ("A Layman's Faith") defends a moderate Anglican position, arguing that reason and Scripture, sensibly applied, are sufficient guides to faith without requiring either Catholic authority or radical Protestant enthusiasm. Only five years later, after his own conversion, *The Hind and the Panther* reverses much of that argument through an animal fable — a milk-white Hind representing the Roman Catholic Church, a spotted Panther representing the Church of England — defending the very Catholic position the earlier poem had argued against. Read together, the two poems are an unusually candid record of a major poet changing his mind in public, under real political pressure, and trying to make that change sound principled rather than merely convenient.
- **Translations: Virgil's *Aeneid* (1697) and *Fables Ancient and Modern* (1700).** Deprived of court income after 1688, Dryden turned his verse skill toward translation, producing an *Aeneid* still read and admired today, and closing his career with *Fables*, a collection that deliberately places Chaucer, Ovid, Boccaccio, and Homer side by side, implicitly arguing that English poetry belongs in the same company as the great classical and continental traditions.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Dryden a turncoat, or a consistent advocate of order?** His shifts — Cromwellian elegist to royalist poet, moderate Anglican to Roman Catholic convert — have drawn both charges. Critics sympathetic to Dryden point out that in every case he sided with the government or institution that seemed, to him, best able to secure political and religious stability after chaos; critics less sympathetic note that this pattern of belief also happened to track his own career interests remarkably closely. Both readings can be true at once, and the tension between them is part of why Dryden remains a genuinely debated figure rather than a settled one.
- **Is *Absalom and Achitophel* still worth reading once you don't know the politics?** The poem is built entirely around a specific crisis of 1679–1681, and its allegorical portraits only land fully once you know who Achitophel and Absalom actually represent. Most critics argue the poem survives this dependence on context because its psychological portraiture — especially of Achitophel's restless ambition — works as genuine character study, independent of the politics, in exactly the way Shakespeare's history plays (Part III) still work for readers who know nothing about the real Plantagenet succession disputes.
- **Does Dryden deserve the title "father of English criticism"?** Johnson's phrase has stuck, and it's worth asking what earns it. Dryden was not the first English writer to comment on literature, but he was arguably the first to treat criticism as a sustained, reasoned discipline, weighing rival positions (ancients versus moderns, rhyme versus blank verse) with real argumentative care rather than simple assertion — a mode of writing about literature this book's own critical apparatus, in its own small way, continues.
- **How should we read mock-heroic satire — as playful or as genuinely cruel?** *Mac Flecknoe* is often taught as a comic tour de force, and it is very funny. But it is also a sustained, public humiliation of a real, living writer, over what amounts to a literary and political disagreement. Holding both facts together — the poem's brilliance and its real cruelty — prepares you well for Pope's *The Dunciad* in Chapter 6, which raises exactly the same tension on a much larger scale.

## Chapter Summary

Beyond the heroic tragedies you studied in Chapter 2, Dryden's lasting reputation rests on his satirical and religious poetry and on his pioneering work as a literary critic. The Exclusion Crisis of 1679–1681, sparked by fears over the Catholic James, Duke of York's succession, gave Dryden the occasion for *Absalom and Achitophel* (1681), a biblical allegory defending Charles II and attacking the Earl of Shaftesbury, while his earlier personal grudge against the poet Thomas Shadwell produced *Mac Flecknoe* (1682), the founding masterpiece of English mock-heroic satire and the direct model for Pope's *The Dunciad* in Chapter 6. Dryden's own religious journey, from moderate Anglicanism in *Religio Laici* (1682) to Roman Catholicism in *The Hind and the Panther* (1687), cost him his royal offices after the Glorious Revolution of 1688 — including the Poet Laureateship, which passed, ironically, to Shadwell himself. His critical essay *Of Dramatick Poesie* (1668) earned him Samuel Johnson's later title "the father of English criticism," and his late translations, especially of Virgil's *Aeneid* (1697) and the Chaucer adaptations in *Fables Ancient and Modern* (1700), closed a career that, across poetry, drama, and criticism, did more than any other single writer's to establish the Augustan style that dominates the rest of Part IV.

## Glossary

- **Augustan** — a term applied to English literature of the Restoration and early eighteenth century, describing its self-conscious imitation of the order, decorum, and classical models associated with the reign of the Roman emperor Augustus.
- **Exclusion Crisis** — the political conflict of 1679–1681 over attempts to exclude the Catholic James, Duke of York, from the English succession.
- **Popish Plot** — a fabricated 1678 conspiracy story invented by Titus Oates, alleging a Catholic assassination plot against Charles II.
- **Allegory** — a narrative in which characters and events systematically represent other, usually more abstract or historically specific, meanings; Dryden uses biblical allegory in *Absalom and Achitophel* to represent contemporary politics.
- **Mock-heroic (mock-epic)** — a satirical mode that applies the grand conventions of epic poetry to a deliberately trivial subject, for ironic and comic effect.
- **Poet Laureate** — an official royal appointment recognizing a nation's leading poet, held by Dryden from 1668 to 1688.
