## Historical and Socio-Political Context

You already know, from Chapter 1, that Parliament closed London's public theatres in 1642 and that they stayed dark for the whole eighteen years of the Interregnum. This chapter picks up exactly where that leaves off: the moment the theatres reopen, and what English drama becomes once they do.

Charles II did not simply lift the ban and let anyone stage a play. In August 1660, he granted royal letters patent — official, exclusive licenses — to two men: Thomas Killigrew and William Davenant. This created a legal monopoly. Only Killigrew's King's Company and Davenant's Duke's Company were permitted to perform "legitimate" spoken drama in London, a restriction that (with later changes of ownership) remained in force until 1843. Keep that word **patent** in mind; it is why this period's whole commercial theatre scene runs through just two companies rather than dozens.

One change mattered more than any other for the plays themselves: for the first time, women were legally permitted to act female roles on the public stage. Before 1660, boy actors had always played women's parts, going all the way back through Shakespeare's original productions in Part III. Samuel Pepys — whom you met in Chapter 1 as a diarist — happened to record the moment for us. On 3 January 1661, he went to see a play called *The Beggar's Bush* and wrote in his diary that it was "the first time that ever I saw women come upon the stage." Actresses quickly became central to Restoration theatre's appeal and its scandal alike; the most famous of them, Nell Gwyn, began as a comic actress in the mid-1660s and later became Charles II's mistress, a career path that tells you a good deal about how closely the Restoration stage and the Restoration court overlapped.

Out of this reopened, patent-controlled, newly mixed-gender theatre, two very different dramatic modes emerged, and this chapter covers both. The first is **comedy of manners**: witty, sexually frank comedies about the courtship, marriage, and social games of fashionable upper-class society, closely tied to the sophisticated, cynical, French-influenced tone of Charles II's own court (recall, from Chapter 1, that Charles spent his exile years in France). The second is **heroic tragedy**, sometimes called the "heroic play": grandiose, elevated tragedies written in rhymed couplets rather than the blank verse Shakespeare and his contemporaries had used, dramatizing extreme conflicts between love and honor among noble, larger-than-life heroes in exotic, distant settings. Where comedy of manners laughs at its own society, heroic tragedy escapes from it entirely — but both, in their different ways, are responses to a generation that had just lived through civil war, regicide, and restoration, and wanted its theatre either to mock its own excesses or to imagine nobility on a scale ordinary recent history hadn't offered.

## Literary Movements

Start with **comedy of manners**. The name tells you what to expect: comedy built around the manners — the social codes, courtship rituals, and hypocrisies — of a narrow, fashionable, wealthy world. Its typical plot involves witty, sexually knowing young men and women (critics often call the male version a "rake") maneuvering through marriage, adultery, and social one-upmanship, all conducted through fast, clever dialogue called repartee. It is deliberately amoral in tone: characters are judged by their wit and social skill, not by conventional virtue, and the genre takes real pleasure in exposing fools, prudes, and social climbers to ridicule. This comedy could not have existed under the Interregnum's Puritan disapproval of the stage; it is, in a real sense, a theatrical celebration of everything Puritanism had tried to suppress, staged for an audience that included the pleasure-loving court of Charles II itself.

**Heroic tragedy** is comedy of manners' opposite in almost every respect. Instead of contemporary London drawing rooms, it is set in distant, exoticized locations — Peru, Granada, Morocco, Mughal India — and instead of colloquial wit, it uses the **heroic couplet**: pairs of rhymed, metrically regular lines, borrowed partly from French tragedy (especially the plays of Pierre Corneille) and from earlier English heroic romance. Its heroes are extraordinary figures torn between love and honor, and its scale is deliberately larger than life. John Dryden, whom you will meet in full in Chapter 3, was the form's leading practitioner and its most articulate theorist; his prefaces explain and defend the heroic play as a serious, elevated alternative to both everyday comedy and Shakespearean tragedy's mixed, less regular structure. Not everyone found the mode convincing — the Duke of Buckingham's 1671 satire *The Rehearsal* mocked its overblown rhetoric mercilessly, through a stand-in playwright character named Bayes — but heroic tragedy dominated serious Restoration drama through the 1670s all the same, until Dryden himself moved toward a calmer blank-verse tragic style with *All for Love* (1677), a deliberate homage to Shakespeare's *Antony and Cleopatra* (Part III, Chapter 6).

## Author Profiles

**Thomas Killigrew** (1612–1683) and **Sir William Davenant** (1606–1668) were the two men Charles II entrusted with rebuilding English professional theatre after 1660. Killigrew, a courtier and playwright who had shared the King's exile, led the King's Company; Davenant, a poet and theatrical entrepreneur who had staged limited entertainments even during part of the Interregnum by carefully avoiding the ban on outright spoken drama, led the Duke's Company. Between them, their patents effectively defined the boundaries of legitimate London theatre for close to two centuries, and their companies trained or employed nearly every major dramatist and actor discussed in this chapter.

**Sir George Etherege** (c. 1636–1692) is often credited with writing the first fully realized Restoration comedy of manners, *The Comical Revenge* (1664), and later perfected the form in *The Man of Mode, or, Sir Fopling Flutter* (1676). Little is known with certainty about his early life, but his own career — a fashionable wit who moved easily through Restoration court society and later served as a diplomat — closely resembles the rakish, socially adept heroes his plays celebrate.

**William Wycherley** (1641–1715), educated partly in France, wrote some of the sharpest and most sexually explicit comedies of the period, including *The Country Wife* (1675) and *The Plain Dealer* (1676). His comedy is notably harder-edged and more satirical than Etherege's: where Etherege's wit is largely good-humored, Wycherley's plays repeatedly expose real cruelty and hypocrisy beneath fashionable manners, which is one reason later, more censorious ages found him considerably more shocking than his contemporaries.

**Aphra Behn** (1640–1689) is one of the most important figures in this entire chapter, and you will meet her again, in much fuller biographical detail, in Chapter 9, which is devoted to women's writing of the period. She is generally recognized as the first Englishwoman to earn her living as a professional writer, and her comedy *The Rover* (1677), set among exiled English cavaliers in Naples, is among the most frequently performed Restoration plays today. Before turning fully to writing, Behn had worked as a spy for Charles II's government in Antwerp during the Second Anglo-Dutch War — a detail worth holding onto, because it says something about how unusually wide a range of roles this period's most remarkable woman writer actually moved through.

**John Dryden** (1631–1700) receives his full biographical treatment in Chapter 3, where you will study him primarily as a poet, satirist, and critic. Here, though, you need to know him as Restoration drama's dominant figure: he wrote extensively for both comedy and tragedy, but his lasting dramatic reputation rests on the heroic plays discussed above, especially *The Conquest of Granada* (1670–71), and on his later move toward blank-verse tragedy in *All for Love* (1677). He also served as England's Poet Laureate from 1668, a royal appointment that placed him at the very center of Restoration literary culture.

**William Congreve** (1670–1729) belongs to a slightly younger generation, and his masterpiece, *The Way of the World* (1700), is often treated as the final, most polished expression of Restoration comedy of manners — arriving just as the mode's popularity was beginning to decline under the pressure of moral criticism discussed below. Congreve's dialogue is famous for its precision and control; the play's central courtship negotiation between its two leads, Mirabell and Millamant, is frequently cited as the wittiest and most psychologically honest scene the genre ever produced.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Etherege, *The Man of Mode* (1676).** This play gives you the genre's defining type in its purest form: Dorimant, a brilliant, charming, thoroughly amoral rake, modeled in part on real Restoration courtiers. The comedy doesn't punish Dorimant for his manipulations of women; it admires his wit even while exposing his cruelty, which is exactly the kind of moral ambiguity that later critics, from Collier onward, found troubling.
- **Wycherley, *The Country Wife* (1675).** The plot turns on a scandalous device: the rake Horner spreads a false rumor that he has been made impotent by venereal disease, so that jealous husbands will let him near their wives unsupervised — a premise built entirely around sexual deception and social hypocrisy. Read alongside Etherege, it shows you how far comedy of manners was willing to push its satire of marriage and reputation.
- **Behn, *The Rover* (1677).** Set among Royalist exiles waiting out the Interregnum abroad, the play gives its female characters — especially the witty Hellena — a degree of agency and voice unusual for the genre, which many modern critics read as reflecting Behn's own position as a woman succeeding in a heavily male-dominated profession. It remains the most widely studied and performed Restoration comedy by a woman playwright.
- **Dryden, *The Conquest of Granada*, Parts I and II (1670–71).** This is heroic tragedy at full scale: a two-part play set during the Christian conquest of Muslim Granada, built around the impossibly noble hero Almanzor, whose conflicts between love and honor are worked out entirely in rhymed heroic couplets. Read it as a demonstration of Dryden's own theory — that tragedy should be elevated, formally rhymed, and larger than ordinary life — before you compare it, in Chapter 3, with his satirical poetry, which uses the very same couplet form for entirely opposite comic and satiric purposes.
- **Congreve, *The Way of the World* (1700).** Often considered the most sophisticated Restoration comedy, its famous "proviso scene" — in which Mirabell and Millamant negotiate, with wit and precision, the exact terms on which they will agree to marry — treats marriage not as a romantic conclusion but as a contract to be argued over as equals. It is a fitting capstone to the genre: self-aware, formally controlled, and already a little nostalgic for a theatrical mode about to fall out of critical favor.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is Restoration comedy immoral, or is it satire?** This question dominated criticism of the genre almost from the start, and it came to a head in 1698, when the clergyman Jeremy Collier published *A Short View of the Immorality and Profaneness of the English Stage*, a furious attack on Restoration drama's casual treatment of adultery, deception, and profanity. Some critics have read the genre's wit and amorality as genuine celebration of aristocratic license; others read it as satire that exposes precisely the hypocrisy and cruelty it depicts, without necessarily endorsing it. Both readings can coexist within a single play, which is part of why the debate has never fully settled.
- **What did heroic tragedy have to do with the trauma of the civil wars?** Critics have suggested that heroic tragedy's obsession with extreme, idealized honor and its noble, uncompromising heroes reflects a generation's wish to imagine a nobility that recent English history — civil war, regicide, an uncertain restoration — had rather brutally failed to supply. Reading the genre this way turns its apparent escapism into something more psychologically pointed: a fantasy compensation for a real and recent political humiliation.
- **How should we place Aphra Behn in literary history?** For much of the eighteenth, nineteenth, and early twentieth centuries, Behn's reputation suffered because of the same sexual frankness that was tolerated, even celebrated, in her male contemporaries. Since the later twentieth century, feminist literary criticism has done enormous work to recover her as a founding figure of women's professional authorship in English, not a marginal curiosity — a reassessment you will see continued and deepened in Chapter 9.
- **Did Collier's attack actually change English comedy?** Historians of the theatre generally agree that Collier's 1698 pamphlet, and the broader shift in taste it both reflected and accelerated, pushed English comedy toward a gentler, more moralized style over the following decades — a development usually called "sentimental comedy," which belongs to the period covered in Part V. Congreve's *The Way of the World* (1700), in this light, can be read as one of the last great examples of the older, unapologetically witty and amoral mode, arriving just as its cultural moment was closing.

## Chapter Summary

The London stage reopened after 1660 under a royal patent system that gave Thomas Killigrew's King's Company and William Davenant's Duke's Company exclusive control of legitimate drama, and for the first time women were legally permitted to act — a change Samuel Pepys happened to record firsthand in January 1661. From this reopened, mixed-gender theatre grew two contrasting dramatic modes: comedy of manners, a witty, sexually frank satire of fashionable society practiced by Etherege, Wycherley, Behn, and, at the century's close, Congreve; and heroic tragedy, a grandiose, rhymed-couplet drama of love and honor dominated by John Dryden, whose full literary career you will study in Chapter 3. Jeremy Collier's 1698 attack on the stage's morality marked a real turning point, pushing English comedy toward the gentler "sentimental" style you will meet in Part V, even as Congreve's *The Way of the World* (1700) closed out the older mode at the height of its formal sophistication. Aphra Behn's dramatic success, introduced here, is only the first half of her significance to this book — Chapter 9 returns to her as a founding figure of English women's professional writing.

## Glossary

- **Patent (theatrical)** — a royal license granting exclusive legal right to stage spoken drama; in 1660, granted only to the King's Company and the Duke's Company.
- **Comedy of manners** — a comic genre satirizing the social codes and sexual intrigues of a fashionable upper-class world, marked by witty repartee and morally ambiguous characters.
- **Rake** — the stock male protagonist of comedy of manners: witty, sexually predatory, and socially skilled, admired for his cleverness despite his moral failings.
- **Heroic tragedy (heroic play)** — a grandiose Restoration tragic form, written in rhymed heroic couplets, dramatizing conflicts between love and honor among noble heroes in exotic settings.
- **Heroic couplet** — a pair of rhymed, metrically regular lines (usually iambic pentameter), the dominant verse form of heroic tragedy and, as you'll see in Chapter 3, of Restoration and Augustan satire alike.
- **Proviso scene** — a scene, most famously in Congreve's *The Way of the World*, in which prospective marriage partners negotiate the explicit terms of their future relationship.
