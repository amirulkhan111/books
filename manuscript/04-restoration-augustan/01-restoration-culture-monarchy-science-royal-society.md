## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Welcome to Part IV. You left Part III with Milton — blind, politically defeated, dictating an epic poem line by line to whoever would sit and write it down for him. It's worth pausing on that image for a moment, because it's also the doorway into this chapter. Milton wrote *Paradise Lost* between roughly 1658 and 1663, and published it in 1667. That means the poem you studied at the end of Part III was composed *inside* the world this chapter is about to describe. Milton had spent the 1650s as a champion of the republic that executed a king. Then, in 1660, that king's son came back — and Milton had to go on living, and writing, in the England that resulted. Understanding that England is the job of this chapter.

Start with the basic political fact. In January 1649, Parliament's army had put Charles I on trial and beheaded him — an almost unthinkable act in seventeenth-century Europe, a king killed by his own subjects. England then spent eleven years without a monarch at all, a period historians call the **Interregnum** (literally, "between reigns"). For most of that time it was governed as a republic, and from 1653 as a Protectorate under Oliver Cromwell, a former Member of Parliament turned military commander who ruled with the title Lord Protector until his death in 1658. Cromwell's regime was dominated by Puritanism — a strict, reform-minded strain of English Protestantism that treated most public entertainment, and especially the theatre, as morally dangerous. Parliament had already closed London's playhouses by ordinance in 1642, at the start of the civil wars, and they stayed shut for the whole Interregnum.

Cromwell's son and successor as Protector, Richard, could not hold the regime together, and by 1660 the political establishment — army officers, Parliament, and the country generally — had decided the safest way out of over a decade of instability was to invite the exiled royal family back. Charles I's son landed in England in May 1660 and was crowned Charles II. This is the **Restoration**: not just a change of ruler, but a deliberate, symbolically loaded return to the monarchy, the Church of England, and much of the social order the civil wars had interrupted. Before he even set foot in England, Charles issued the Declaration of Breda, promising a general pardon to his former enemies (with a short list of exceptions, chiefly the men who had actually signed his father's death warrant) and a degree of religious toleration. Parliament confirmed most of this in the Act of Indemnity and Oblivion — but toleration did not last. Over the next five years, a set of laws now known as the Clarendon Code narrowed the Church of England back down to a single, conformist national church, and pushed Puritans and other Nonconformists — Protestants who would not accept the re-established Church's rules — to the margins of public life.

Why should a student of *literature* care about all this parliamentary and religious maneuvering? Because Restoration literary culture is, in a real sense, a culture of return and reopening. The theatres reopened, for the first time with women legally permitted to act female roles on the public stage (Chapter 2 covers this in full). The court reopened, and Charles II — who had spent his exile years in France — brought back a taste for French and continental fashions in wit, drama, and manners that shaped English poetry and drama for the next several decades (you'll meet this current running through Dryden in Chapter 3 and Pope in Chapter 6). And, as this chapter's second half explains, organized experimental science reopened too, in a newly formal and public way, through the founding of the Royal Society. All three reopenings share a common temper: a self-conscious turn away from what the Restoration generation increasingly characterized as Puritan enthusiasm, dogmatism, and gloom, toward reason, observation, wit, and sociable good sense.

That temper had a dark undertow, though, and this chapter would be misleading if it left it out. The 1660s were not simply an escape into gaiety. In 1665, bubonic plague swept London, killing perhaps 100,000 people — roughly a fifth of the city's population — in what is now called the Great Plague. The following year, a fire that started in a Pudding Lane bakery burned for four days and destroyed most of the medieval City of London, including St Paul's Cathedral, in the Great Fire of London (2–6 September 1666). You will meet both catastrophes again shortly, because the two diarists at the center of this chapter, Samuel Pepys and John Evelyn, lived through them and wrote them down almost as they happened.

## Literary Movements

This early Restoration moment does not yet have the settled poetic movements you will meet later in Part IV — Dryden's satire, Pope's mock-epic, the periodical essay. What it has instead is a *cultural mood*, and one specific institutional development so important to English prose that it belongs alongside any literary movement: the rise of the **Royal Society** and the style of writing it deliberately promoted.

The Royal Society (its full early name was the Royal Society of London for Improving Natural Knowledge) grew out of informal gatherings of scholars, some going back to the 1640s, who met to discuss what they called the "new philosophy" — what we would now call experimental science, built on direct observation and repeatable experiment rather than on inherited authority from ancient writers like Aristotle. After the Restoration, this informal circle organized itself formally, meeting from November 1660 and receiving its first royal charter from Charles II in 1662. Its unofficial motto, *Nullius in verba* — roughly, "take nobody's word for it" — tells you everything about its values: trust evidence, not authority.

That value system had a direct literary consequence. In 1667, one of the Society's own members, Thomas Sprat, published *The History of the Royal Society*, which included a famous call for a new kind of English prose: plain, exact, and stripped of ornamental rhetoric — what Sprat described as returning language to "a close, naked, natural way of speaking." This is often called the Royal Society's **plain style** program. You should not take it as a claim that all Restoration prose instantly became simple (plenty of it stayed elaborate), but as a real and influential ideal that pushed English prose steadily toward clarity, precision, and economy over the following decades — an ideal you will see pay off directly when you reach the periodical essayists Addison and Steele in Chapter 4, whose famously clear, conversational prose descends in part from exactly this program.

Alongside this institutional development, the diary itself — a private, day-by-day record, not originally written for publication — becomes an unexpectedly major literary form in this period, thanks above all to Pepys and Evelyn, whom you'll meet properly below. And Thomas Hobbes's *Leviathan*, though written and published slightly earlier, during the Interregnum, supplies much of the political vocabulary the entire Restoration period argues with and against — so it belongs here too, as essential background to everything else in Part IV.

## Author Profiles

**Thomas Hobbes** (1588–1679) was an English philosopher who lived through the entire upheaval this chapter describes: he was already sixty when Charles I was executed, and he lived another twenty-one years into the Restoration. His major work, *Leviathan* (1651), argues that human life without strong, centralized government is a "war of all against all," and that people are better off surrendering their individual freedoms to an absolute sovereign power in exchange for peace and security. Hobbes wrote this during the Interregnum, partly to justify obedience to whatever government could actually keep order — a strikingly pragmatic argument for a period when governments kept changing. After the Restoration, *Leviathan*'s materialism and its unsettling implication that religious authority, too, should answer to the sovereign made Hobbes a controversial figure; the Royal Society itself, despite sharing his interest in the "new philosophy," never admitted him as a member.

**Samuel Pepys** (1633–1703) was a naval administrator whose surviving personal diary, kept in shorthand between 1660 and 1669, has become one of the most valuable and most enjoyable documents to survive from the period. Pepys was there for the Restoration itself, for the Great Plague, and for the Great Fire, and he wrote all three down in vivid, unguarded, often very funny detail — alongside a running account of his own career, marriage, ambitions, and vanities that he clearly never expected anyone else to read. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1665 and later served as its President from 1684 to 1686. He stopped keeping the diary in 1669, believing (mistakenly, as it turned out) that his failing eyesight meant he could no longer safely write in the dark shorthand he used to keep it private.

**John Evelyn** (1620–1706) was a landed gentleman, writer, and founding member of the Royal Society whose own diary spans a much longer stretch of his life than Pepys's — from the 1640s until shortly before his death in 1706. Evelyn's entries tend to be more formal and reflective than Pepys's, but he too gives you an eyewitness account of the Plague and the Fire, and his personal friendship with Pepys links the two diarists directly. Evelyn was also a serious and practical scientific writer in his own right: his book *Sylva* (1664), commissioned by the Royal Society, is a treatise on tree cultivation written partly to address England's growing shortage of timber, and it became one of the most frequently reprinted books of practical natural history of its century.

**Robert Boyle** (1627–1691) was a chemist and physicist, and one of the Royal Society's founding members, whose careful experimental method made him something like a living demonstration of the Society's own values. His 1662 formulation of what is now called Boyle's Law (describing the relationship between the pressure and volume of a gas) came out of exactly the kind of repeatable, evidence-based experiment the Society's motto demanded. Boyle wrote extensively and clearly about his experiments and their reasoning, and later historians of science have often pointed to his prose, alongside Sprat's manifesto, as an example of the new plain, demonstrative style the Society encouraged.

**Thomas Sprat** (1635–1713) was a clergyman and writer, later Bishop of Rochester, whose *History of the Royal Society* (1667) is less a neutral history than a piece of advocacy — a deliberate, persuasive account of what the Society was for and why English readers should support it. Its call for plain, unornamented prose, discussed above, makes it one of the most influential single pieces of writing about English prose style produced in the entire seventeenth century, even though few readers today approach it as literature in its own right.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Hobbes, *Leviathan* (1651).** Picture a book trying to solve one problem: why should anyone obey a government at all? Hobbes's answer is that without one, life would be, in his own famous phrase, "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." His solution — a single, near-absolute sovereign authority that citizens agree to obey in exchange for protection — was radical in its reasoning (built from first principles about human nature, not from religious tradition) even though its conclusion (support strong monarchy) suited the Restoration establishment. That combination of radical method and conservative-looking conclusion is exactly why *Leviathan* remained controversial on all sides for the rest of the century.
- **Pepys, *The Diary of Samuel Pepys* (1660–1669, not published until the nineteenth century).** This is not a literary work in the sense Milton's epic is — Pepys never intended it for readers. That is precisely what makes it so valuable and so readable: you get Restoration London completely unfiltered, from the excitement of the King's return, through Pepys's own career anxieties and household squabbles, to his terror during the Fire as he buried his prized parmesan cheese and wine in his garden to save them. Modern critics increasingly read the *Diary* as a genuine literary achievement in its own right — an early, unintentional masterpiece of self-observation and narrative pacing — rather than merely a historical record that happens to be well written.
- **Evelyn, *Sylva* (1664).** On its surface, this is a practical manual about planting and managing trees, commissioned because England's navy and building trade were consuming timber faster than the country's woodlands could replace it. Read alongside the Royal Society's broader mission, though, *Sylva* is a good example of what "improving natural knowledge" actually meant in practice: useful, applied science, written to be read and acted on by landowners, not just debated by scholars.
- **Sprat, *The History of the Royal Society* (1667).** This is best read less as history and more as manifesto. Sprat is writing to defend the young Society against critics who saw experimental science as either impious or simply a waste of gentlemen's time, and to recruit support for it. His argument for plain, "naked" prose is itself a piece of persuasive rhetoric — which is a useful irony to notice: a call for stripped-down language, delivered in carefully crafted sentences designed to win an argument.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is Pepys's diary "literature"?** For a long time, scholars treated the *Diary* mainly as a historical source — a wonderfully detailed window onto Restoration London, but not a text to be read the way you'd read a poem or a play. More recent criticism pushes back on that division, arguing that Pepys's control of pace, detail, and self-revelation (his honesty about his own jealousy, ambition, and infidelities, for instance) shows real narrative skill, whether or not he thought of himself as a writer. The disagreement is really a disagreement about what counts as literature in the first place — a question worth holding onto as you move through the rest of this book.
- **Did the Royal Society actually change English prose, or just claim to?** Sprat's plain-style manifesto is famous, but historians of the English language caution against assuming it single-handedly reformed English prose overnight. Restoration and eighteenth-century writing did move, gradually, toward greater clarity and less ornamentation — but that shift had multiple causes (growing literacy, a growing middle-class readership, the rise of newspapers and periodicals) and the Royal Society's manifesto is best understood as one influential voice in a broader trend, not its sole cause.
- **How much did the Restoration actually restore?** It's tempting to read 1660 as a simple reset button — Puritan rule out, monarchy and old customs back in. Historians increasingly resist that picture. Eleven years of republican and Protectorate government had changed English political culture in ways that couldn't simply be undone: Parliament had demonstrated it could depose and even execute a king, and no later Stuart monarch, including Charles II, could fully act as if that fact wasn't now part of English political memory. Keep this in mind through the rest of Part IV — the political tension between monarchy and Parliament keeps resurfacing, most sharply in the Glorious Revolution of 1688, a few decades after this chapter.
- **What was Hobbes's real relationship to the "new science"?** Hobbes wanted to be taken seriously as a natural philosopher himself, and he engaged directly, and combatively, with Boyle over the correct interpretation of some of Boyle's air-pump experiments. The Royal Society's founders, despite sharing Hobbes's general commitment to observation and reasoned argument, kept him at arm's length — a reminder that the boundaries of a new intellectual movement are often drawn as much by personality and politics as by pure method.

## Chapter Summary

The Restoration of 1660 was far more than a change of monarch. It ended eleven years of republican and Protectorate government, reopened the theatres Parliament had shut in 1642, and reshaped English religious life through the restrictive Clarendon Code — while also opening space for a newly organized, newly public culture of experimental science through the founding of the Royal Society in 1660 and its royal charter in 1662. Thomas Hobbes's *Leviathan* (1651), written just before this period but essential to understanding it, supplied a controversial new argument for absolute sovereignty that the Restoration establishment could use, even as Hobbes himself remained an outsider to the Royal Society he resembled in method. Samuel Pepys and John Evelyn, both Royal Society members, kept diaries that turned out to be extraordinary literary documents almost by accident, capturing the Restoration's greatest catastrophes — the Great Plague of 1665 and the Great Fire of 1666 — in unforgettable first-hand detail. And Thomas Sprat's call for a plain, unornamented English prose, made on the Society's behalf in 1667, set a direction English writing would keep moving toward for decades. All of this — restored monarchy, reopened stage, chartered science, and a new prose ideal — is the world Milton's *Paradise Lost* was written and published into in 1667 (Part III, Chapter 12), and it is the world the rest of Part IV explores: the reopened theatres of Chapter 2, Dryden's poetry and criticism in Chapter 3, and the periodical essay's debt to the plain style in Chapter 4.

## Glossary

- **Interregnum** — the period between 1649 and 1660 when England had no monarch, governed first as a republic and then as a Protectorate under Oliver Cromwell.
- **Restoration** — the return of the monarchy to England in 1660 with Charles II, and by extension the cultural period that followed.
- **Clarendon Code** — a series of laws passed 1661–1665 re-establishing the Church of England and restricting the civil and religious rights of Nonconformists.
- **Nonconformist** — a Protestant who, after the Restoration, would not accept the doctrine or discipline of the re-established Church of England.
- **Royal Society** — the scientific society founded in 1660 and chartered in 1662 to promote experimental, evidence-based "natural knowledge."
- **Plain style** — the ideal, promoted by Thomas Sprat on the Royal Society's behalf, of clear, unornamented English prose, in contrast to more elaborate rhetorical styles.
