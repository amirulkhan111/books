## Historical and Socio-Political Context

This Part has followed a quiet but persistent theme: writers gradually escaping dependence on royal office or aristocratic patronage, and building careers instead on the open literary market. Dryden held royal appointments (Chapter 3); Addison worked in Whig government (Chapter 4); but Pope, in Chapter 6, became the first English poet to support himself entirely through sales of his own work, funded by subscription rather than a patron's goodwill. Samuel Johnson completes this story, and does so through one of the most famous single episodes in English literary history — an episode this chapter builds toward.

Johnson was born in 1709 in Lichfield, the son of a struggling bookseller, and his early life was marked by poverty and illness: as an infant he contracted scrofula, a tubercular infection then popularly called the "King's Evil," and in 1712 his mother took him to London to be ceremonially "touched" by Queen Anne, following an old royal tradition that a monarch's touch could cure the disease. It didn't work — Johnson was left partially blind and deaf, with lasting scars — and Anne herself was among the last British monarchs to perform the ceremony at all, since her Hanoverian successors abandoned it. Johnson entered Pembroke College, Oxford, in 1728, but poverty forced him to leave without a degree after little more than a year. He arrived in London in 1737 essentially penniless, alongside his former pupil David Garrick, who would go on to become the century's most celebrated actor while Johnson spent years grinding out anonymous journalism for magazines just to survive.

That hand-to-mouth beginning makes what happened next all the more striking. In 1746, a group of London booksellers — not a royal or aristocratic patron, but publishers acting as a commercial syndicate — contracted Johnson to produce a comprehensive dictionary of the English language, a project that would occupy him for nearly a decade. Lord Chesterfield, a prominent nobleman, was nominally attached to the project as its patron, but did little to support Johnson through the years of grinding work that followed. When the *Dictionary* was finally nearing completion in 1754 and its success seemed assured, Chesterfield published two magazine essays praising it in advance, apparently hoping to be publicly credited as its sponsor after all. Johnson's response — a letter you will study directly below — became one of the decisive symbolic moments marking English literature's transition away from the patronage system altogether, toward the market-driven, bookseller-funded model this whole chapter, and much of this Part, has been quietly building toward.

## Literary Movements

This chapter doesn't describe a single literary movement so much as a single figure who came to embody an entire era's critical authority — historians of literature have long called the mid-eighteenth century, only half-jokingly, "the Age of Johnson." Three overlapping projects built that authority: **lexicography** (dictionary-making), periodical and philosophical prose, and literary **criticism and biography**, and this chapter takes them in turn.

Johnson's *Dictionary* (1755) was not the first English dictionary, but it was the first genuinely comprehensive one, and its defining innovation was the **quotation-illustration method**: rather than simply stating a word's meaning, Johnson illustrated each sense with real quotations drawn from English literature — roughly 114,000 of them, taken from writers like Shakespeare, Milton, Dryden, and Pope, several of whom you've already studied in this book. This turned the *Dictionary* into something closer to a portable library of English literary usage than a simple word-list, and it became the direct model for the much later *Oxford English Dictionary*. It's worth correcting a popular misconception here: Johnson is often imagined as a rigid enforcer of "correct" English, but in his own preface he explicitly doubted that any dictionary could permanently "fix" a living language — a notably humble, historically minded position from a writer often remembered as anything but humble.

Johnson's prose style itself became so recognizable that critics still call it **"Johnsonese"**: long, carefully balanced sentences, built on Latinate vocabulary and firm antithesis, weighing one idea against its opposite with real rhetorical control. Compare this with the plainer, more conversational style Addison cultivated in *The Spectator* (Chapter 4): where Addison aimed for easy, sociable clarity, Johnson's prose in *The Rambler* (1750–1752), his own solo periodical (written almost entirely by himself, unlike Addison and Steele's collaboration), is denser, more moralistic, and more overtly philosophical — closer to a sermon or a formal essay than a coffeehouse conversation.

Finally, this period sees literary **criticism and biography** mature into serious, sustained critical practices in their own right. Johnson's *Preface to Shakespeare* (1765) and his *Lives of the Poets* (1779–1781) treat close critical judgment of English writers as work worth doing carefully and at length — and, as you'll see below, James Boswell's biography of Johnson himself completes this development, turning the writer's own life into one of English literature's most celebrated critical and narrative achievements.

## Author Profiles

**Samuel Johnson** (1709–1784) rose from provincial poverty and near-blindness to become, by the last decades of his life, the single most authoritative critical voice in English letters. His early satirical poems, *London* (1738) and *The Vanity of Human Wishes* (1749), both imitations of the Roman satirist Juvenal in the same tradition you met through Swift's Juvenalian intensity in Chapter 5, already show Johnson's characteristic blend of moral seriousness and hard-won personal experience — *The Vanity of Human Wishes* even glances forward, with real pathos, to Swift's own decline into madness and death: "Swift expires a driv'ler and a show." The *Dictionary* (1755) made his reputation permanent, and his Letter to Lord Chesterfield that same year, refusing the belated patronage of a nobleman who had ignored him through nine years of work, became a landmark declaration of the professional writer's independence from aristocratic favor. Johnson wrote across an extraordinary range afterward: the moral essays of *The Rambler* and *The Idler*, the philosophical tale *Rasselas* (1759, reportedly written in the evenings of a single week to pay for his mother's funeral), a critically important edition of Shakespeare (1765), and, in his final major project, the *Lives of the Poets* (1779–1781) — critical and biographical prefaces to editions of fifty-two English poets, including figures you already know well: Milton, Dryden, Addison, and Pope. Notably, in his *Life of Cowley*, Johnson coined the term you first met in Part III, Chapter 8 — "metaphysical poets" — to describe the learned, difficult, image-driven style of Donne, Cowley, and their contemporaries, meaning that a label this book has used throughout Part III originates directly from Johnson's own critical judgment here in Part IV. Johnson's personal life combined real moral seriousness — he was a devout, High Church Anglican and a forceful, outspoken opponent of slavery — with a large, gregarious social presence at the center of London's literary and intellectual life, especially through The Club, the dining and conversation society he founded with the painter Sir Joshua Reynolds in 1764.

**James Boswell** (1740–1795) was a Scottish lawyer and diarist who met Johnson in 1763 and became his close friend, frequent travel companion, and eventual biographer. Boswell's meticulous journals, recording Johnson's conversation in remarkably vivid, near-verbatim detail over more than two decades, became the foundation of *The Life of Samuel Johnson* (1791), published seven years after Johnson's death. The book is widely regarded as one of the greatest biographies in the English language, not simply for its factual thoroughness but for how completely it captures its subject's speaking voice, wit, and personality — establishing a new, much more intimate and dramatic standard for literary biography than anything written before it.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***London* (1738) and *The Vanity of Human Wishes* (1749).** Both poems adapt satires by the Roman poet Juvenal to contemporary English subjects, and both carry real personal weight: *The Vanity of Human Wishes*, written from Johnson's own hard experience of literary poverty, surveys the collapse of scholarly, military, and political ambition alike, and is widely considered his greatest poem.
- ***A Dictionary of the English Language* (1755).** Beyond its historic scale, the *Dictionary*'s real personality shows in Johnson's own occasional wit — his definition of "lexicographer," for instance, as "a writer of dictionaries; a harmless drudge," or his famously partisan definition of "oats" as a grain that in England feeds horses but in Scotland "supports the people." These flashes of humor sit alongside genuinely rigorous scholarly method, a combination that says a great deal about Johnson's personality as a critic: exacting, but never solemn for its own sake.
- **Johnson's Letter to Lord Chesterfield (1755).** Refusing Chesterfield's belated offer of public association with the now-successful *Dictionary*, Johnson's letter memorably redefines the very idea of a patron as someone who "looks with unconcern on a man struggling for life in the water, and, when he has reached ground, encumbers him with help." Read this alongside Pope's earlier, market-funded independence in Chapter 6: together, the two moments mark the practical and symbolic end of literary patronage as English writers' primary means of financial survival.
- ***Rasselas* (1759).** This short philosophical tale follows Prince Rasselas as he leaves the secluded, comfortable "Happy Valley" to search the wider world for a durable, complete form of happiness — visiting philosophers, hermits, and rulers along the way — only to conclude that no such permanent contentment actually exists. Its famously unresolved final chapter, titled "The Conclusion, in which Nothing is Concluded," captures Johnson's characteristic, hard-earned skepticism about easy answers to human unhappiness.
- ***Preface to Shakespeare* (1765).** Here Johnson defends Shakespeare (Part III, Chapter 6) against strict neoclassical critics who faulted him for ignoring the classical "unities" of time and place and for freely mixing comedy and tragedy within single plays. Johnson argues that this mixture is not a fault but a truer reflection of real life, which itself refuses to separate cleanly into pure comedy or pure tragedy — a critical judgment that helped secure Shakespeare's reputation against generations of narrowly rule-bound criticism.
- ***Lives of the Poets* (1779–1781).** Across fifty-two critical-biographical essays, Johnson combines close literary judgment with often blunt personal assessment — his *Life of Milton* admires *Paradise Lost*'s sublimity while sharply disapproving of Milton's republican politics (Part III, Chapters 11–12), and his *Life of Pope* calls Pope's Homer translation, which you studied in Chapter 6, "a performance which no age or nation could hope to equal." The collection's most historically consequential single essay may be the *Life of Cowley*, where Johnson's coinage of "metaphysical poets" gave English criticism a term still in standard use today.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Johnson a rigid prescriptivist, or something more complicated?** The popular image of Johnson as language's stern rule-enforcer doesn't match his own preface to the *Dictionary*, which openly acknowledges that living languages inevitably change and that no dictionary can permanently arrest that process. Modern linguists still find Johnson's actual practice — grounding definitions in how writers had actually used words, rather than in abstract rules — closer to careful historical description than to simple prescription.
- **Did the Chesterfield letter really end literary patronage?** The letter is often treated as a clean, decisive break, but historians caution against overstating this: patronage in various forms persisted well beyond 1755, and Johnson himself accepted a royal pension in 1762. The more accurate reading is that the letter marks a powerful symbolic moment in a much longer, gradual shift — the same shift you've already traced through Pope's subscription-funded Homer in Chapter 6 — rather than a single clean turning point.
- **How should we read Johnson's ambivalence toward Milton?** The *Life of Milton*'s combination of aesthetic admiration and political disapproval is a useful case study in separating literary judgment from political or personal judgment of an author — a distinction worth holding onto whenever you encounter a writer, in this book or elsewhere, whose politics and artistry pull a reader in different directions.
- **Is Boswell's *Life of Johnson* objective biography, or a kind of literary construction in its own right?** Boswell selected, arranged, and occasionally polished the conversations he recorded, shaping a particular, vivid image of Johnson for his readers. Most modern critics treat this not as a flaw but as an inherent feature of biography as a genre: even meticulously documented life-writing involves narrative choices, and Boswell's undeniable skill as a shaper of scenes and dialogue is precisely what makes the *Life* a landmark of literature, not merely of historical record.

## Chapter Summary

Rising from provincial poverty and childhood illness, Samuel Johnson became the dominant critical authority of the mid-eighteenth century through an extraordinary range of work: the monumental *Dictionary of the English Language* (1755), built on a quotation-illustration method drawn from English literature itself; the moral seriousness of *The Rambler* and the philosophical skepticism of *Rasselas* (1759); a critically important *Preface to Shakespeare* (1765) defending Shakespeare's disregard for neoclassical rules; and the *Lives of the Poets* (1779–1781), which, among much else, gave English criticism the term "metaphysical poets" you first met in Part III, Chapter 8. His 1755 Letter to Lord Chesterfield, refusing belated aristocratic patronage after years of unsupported labor, stands alongside Pope's market-funded independence in Chapter 6 as one of the decisive symbolic moments in English writers' shift from patronage to the professional literary marketplace. James Boswell's *Life of Samuel Johnson* (1791), built from years of closely observed conversation, then did for Johnson's own life what Johnson's *Lives of the Poets* had done for his subjects — turning biography itself into a major literary achievement, and closing out this Part's long story of the writer's changing place in English society.

## Glossary

- **Lexicography** — the practice and craft of compiling dictionaries.
- **Quotation-illustration method** — Johnson's technique of illustrating a word's meaning with quotations drawn from real literary usage, later the model for the *Oxford English Dictionary*.
- **Patronage** — financial and social support of a writer by a wealthy or aristocratic sponsor, the dominant model of authorial support before the rise of the market-driven literary marketplace this Part traces.
- **"Johnsonese"** — a term for Johnson's characteristic prose style: long, balanced, Latinate sentences built on careful antithesis.
