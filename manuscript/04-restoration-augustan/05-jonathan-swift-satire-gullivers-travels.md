## Historical and Socio-Political Context

You have now met three Augustan satirists in this Part — Dryden's political and mock-heroic satire in Chapter 3, and, more gently, Addison and Steele's moral satire of manners in Chapter 4. Jonathan Swift takes the same historical moment — Augustan order, classical models, the heroic couplet's prose cousin, careful irony — and pushes it toward something darker and more unsettling than either. To understand why, you need to know where Swift actually stood, which was never quite comfortably in one place.

Swift was born in Dublin in 1667 to English parents, making him what historians call **Anglo-Irish**: ethnically and religiously English (a member of the Church of Ireland, the Irish branch of the established Anglican Church), but Irish by birth and, for most of his career, by residence. This double position matters enormously for his writing. England governed Ireland as a subordinate kingdom throughout this period, restricting Irish trade and manufacturing to protect English economic interests, and Swift — despite belonging to the ruling Protestant establishment that benefited from English rule — became one of the sharpest critics of exactly those restrictions, as you'll see in this chapter's discussion of *The Drapier's Letters* and *A Modest Proposal*. Hold this tension in mind; you'll meet it again in Part X, Chapter 2, which studies Swift as a founding figure of a distinct Irish literary tradition.

Swift's early career took him to England instead. From 1689, with interruptions, he worked as secretary to the diplomat and essayist Sir William Temple at Moor Park in Surrey — a position that gave him access to Temple's library and to the fashionable literary debate of the moment, the "Battle of the Books," a dispute over whether modern learning had surpassed or merely imitated the wisdom of the ancient Greeks and Romans. It was also at Moor Park that Swift met a young girl in Temple's household, Esther Johnson, whom he nicknamed "Stella" and who remained his closest lifelong companion; his letters to her, later collected as the *Journal to Stella* (1710–1713), give an unusually intimate view of his years working in London for the Tory government.

That government work defined Swift's middle career. Queen Anne's reign (1702–1714) was dominated by the War of the Spanish Succession, a costly European conflict, and by fierce rivalry between the Whig and Tory parties over how to end it. From 1710, Swift wrote political journalism for the Tory ministers Robert Harley and Henry St John, editing their party paper *The Examiner* for its first several months before handing that role to Delarivier Manley in 1711 (Chapter 9), and defending the ministry's eventual peace settlement, the Treaty of Utrecht (1713), in the pamphlet *The Conduct of the Allies* (1711). Swift hoped this loyal service would earn him a bishopric in England. Instead, when Queen Anne died in 1714 and the Tory government collapsed, replaced by the incoming Hanoverian king George I and a restored Whig government, Swift's English prospects collapsed with it. He returned to Ireland to take up the position he had already been given as a consolation two years earlier — Dean of St Patrick's Cathedral, Dublin — and it was from that Irish vantage point, not the English career he had wanted, that he wrote nearly everything for which he is remembered today.

## Literary Movements

Swift's satire is built on a single, powerful technique that this chapter needs you to understand clearly before anything else: the **sustained ironic persona**. Rather than telling readers directly what he thinks, Swift adopts, and holds without breaking, the voice of a narrator whose views are subtly or grotesquely wrong — and lets the gap between that narrator's calm sincerity and the reader's own growing horror or laughter do the satirical work. You already saw a milder version of this device in Chapter 4, where Swift's "Isaac Bickerstaff" persona cheerfully insisted a living man was dead. Swift takes the same technique to far more serious extremes in the two works this chapter treats as his masterpieces, *A Modest Proposal* and *Gulliver's Travels*.

It's also worth returning to a distinction Dryden had already introduced into English criticism, in the *Discourse Concerning Satire* mentioned in Chapter 3: the difference between **Horatian satire**, named for the Roman poet Horace, which mocks folly gently, with amused tolerance, and **Juvenalian satire**, named for the Roman poet Juvenal, which attacks vice and corruption with real anger and moral urgency. Addison and Steele's periodical essays, from Chapter 4, sit mostly on the Horatian end of this spectrum — gentle correction offered over coffee. Swift sits much closer to the Juvenalian end: his satire wants not just to amuse you but to make you genuinely uncomfortable about human cruelty, greed, and self-delusion. Swift himself chose the Latin phrase for his own epitaph — *ubi saeva indignatio ulterius cor lacerare nequit*, "where fierce indignation can no longer tear his heart" — a fair one-line description of his entire satiric temperament.

Finally, in 1714, Swift co-founded the **Scriblerus Club** with Alexander Pope (whom you'll meet fully in Chapter 6), the poet John Gay, and the physician and writer John Arbuthnot. The group collaborated on satirizing pedantry and false learning through an invented collective author, "Martinus Scriblerus," and though the wider Scriblerus project never fully materialized, the friendships it built — especially between Swift and Pope — shaped English satire for the rest of the Augustan period.

## Author Profiles

**Jonathan Swift** (1667–1745) spent his career oscillating between English ambition and Irish reality, and his writing is best understood as a running argument between the two. His early satires, *A Tale of a Tub* and *The Battle of the Books* (both 1704, published together and anonymously), grew directly out of his years at Moor Park: *A Tale of a Tub* allegorizes the split within Western Christianity through three brothers — Peter (representing Roman Catholicism), Martin (representing the Anglican Church, named for Martin Luther), and Jack (representing radical Protestant Dissent) — who quarrel bitterly over how to interpret their father's testament, while a series of digressions satirizes modern intellectual fashion and hack writing. His years writing Tory propaganda in London (1710–1714) show Swift as a sharp, effective political journalist, but it was his return to Ireland after 1714, embittered by dashed English hopes, that produced his greatest work. *The Drapier's Letters* (1724–1725), published under the pseudonym "M. B., Drapier," rallied Irish public opinion against a scheme to flood Ireland with a new, England-minted copper coinage — a campaign so effective that the British government withdrew the patent, and Swift, despite being widely known as the author, was never prosecuted, because no one in Ireland would betray him. *Gulliver's Travels* (1726) and *A Modest Proposal* (1729), both discussed in detail below, followed from this same Irish vantage point, and remain the works modern readers know him by. Swift's personal life was closely bound up with two women: Esther Johnson ("Stella"), his lifelong companion from the Moor Park years until her death in 1728, and Esther Vanhomrigh ("Vanessa"), a younger woman whose unrequited devotion to Swift he addressed in the poem "Cadenus and Vanessa," and who died in 1723. Neither relationship's exact nature is fully documented, and both remain subjects of biographical speculation.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***A Tale of a Tub* (1704).** Beneath its comic allegory of three quarreling brothers, this is a genuinely difficult, digressive satire aimed simultaneously at religious extremism (on both the Catholic and radical Protestant ends) and at the intellectual arrogance of "modern" writers who Swift felt had abandoned the discipline of classical learning for empty cleverness — a position that aligns him firmly with the "ancients" side of the Battle of the Books debate he'd absorbed at Moor Park.
- **The Bickerstaff Papers (1708).** As Chapter 4 already told you, Swift's fictional astrologer "Isaac Bickerstaff" predicted the death of the real almanac-maker John Partridge on a specific date, then insisted, against Partridge's own furious public denials, that the prediction had come true. The hoax is often read as more than a joke: it's also a serious satire on the popular astrology Swift considered a form of superstitious fraud on the public.
- ***The Drapier's Letters* (1724–1725).** Writing as a humble Dublin shopkeeper rather than a Church of Ireland Dean, Swift argued that the Wood's halfpence scheme would devalue Irish currency and enrich English profiteers at Ireland's expense. The campaign's success made Swift, for the first time, a genuine popular hero in Ireland — a striking reversal for a writer who had spent the previous decade chasing advancement in England.
- ***Gulliver's Travels* (1726).** Structured as four separate voyages, each governed by its own satirical logic (see the comparative table below), the book uses its narrator, the ordinary and initially reasonable-seeming ship's surgeon Lemuel Gulliver, to carry the reader through progressively stranger and more troubling encounters with human folly, viewed always from an unfamiliar scale or angle. Crucially, Gulliver himself changes across the four books — starting as a fairly neutral, curious observer and ending, after his time among the rational horses of Book IV, so disgusted with humanity that he can barely tolerate his own family. Whether Swift wants readers to share that final disgust, or to see it as itself a kind of failure or madness, is the single most debated question in Swift criticism, addressed directly below.
- ***A Modest Proposal* (1729).** This short pamphlet is Swift's purest demonstration of the sustained ironic persona. Its narrator, addressing Ireland's poverty and overpopulation with the calm, reasonable tone of a professional economic planner, proposes that poor Irish families sell their one-year-old children as food for wealthy tables. The horror of the proposal is never once acknowledged by the narrator's flat, sensible prose — which is exactly Swift's point: he is attacking not poor Irish parents but the callous, purely economic logic English policy had applied to Ireland's suffering, a logic just as monstrous as the narrator's proposal once you actually follow it to its conclusion.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is Swift a misanthrope?** Book IV of *Gulliver's Travels*, where Gulliver comes to revere the purely rational horse-like Houyhnhnms and to despise the brutish, degraded human-like Yahoos he lives among, has divided critics for centuries. Some — sometimes called the "hard school" of Swift criticism — read Gulliver's final contempt for humanity as close to Swift's own considered judgment. Others — the "soft school" — argue that Swift is satirizing Gulliver's own excess: a man so seduced by an impossible ideal of pure rationality that he loses ordinary human sympathy, including for his own wife and children, is not a reliable spokesman for the author's views but another one of Swift's targets. The disagreement matters because it changes what kind of book you think you're reading — a savage indictment of the human species, or a warning about the dangers of demanding more purity from human nature than it can bear.
- **Does *A Modest Proposal*'s irony risk being misunderstood?** Because the narrator never breaks character, some first-time readers have genuinely mistaken the pamphlet's proposal for a sincere one. Most critics treat this risk as part of the satire's power rather than a flaw in it: the discomfort of briefly wondering whether anyone could really mean this is precisely what forces readers to confront how emotionally detached real contemporary economic writing about Ireland's poor had already become.
- **How should we read Swift's position as an Anglo-Irish satirist?** Swift benefited from the same English Protestant establishment in Ireland whose economic policies he attacked in *The Drapier's Letters* and *A Modest Proposal*. Critics increasingly read this not as simple hypocrisy but as a genuinely complicated position: a writer using an insider's authority and access to attack the system that gave him that authority in the first place. This tension is central to how Part X, Chapter 2, situates Swift at the start of a distinct Irish literary tradition.
- **What does the sustained ironic persona demand of a reader?** Both *A Modest Proposal* and *Gulliver's Travels* only work if the reader actively notices the gap between what the narrator says and what the text as a whole implies. This makes Swift a genuinely demanding writer — one who trusts, or perhaps tests, his audience's own critical judgment, rather than simply telling them what to think.

## Chapter Summary

Jonathan Swift's career moved between English political ambition and Irish reality, and it was his final, disappointed settlement in Ireland after 1714 that produced his greatest satire. *A Tale of a Tub* (1704) allegorizes religious and intellectual corruption; *The Drapier's Letters* (1724–1725) turned Swift into an Irish popular hero by defeating an exploitative coinage scheme; and *Gulliver's Travels* (1726) uses four distinct imaginative voyages — tiny Lilliput, giant Brobdingnag, the abstracted science of Laputa, and the disturbingly rational Houyhnhnms — to satirize human folly from every possible angle, ending in a genuinely unresolved debate about how far Swift wants readers to share Gulliver's final disgust with his own species. *A Modest Proposal* (1729) applies the same sustained ironic persona to Ireland's poverty with devastating economy. Swift's Juvenalian intensity, building on the satiric terminology Dryden supplied in Chapter 3, and his founding role in the Scriblerus Club alongside Alexander Pope, set the stage directly for Chapter 6, where Pope brings a comparable satiric ambition to poetry rather than prose.

## Glossary

- **Anglo-Irish** — describing people of English descent, and usually of the established Protestant church, born or settled in Ireland during the period of English/British rule.
- **Sustained ironic persona** — a satirical technique in which a writer maintains a fictional narrating voice, whose flawed or extreme views differ from the author's own, without ever breaking character to clarify the difference.
- **Horatian satire** — gentle, good-humored satire that mocks folly with tolerance, named for the Roman poet Horace.
- **Juvenalian satire** — angry, morally urgent satire that attacks vice and corruption, named for the Roman poet Juvenal.
- **Scriblerus Club** — a literary group founded in 1714 by Swift, Pope, John Gay, and John Arbuthnot, dedicated to satirizing false learning and pedantry.
