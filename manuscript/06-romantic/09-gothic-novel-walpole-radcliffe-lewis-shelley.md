## Historical and Socio-Political Context

You've been meeting the Gothic novel in pieces throughout this Part, and this chapter finally gives it sustained, direct attention. You already know, from Part V, Chapter 4, that Horace Walpole's *The Castle of Otranto* (1764) is widely considered the genre's founding text, and from Part V, Chapter 2, that Edmund Burke's 1757 theory of the sublime — the pleasurable terror produced by vastness, obscurity, and power — supplies the genre's essential aesthetic justification. You've also seen the genre's uneasy relationship with the rest of the period this Part covers: Wordsworth's 1800 Preface (Chapter 2) explicitly attacked the public appetite for "frantic novels," even as Coleridge's own "Christabel" (Chapter 4) borrowed Gothic atmosphere freely, and Byron's circle in Geneva (Chapter 5) produced, almost as a party game, one of the genre's most enduring masterpieces.

It's worth pausing on the word "Gothic" itself. Originally, the term described the Germanic tribes historians blamed for the fall of the Roman Empire, and by extension came to describe medieval culture generally — crude, superstitious, "barbaric," in pointed contrast to the classical order and reason the Augustan writers of Part IV so admired. Walpole's deliberate choice to label his own novel "A Gothic Story," added to its 1765 second edition, flips this old insult into a badge of genre identity: a crumbling medieval world of superstition, feudal tyranny, and the supernatural, offered up now as a source of thrilling, pleasurable terror rather than embarrassment. Notice, too, an odd pattern connecting this chapter to Part V: Walpole's first edition of *Otranto* was published anonymously, presented as a genuine translation of an old Italian manuscript — the same "found document" technique Macpherson used for Ossian (Part V, Chapter 3) and Chatterton used for the Rowley poems (Part V, Chapter 4). Unlike those two cases, though, readers quickly understood Walpole's "translation" claim as a fictional framing device and genre convention rather than a genuine deception, once he openly acknowledged his own authorship in the second edition.

## Literary Movements

The Gothic novel's central conventions — an ancient castle or ruined abbey setting, a tyrannical villain, a persecuted heroine, mysterious family secrets, and supernatural or apparently supernatural occurrences — all appear fully formed in Walpole's *Otranto*. What later writers developed was the genre's psychological sophistication, and the genre split, in the 1790s, into two recognizably different branches. **Ann Radcliffe** perfected what critics now call the **"explained supernatural"**: extended, atmospheric build-up of apparent hauntings and terrors that the novel's end reveals to have entirely natural, rational causes — a hidden passage, a disguise, a trick of candlelight. **Matthew Lewis**, in *The Monk*, took the opposite path, embracing genuinely, unambiguously supernatural events — real demons, a literal pact with the Devil — alongside far more explicit sexual and violent content than Radcliffe ever allowed herself. Some critics, following the pioneering feminist scholar Ellen Moers, describe this split as a distinction between "female Gothic" (Radcliffe's more psychologically restrained, ultimately rationalized terror) and "male Gothic" (Lewis's more sensational, unresolved supernatural horror) — a useful framework, though one worth applying carefully rather than treating as an absolute rule.

By the time Mary Shelley's *Frankenstein* appeared in 1818, the Gothic novel had become popular enough to attract open mockery — Jane Austen's *Northanger Abbey* (Chapter 10), whose heroine comically misreads her own mundane life through the lens of Radcliffe's novels, is direct evidence of just how thoroughly Gothic conventions had saturated popular reading by this point. *Frankenstein* itself, though, pushes the Gothic in a genuinely new direction: rather than medieval castles and feudal tyranny, it locates its horror in contemporary scientific ambition, making it, alongside its status as a Gothic novel, a strong candidate for the first true work of science fiction.

## Author Profiles

**Horace Walpole** (1717–1797), already introduced in Part V, Chapter 4, as the man who coldly rejected Thomas Chatterton's forged manuscripts, wrote *The Castle of Otranto* (1764) after, by his own account, a vivid dream — an origin story worth comparing to Coleridge's later claim about "Kubla Khan" (Chapter 4). Walpole's novel, once its authorship was revealed, established nearly every convention later Gothic writers would develop further.

**Ann Radcliffe** (1764–1823) became the Gothic novel's most commercially successful and critically influential practitioner, refining Walpole's basic conventions into something psychologically subtler across novels including *The Romance of the Forest* (1791) and her masterpiece, *The Mysteries of Udolpho* (1794). Radcliffe's extended descriptions of sublime mountain and castle landscapes translate Burke's aesthetic theory (Part V, Chapter 2) directly into narrative practice, generating suspense and dread through atmosphere and suggestion rather than explicit supernatural event — a technique so influential that later readers, including Jane Austen's own comic heroine in *Northanger Abbey*, treated Radcliffe's novels as the genre's definitive model.

**Matthew Gregory "Monk" Lewis** (1775–1818), a sitting Member of Parliament at the time, published *The Monk* (1796) to immediate scandal: its story of the outwardly virtuous monk Ambrosio, corrupted by pride and demonic temptation into rape, murder, and an ultimately damning pact with the Devil, includes genuinely explicit sexual and violent content and unambiguous, unexplained supernatural events, provoking harsh criticism, including from Coleridge himself, even as the novel's raw power secured Lewis's own lasting nickname.

**Mary Shelley** (1797–1851), daughter of William Godwin and Mary Wollstonecraft (Part VI, Chapter 1), wife of Percy Bysshe Shelley (Chapter 6), and participant in the 1816 Geneva summer alongside Byron (Chapter 5), wrote *Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus* at just eighteen or nineteen years old, publishing it anonymously in 1818 and only claiming public authorship with a revised edition in 1831. The novel tells the story of Victor Frankenstein, a young scientist who discovers how to animate dead matter and assembles a living being from corpse parts, only to be horrified by his own creation's appearance and abandon it immediately. It's worth correcting a common misconception here: "Frankenstein" is the scientist's name, not the creature's, who remains unnamed throughout the novel. Initially gentle and desperate for human connection and acceptance, the creature turns to vengeance against his creator only after repeated, brutal rejection by every human he encounters — a pattern that has made the novel a lasting touchstone for questions about nature, nurture, and the social construction of monstrousness. Mary Shelley went on to write further significant fiction, including *The Last Man* (1826), an early and influential work of apocalyptic fiction imagining a future plague that destroys humanity, but *Frankenstein* remains her defining achievement.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***The Castle of Otranto* (1764).** Read this as the Gothic novel's founding template: a usurped inheritance, a tyrannical prince, supernatural portents (including a giant helmet falling from the sky to crush an heir), and a crumbling medieval setting establish conventions nearly every later Gothic novel would draw on, directly or through parody.
- ***The Mysteries of Udolpho* (1794).** Emily St. Aubert's prolonged imprisonment in a remote Italian castle, menaced by her sinister guardian Montoni and a series of apparently supernatural terrors later revealed to have entirely natural explanations, made Radcliffe's "explained supernatural" technique the genre's dominant model for a generation of readers.
- ***The Monk* (1796).** Ambrosio's genuine, unambiguous descent into supernatural corruption and damnation, rendered in far more explicit and disturbing detail than Radcliffe ever attempted, shows the Gothic novel's alternative, more sensationalist possibilities.
- ***Frankenstein* (1818).** The creature's own intellectual and emotional development is the novel's most philosophically ambitious achievement: having taught himself to read partly through Milton's *Paradise Lost* (Part III, Chapters 11–12), the creature explicitly compares himself to both Adam, created and then abandoned by his maker, and to Satan, cast out and embittered by rejection — a genuinely sophisticated act of literary self-understanding built directly into the novel's own plot. The subtitle, *The Modern Prometheus*, invites direct comparison with Percy Shelley's *Prometheus Unbound* (Chapter 6): where Percy's Prometheus is a liberating hero whose defiance of tyranny brings humanity's regeneration, Mary's "modern Prometheus," Victor Frankenstein, is a cautionary figure whose theft of creative power brings only horror and loss — husband and wife using the very same myth to make strikingly opposed arguments about ambition, creation, and consequence.

## Critical Perspectives

- **What does the recurring "found manuscript" device suggest about this era's relationship to authenticity?** Walpole's fictional translation-claim, Macpherson's Ossian, and Chatterton's Rowley poems all use a similar authenticating fiction, but only Walpole's was quickly and comfortably accepted as a harmless genre convention rather than a genuine deception — a useful reminder that the same literary device can carry very different ethical weight depending on how, and how long, an author sustains the claim.
- **Is the "female Gothic"/"male Gothic" distinction useful, or does it oversimplify?** Ellen Moers's framework usefully highlights real differences between Radcliffe's and Lewis's approaches to the supernatural, but applying it too rigidly risks flattening real variation within each writer's work and ignoring how thoroughly later writers, including Mary Shelley, blended elements from both traditions.
- **Is Frankenstein's creature truly monstrous, or made monstrous by rejection?** The creature commits real violence, but only after prolonged, repeated abuse by a society that judges him entirely by his appearance — a pattern that has made the novel one of literature's most enduring test cases for questions about whether evil is innate or produced by circumstance and treatment.
- **What does the Shelleys' opposing use of the Prometheus myth reveal about their different temperaments?** Percy's persistent political and imaginative optimism (Chapter 6) and Mary's much more cautionary, skeptical treatment of the same myth suggest that even within a single household, closely allied by marriage and shared intellectual circles, Romantic writers could reach genuinely opposite conclusions about the promise and danger of transformative, quasi-divine ambition.

## Chapter Summary

Beginning with Horace Walpole's dream-inspired *The Castle of Otranto* (1764), the Gothic novel established a durable set of conventions — ancient settings, supernatural terror, tyranny, and persecuted heroines — that Ann Radcliffe refined into psychologically suspenseful "explained supernatural" narratives and Matthew Lewis pushed toward genuinely unresolved supernatural sensationalism in *The Monk* (1796). Mary Shelley's *Frankenstein* (1818), born from the 1816 Geneva summer alongside Byron and Percy Shelley, transformed the genre once more, relocating Gothic horror from medieval superstition to contemporary scientific ambition, and giving its creature — self-educated through Milton's *Paradise Lost* — a philosophical depth that continues to generate genuine critical debate about nature, nurture, and the responsibilities of creators toward their creations. The novel's subtitle, *The Modern Prometheus*, sets it in direct, illuminating contrast with Percy Shelley's own use of the same myth in *Prometheus Unbound*, showing how differently even two closely allied Romantic writers could read the same ancient story. Chapter 10 now turns to Jane Austen, whose comic novel *Northanger Abbey* satirizes the very Gothic conventions this chapter has just traced.

## Glossary

- **Gothic novel** — a genre, originating with Walpole's *The Castle of Otranto* (1764), centered on ancient or ruined settings, supernatural or apparently supernatural events, tyranny, and persecuted heroines.
- **Explained supernatural** — Ann Radcliffe's technique of building extended suspense around apparently supernatural events that are ultimately revealed to have natural, rational causes.
- **"Modern Prometheus"** — Mary Shelley's subtitle for *Frankenstein*, framing Victor Frankenstein's scientific ambition as a cautionary retelling of the Prometheus myth, in direct contrast to Percy Shelley's more celebratory use of the same story.
