## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Part V closed with Blake and Burns both greeting the French Revolution as the dawn of a freer, more equal world. This chapter takes that hope and follows it all the way through its actual, far more complicated history — because understanding what really happened in France between 1789 and the early 1800s is essential to understanding almost everything the rest of Part VI studies.

On 14 July 1789, a Parisian crowd stormed the Bastille, a royal fortress-prison, in an act that quickly came to symbolize the overthrow of absolute monarchy itself. For its first two years or so, the Revolution unfolded as a constitutional reform movement — establishing a constitutional monarchy, a new national assembly, and a declaration of the rights of man — and English reformist and radical opinion welcomed it warmly, often reading it as France simply catching up to the constitutional principles England had already secured in its own Glorious Revolution of 1688 (Part IV, Chapter 3). William Wordsworth, whose full biography and poetry you'll study in Chapter 3, traveled to France in 1790 and again, for a much longer stay, in 1791–92, where he formed a relationship with a Frenchwoman, Annette Vallon, and became genuinely swept up in revolutionary enthusiasm; decades later, in his long autobiographical poem *The Prelude* (substantially composed 1798–1805, though not published until after his death in 1850), he remembered this moment with unforgettable intensity: "Bliss was it in that dawn to be alive, / But to be young was very heaven!"

That early hope did not last. The Revolution radicalized sharply from 1792 onward: the monarchy was abolished, King Louis XVI was executed in January 1793, and France declared war on Britain the following month, instantly turning any continued English enthusiasm for the Revolution into something that could be read as support for a wartime enemy. Worse followed at home: under Maximilien Robespierre and the Committee of Public Safety, France entered the **Reign of Terror** (1793–1794), a period of mass executions by guillotine, including that of Queen Marie Antoinette in October 1793. For many English sympathizers, the Terror was a devastating shock — proof, to critics like Edmund Burke, that abstract revolutionary principles, cut loose from inherited tradition and institutions, led directly to violence and chaos. The Revolution's eventual outcome compounded the disappointment further: Napoleon Bonaparte seized power in a 1799 coup and crowned himself Emperor in 1804, replacing revolutionary republicanism with a new, militarized authoritarian rule, and Britain and France remained at war, on and off, for most of the next two decades — a near-constant wartime backdrop to everything else this Part studies.

Fear of revolutionary contagion produced real domestic repression in Britain itself. The government suspended Habeas Corpus (the legal right against arbitrary imprisonment) in 1794, and Parliament passed the so-called "Two Acts" in 1795, sharply restricting political meetings and criminalizing speech deemed seditious. Radical reformers including Thomas Hardy (the shoemaker and political organizer, not the later novelist you'll meet in Part VII), John Horne Tooke, and John Thelwall were tried for treason in 1794 — and acquitted, but only after the government had made its readiness to prosecute dissent unmistakably clear. Even Wordsworth and Coleridge, whom you'll meet fully in Chapters 3 and 4, found themselves under government surveillance in 1797, when a suspicious neighbor's report led an agent to investigate the two poets, then living together in Somerset, on suspicion of being French sympathizers or spies — an episode as revealing of the era's paranoid political climate as it is, in hindsight, faintly absurd.

## Literary Movements

You should treat the word **Romanticism** the same way Part V taught you to treat "sensibility" and the "Graveyard School": as a retrospective critical label, applied later by literary historians, not a name the poets themselves used for a shared, organized movement. What unites the writers Part VI studies is not a manifesto but a recognizable family of concerns, and this chapter's central term for that family of concerns is **the Romantic imagination** — a newly intense conviction that individual creative and visionary power, rather than reasoned argument or inherited social convention, offers the truest access to meaning and value.

You already have real groundwork for this idea. Sensibility, across Part V, had already elevated feeling over pure reason (Chapter 1), and Edward Young's *Conjectures on Original Composition* (Part V, Chapter 2) had already argued for individual creative genius over classical imitation. What Romanticism adds to this inheritance is a specific historical wound: the Revolution's collapse from liberating hope into Terror, war, and Napoleonic empire left an entire generation of English writers needing somewhere else to locate the freedom and renewal the Revolution had promised but failed to deliver. The Romantic imagination becomes that "somewhere else" — an inward, personal, visionary faculty that could preserve revolutionary ideals of liberty and transformation even after the actual political revolution had gone so badly wrong.

This chapter's roadmap for the rest of Part VI turns on one further distinction worth holding onto now: literary historians generally separate a **first generation** of Romantic poets — Wordsworth and Coleridge, old enough to have experienced the Revolution's hope and disillusionment firsthand as young adults, and who both grew markedly more politically cautious as they aged — from a **second generation** — Byron, Shelley, and Keats, all born in the 1790s, who inherited the Revolution as recent history rather than lived youthful experience, and who, Byron and Shelley especially, often remained considerably more politically radical than the older poets they sometimes accused of losing their nerve. You'll meet the first generation directly in Chapters 2 through 4, and the second in Chapters 5 through 7.

## Author Profiles

**Edmund Burke** (1729–1797), whose theory of the sublime you already met in Part V, Chapter 2, became the era's most influential conservative political voice with *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790). Writing remarkably early, before the Terror had actually happened, Burke argued that the Revolution's abstract, purely rational approach to remaking society from first principles — discarding inherited institutions, customs, and gradual constitutional development — would inevitably collapse into violence and tyranny, since real social order depends on accumulated tradition and practical wisdom, not abstract theory alone. Many readers at the time found this prediction alarmist; the Terror that followed made it look, to many others, unnervingly prophetic.

**Thomas Paine** (1737–1809) wrote *Rights of Man* (1791, with a second part in 1792) explicitly to rebut Burke, defending the Revolution and arguing for natural, universal human rights and republican government, in prose deliberately aimed at a broad, non-elite readership rather than a narrow educated class. The book sold in enormous numbers, especially in cheap editions reaching working-class readers who had never before had direct access to this kind of political argument, and it became foundational reading for the radical London circles you already met through William Blake in Part V, Chapter 6. Paine was tried in absentia for seditious libel and spent much of the rest of his life in France and America rather than risk arrest in Britain.

**Mary Wollstonecraft** (1759–1797) responded to Burke even before Paine did, with *A Vindication of the Rights of Men* (1790), and then extended the era's rights-based arguments in a genuinely original direction with *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman* (1792), insisting that the same principles of reason, education, and natural rights invoked on behalf of male citizens applied equally to women, who were denied full personhood not by nature but by inadequate education and social convention. Wollstonecraft, like Paine, moved in the radical circle around the publisher Joseph Johnson that you met alongside Blake; she later married the radical philosopher William Godwin, and their daughter, Mary Shelley, will return to this book directly in Chapter 9, as the author of *Frankenstein*.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Burke, *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790).** Read this as the founding text of a whole tradition of conservative political thought: Burke's argument that liberty must grow out of, rather than replace, a society's inherited institutions became the standard conservative response to revolutionary change for generations afterward.
- **Paine, *Rights of Man* (1791–1792).** Paine's plain, accessible prose style was itself a political statement — a deliberate refusal of Burke's more elaborate, learned rhetoric, aimed at making radical political argument available to readers Burke's own style effectively excluded.
- **Wollstonecraft, *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman* (1792).** Often considered a founding text of English feminist thought, the book's core argument — that apparent differences between men's and women's rational capacities reflect unequal education rather than natural inferiority — extended the Revolution's rights-based logic further than either Burke or Paine had taken it.
- **Wordsworth, *The Prelude* (composed 1798–1805, published 1850).** Though its full, final form falls outside this Part's own 1798–1837 span, *The Prelude*'s account of youthful revolutionary hope curdling into disillusionment is essential background for understanding the entire Romantic generation's relationship to the Revolution, and you will return to it in full in Chapter 3.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Burke's prediction wisdom or fear-mongering?** Burke wrote *Reflections* before the Terror occurred, and its accuracy about the Revolution's eventual violence has led some readers to treat him as remarkably prescient. Others note that Burke's argument rests on a deeply conservative assumption — that gradual, tradition-respecting change is always safer than rapid, principle-driven transformation — which is itself a contestable political position, not a neutral prediction that simply came true.
- **Did Wordsworth and Coleridge "sell out"?** The first generation's later, more politically cautious years drew sharp criticism from the younger, still-radical Byron and Shelley, who saw their older contemporaries' shift as a betrayal of youthful revolutionary ideals. A more sympathetic reading treats this shift as a genuine, hard-won response to watching a real revolution collapse into violence and empire — disillusionment rather than simple cowardice, even if the political consequences (both poets eventually held minor government positions) looked, to their critics, uncomfortably convenient.
- **Is "Romanticism" a coherent movement or a retrospective convenience?** Given how differently the first and second generations of Romantic poets responded to the Revolution's legacy, some critics question whether "Romanticism" describes a genuinely unified literary movement at all, rather than a useful shorthand imposed after the fact on writers who actually disagreed substantially among themselves.
- **Is the Romantic imagination's inward turn a retreat from politics, or a different kind of political engagement?** When a revolution in the world fails, turning toward imaginative and visionary experience can look like giving up on politics altogether. Many critics argue instead that the Romantic imagination is politics carried on by other means — an attempt to keep revolutionary ideals of freedom and renewal alive internally, in individual consciousness, when they could no longer be secured externally, in actual government and society.

## Chapter Summary

The French Revolution's arc — from the hopeful storming of the Bastille in 1789, through Burke's early conservative warning, Paine's and Wollstonecraft's radical rebuttals, the execution of the French king and queen, the Reign of Terror, war with Britain, and Napoleon's eventual authoritarian rule — gave the English writers of Part VI a defining historical wound: revolutionary hope raised and then, in the world of actual politics, devastatingly disappointed. Domestic British repression, from the suspension of Habeas Corpus to the surveillance of Wordsworth and Coleridge themselves, made that disappointment feel personally dangerous as well as politically disheartening. Out of this history grew the Romantic imagination: an intensified faith in individual creative and visionary power as a place to preserve the freedom and renewal the Revolution had promised, even after politics itself had failed to deliver them. The following chapters trace this imagination across two generations — Wordsworth and Coleridge in Chapters 2 through 4, and Byron, Shelley, and Keats in Chapters 5 through 7 — before Part VI turns to Romantic prose, the Gothic novel, Jane Austen, and the women Romantic poets who complete this book's account of one of English literature's most consequential periods.

## Glossary

- **Romanticism / Romantic** — a retrospective critical label for a loosely connected group of English writers, roughly 1798–1837, united less by shared manifesto than by common concerns, above all the value placed on individual imagination and feeling.
- **The Romantic imagination** — the conviction that individual creative and visionary power offers privileged access to truth and meaning, intensified in this period by disillusionment with the French Revolution's political outcome.
- **Reign of Terror** — the period (1793–1794) of mass executions in Revolutionary France under Robespierre and the Committee of Public Safety.
- **Pantisocracy** — Coleridge and Robert Southey's unrealized 1794 plan for a small utopian, egalitarian community in America.
