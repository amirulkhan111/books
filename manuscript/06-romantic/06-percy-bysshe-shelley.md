## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Where Byron, in Chapter 5, moved from Romantic melancholy toward worldly satire, Percy Bysshe Shelley took the opposite path: he remained, across his short life, one of the most persistently and ambitiously utopian of all the Romantic poets, never fully surrendering the revolutionary hope that Chapter 1 showed collapsing for so many of his contemporaries.

Born in 1792 into a wealthy Sussex landed family, heir to a baronetcy his radical convictions would spend a lifetime alienating him from, Shelley was expelled from University College, Oxford, in March 1811, after only one term, for co-authoring and distributing a pamphlet titled *The Necessity of Atheism* — an early, deliberately provocative act that set the pattern for the rest of his career. That same year, he eloped with and married sixteen-year-old Harriet Westbrook against his family's wishes, and the following year he traveled to Ireland to campaign directly for Catholic emancipation and Irish independence, distributing his own political pamphlets there. In 1814, already married to Harriet, Shelley fell in love with Mary Wollstonecraft Godwin, daughter of the philosopher William Godwin and the writer Mary Wollstonecraft, whose *Vindication of the Rights of Woman* you met in Chapter 1; Shelley eloped with Mary to the Continent, accompanied by her stepsister Claire Clairmont, in a scandal that shadowed him for years. Harriet drowned in London's Serpentine in 1816, apparently by suicide, after Shelley had already left her; Shelley and Mary married shortly afterward, partly in hopes of strengthening Shelley's case for custody of his children with Harriet, a case he ultimately lost when the Lord Chancellor ruled his atheism and radical reputation made him morally unfit to raise them.

That same year, 1816, Shelley and Mary joined Byron at Lake Geneva for the summer already described in full in Chapter 5 — the ghost-story competition that produced *Frankenstein* and *The Vampyre*. Two years later, in 1818, mounting scandal, debt, and Shelley's declining health led the couple to leave England permanently for Italy, where Shelley produced nearly all the major poetry for which he is remembered, before his death in 1822.

## Literary Movements

Shelley's career is best understood as a sustained argument, across several different poetic modes, for the possibility of genuine political and moral transformation — a position that sets him apart from both Wordsworth's and Coleridge's later disillusionment and Byron's ironic detachment. *Queen Mab* (1813), his first major long poem, draws heavily on the Enlightenment rationalism of William Godwin's *Political Justice* (1793) to attack religion, monarchy, and social injustice directly, in the confident, argumentative mode of a young radical certain that reasoned critique alone could reshape society.

By 1819, Shelley's political poetry had grown considerably more urgent and immediate. On 16 August of that year, cavalry charged a peaceful crowd gathered in Manchester to demand parliamentary reform, killing and wounding many — an event that became known as the **Peterloo Massacre**, a grim continuation of the domestic repression Chapter 1 already traced through the 1790s. Shelley's direct response, "The Mask of Anarchy," imagines a grotesque procession of allegorical figures — Murder, Fraud, and Hypocrisy, wearing the masks of government ministers — followed by a vision of the oppressed people rising in disciplined, nonviolent mass resistance: "Rise like Lions after slumber / In unvanquishable number— / Shake your chains to earth like dew." Shelley judged the poem too dangerous to publish during his own lifetime, given the same repressive legal climate Chapter 1 described; it appeared only in 1832, a decade after his death, and went on to influence later nonviolent resistance movements considerably beyond its own era.

Shelley's most philosophically ambitious statement of hope, though, comes in *Prometheus Unbound* (1820), a lyrical drama — verse drama written to be read and imagined rather than staged — reworking the Greek myth of Prometheus, punished eternally by Jupiter for bringing knowledge to humanity. Where the original myth offers no resolution, Shelley's version ends with Prometheus liberated and Jupiter's tyranny overthrown, symbolizing what Shelley believed was genuinely possible: humanity's moral and political regeneration through love and imaginative sympathy, rather than violent revolution. This idea of sympathy as poetry's true social power receives its fullest theoretical statement in "A Defence of Poetry," discussed below, where Shelley develops an argument with real roots in Adam Smith's account of sympathetic imagination (Part V, Chapter 1) — though Shelley pushes the claim much further, insisting that poetry's capacity to make readers feel what others feel is the essential engine of all moral and political progress.

## Author Profiles

**Percy Bysshe Shelley** (1792–1822) wrote almost all his major mature poetry during his final four years in Italy. "Ozymandias" (1818), a compact sonnet describing a ruined desert statue of an ancient tyrant whose boastful inscription — "Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!" — now presides over nothing but empty sand, remains one of the most widely anthologized poems in English, a devastatingly economical meditation on the impermanence of political power. "Ode to the West Wind" (1819) asks the autumn wind to scatter the poet's own words across the world like seeds, closing with a famously hopeful rhetorical question — "If Winter comes, can Spring be far behind?" — that captures Shelley's persistent faith in eventual renewal even amid personal and political disappointment. When his friend and fellow poet John Keats died in 1821, Shelley wrote "Adonais," a major pastoral elegy that, somewhat unfairly, blamed hostile critical reviews for hastening Keats's death from tuberculosis — a claim modern scholars regard as exaggerated, but one that helped fix a "doomed genius destroyed by an uncaring world" myth around Keats strikingly similar to the pattern you already met around Thomas Chatterton in Part V, Chapter 4. Shelley himself died the following year, in July 1822, when his sailing boat sank in a sudden storm off the Italian coast; his body, washed ashore days later, was cremated on the beach in a scene that quickly became its own literary legend, and he was buried in Rome's Protestant Cemetery, near Keats's own grave.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Queen Mab* (1813).** Written under the direct influence of William Godwin's rationalist philosophy, this early long poem attacks religious and political authority with the confident, unambiguous certainty of youthful radicalism, before Shelley's later work develops more complex, mythic, and lyrical strategies for making similar arguments.
- **"Ozymandias" (1818).** In just fourteen lines, the poem compresses an entire argument about power's inevitable decay: the tyrant's own inscription, meant to intimidate, now reads as unintentional irony, surrounded by nothing the "Mighty" ever actually controlled.
- **"Ode to the West Wind" (1819).** Addressing the wind as both destroyer and preserver, the poem asks it to carry the poet's own "dead thoughts" across the world "to quicken a new birth" — poetry imagined literally as a seed-scattering, world-changing force.
- **"The Mask of Anarchy" (1819, published 1832).** Written in direct, urgent response to the Peterloo Massacre, the poem's vision of disciplined, nonviolent mass resistance anticipates political strategies that would prove enormously influential in later centuries, well beyond anything Shelley could have foreseen.
- ***Prometheus Unbound* (1820).** Shelley's rewriting of the Prometheus myth toward eventual liberation, rather than eternal punishment, makes the clearest possible statement of his belief that love and imaginative sympathy, not violent force, could achieve real political transformation.
- **"A Defence of Poetry" (1821, published 1840).** Written partly to answer his friend Thomas Love Peacock's satirical claim that poetry was a declining, outdated art in an increasingly scientific age, Shelley's essay makes an extraordinarily ambitious counter-claim, ending with one of Romanticism's most quoted lines: "Poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the world."

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is Shelley's political optimism naive, or a genuinely valuable counter-current?** Set against Wordsworth's and Coleridge's disillusionment and Byron's ironic detachment, Shelley's sustained hope for real transformation through love and imagination can look either admirably principled or dangerously unrealistic, depending on how much weight you're willing to place on poetry's actual capacity to change political reality.
- **How radical was "The Mask of Anarchy," really?** Its vision of disciplined nonviolent resistance to state violence anticipates strategies later associated with twentieth-century movements far beyond Shelley's own historical moment. That the poem was too dangerous to publish during his lifetime is itself a measure of how seriously contemporary authorities took the threat of exactly this kind of writing.
- **Is "poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the world" a serious claim or Romantic overstatement?** Shelley's argument in "A Defence of Poetry" asks readers to take poetic imagination as seriously as legislation itself — a claim some critics find genuinely illuminating about how sympathetic imagination shapes moral progress, and others find characteristic of Romanticism's tendency to inflate poetry's real-world social importance.
- **What does the recurrence of the "doomed genius" myth suggest?** Shelley's own mythologizing of Keats's death in "Adonais" repeats, almost exactly, the pattern already established around Chatterton in Part V, Chapter 4 — a poet supposedly destroyed by an indifferent or hostile world. Seeing this pattern recur across two quite different poets suggests it tells us as much about what the Romantic era wanted its poets' deaths to mean as it does about the actual circumstances of either death.

## Chapter Summary

Percy Bysshe Shelley combined an unusually radical personal life — expulsion from Oxford, elopement, scandal, and eventual permanent exile from England — with a persistent, unwavering commitment to the possibility of genuine political and moral transformation that set him apart from the disillusionment traced through much of this Part. "Ozymandias" (1818) and "Ode to the West Wind" (1819) meditate on power's impermanence and poetry's transformative potential; "The Mask of Anarchy" (1819), written in direct response to the Peterloo Massacre, imagines nonviolent mass resistance with an influence that reached far beyond Shelley's own century; and *Prometheus Unbound* (1820) makes his fullest mythic statement of hope for humanity's regeneration through love and imagination. "A Defence of Poetry" (1821) argues, in Romanticism's most ambitious single claim for poetry's social importance, that "poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the world." Shelley's drowning in 1822, and his own earlier mythologizing of Keats's death in "Adonais," extend the "doomed genius" pattern this book has now traced from Chatterton through Keats, whose own life and poetry Chapter 7 now takes up directly.

## Glossary

- **Peterloo Massacre** — the 16 August 1819 killing and wounding of peaceful pro-reform demonstrators by cavalry in Manchester, a defining act of the era's political repression.
- **Lyrical drama** — verse drama, like *Prometheus Unbound*, written primarily to be read and imagined rather than staged, combining dramatic dialogue with lyric poetry.
- **"Unacknowledged legislators of the world"** — Shelley's famous closing claim in "A Defence of Poetry" that poets, through imaginative sympathy, shape moral and political progress as significantly as lawmakers do.
