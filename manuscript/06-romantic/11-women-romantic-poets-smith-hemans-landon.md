## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Every chapter so far in this Part has followed a version of the Romantic canon most twentieth-century criticism settled on: Wordsworth, Coleridge, Byron, Shelley, Keats, plus Blake. This closing chapter corrects an important gap in that story, and it's worth being direct about why the gap exists. Charlotte Smith, Felicia Hemans, and Letitia Elizabeth Landon were, in their own lifetimes, read as widely as — in Hemans's case, arguably more widely than — most of the male poets this Part has already covered in dedicated chapters. Their later exclusion from the "canon" that crystallized in twentieth-century academic criticism says at least as much about how literary reputations get made and unmade as it does about the actual quality or importance of their work — a pattern you've now met repeatedly across this book, from Aphra Behn's long marginalization (Part IV, Chapter 9) to Chatterton's posthumous mythologizing (Part V, Chapter 4) to Blake's own rediscovery (Part V, Chapter 6).

Charlotte Smith's case is especially striking, because her exclusion from the canon obscures a genuine, acknowledged debt: both Wordsworth and Coleridge directly credited her sonnets as an influence on their own early poetic development, meaning the "solitary male genius" story often told about Romanticism's origins depends on erasing a woman poet whose work the movement's most celebrated founders themselves recognized as formative. Felicia Hemans and Letitia Elizabeth Landon, writing slightly later, built successful public careers within a flourishing market for women's poetry — a market this chapter will show you was neither small nor marginal, whatever its later critical reputation.

## Literary Movements

Charlotte Smith's *Elegiac Sonnets* (1784) revived a form that had lain largely dormant in serious English poetry since the Renaissance sonnet sequences of Part III and Milton's own sonnets — combining personal, melancholic reflection, often drawing directly on her own difficult biography, with attentive, closely observed natural description. This combination of intimate feeling and landscape observation anticipates, quite specifically, the poetic method Wordsworth would make famous a decade later, and both Wordsworth and Coleridge explicitly acknowledged Smith's sonnets as a genuine influence on their own early work — making her not simply a contemporary working in a similar vein, but a direct forerunner whose innovations the "official" Romantic generation actively drew on.

By the 1820s, a new publishing format had transformed the market for poetry: the **literary Annuals** — lavishly illustrated, expensively produced gift-books, published yearly and marketed heavily toward women readers, combining poetry, short prose, and engraved illustrations in a single fashionable volume. Letitia Elizabeth Landon, publishing under the initials **"L.E.L.,"** became one of the format's most successful and prolific contributors, and her career is inseparable from this specific commercial and cultural context. Felicia Hemans's poetry, meanwhile, achieved a level of popular reach few Romantic poets, male or female, ever matched: "Casabianca" (1826), her poem describing a boy who remained obediently at his post on a burning ship until it exploded, became one of the most widely memorized poems in the English language for over a century, a staple of school recitation on both sides of the Atlantic.

This chapter's three poets also share a recurring theme worth naming directly: the particular social risk women faced in claiming public literary fame at all. Where Byron's own scandal (Chapter 5) only deepened his celebrity, a woman poet's public visibility carried real danger to her reputation and respectability, and Landon's poetry in particular returns again and again to the vulnerability and cost of female poetic ambition and public exposure — a theme her own difficult, scandal-shadowed life made painfully concrete.

## Author Profiles

**Charlotte Smith** (1749–1806) was forced into an unhappy marriage at fifteen to Benjamin Smith, whose reckless financial mismanagement and eventual imprisonment for debt left Charlotte, the mother of twelve children, needing to write for money to support her family — a path into professional authorship driven by exactly the same economic necessity that shaped the careers of Aphra Behn, Delarivier Manley, and Eliza Haywood a century earlier (Part IV, Chapter 9). *Elegiac Sonnets* (1784), continuously revised and expanded across many subsequent editions, became her most influential work, and beyond her poetry she also wrote successful novels, including *Emmeline* (1788) and *The Old Manor House* (1793), demonstrating the same versatile, necessity-driven productivity that had defined professional women's authorship since Behn's own career.

**Felicia Hemans** (1793–1835) published her first poetry collection at just fourteen and went on to become one of the best-selling poets of the entire Romantic period. "Casabianca" (1826), based on a real incident from the 1798 Battle of the Nile, and "The Landing of the Pilgrim Fathers" (1825), which contributed directly to America's own founding mythology, both achieved massive, sustained popular readership well beyond Hemans's own lifetime. *Records of Woman* (1828) gathers poems centered specifically on women's historical and legendary experience — heroism, sacrifice, and suffering across a wide range of historical and cultural settings — and while her work is often read, sometimes reductively, as simply endorsing conventional domestic femininity, more recent criticism has recovered subtler currents within it: poems that appear to celebrate domestic duty while also registering, quite clearly, the real cost that duty exacts, especially from women.

**Letitia Elizabeth Landon** (1802–1838), publishing as "L.E.L.," built her career substantially within the Annuals publishing phenomenon described above, and her long narrative poem *The Improvisatrice* (1824) — following a gifted, passionate Italian female poet-performer whose talent brings fame but also real emotional vulnerability and suffering in love — reads as a genuine meditation on the costs of female poetic ambition, a theme that shadowed Landon's own life directly. Persistent rumors linking her romantically to several literary and editorial men, including her editor William Jerdan, damaged her public reputation and contributed to a broken engagement. She eventually married George Maclean, governor of a British colonial post at Cape Coast Castle in West Africa, and died there only weeks after her 1838 arrival, from an overdose of prussic acid officially ruled accidental — though the circumstances remain genuinely uncertain, and her early, mysterious death has since taken on something of the same mythic charge as the "doomed genius" narratives already traced through Chatterton (Part V, Chapter 4) and the Keats myth (Chapter 7).

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Elegiac Sonnets* (1784).** Read Smith's revival of the sonnet form as a genuine technical and thematic bridge between the sensibility of Part V and the Romantic poetry of this Part's earlier chapters: personal melancholy grounded in real biographical suffering, combined with careful natural observation, in a form both Wordsworth and Coleridge would later adopt and adapt themselves.
- **Hemans, "Casabianca" (1826).** The poem's enormous, sustained popularity — memorized by generations of schoolchildren well into the twentieth century — is itself a historical fact worth taking seriously: whatever twentieth-century criticism's later dismissal of "sentimental" verse (a reputational problem you already met in Part V, Chapter 1), Hemans's actual contemporary reach dwarfed that of many poets now considered far more central to the period.
- ***Records of Woman* (1828).** By deliberately centering women's historical experience as its explicit subject, Hemans's collection does more than simply depict conventional domestic virtue; read closely, several of its poems register real ambivalence about the costs that domestic duty and self-sacrifice impose specifically on women.
- **Landon, *The Improvisatrice* (1824).** The poem's narrative of a brilliant female performer whose public gift brings both fame and genuine personal cost offers a strikingly direct meditation on the risks Landon's own career and reputation would soon bear out in reality.

## Critical Perspectives

- **What does Charlotte Smith's acknowledged influence on Wordsworth and Coleridge do to the "solitary genius" story of Romanticism's origins?** Recognizing Smith's direct, credited influence on the very poets this Part treats as Romanticism's founders complicates any account of the movement's origins that omits her — a useful reminder that literary "firsts" often depend on erasing genuine predecessors.
- **Why did "sentimental" become such a dismissive term for Hemans's work?** Hemans's massive popularity with contemporary readers did not protect her from later critical condescension, once "sentimental" hardened into an insult (Part V, Chapter 1). Recovering her actual technical skill and genuine cultural reach complicates any simple dismissal of her work as merely popular rather than serious.
- **What does Landon's persistent theme of female poetic vulnerability suggest about gendered literary celebrity?** Byron's own scandal-touched fame (Chapter 5) enhanced his celebrity; Landon's comparable public visibility instead threatened her reputation and, eventually, her marriage prospects — a genuinely different set of social stakes attached to public authorship depending on gender.
- **What does the exclusion and recovery of these three poets suggest about how literary canons form?** Their case, alongside Behn's, Chatterton's, and Blake's earlier in this book, suggests that a writer's contemporary popularity is a poor predictor of their later canonical status, and that canons themselves are historically contingent constructions, actively made and remade by later critics and readers rather than simple, neutral records of enduring literary quality.

## Chapter Summary

Charlotte Smith's *Elegiac Sonnets* (1784) directly shaped the sonnet-writing of Wordsworth and Coleridge, a genuine influence the traditional Romantic canon's exclusion of Smith herself has long obscured. Felicia Hemans achieved a scale of popular readership through poems like "Casabianca" (1826) that few poets now considered central to the period ever matched, even as later critics dismissed her work as merely "sentimental." Letitia Elizabeth Landon built a major public career within the era's literary Annuals, her own poetry repeatedly meditating on the vulnerability and cost of female poetic ambition — a theme her scandal-touched life and early, uncertain death made painfully real. Together, these three poets complete a pattern this book has traced since Behn, Manley, and Haywood in Part IV: writers whose genuine contemporary significance later canon formation obscured, and whose recovery changes how completely we can understand any given literary period.

That closes Part VI. Across eleven chapters, you've traced English Romanticism from the French Revolution's collapsed hopes, through the shared manifesto of *Lyrical Ballads*, the full careers of Wordsworth and Coleridge, the second generation's Byron, Shelley, and Keats, Romantic prose, the Gothic novel, Jane Austen's novel of manners, and now the women poets whose work ran alongside, and in Smith's case helped enable, everything else this Part has studied. Part VII picks up the story in 1837, as Britain's industrialization, expanding empire, and the pressing social question Victorian writers called the "Condition of England" reshape English literature once again.

## Glossary

- **Elegiac Sonnets** — Charlotte Smith's title for her influential 1784 collection; here "elegiac" describes the poems' melancholic mood and theme, not a distinct formal stanza (compare the different, formally specific "elegiac stanza" defined in Part V, Chapter 2).
- **The Annuals** — lavishly illustrated literary gift-books, published yearly beginning in the 1820s and marketed heavily toward women readers, combining poetry, prose, and engravings.
- **"L.E.L."** — Letitia Elizabeth Landon's publishing byline, an adopted literary identity comparable to earlier personas like Addison and Steele's (Part IV, Chapter 4) or Lamb's "Elia" (Chapter 8).
