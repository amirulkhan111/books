## Historical and Socio-Political Context

You already have a good deal of Wordsworth's story from Chapters 1 and 2: his youthful enthusiasm for the French Revolution, his collaboration with Coleridge, and the 1798 *Lyrical Ballads* that made his theoretical case for a new kind of poetry. This chapter fills in what came before and after, and gives his major poems the sustained attention *Lyrical Ballads*' shared chapter could only preview.

Wordsworth was born in 1770 in Cockermouth, in England's Lake District, and lost both parents by the age of thirteen — his mother in 1778, his father in 1783 — after which he and his four siblings, including his sister Dorothy, were separated among different relatives. He attended St John's College, Cambridge, from 1787 to 1791, more engaged by long walking tours than by formal study, and it was during a break from Cambridge, in 1790, that he first visited Revolutionary France. His much longer stay there in 1791–92 (Chapter 1) produced both genuine political radicalization and a personal relationship with a Frenchwoman, Annette Vallon, with whom he had a daughter, Caroline, born in December 1792. The outbreak of war between Britain and France in February 1793 made a return to France difficult and then, for years, effectively impossible; Wordsworth did not marry Annette, and instead married a childhood friend, Mary Hutchinson, in 1802 — though not before making one final visit to France, during a brief peace that year, to see Annette and their daughter in person. He continued supporting Caroline financially for the rest of his life. It's worth noticing how closely this personal arc — youthful hope, disrupted by war and circumstance, followed by a different, more settled resolution — echoes the larger national pattern Chapter 1 traced through the Revolution itself.

A turning point in Wordsworth's practical fortunes came in 1795, when a friend, Raisley Calvert, died and left him a small legacy, freeing him from needing a conventional profession and enabling the full-time devotion to poetry that produced *Lyrical Ballads* three years later. After the German trip that followed *Lyrical Ballads*' publication (Chapter 2), Wordsworth and Dorothy settled in 1799 at Dove Cottage in Grasmere, in the heart of the Lake District — his home for the rest of his life, and the landscape that gives his poetry its deepest, most recurring source of imagery. Over the following decades, Wordsworth's politics shifted markedly rightward: by 1813 he had accepted a government position as Distributor of Stamps for Westmorland, and by his later years he supported the conservative political establishment he had once opposed as a young radical — a trajectory that drew open scorn from the younger, still-radical poets you'll meet in Chapters 5 and 6.

## Literary Movements

Chapter 2 already gave you Wordsworth's public theory of poetry, from the 1800 Preface. This chapter's job is to show you the deeper philosophy that theory rests on, developed most fully in his private, unpublished masterpiece, *The Prelude*. Wordsworth uses a striking phrase in that poem for certain especially vivid childhood memories that retain, throughout a person's life, an unusual power to restore and nourish the mind in later times of exhaustion or sorrow: **"spots of time."** These are not simply pleasant memories; they are moments when perception felt unusually intense and alive, and Wordsworth argues that returning to them, even decades later, can genuinely renew a person's capacity for feeling and understanding.

Underneath this idea sits Wordsworth's mature account of the relationship between mind and nature, which is more complex than simple passive observation. Nature, for Wordsworth, is not simply *there*, waiting to be looked at; the perceiving mind actively shapes what it perceives, even as it is shaped by it in turn — a genuinely reciprocal relationship he describes in "Tintern Abbey," from Chapter 2, as nature giving "half create, / And what perceive." This reciprocity matters because it makes imagination itself a form of real perception, not mere fantasy layered over a passive, external world.

That philosophy organizes Wordsworth's account of childhood and loss, most fully worked out in the "Immortality Ode," discussed below. Children, in Wordsworth's account, are born with an intuitive, almost visionary closeness to some prior, more glorious existence — "trailing clouds of glory do we come / From God, who is our home" — which fades steadily as they grow older and take on adult habits of thought, until "shades of the prison-house begin to close / Upon the growing Boy." But this is not simply a story of loss without recovery: Wordsworth insists that maturity brings its own compensation, a "philosophic mind" capable of finding, through memory and reflection, a different and in some ways deeper strength than childhood's direct visionary intensity ever offered. This loss-and-compensation pattern — something valuable disappears, but something else, harder-won, replaces it — is one of Romanticism's most influential and recurring structures, and it maps closely onto the larger pattern Chapter 1 traced in the Revolution's own disappointed hopes.

## Author Profiles

**William Wordsworth** (1770–1850) conceived of *The Prelude* — his great autobiographical poem, subtitled "Growth of a Poet's Mind" — as merely an introductory, preparatory work for a much larger philosophical epic he called *The Recluse*, which he never completed. Composed initially in a shorter two-part version in 1799, then substantially expanded into thirteen books by 1805, and revised once more into its final form shortly before his death, *The Prelude* traces Wordsworth's own development from childhood experiences of nature in the Lake District, through his Cambridge years, his time in Revolutionary France and his subsequent disillusionment, to his eventual recovery of poetic and imaginative power. Wordsworth kept the poem substantially private during his lifetime, sharing manuscript versions mainly with Coleridge, to whom the poem is addressed throughout; it appeared in print only in 1850, shortly after his death, under the title *The Prelude* — a name Wordsworth himself never used, having referred to it privately simply as "the poem to Coleridge" or "the poem on my own life." Beyond this central, hidden masterpiece, Wordsworth's public reputation rested on *Lyrical Ballads* (Chapter 2), the 1807 *Poems, in Two Volumes* (containing the "Immortality Ode" and poems like "I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud," whose famous image of daffodils drew directly on a passage from Dorothy Wordsworth's journal), and *The Excursion* (1814), a long philosophical poem salvaged from the larger *Recluse* project. Wordsworth's later acceptance of a government sinecure in 1813 and his growing conservatism drew real hostility from younger writers who felt he had betrayed the radical hopes of his youth, even as his reputation with the broader reading public only grew, culminating in his appointment as Poet Laureate in 1843.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***The Prelude* (1799/1805/1850).** Read this as English Romanticism's central autobiographical achievement: an epic-scale poem about the formation of one particular imagination, using Wordsworth's own life — nature, education, revolution, disillusionment, recovery — as material serious enough to bear epic treatment, a genuinely novel claim for a poem to make about an ordinary individual life rather than a nation's legendary founding, as earlier epics (Part I, Chapter 3; Part III, Chapter 12) had done.
- **"Tintern Abbey" (1798), revisited.** Building on Chapter 2's introduction, notice how the poem's closing address to Dorothy extends its argument about memory into the future: Wordsworth hopes his sister will one day recall this same landscape and draw the same restorative strength from remembering it that he now draws from remembering his own earlier visit.
- **"London, 1802."** Written the same year as his French visit and marriage, this sonnet directly addresses Milton — "Milton! thou shouldst be living at this hour: / England hath need of thee" — lamenting what Wordsworth saw as a decline in English public and moral life, and calling on the example of Milton's own combination of poetic greatness and civic seriousness (Part III, Chapters 11–12) as a needed corrective.
- **"I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud" (1807).** This poem's famous image of daffodils "fluttering and dancing in the breeze" draws directly on an entry from Dorothy Wordsworth's journal describing a walk the siblings took together — a clear instance of the uncredited creative contribution Chapter 2 already asked you to notice.
- **The "Ode: Intimations of Immortality from Recollections of Early Childhood" (1804–1807).** The Ode's arc — from childhood's visionary "clouds of glory," through their gradual fading, to maturity's compensating "philosophic mind" — is Wordsworth's fullest single statement of his theory of memory, loss, and imaginative recovery, and arguably his most philosophically ambitious poem.
- ***The Excursion* (1814).** Salvaged from the larger, uncompleted *Recluse* project *The Prelude* was meant only to introduce, this long philosophical poem shows Wordsworth attempting, less successfully than in his shorter lyric and autobiographical work, to build sustained philosophical argument directly into extended narrative verse.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Did Wordsworth betray his own youthful radicalism?** His acceptance of a government sinecure in 1813 and his later support for conservative politics drew real contempt from Byron, Shelley, and the critic William Hazlitt, who saw a poet who had once celebrated revolutionary hope settling comfortably into the establishment he'd once opposed. A more generous reading treats this shift as a genuine, if disappointing, response to watching revolution curdle into Terror and empire — the same disillusionment Chapter 1 described at a national scale, played out here in one individual life.
- **Is the Immortality Ode's compensation actually convincing?** The poem insists that the "philosophic mind" adequately replaces childhood's lost visionary intensity, but many readers have found this resolution more asserted than genuinely earned — a hopeful claim the poem wants to believe rather than one it has fully demonstrated. Whether you find the Ode's ending consoling or merely wishful is a real interpretive choice, not a settled question.
- **Which version of *The Prelude* should we read?** The poem exists in genuinely different forms — a shorter, more radical 1799 text, a fuller 1805 version, and a final 1850 text revised by an older, more conservative Wordsworth, who softened some of his own youthful political statements along the way. Reading only the 1850 version, the one most widely printed for over a century, risks mistaking the older poet's revised self-image for the younger poet's actual, more radical experience.
- **How should Wordsworth's relationship with Annette Vallon inform readings of his poetry?** The Prelude includes an episode, "Vaudracour and Julia," that thinly fictionalizes his own French relationship; critics generally read this as evidence that even Wordsworth's most personal material passed through deliberate poetic transformation, a useful reminder against treating any of his poetry as simple, unmediated autobiography.

## Chapter Summary

Beyond the public theory of *Lyrical Ballads* studied in Chapter 2, Wordsworth's deepest achievement lies in *The Prelude*, a private autobiographical epic tracing his own imaginative development from Lake District childhood through Cambridge, revolutionary France, personal disillusionment, and eventual recovery — composed across decades (1799, 1805, 1850) and published only after his death. His concept of "spots of time" and his account of a reciprocal relationship between mind and nature underpin his fullest philosophical statement, the "Immortality Ode" (1804–1807), which asks whether maturity's "philosophic mind" can really compensate for childhood's lost visionary intensity. Wordsworth's own life recapitulated, in miniature, the national arc Chapter 1 traced from revolutionary hope to disillusionment — through his relationship with Annette Vallon, his eventual settled marriage to Mary Hutchinson, and his gradual, controversial shift from youthful radical to establishment Poet Laureate. Chapter 4 now turns to his closest collaborator, Samuel Taylor Coleridge, whose own poetry and criticism developed alongside Wordsworth's throughout this entire period.

## Glossary

- **Spots of time** — Wordsworth's term, from *The Prelude*, for especially vivid childhood memories that retain a lasting power to restore and renew the mind.
- **The Recluse** — Wordsworth's planned, never-completed large-scale philosophical epic, of which *The Prelude* was intended only as an introduction and *The Excursion* a surviving fragment.
