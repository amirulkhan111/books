## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 1 introduced a distinction worth returning to directly now: a "second generation" of Romantic poets, born in the 1790s, who inherited the French Revolution's legacy as recent history rather than personal youthful experience, and who generally stayed considerably more politically radical than the older Wordsworth and Coleridge. George Gordon Byron, born in London in 1788, is this generation's most famous and most flamboyant figure.

Byron's early life combined genuine hardship with unexpected grandeur. His father, a dissolute former army officer nicknamed "Mad Jack" Byron, abandoned the family and died when Byron was three; Byron himself was born with a deformity affecting his right foot that caused him lifelong physical self-consciousness, which he answered partly through defiant athleticism — he famously swam the Hellespont in 1810, re-enacting the mythical swim of Leander. In 1798, at age ten, he unexpectedly inherited the title of Baron Byron and the crumbling ancestral estate of Newstead Abbey from a great-uncle, gaining aristocratic status without matching wealth, a mismatch that shadowed his finances for the rest of his life. After Harrow and Trinity College, Cambridge — where he kept a pet bear as a provocative joke against college rules banning dogs — Byron published his first collection, *Hours of Idleness* (1807), which the *Edinburgh Review* mocked harshly; his sharp verse reply, *English Bards and Scotch Reviewers* (1809), not only attacked his critics but also mocked Wordsworth, Coleridge, and Southey as a pretentious "Lake School" — an early sign that Byron's relationship to the older Romantic generation was, from the start, considerably more combative than reverent.

Between 1809 and 1811, with much of continental Europe closed to British travelers by the Napoleonic Wars, Byron took an unusually adventurous Grand Tour through Portugal, Spain, Malta, Albania, Greece, and the Ottoman Empire — a journey that gave him the material for the poem that made him instantly, overwhelmingly famous.

## Literary Movements

Byron's most influential single contribution to world literature is a character type modern criticism calls the **Byronic hero**: a brooding, charismatic, morally ambiguous figure marked by a mysterious, often guilt-shadowed past, intense and barely controlled passions, deep melancholy, and a defiant, individualistic rejection of ordinary social convention. *Childe Harold's Pilgrimage* introduces this figure in his most fully developed early form — a jaded young nobleman touring Europe and the Near East, meditating on ruined civilizations and his own restless dissatisfaction — and *Manfred* (1817), a dramatic poem about a guilt-ridden nobleman with supernatural powers who refuses religious repentance even at the point of death, pushes the type to its starkest extreme. The Byronic hero's influence proved enormous and long-lasting, shaping later figures across European literature, from Pushkin's Eugene Onegin to the brooding heroes of the Brontës' novels you'll meet in Part VII.

The summer of 1816, spent at Lake Geneva alongside Percy Bysshe Shelley (Chapter 6) and Mary Shelley (Chapter 9), turned out to be one of the single most productive gatherings in literary history. Unusually poor weather that year — caused, as scientists have since established, by the 1815 eruption of Mount Tambora in Indonesia — kept the group largely indoors, where they entertained themselves reading and telling ghost stories, eventually proposing that each member write one of their own. Mary Shelley's contribution became *Frankenstein*, which you'll study fully in Chapter 9; Byron's own fragmentary vampire tale, abandoned unfinished, was later developed by his physician and traveling companion, John Polidori, into "The Vampyre" (1819) — the first significant vampire story in English, whose aristocratic villain, Lord Ruthven, was widely read at the time as a thinly disguised, unflattering portrait of Byron himself, evidence of how thoroughly the "Byronic" persona had already entered wider literary culture by this point.

Byron's later masterpiece, *Don Juan* (1819–1824), represents a genuine shift in register: written in **ottava rima**, an eight-line Italian stanza form (rhymed ABABABCC) that Byron uses for sprawling, digressive, wickedly funny social and political satire, reworking the legendary seducer Don Juan into a more passive figure pursued by women rather than pursuing them. This satirical, worldly mode owes an openly acknowledged debt to Pope and the Augustan tradition of Part IV — Byron considered himself, in some respects, a defender of classical literary values against what he saw as the excesses of Wordsworth's and Coleridge's "new school," a stance worth holding onto as a genuine complication of any simple, unified picture of what "Romantic" writing actually looked like.

## Author Profiles

**George Gordon, Lord Byron** (1788–1824) became instantly and overwhelmingly famous on the 1812 publication of *Childe Harold's Pilgrimage*, Cantos I and II, prompting his own widely quoted remark: "I awoke one morning and found myself famous." That same year, he delivered his maiden speech in the House of Lords defending Nottinghamshire Luddite frame-breakers against a harsh government crackdown bill — an early, genuine sign of the political sympathies that distinguished him sharply from the older Romantic generation's later conservatism (Chapters 1 and 3). Byron's 1815 marriage to Annabella Milbanke collapsed within a year amid rumors of cruelty, debt, and, most damagingly, an incestuous relationship with his half-sister Augusta Leigh; the resulting public scandal forced Byron to leave England in 1816, never to return. He spent his remaining years in Switzerland and Italy — where he completed *Childe Harold*'s final cantos, wrote *Manfred*, and produced his comic masterpiece, *Don Juan* — before devoting his final years and much of his personal fortune to the Greek War of Independence against Ottoman rule, joining the cause directly in 1823. He died at Missolonghi in 1824, not in battle but from a fever, worsened by the contemporary medical practice of bloodletting, and became an enduring symbol of European philhellenism and of Greek national independence itself, still celebrated as a hero in Greece today.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***English Bards and Scotch Reviewers* (1809).** Byron's early satirical response to his critics, modeled openly on Pope's couplet satire (Part IV, Chapter 6), already shows the combative, classically inflected wit that would resurface, transformed, in *Don Juan* a decade later.
- ***Childe Harold's Pilgrimage*, Cantos I–IV (1812, 1816, 1818).** The poem's semi-autobiographical traveler, meditating melancholically on ruined civilizations and his own restless dissatisfaction, established the Byronic hero as a recognizable literary type and made Byron the most famous poet in Europe almost overnight.
- ***Manfred* (1817).** Composed during Byron's Swiss exile, this dramatic poem's guilt-haunted, supernaturally empowered protagonist, who refuses conventional religious redemption even facing death, represents the Byronic hero at its most extreme and most explicitly autobiographical, widely read as reflecting Byron's own anguish over the Augusta Leigh scandal.
- ***Don Juan* (1819–1824).** Left unfinished at Byron's death, this sprawling comic epic in ottava rima marks a genuine artistic shift from Byronic melancholy toward satiric wit, social critique, and self-aware digression, and many critics consider it Byron's greatest single achievement — proof that the poet of *Childe Harold*'s brooding intensity could also write some of the era's funniest and most socially perceptive verse.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is Byron really a "Romantic" in the Wordsworthian sense?** Byron's professed admiration for Pope, and his open mockery of Wordsworth's and Coleridge's poetic theory, complicate any simple, unified account of "Romanticism" as a coherent movement — echoing the very question Chapter 1 already raised about the usefulness of the label. Byron sits genuinely awkwardly inside a category modern literary history built partly around the poets he spent much of his career satirizing.
- **Is the Byronic hero a serious literary achievement, or a celebrity performance?** Byron's own dramatic public life — the scandal, the exile, the eventual death in a foreign revolutionary cause — blurred so thoroughly with the Byronic hero's fictional characteristics that separating the literary achievement from Byron's own carefully cultivated persona is genuinely difficult. Most critics now treat this blurring itself as part of Byron's significance, rather than as a problem to be resolved.
- **Was Byron's death at Missolonghi genuine commitment or perfect theater?** Both readings are defensible and not mutually exclusive: Byron's support for Greek independence involved real financial sacrifice and personal risk, but his death also completed, almost too perfectly, the Byronic hero's narrative arc of dying young in a noble, doomed cause — a coincidence of life and legend few other writers' biographies offer so cleanly.
- **Does *Don Juan*'s comic turn represent growth beyond the Byronic hero, or a different performance of the same underlying sensibility?** Some critics read *Don Juan*'s satiric wit as evidence of real artistic maturation past Byronic melancholy's limitations; others argue that the same ironic, self-aware performance of identity runs through both modes, simply directed at different targets.

## Chapter Summary

George Gordon, Lord Byron, gave the second generation of Romantic poets its most famous public figure and its most influential literary type, the Byronic hero — a brooding, morally ambiguous, defiantly individualistic outsider first fully realized in *Childe Harold's Pilgrimage* (1812) and pushed to its starkest form in *Manfred* (1817). The 1816 Geneva summer with the Shelleys, disrupted by the "Year Without a Summer," produced not only Byron's own work but the ghost-story competition that generated Mary Shelley's *Frankenstein* (Chapter 9) and Polidori's *The Vampyre*. Byron's later masterpiece, *Don Juan* (1819–1824), shifted toward comic, satiric ottava rima verse indebted to Pope and the Augustan tradition (Part IV), complicating any simple account of Byron as a straightforward Wordsworthian Romantic. His genuine political radicalism, from defending Luddite workers in 1812 to dying in 1824 for Greek independence, sets him apart from the older Romantic generation's later conservatism, and his death at Missolonghi fused his life and his poetry's own central myth into one of literary history's most legendary endings. Chapter 6 now turns to Byron's close friend and fellow Geneva companion, Percy Bysshe Shelley.

## Glossary

- **Byronic hero** — a brooding, charismatic, morally ambiguous literary character type, marked by a mysterious past, intense passion, melancholy, and defiant individualism, originating in Byron's own poetry.
- **Ottava rima** — an eight-line Italian stanza form (rhymed ABABABCC), used by Byron in *Don Juan* for sprawling comic and satiric verse.
- **Philhellenism** — a European cultural and political movement of sympathy and support for Greek independence and classical Greek culture, which Byron's death helped make famous.
