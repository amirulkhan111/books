## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Coleridge has appeared throughout this Part already — as Wordsworth's collaborator on *Lyrical Ballads* (Chapter 2), the object of government suspicion at Nether Stowey (Chapter 1), and the friend whose closeness to Wordsworth shaped both poets' work in ways still difficult to fully untangle (Chapter 3). This chapter finally gives him his own sustained attention, as both a poet in his own right and, arguably even more importantly, as one of the most influential literary critics in the English language.

Coleridge was born in 1772 in Ottery St Mary, Devon, the youngest of ten children of a vicar and schoolmaster. After his father's death in 1781, he was sent to Christ's Hospital, a London charity school, where he met Charles Lamb, whom you'll encounter again in Chapter 8; the two remained close friends for life. After an unsettled time at Jesus College, Cambridge — including a brief, strange episode in 1793 when he enlisted in the army under the false name "Silas Tomkyn Comberbacke" to escape personal and financial difficulties, before his family arranged his discharge — Coleridge met the poet Robert Southey in 1794, and together they conceived **Pantisocracy**: a planned utopian community, to be founded on the banks of Pennsylvania's Susquehanna River, organized around shared property and genuine social equality. The scheme never came close to realization, undone by lack of money and practical planning, but it committed Coleridge to marrying Sara Fricker in 1795 (Southey married her sister Edith), a marriage that grew increasingly unhappy over the following years, though the couple never formally separated. That same year, Coleridge met Wordsworth, and the intense creative partnership that followed — described fully in Chapters 1 through 3 — produced *Lyrical Ballads* in 1798.

Running underneath all of this, and shaping much of what follows in this chapter, was Coleridge's lifelong dependence on opium, initially taken as a medical treatment for physical pain and illness, which developed into a severe and lasting addiction. Its effects on his health, his relationships, and his creative output were profound, and this chapter treats that fact honestly rather than either sensationalizing it or passing over it in silence, since it bears directly on several of his most important poems.

## Literary Movements

Coleridge developed a distinctive poetic form modern critics call the **conversation poem**: a quiet, meditative blank-verse poem, addressed to a silent listener (sometimes present, sometimes absent), that moves from close description of an actual scene toward a reflective, often consoling conclusion. "This Lime-Tree Bower My Prison" (1797), written while Coleridge was confined at home with an injury while his friends walked without him, and "Frost at Midnight" (1798), addressed to his infant son Hartley while the poet sits alone by the fire at night, are the genre's clearest examples — modest in scale compared with "The Rime of the Ancient Mariner," but genuinely influential on the more famous meditative lyric poems this Part will study in Chapters 6 and 7.

Coleridge's most lasting contribution to literary theory, though, comes from *Biographia Literaria* (1817), where he draws a distinction that has shaped criticism ever since: between **Fancy** and **Imagination**. Fancy, in Coleridge's account, is a lower mental faculty that simply combines and rearranges existing memories and images, mechanically, without genuinely creating anything new — decorative association, not real creation. Imagination is something else entirely, and Coleridge further divides it into two levels. **Primary imagination** is the basic, universal, largely unconscious power by which every human mind actively shapes raw sensory experience into coherent perception in the first place — present in everyone, all the time, simply as part of ordinary perception. **Secondary imagination** is a conscious echo of this same power, operating deliberately in the poet: it dissolves and dissipates ordinary, already-formed perceptions in order to recreate and unify them into new, organically whole works of art. Coleridge goes so far as to compare primary imagination to the divine creative act itself — "a repetition in the finite mind of the eternal act of creation in the infinite I AM" — reflecting his deep engagement with German philosophy, especially Kant and Schelling, whose ideas he introduced to English readers, not without later controversy over how much of this material he borrowed without full acknowledgment.

This same organicist thinking shapes Coleridge's influential Shakespeare criticism, delivered in a series of London lectures between 1811 and 1819. Where earlier critics, including Pope (Part IV, Chapter 6) and, to a lesser extent, Johnson (Part IV, Chapter 8), had judged literature partly against external, rule-based standards, Coleridge argues that truly great art, like Shakespeare's, has **organic form**: it grows according to its own inner law, the way a living plant grows into its own natural shape, rather than being shaped from outside by rules imposed on it after the fact. This builds directly on Johnson's earlier defense of Shakespeare's disregard for neoclassical rules, but grounds that defense in a fuller philosophical account of how genuine creative form actually works.

## Author Profiles

**Samuel Taylor Coleridge** (1772–1834) produced, within a remarkably short and concentrated period around 1797–1798, nearly all the poetry for which he is remembered — "The Rime of the Ancient Mariner," "Kubla Khan," the first part of "Christabel," and the conversation poems — after which his poetic output declined sharply, even as his critical and philosophical writing continued for decades. "Kubla Khan," not published until 1816, carries with it Coleridge's own famous account of its origin: composed, he claimed, in a vivid dream-vision after reading a passage about the Mongol emperor Kublai Khan, under the influence of opium, and left permanently unfinished when a visitor — "a person from Porlock," on business — interrupted him and destroyed his ability to recall the rest of the vision. "Christabel," a Gothic narrative poem about a young woman who brings a mysterious, possibly sinister female stranger into her father's house, similarly remained unfinished, composed in two parts six years apart and never completed at all. Coleridge's marriage to Sara Fricker deteriorated over these same years, compounded by his opium addiction and by an unrequited love for Sara Hutchinson, the sister-in-law of Wordsworth after Wordsworth's 1802 marriage to Mary Hutchinson — a painful entanglement that shapes "Dejection: An Ode," discussed below. In his final decades, Coleridge moved in 1816 into the Highgate household of Dr. James Gillman, who helped him manage, though never fully overcome, his addiction; there Coleridge became known as "the Sage of Highgate," hosting regular Thursday evening gatherings that drew admiring visitors eager to hear him talk, even as his own poetic productivity had largely ceased. *Biographia Literaria* (1817), his major work of criticism and intellectual autobiography, remains his most influential single book, whatever its debts to German philosophy.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"The Rime of the Ancient Mariner" (1798), revisited.** Building on Chapter 2's introduction, notice how thoroughly the poem's moral — that reverence for all living creatures, however humble, is the path back from guilt and isolation — depends on genuinely supernatural machinery to make its point, exactly the kind of imaginative territory Coleridge assigned himself in the *Lyrical Ballads* collaboration.
- **"Kubla Khan" (1797/1816).** Whatever the precise truth of its interruption story, the poem's genuinely dreamlike, fragmentary intensity — the "stately pleasure-dome," the "caves of ice," the poet's closing fantasy of recovering supreme creative power by reviving a vision of "an Abyssinian maid" — makes it Coleridge's most vivid single demonstration of imagination operating, or claiming to operate, almost outside conscious control.
- **"Christabel" (1797, 1800/1816).** The poem's atmosphere of ambiguous, half-suggested supernatural threat anticipates the Gothic novel you'll study fully in Chapter 9, and its permanent incompleteness, like "Kubla Khan"'s, has become part of its critical fascination: readers are left to speculate about a resolution Coleridge himself never supplied.
- **"Frost at Midnight" (1798).** As the clearest example of Coleridge's conversation-poem form, the poem moves from a description of frost forming silently outside the poet's cottage to a tender hope for his infant son's future, imagining a childhood spent directly amid nature rather than the "great city" confinement of Coleridge's own upbringing.
- **"Dejection: An Ode" (1802).** Written in genuine personal crisis — failing health, opium dependency, marital unhappiness, and unrequited love — the poem confronts a loss of imaginative power with unusual directness: "I see, not feel, how beautiful they are." Where Wordsworth's roughly contemporaneous "Immortality Ode" (Chapter 3) ultimately claims a compensating "philosophic mind," Coleridge's poem offers far less consolation, locating whatever hope remains only in "the passion and the life, whose fountains are within" — a genuinely bleaker resolution to a strikingly similar crisis.
- **Fancy and Imagination, from *Biographia Literaria* (1817).** Coleridge's distinction gave English criticism a durable vocabulary for describing creative power as something more than clever recombination — a vocabulary still shaping how critics discuss originality and creativity today.

## Critical Perspectives

- **How should we read the opium origin story behind "Kubla Khan"?** Coleridge's own account, presenting the poem as an involuntary dream-vision interrupted before completion, has been read both as sincere reportage and as a partly self-mythologizing frame designed to excuse the poem's fragmentary state as something other than simple unfinished work. Either way, the story has become inseparable from how readers approach the poem.
- **What does the recurring incompleteness of Coleridge's major poems suggest about his creative temperament?** Both "Kubla Khan" and "Christabel" remain permanently unfinished, in real contrast to Wordsworth's *The Prelude* (Chapter 3), which Wordsworth did eventually complete, if only in private. Some critics read this pattern as evidence of genuine creative crisis or self-doubt; others suggest that incompleteness itself became, for Coleridge, an aesthetic and even philosophical statement about the impossibility of ever fully capturing visionary experience in finished form.
- **How much of *Biographia Literaria* is really Coleridge's own thinking?** Scholars have long documented substantial, largely unacknowledged borrowing from the German philosopher Friedrich Schelling in Coleridge's philosophical chapters, raising real questions about intellectual originality. Most critics now treat Coleridge less as a plagiarist in the simple sense than as a genuinely original synthesizer, who took German Idealist philosophy and applied it, with real innovation, to English literary criticism in ways Schelling himself never attempted.
- **Dejection versus the Immortality Ode: which response to lost imaginative power is more honest?** Wordsworth's poem asserts that maturity compensates for childhood's lost vision; Coleridge's poem largely refuses that consolation. Neither position is simply "correct," but comparing them directly shows two of the era's greatest poets responding to strikingly similar personal crises with genuinely different conclusions.

## Chapter Summary

Beyond his crucial collaboration with Wordsworth, Coleridge produced, in a brief but extraordinary burst around 1797–1798, the poems that secure his individual reputation: the supernatural "Rime of the Ancient Mariner," the dreamlike, unfinished "Kubla Khan," the Gothic-inflected, equally unfinished "Christabel," and the quieter "conversation poems" like "Frost at Midnight." "Dejection: An Ode" (1802) confronts a crisis of lost imaginative power with a bleaker honesty than Wordsworth's roughly contemporaneous "Immortality Ode," even as both poems wrestle with strikingly similar losses. Coleridge's opium addiction, unhappy marriage, and declining poetic output shadowed the rest of his life, but his critical achievement, above all *Biographia Literaria*'s distinction between Fancy and Imagination and his organicist Shakespeare criticism, secured him a lasting place as one of English literature's most influential theorists of creative power — a role that outlasted, in the end, his own diminished output as a poet. Chapter 5 now turns to the younger, "second generation" Romantics, beginning with Byron.

## Glossary

- **Conversation poem** — a meditative blank-verse poem, developed by Coleridge, addressed to a silent listener and moving from description of an actual scene to reflective conclusion.
- **Fancy** — in Coleridge's theory, a lower mental faculty that merely combines and rearranges existing images without genuine creation.
- **Primary imagination** — the basic, universal, largely unconscious power by which the mind shapes sensory experience into perception.
- **Secondary imagination** — the poet's conscious, deliberate echo of primary imagination, dissolving and recreating perceptions into new organic wholes.
- **Organic form** — Coleridge's concept of artistic form as growing according to a work's own inner law, like a living organism, rather than being imposed by external rules.
