## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Where Byron was born a baron and Shelley into wealthy gentry, John Keats came from a considerably humbler background: his father managed a London livery stable, and Keats's own social origins, along with his lack of a classical Oxbridge education, later became a direct target of snobbish literary attack, as you'll see below. Keats was born in 1795, and his family's story is shadowed throughout by tuberculosis, then called "consumption": his mother died of the disease in 1810, when Keats was fourteen, and his younger brother Tom died of it in 1818, nursed by Keats himself in the disease's final, difficult stages — direct, intimate experience of the illness that would eventually kill Keats too.

Rather than pursuing formal literary training, Keats trained for a medical career, apprenticed to a surgeon-apothecary from 1810 and continuing his studies at Guy's Hospital, London, where he qualified as a licensed apothecary in 1816. He abandoned medicine for poetry shortly afterward, but his clinical training left a lasting mark on his verse's often intensely physical, bodily sensory imagery. In 1816, Keats met Leigh Hunt, the radical journalist and poet who edited a periodical called *The Examiner* — worth distinguishing clearly from the very different, Tory-aligned *Examiner* Jonathan Swift had edited a century earlier (Part IV, Chapter 5); Hunt's circle, which also included Shelley (Chapter 6), gave Keats his first literary community and his first publishing opportunities.

## Literary Movements

Keats's most influential critical idea appears not in a published poem or essay but in a private letter, written to his brothers in December 1817. Describing what he admired most in Shakespeare, Keats coined the term **negative capability**: the capacity to remain, as he put it, "in uncertainties, Mysteries, doubts, without any irritable reaching after fact and reason." This is worth setting directly against Coleridge's theory of Imagination from Chapter 4. Where Coleridge builds an elaborate, systematic philosophical account of how the creative mind actively shapes and unifies experience, Keats celebrates almost the opposite quality — a willingness to dwell inside contradiction and uncertainty without forcing premature resolution, treating the writer's tolerance for the unresolved as itself a mark of genuine imaginative power.

You should also read Keats's 1819 odes as the culmination of a tradition this book has traced across two full Parts: Gray's "Ode on a Distant Prospect of Eton College" (Part V, Chapter 2), Wordsworth's "Immortality Ode" (Chapter 3), and Coleridge's "Dejection: An Ode" (Chapter 4) all use the ode form to confront loss, mortality, and the fading of youthful or visionary power. Keats's odes take up exactly this tradition and push it toward its most fully achieved expression, repeating a recognizable structural pattern: a desire to escape mortal suffering and transience through art or imagination, followed by an inevitable, often painful return to ordinary mortal consciousness. "Ode to a Nightingale" and "Ode on a Grecian Urn," discussed below, both follow this pattern; "To Autumn," Keats's final ode, resolves it more calmly than either.

## Author Profiles

**John Keats** (1795–1821) produced nearly all the poetry for which he is remembered within an extraordinarily concentrated period conventionally called his **"great year"** — roughly 1818 to 1819 — composing his major odes within just a few months even as he nursed his dying brother, fell in love with his neighbor Fanny Brawne (to whom he became engaged but, owing to his poverty and failing health, could never marry), and began experiencing the first serious symptoms of the tuberculosis that would kill him. His long mythological poem *Endymion* (1818), which he had dedicated to the memory of Thomas Chatterton (Part V, Chapter 4), was met with vicious, class-inflected reviews from the Tory *Quarterly Review* and *Blackwood's Magazine*, which coined the mocking label "Cockney School" for Keats, Hunt, and their circle, dismissing them as vulgar pretenders unfit for serious poetry because of their non-aristocratic backgrounds and lack of classical education — one infamous review told Keats to go "back to the shop" and his "plasters, pills, and ointment boxes." This hostility, as you already know from Shelley's "Adonais" in Chapter 6, was later blamed for hastening Keats's death; most modern scholars reject that account as an exaggerated myth, noting both that Keats died of tuberculosis, a disease he had already watched kill his mother and brother, and that his own letters show him remarkably self-possessed about criticism, observing that "praise or blame has but a momentary effect on the man whose love of beauty in the abstract... makes him a severe critic of his own works." On medical advice, Keats traveled to Rome in September 1820 with his friend, the painter Joseph Severn, hoping the warmer climate might help; it did not, and he died there on 23 February 1821, aged twenty-five. At his own request, his gravestone in Rome's Protestant Cemetery does not give his name, reading instead: "Here lies One whose Name was writ in Water" — a self-effacing epitaph that reads, in hindsight, as one of literary history's more striking ironies, given his subsequent reputation as one of the greatest poets in the English language.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"On First Looking into Chapman's Homer" (1816).** This early sonnet records Keats's own excited discovery of Homer through George Chapman's Elizabethan translation, comparing the experience to an explorer's first sight of a new ocean — though Keats names the wrong explorer, "stout Cortez," when the historical distinction actually belongs to Balboa, a small factual slip that has never dented the poem's celebrated evocation of imaginative discovery.
- **The "negative capability" letter (December 1817).** As discussed above, this private letter gives English criticism one of its most durable concepts: the idea that great writers, Shakespeare above all, achieve their effects by tolerating uncertainty and contradiction rather than resolving them into tidy philosophical system.
- ***Endymion* (1818).** Beyond the hostile reviews it provoked, the poem's own sprawling, sensuously overwrought mythological narrative shows a still-developing poet reaching for an ambition his shorter, more disciplined 1819 odes would realize far more successfully.
- **"Ode to a Nightingale" (1819).** The poem's speaker longs to escape mortal suffering by merging imaginatively with the nightingale's seemingly eternal, unchanging song, before being pulled back, in the poem's famously ambiguous final line, to ordinary consciousness: "Do I wake or sleep?"
- **"Ode on a Grecian Urn" (1819).** A Greek urn's frozen images of youth, music, and love-just-before-fulfillment offer a version of eternal beauty mortal life cannot have, but at the cost of eternally suspended, never-completed longing. The poem's closing claim — "'Beauty is truth, truth beauty,—that is all / Ye know on earth, and all ye need to know'" — remains one of the most quoted and most contested single statements in English poetry, discussed fully below.
- **"To Autumn" (September 1819).** Widely considered Keats's most perfectly achieved poem, this ode offers rich, unhurried sensory description of harvest-time ripeness, and, notably, none of the anxious philosophical questioning of "Nightingale" or the "Grecian Urn" — instead accepting the season's, and by clear implication life's, movement toward its own natural ending: "Where are the songs of Spring? Ay, where are they? / Think not of them, thou hast thy music too."

## Critical Perspectives

- **Did hostile reviews really hasten Keats's death?** Byron and Shelley both promoted a version of this story, memorialized in "Adonais" (Chapter 6), but modern medical and biographical scholarship treats it as a myth: Keats died of the same disease that had already killed his mother and brother, and his own letters show real critical resilience rather than fragile sensitivity. The myth's persistence says more about the Romantic era's appetite for stories of poetic genius destroyed by an uncaring world — the same pattern you met with Chatterton in Part V, Chapter 4 — than about the actual medical facts of Keats's death.
- **Who is speaking at the end of "Ode on a Grecian Urn," and what do they mean?** Critics have never fully agreed whether "Beauty is truth, truth beauty" is spoken by the urn itself, by the poet addressing the reader, sincerely or ironically, or whether the whole equation should be read as a profound philosophical claim or as itself an example of the urn's limited, frozen perspective on a life it can never actually experience. The line's very unresolvability is, fittingly, a good demonstration of Keats's own concept of negative capability in action.
- **Negative capability versus Coleridge's Imagination: which better describes poetic creativity?** Coleridge's Fancy/Imagination distinction (Chapter 4) offers a systematic philosophical account of how creative power works; Keats's negative capability instead celebrates the writer's capacity to resist exactly that kind of systematizing. Neither framework is simply more correct than the other — they represent two genuinely different Romantic accounts of where poetic power comes from.
- **How much of the "Cockney School" hostility was genuine literary judgment, and how much was class snobbery?** The reviews' explicit mockery of Keats's medical training and non-aristocratic background suggests that at least part of the attack on *Endymion* reflected anxiety about who was entitled to write serious poetry at all, not simply a judgment of the poem's actual literary quality.

## Chapter Summary

Trained as a physician and shadowed throughout his short life by the tuberculosis that killed his mother, his brother, and eventually himself, John Keats produced, within his 1819 "great year," some of the most celebrated poetry in the English language: "Ode to a Nightingale" and "Ode on a Grecian Urn," both wrestling with the tension between imaginative escape from mortality and its inevitable return, and "To Autumn," which resolves that same tension with unusually calm acceptance. His concept of "negative capability," from an 1817 letter, offers a distinctly anti-systematic account of creative power, standing in instructive contrast to Coleridge's more philosophically elaborate theory of Imagination from Chapter 4. Class-inflected "Cockney School" attacks on *Endymion* (1818) helped generate a "doomed genius destroyed by hostile critics" myth, memorialized in Shelley's "Adonais" (Chapter 6), that modern scholarship now largely rejects in favor of the more accurate, equally tragic fact of his early death from disease. Keats died in Rome in 1821, aged twenty-five, choosing an epitaph — "Here lies One whose Name was writ in Water" — that reads today as a poignant irony, given his lasting stature as one of the greatest poets of the entire Romantic period. Chapter 8 now turns from Romantic poetry to Romantic prose, and the essayists Lamb, Hazlitt, and De Quincey.

## Glossary

- **Negative capability** — Keats's term, from an 1817 letter, for the capacity to remain in uncertainty and doubt without forcing premature rational or philosophical resolution.
- **"Great year"** — the informal term for Keats's extraordinarily productive period, roughly 1818–1819, during which he composed nearly all his major odes.
- **Ode** — a formal, often elevated lyric poem, typically addressing or meditating on a serious subject; used across this book by Gray, Wordsworth, Coleridge, and, most famously, Keats.
