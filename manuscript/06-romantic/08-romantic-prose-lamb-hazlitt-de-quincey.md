## Historical and Socio-Political Context

This chapter turns from Romantic poetry to Romantic prose, and specifically to a new development in English essay-writing: the **familiar essay**, a personal, digressive, deliberately idiosyncratic form quite different from the periodical essay tradition you met in Addison and Steele (Part IV, Chapter 4). Where *The Spectator* addressed a broad readership in a shared, sociable voice aimed at gentle moral improvement, the Romantic familiar essay makes the author's own distinctive personality, memory, and private experience its central subject — a natural prose extension of the individualist "Romantic imagination" Chapter 1 introduced for poetry.

This new form flourished in a genuinely thriving magazine culture. Charles Lamb's *Essays of Elia* and Thomas De Quincey's *Confessions of an English Opium-Eater* both first appeared in the *London Magazine*, while journals like the *Edinburgh Review* and *Blackwood's Magazine* — already familiar to you from Chapter 7's account of the "Cockney School" attacks on Keats — gave essayists, critics, and reviewers a genuinely mass readership. All three of this chapter's writers made their living substantially through this periodical marketplace, extending, in a new and more personal direction, the print culture Part IV traced from the Restoration onward.

## Literary Movements

The Romantic familiar essay shares one crucial assumption with Wordsworth's "a man speaking to men" (Part VI, Chapter 2): that ordinary, individual experience, honestly rendered, is worth serious literary attention. But where Wordsworth applied that principle to rustic figures observed from outside, Lamb, Hazlitt, and De Quincey each turn it inward, making their own memory, temperament, and private history the essay's actual subject. This produces three genuinely distinct prose voices, and part of this chapter's job is teaching you to recognize each one on sight: Lamb's warm, whimsical, digressive nostalgia; Hazlitt's direct, forceful, often combative conviction; and De Quincey's ornate, musically elaborate, almost hypnotic rhetorical richness. All three, in their different ways, treat prose style itself as an expressive extension of personality — a marked shift from the more socially oriented, self-effacing plain style Addison had cultivated a century earlier (Part IV, Chapter 4).

## Author Profiles

**Charles Lamb** (1775–1834) worked as a clerk at the East India Company for over thirty years, a steady day job that supported a personal life marked by real tragedy: in 1796, his sister Mary, during an episode of mental illness, killed their mother, and Lamb spent the rest of his life caring for her, taking personal guardianship to keep her out of permanent institutional confinement between recurring episodes of illness. He never married. Despite this, Lamb's essays are celebrated for their gentle humor, warmth, and nostalgic charm — a striking contrast between private suffering and public literary voice that criticism has long found genuinely moving rather than simply contradictory. Charles and Mary collaborated on *Tales from Shakespear* (1807), prose retellings of Shakespeare's plays for children (Mary wrote the comedies, Charles the tragedies) that introduced generations of young readers to Shakespeare, and Lamb's own *Specimens of English Dramatic Poets* (1808) helped revive scholarly interest in lesser-known Jacobean dramatists (Part III, Chapter 7). His major achievement, the *Essays of Elia*, published under the pseudonym "Elia" beginning in the *London Magazine* in 1820 and collected in 1823 and 1833, ranges across childhood memory, food, old acquaintances, and everyday pleasures with a distinctive personal charm that remains, alongside Addison's and Steele's periodical persona-writing (Part IV, Chapter 4), one of English prose's most successful uses of an adopted literary identity.

**William Hazlitt** (1778–1830) trained first for the ministry and then briefly as a painter before turning to writing and journalism as his career. He remained, throughout his life, an unwavering supporter of the French Revolution's original ideals, and one of the sharpest and most consistent critics of Wordsworth's and Coleridge's later political conservatism, the very trajectory Chapters 1 and 3 already asked you to weigh critically — Hazlitt considered it something close to a genuine betrayal. His continued admiration for Napoleon, expressed at length in his multi-volume *Life of Napoleon Buonaparte* (1828–1830), even after Napoleon's fall from power, reflects the same stubborn political consistency, whatever its own complications. Hazlitt's critical range was exceptional: *Characters of Shakespeare's Plays* (1817) and his *Lectures on the English Poets* (1818) and *Lectures on the English Comic Writers* (1819) surveyed vast stretches of English literary history, but his most celebrated single work, *The Spirit of the Age* (1825), is a collection of sharply observed, often devastatingly acute character portraits of his own contemporaries — Wordsworth, Coleridge, Byron, and others — combining psychological insight, political judgment, and some of the finest prose style of the entire period.

**Thomas De Quincey** (1785–1859), the youngest of the three, ran away from school as a teenager and spent a period living in real poverty and homelessness in London, an experience that directly shapes his most famous work. He later grew close to Wordsworth and Coleridge, moving to the Lake District to be near them, and his personal knowledge of the "first generation" Romantics informs much of his later biographical writing about them. *Confessions of an English Opium-Eater* (1821) is his defining achievement: a strikingly candid autobiographical account of his opium addiction — considerably more extensive and explicit than anything Coleridge himself published on the subject (Chapter 4) — combining memories of his earlier London poverty with extraordinarily vivid, dreamlike descriptions of opium-induced visions and nightmares. The book is widely regarded as a founding text of addiction literature and a major influence on later writing exploring altered consciousness and psychological interiority.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Tales from Shakespear* (1807).** Charles and Mary Lamb's prose retellings made Shakespeare genuinely accessible to child readers for the first time, extending the broader Romantic reassessment of Shakespeare already underway through Coleridge's criticism (Chapter 4).
- ***Essays of Elia* (1820–1823, 1833).** Essays like "Dream Children: A Reverie," an imaginative address to the children Lamb never had, and "A Dissertation upon Roast Pig," a comic mock-serious account of pork's supposed accidental discovery, show the essay collection's characteristic range between genuine poignancy and playful whimsy, both delivered in Lamb's distinctively warm, digressive voice.
- ***The Spirit of the Age* (1825).** Hazlitt's collected portraits of his contemporaries remain a landmark of English critical prose, combining unusually sharp psychological observation with direct, forceful, often provocative judgment — a very different critical register from Johnson's more measured *Lives of the Poets* (Part IV, Chapter 8).
- **"On the Pleasure of Hating."** This characteristically dark, unsparing Hazlitt essay examines the genuine human pleasure taken in resentment and animosity with a directness that stands in sharp contrast to Lamb's gentler, more forgiving prose temperament.
- ***Confessions of an English Opium-Eater* (1821).** De Quincey's candid, richly written account of addiction and its visionary aftermath gives English prose one of its earliest sustained explorations of altered psychological states, written in a deliberately ornate, almost musical style quite unlike either Lamb's whimsy or Hazlitt's force.
- **"On Murder Considered as one of the Fine Arts" (1827).** This darkly ironic essay, treating murder as a subject for aesthetic connoisseurship, uses a sustained ironic persona reminiscent of Swift's *A Modest Proposal* (Part IV, Chapter 5), demonstrating De Quincey's command of deliberate, unsettling dark comedy alongside his more famous confessional writing.

## Critical Perspectives

- **How should we read the gap between Lamb's private tragedy and his public warmth?** Rather than treating this as contradiction, most critics read Lamb's essays' gentle humor as a genuine, hard-won response to real suffering — not denial of grief, but a chosen, disciplined form of consolation, made all the more moving once you know the biography behind it.
- **Was Hazlitt's harsh criticism of Wordsworth and Coleridge fair?** Hazlitt's consistent radicalism gives real force to his critique of the older poets' later conservatism, but his own continued admiration for Napoleon — hardly an uncomplicated democratic hero — complicates any simple picture of Hazlitt as the era's uniquely principled political conscience.
- **Is De Quincey's *Confessions* a serious contribution to understanding addiction, or primarily a literary experience of dream and altered consciousness?** The book arguably works as both at once: a pioneering, unusually honest account of dependency's real psychological toll, and a deliberately crafted literary performance of dream-vision, in the same visionary tradition as Coleridge's "Kubla Khan" (Chapter 4).
- **What does the range across these three prose styles suggest about Romantic individualism applied to prose?** Lamb, Hazlitt, and De Quincey demonstrate that the Romantic emphasis on individual voice and experience could produce genuinely distinct literary personalities within the same broad essay form — evidence that "Romantic prose" describes a shared assumption about authorial individuality more than a single uniform style.

## Chapter Summary

Extending the periodical essay tradition of Part IV into a more personal, idiosyncratic register, Charles Lamb, William Hazlitt, and Thomas De Quincey each developed a genuinely distinct prose voice within the era's thriving magazine culture. Lamb's *Essays of Elia*, written against the backdrop of his lifelong care for his sister Mary, combine warmth and whimsy with real emotional depth; Hazlitt's *The Spirit of the Age* applies his unwavering political radicalism and forceful critical judgment to sharp, memorable portraits of his contemporaries, including Wordsworth and Coleridge; and De Quincey's *Confessions of an English Opium-Eater* gives English literature one of its first sustained, candidly written explorations of addiction and altered consciousness, in a richly ornate prose style all his own. Together, these three writers show that the Romantic era's emphasis on individual imagination and experience, already traced through poetry across Chapters 1 through 7, extended just as powerfully into prose. Chapter 9 now turns to the Gothic novel, another prose tradition this period developed alongside, and sometimes in tension with, the poetry and personal essay writing studied so far.

## Glossary

- **Familiar essay** — a personal, digressive prose form, developed in this period, that makes the author's own memory, temperament, and private experience its central subject, distinct from the more socially oriented periodical essay of Part IV.
- **The "Elia" persona** — Charles Lamb's pseudonym for his essays, an adopted literary identity comparable to Addison and Steele's earlier personas (Part IV, Chapter 4).
- **Gusto** — Hazlitt's critical term for intense, vivid expressive vitality in art and writing.
