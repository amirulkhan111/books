## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 1 explained why 1798 is the year this whole Part starts from. This chapter tells you exactly what happened that year: William Wordsworth and Samuel Taylor Coleridge, who had met in 1795 and formed an intense creative friendship, published a slim, anonymous volume titled *Lyrical Ballads, with a Few Other Poems*. Literary historians have long treated this modest book as English Romanticism's founding document — not because it was recognized as revolutionary at the time (it sold modestly and drew mixed reviews), but because of the theory Wordsworth later attached to it, and because of how completely its actual poems broke with the Augustan and sensibility traditions you studied in Parts IV and V.

The collaboration grew out of real daily proximity. In 1797, Wordsworth and his sister Dorothy moved to Alfoxden House in Somerset specifically to be near Coleridge, who was living at nearby Nether Stowey — the same setting, and the same year, as the government surveillance episode you met in Chapter 1. The three of them spent long stretches walking together through the Quantock Hills, talking continuously about poetry, philosophy, and nature, and Dorothy Wordsworth's detailed journals from this period — the *Alfoxden Journal* and, later, the *Grasmere Journal* — recorded exactly the kind of closely observed natural detail that fed directly into both poets' work, even though she received no public authorial credit for the collection at the time. *Lyrical Ballads* was published anonymously in Bristol in 1798 by the printer Joseph Cottle; only with its expanded 1800 edition did Wordsworth's name appear as author, alongside the critical Preface that this chapter treats as its central document.

## Literary Movements

Wordsworth's Preface — first added in 1800, expanded in 1802 — is the closest thing English Romanticism has to a manifesto, and its central argument is a direct rejection of what he calls **poetic diction**: the elevated, ornamental, specialized vocabulary that eighteenth-century poets, working in the Augustan tradition you studied in Part IV, had treated as appropriate to serious verse. Wordsworth insists instead on "a selection of language really used by men" — plain, everyday speech — and argues that this plainer language should describe **"low and rustic life,"** because rural people, less shaped by the vanity and social artifice of fashionable urban society, express fundamental human emotions in a purer, more essential form. This is worth connecting back to the primitivism you met in Part V, Chapter 3: where Macpherson had located authentic feeling in an imagined ancient past, Wordsworth locates it in contemporary rural England, among living shepherds, beggars, and children.

The Preface's single most quoted sentence defines poetry itself: **"Poetry is the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings: it takes its origin from emotion recollected in tranquillity."** Read this carefully, because it describes a two-stage process, not simply raw emotional outburst. A powerful feeling occurs; then, later, in a calmer state, the poet recalls that feeling, and the poem emerges from this act of reflective recollection rather than from the original emotion alone. This lets Wordsworth claim that his poetry is both spontaneous (genuinely rooted in real feeling) and crafted (shaped by considered reflection) at the same time. He extends this into a deliberately democratic account of poetic authority, describing the poet as **"a man speaking to men"** — someone with unusually developed sensibility and expressive skill, certainly, but fundamentally sharing the same human nature as any reader, not a remote, specially inspired genius standing apart from ordinary people.

Coleridge later described their collaborative division of labor in *Biographia Literaria* (1817) — a text you'll study fully in Chapter 4, but worth flagging now for its account of how the two poets split *Lyrical Ballads*' imaginative territory. Coleridge's own poems, he wrote, were to concern "persons and characters supernatural, or at least romantic," treated realistically enough to procure what he memorably called "that willing suspension of disbelief for the moment, which constitutes poetic faith." Wordsworth's task, by contrast, was to give "the charm of novelty to things of every day," awakening readers to the wonder already present in ordinary life. Since this account comes from 1817, nearly twenty years after the fact, treat it as Coleridge's later, retrospective shaping of their earlier collaboration, not as a plan the two poets necessarily followed consciously in 1798 itself.

Curiously, the Preface also attacks contemporary taste for "frantic novels" and sensational stage tragedies, worrying that a public appetite for cheap, extreme sensation was dulling readers' capacity for genuine poetic feeling — a complaint that lands, whether Wordsworth fully intended it or not, uncomfortably close to the Gothic fiction you'll study in Chapter 9. Keep this tension in mind: the same broad period modern literary history calls "Romantic" contains writers who saw themselves, at least in this instance, as working in direct opposition to one another's tastes.

## Author Profiles

**William Wordsworth** (1770–1850) receives his full biographical treatment in Chapter 3; here, focus on his role as *Lyrical Ballads*' primary author and theorist. Having already visited Revolutionary France in the early 1790s (Chapter 1), Wordsworth settled in Somerset in 1797 specifically to work alongside Coleridge, and it was Wordsworth who wrote the great majority of the 1798 volume's poems, and who alone composed and later expanded the Preface that gives the collection its lasting theoretical importance.

**Samuel Taylor Coleridge** (1772–1834), whose full biography and criticism you'll study in Chapter 4, contributed fewer poems to the 1798 volume than Wordsworth did, but one of the most consequential: "The Rime of the Ancient Mariner," discussed below, opens the collection and remains its single most famous poem. Coleridge's intense intellectual friendship with Wordsworth during this period — walking, talking, and reading together in the Quantock Hills — shaped both poets' work so thoroughly that untangling exactly who influenced whom on any given point is often genuinely difficult.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Lyrical Ballads* (1798), as a whole.** The volume's structure is itself an argument: it opens with Coleridge's fully supernatural "Ancient Mariner" and closes with Wordsworth's meditative, philosophically serious "Tintern Abbey," deliberately bracketing the collection between the marvelous and the everyday, exactly the division of labor Coleridge later described.
- **Coleridge, "The Rime of the Ancient Mariner" (1798).** A sailor shoots an albatross, a bird of good omen, for no clear reason, and brings a curse upon his ship and crew; his eventual partial redemption comes only when he learns to bless, without conscious effort, the sea-creatures around him — "He prayeth best, who loveth best / All things both great and small." Told in deliberately archaic ballad language and framed as a compulsive tale forced on an unwilling wedding guest, the poem is Coleridge's clearest demonstration of "willing suspension of disbelief" in action; you'll study it in full in Chapter 4.
- **Wordsworth, "Tintern Abbey" (1798).** Returning to a landscape on the River Wye five years after his first visit, the poem's speaker reflects on how his relationship to nature has matured — from the "thoughtless" physical delight of youth to a more contemplative sense of nature as a moral and spiritual presence, "a motion and a spirit, that impels / All thinking things." The poem closes by addressing Wordsworth's sister Dorothy directly, hoping she will carry forward similar memories of the landscape herself; you'll study it fully in Chapter 3.
- **"We Are Seven."** A simply narrated conversation between an adult speaker and a child who insists her deceased siblings still count among "seven" brothers and sisters demonstrates Wordsworth's Preface in practice: plain language, a "low and rustic" child subject, and a serious philosophical point — here, about how differently children and adults reason about death — delivered without ornamental elevation.
- **"Expostulation and Reply" and "The Tables Turned."** These companion poems argue, provocatively, that direct experience of the natural world teaches more than scholarly study: "One impulse from a vernal wood / May teach you more of man, / Of moral evil and of good, / Than all the sages can." Read together, they state one of Romanticism's most consequential epistemological claims — that intuitive, felt experience of nature can outstrip abstract book learning.
- **The Preface to *Lyrical Ballads* (1800, expanded 1802).** As a critical document in its own right, the Preface does for Romantic poetic theory something like what Dryden's *Of Dramatick Poesie* (Part IV, Chapter 3) did for Restoration drama: it makes a sustained, reasoned case for a new way of writing, against an existing dominant tradition, rather than simply demonstrating that new way through poems alone.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is 1798 really where Romanticism "starts"?** Chapter 1 already showed you that sensibility's trust in feeling, Young's celebration of poetic genius, and the French Revolution's disillusioning arc were all well underway before *Lyrical Ballads* appeared. Most literary historians now treat 1798 as a convenient, symbolically useful marker — the moment these already-developing currents crystallized into a stated theory — rather than a genuine, sudden invention out of nothing.
- **Does Wordsworth's practice actually match his theory?** Critics have long noted that Wordsworth's own diction, even in poems explicitly written to use "the language really used by men," is often more carefully patterned and literary than his Preface's stated ideal would suggest. This gap between manifesto and practice is worth remembering whenever you encounter a writer's own account of their method — it's a claim to evaluate against the actual poems, not simply to accept.
- **How should we weigh the Preface's attack on Gothic fiction?** Wordsworth's complaint about "frantic novels" and sensational tragedy sits in real tension with Chapter 9's serious treatment of the Gothic novel as a genuine literary achievement of this same broad period. Rather than treating one side as simply right, it's more useful to notice that "Romanticism" contained real internal disagreements about which kinds of imaginative intensity counted as legitimate.
- **What was Dorothy Wordsworth's actual contribution?** Modern scholarship increasingly recognizes that Dorothy Wordsworth's journals supplied specific, closely observed natural detail that shaped poems credited to her brother and to Coleridge, even though she received no authorial credit in 1798. This pattern — a woman's significant creative contribution obscured behind a male author's name — is worth holding onto as Part VI later turns, in Chapter 11, to women Romantic poets whose own work faced related, if not identical, obstacles to recognition.

## Chapter Summary

Grown out of Wordsworth and Coleridge's close collaboration in Somerset during 1797–1798, *Lyrical Ballads* paired Coleridge's supernatural "Rime of the Ancient Mariner," which opens the volume, with Wordsworth's meditative "Tintern Abbey," which closes it, embodying a deliberate division between the marvelous and the everyday that Coleridge would later describe explicitly in *Biographia Literaria* (Chapter 4). Wordsworth's 1800 Preface, expanded in 1802, gave the volume its lasting theoretical significance: a rejection of ornamental "poetic diction" in favor of plain language and rustic subjects, a definition of poetry as "the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings" recollected "in tranquillity," and a democratic vision of the poet as "a man speaking to men." Though the Preface's own attack on sensational Gothic fiction sits in real tension with the serious treatment that genre receives in Chapter 9, and though Dorothy Wordsworth's uncredited journal-keeping shaped the collection more than its title page ever acknowledged, *Lyrical Ballads* remains the conventional starting point for everything the rest of Part VI now explores in full: Wordsworth's complete body of work in Chapter 3, and Coleridge's in Chapter 4.

## Glossary

- **Poetic diction** — the elevated, specialized vocabulary eighteenth-century poetry treated as proper to serious verse; rejected by Wordsworth in favor of plain, everyday language.
- **"Spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings"** — Wordsworth's definition of poetry's origin, combined with the idea of "emotion recollected in tranquillity": a two-stage process of genuine feeling followed by reflective composition.
- **"A man speaking to men"** — Wordsworth's democratic description of the poet as sharing fundamental human nature with readers, rather than standing apart as a remote genius.
- **Willing suspension of disbelief** — Coleridge's term, from *Biographia Literaria* (1817), for a reader's voluntary acceptance of a supernatural or fantastic premise as emotionally real within a poem.
