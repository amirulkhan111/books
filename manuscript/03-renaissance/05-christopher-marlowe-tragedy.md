## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Christopher Marlowe was born in the same year as Shakespeare, 1564, and he wrote his entire body of work inside the commercial theatre infrastructure you just learned about in Chapter 4. What he achieved in that time is remarkable given how little time he actually had: fame and real controversy, packed into a working career of scarcely six years, before he was killed at twenty-nine.

His life brushes up directly against the secretive world of Elizabethan state intelligence. In 1587, the Privy Council itself wrote to Cambridge defending Marlowe's degree against rumours that he had defected to a Catholic seminary abroad — and the letter's wording implies he had actually been "employed" in unspecified government service, almost certainly intelligence work of some kind. The religious tensions of the period caught up with him too: in May 1593, just days before his death, an informer named Richard Baines submitted a note to the authorities accusing Marlowe of shocking blasphemies and "monstrous opinions," and the Privy Council had already ordered his arrest and daily attendance pending investigation.

## Literary Movements

Among the University Wits you met in Chapter 4, Marlowe stands out as the outstanding dramatic voice, and he's the single most consequential figure in turning **blank verse** into the standard medium of Elizabethan and Jacobean tragedy. Remember, Surrey had introduced blank verse into English a full half-century earlier, in Chapter 2 — but nobody had deployed it with anything like Marlowe's dramatic power before. Ben Jonson later paid tribute to this, in verses prefixed to Shakespeare's First Folio, praising "Marlowe's mighty line" — and that phrase captures something historically real: Marlowe was the first English dramatist to show what blank verse could actually do, turning it from a merely available technical option into the defining instrument of the era's drama.

Look for a single unifying thread running through Marlowe's major tragedies: the **overreacher**. This is a protagonist of extraordinary will, ambition, or appetite — for conquest, forbidden knowledge, wealth, or power — who pushes past conventional moral, religious, or political limits, and typically meets a catastrophic downfall that the play never quite lets you read as simple, comfortable moral punishment.

## Author Profiles

**Christopher Marlowe** (1564–1593) was born in Canterbury, the son of a shoemaker, and educated at Corpus Christi College, Cambridge — where, as you read above, a 1587 Privy Council letter defending his degree strongly implies he'd been doing undisclosed government work, probably intelligence-related. In a dramatic career of only about six years, he wrote *Tamburlaine the Great* (Parts I and II), *Doctor Faustus*, *The Jew of Malta*, and *Edward II*, establishing blank verse as English tragedy's defining medium and the "overreacher" as its signature protagonist. He was killed in a Deptford lodging house on 30 May 1593 by a man named Ingram Frizer, reportedly in a dispute over a bill, just days after his arrest on blasphemy charges. A coroner's inquest ruled it self-defence, but the surrounding circumstances — his recent arrest, his companions' own ties to espionage — have kept scholars arguing about what really happened ever since.

**Richard Baines** was a government informer and part-time intelligence agent. His note to the authorities, compiled in May 1593 shortly before Marlowe's death, accused the playwright of a string of blasphemous and heretical statements — including claims that Moses was a "juggler" and that Marlowe himself could "make a better religion." Whether the note accurately records Marlowe's real views, or is politically motivated exaggeration, is still debated.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Tamburlaine the Great*** (Parts I and II, c. 1587–88). This play dramatizes the rise of the real historical Central Asian conqueror Timur (Tamerlane), tracking his path from shepherd to world-conquering emperor through relentless, unapologetic ambition and violence — delivered in soaring, rhetorically extravagant blank verse, described in the Prologue itself as "high astounding terms." Notice that Part I never punishes Tamburlaine in any conventional tragic sense — the play's amoral celebration of raw will and conquest scandalized and thrilled contemporary audiences in roughly equal measure.
- ***Doctor Faustus*** (c. 1588–92). This play adapts the German Faustbuch legend: a scholar sells his soul to the devil, through the tempter Mephistophilis, in exchange for twenty-four years of unlimited knowledge and power, and the play builds to a famously harrowing damnation scene. Here's a complication worth knowing: the play survives in two substantially different printed texts — the shorter 1604 "A-text" and the longer, more theatrically elaborate 1616 "B-text." Whether one is Marlowe's original design and the other a later addition, or whether both reflect different performance traditions, remains a genuinely unresolved textual puzzle.
- ***The Jew of Malta*** (c. 1589–90). A darkly comic tragedy centred on Barabas, a Maltese Jewish merchant pushed into escalating revenge and villainy after Christian authorities confiscate his wealth. Barabas is morally ambiguous and theatrically self-aware as a villain, and this characterization directly shaped Shakespeare's later, more psychologically developed treatment of similar material in *The Merchant of Venice*, which you'll meet in Chapter 6.
- ***Edward II*** (c. 1592). A history play dramatizing the reign and deposition of the real Edward II, notable for how intimately it treats the king's relationship with his favourite, Piers Gaveston — widely read as homoerotic — and for how sympathetically, from the inside, it renders a king's downfall. You can trace this influence into Shakespeare's own history plays, and more distantly into *Richard II*.

## Critical Perspectives

- **The "overreacher" thesis.** Following the influential formulation of Harry Levin and others, critics read Marlowe's protagonists — Tamburlaine, Faustus, Barabas — as variations on a single tragic type: figures whose extraordinary aspiration exceeds them and ultimately destroys them, without the plays ever offering a straightforward moral condemnation of that aspiration itself. That refusal to moralize cleanly produces a distinctive tonal ambiguity — admiration mixed with horror — that later shaped Marlowe's most famous successor, Shakespeare.
- **The A-text/B-text problem in *Doctor Faustus*.** The two surviving texts differ substantially in length, content, and theatrical elaboration, especially in the comic subplot material. Editors and critics remain divided: which text better represents Marlowe's original design? Is either version wholly his unaided work, or does each carry collaborative or later theatrical revision? This is a useful case study in a wider problem — just how textually unstable much Elizabethan printed drama actually is.
- **Was Marlowe's death a conspiracy, or just a tragic coincidence?** His arrest on blasphemy charges came only days before his violent death, and his killer and companions had documented connections to government espionage circles. That proximity has fuelled endless scholarly and popular speculation: was this a genuine tavern dispute, or a politically motivated assassination? Mainstream scholarship generally treats the coroner's self-defence verdict as the best-supported account, while acknowledging that the surrounding circumstances remain genuinely murky.
- **How much did Marlowe shape Shakespeare?** A great deal, most scholars agree. Marlowe's mastery of blank verse, his morally ambiguous protagonists, and specific plays — *The Jew of Malta*'s clear influence on *The Merchant of Venice*, *Edward II*'s influence on the history-play genre and on *Richard II* — are widely regarded as directly formative for Shakespeare's own development as a dramatist. That makes Marlowe's brief career, cut short right as Shakespeare's was beginning in earnest, one of the single most consequential influences on everything you'll read in Chapter 6.

## Chapter Summary

In a dramatic career of only about six years before his violent death at twenty-nine, Christopher Marlowe transformed blank verse — which Surrey had introduced a generation earlier, in Chapter 2 — into "Marlowe's mighty line," the defining medium of English Renaissance tragedy, and established the "overreacher" protagonist (Tamburlaine, Faustus, Barabas) as one of the period's most influential tragic types. *Doctor Faustus*'s two divergent printed texts (1604 and 1616) show you the textual instability common across Elizabethan drama, while Marlowe's own biography — probable intelligence work, arrest on blasphemy charges via the Baines Note, and a violent, still-debated death in a Deptford lodging house in May 1593 — has generated nearly as much scholarly fascination as his plays themselves. Marlowe's direct influence on Shakespeare, in verse style, morally ambiguous protagonists, and specific dramatic material, makes his brief career foundational for everything you'll read in the next chapter.

## Glossary

- **Baines Note** — the May 1593 informer's report accusing Marlowe of blasphemous and heretical statements.
- **Blank verse** — unrhymed iambic pentameter; see also Chapter 2 (Surrey) and Chapter 4.
- **Overreacher** — a critical term for Marlowe's characteristic tragic protagonist type, defined by transgressive, ultimately self-destructive ambition.
- **"Mighty line"** — Ben Jonson's phrase (from his prefatory verses to Shakespeare's First Folio) for Marlowe's powerful blank verse style.
