## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Christopher Marlowe, born the same year as Shakespeare (1564), wrote his entire dramatic output within the newly established commercial theatre infrastructure surveyed in Chapter 4, achieving fame and controversy within a working career of scarcely six years before his violent death at twenty-nine. His biography intersects directly with the secretive world of Elizabethan state intelligence — a 1587 Privy Council letter to Cambridge, defending his degree against rumours he had defected to the Catholic seminary at Rheims, indicates he had been "employed" in unspecified government service — and with the religious tensions of the period: in May 1593, days before his death, the informer Richard Baines submitted a note to the authorities accusing Marlowe of shocking blasphemies and "monstrous opinions," while the Privy Council had already ordered his arrest and daily attendance pending investigation.

## Literary Movements

Marlowe stands as the outstanding dramatic voice among the University Wits introduced in Chapter 4, and the single most consequential figure in establishing **blank verse** — introduced into English by Surrey a half-century earlier (Chapter 2) but never before deployed with comparable dramatic power — as the standard medium of Elizabethan and Jacobean tragedy. Ben Jonson's later tribute, in verses prefixed to Shakespeare's First Folio, to "Marlowe's mighty line," captures the historical judgment that Marlowe was the first English dramatist to demonstrate blank verse's full expressive and rhetorical capacity, transforming it from a technically available option into the era's defining dramatic instrument. Thematically, Marlowe's major tragedies are unified by the figure of the **overreacher**: a protagonist of extraordinary will, ambition, or appetite (for conquest, knowledge, wealth, or power) who transgresses conventional moral, religious, or political limits, typically meeting a catastrophic downfall that is neither wholly condemnatory nor wholly redemptive in its moral framing.

## Author Profiles

**Christopher Marlowe** (1564–1593) — Canterbury-born son of a shoemaker; educated at Corpus Christi College, Cambridge, where a 1587 Privy Council letter defending his MA degree against rumours of Catholic defection strongly implies undisclosed government service, probably intelligence-related. Over a dramatic career of roughly six years, Marlowe wrote *Tamburlaine the Great* (Parts I and II), *Doctor Faustus*, *The Jew of Malta*, and *Edward II*, establishing blank verse as English tragedy's defining medium and the "overreacher" as its characteristic protagonist. Arrested amid accusations of blasphemy and atheism in May 1593 (via the informer Richard Baines's note to the authorities), Marlowe was killed in a Deptford lodging house on 30 May 1593 by Ingram Frizer, reportedly in a dispute over a bill; the coroner's inquest ruled the killing self-defence, though the circumstances (Marlowe's recent arrest, his companions' own connections to espionage) have generated extensive scholarly speculation and remain incompletely resolved.

**Richard Baines** — Government informer and part-time intelligence agent whose May 1593 note to the authorities, compiled shortly before Marlowe's death, accused the playwright of a series of blasphemous and heretical statements (including claims that Moses was a "juggler" and that he could "make a better religion"); the note's reliability as an accurate record of Marlowe's actual views, as opposed to politically motivated fabrication or exaggeration, remains debated by scholars.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Tamburlaine the Great*** (Parts I and II, c. 1587–88) — Dramatizes the rise of the historical Central Asian conqueror Timur (Tamerlane) from shepherd to world-conquering emperor through relentless, unapologetic ambition and violence, delivered in soaring, rhetorically extravagant blank verse ("high astounding terms," in the Prologue's own self-description); the play's amoral celebration of raw will and conquest, without conventional tragic punishment in Part I, scandalized and thrilled contemporary audiences in roughly equal measure.
- ***Doctor Faustus*** (c. 1588–92) — Adapts the German Faustbuch legend of a scholar who sells his soul to the devil (via the tempter Mephistophilis) in exchange for twenty-four years of unlimited knowledge and power, culminating in a famously harrowing damnation scene. The play survives in two substantially different printed texts — the shorter 1604 "A-text" and the longer, more theatrically elaborate 1616 "B-text" — whose relationship (authorial revision, differing performance traditions, or later interpolation by other hands) remains a major textual-critical problem.
- ***The Jew of Malta*** (c. 1589–90) — A darkly comic tragedy centred on Barabas, a Maltese Jewish merchant driven to escalating revenge and villainy after Christian authorities confiscate his wealth; its morally ambiguous, theatrically self-aware villain-protagonist directly influenced Shakespeare's later, more psychologically developed treatment of comparable material in *The Merchant of Venice* (Chapter 6).
- ***Edward II*** (c. 1592) — A history play dramatizing the reign and deposition of the historical Edward II, notable for its psychologically intimate treatment of the king's relationship with his favourite Piers Gaveston (widely read as homoerotic) and for its unusually sympathetic, interiorized treatment of a king's downfall — an influence discernible in Shakespeare's own history plays and, more distantly, in *Richard II*.

## Critical Perspectives

- **The overreacher thesis.** Critics, following the influential formulation of Harry Levin and others, read Marlowe's protagonists — Tamburlaine, Faustus, Barabas — as variations on a single tragic type: figures whose extraordinary aspiration exceeds and ultimately destroys them, without the plays offering straightforward moral condemnation of that aspiration itself, producing a distinctive tonal ambiguity (admiration mixed with horror) that later influenced Marlowe's most famous successor, Shakespeare.
- **The A-text/B-text problem in *Doctor Faustus*.** Because the two surviving texts differ substantially in length, content, and theatrical elaboration (particularly in comic subplot material), editors and critics remain divided over which better represents Marlowe's original design, whether either is wholly Marlowe's unaided work, and how much collaborative or later theatrical revision each incorporates — a case study in the broader textual instability affecting much Elizabethan printed drama.
- **Marlowe's death: conspiracy or coincidence?** The proximity of Marlowe's arrest on blasphemy charges to his violent death, and his killers' and companions' documented connections to government espionage circles, have fuelled extensive (and largely unresolved) scholarly and popular speculation about whether his death was a genuine tavern dispute or a politically motivated assassination; mainstream scholarship generally treats the coroner's self-defence verdict as the best-supported account while acknowledging the surrounding circumstances remain genuinely murky.
- **Marlowe and Shakespeare.** Marlowe's blank verse mastery, his morally ambiguous protagonists, and specific plays (*The Jew of Malta*'s influence on *The Merchant of Venice*; *Edward II*'s influence on the history-play genre and on *Richard II*) are widely regarded as directly formative for Shakespeare's own dramatic development, making Marlowe's brief career (cut short at the moment Shakespeare's was beginning in earnest) one of the most consequential single influences on the drama treated in Chapter 6.

## Chapter Summary

Christopher Marlowe, in a dramatic career of only about six years before his violent death at twenty-nine, transformed blank verse — introduced by Surrey a generation earlier (Chapter 2) — into "Marlowe's mighty line," the defining medium of English Renaissance tragedy, and established the "overreacher" protagonist (Tamburlaine, Faustus, Barabas) as one of the period's most influential tragic types. *Doctor Faustus*'s two divergent printed texts (1604 and 1616) exemplify the textual instability common to Elizabethan drama, while Marlowe's biography — probable intelligence work, arrest on blasphemy charges via the Baines Note, and violent, still-debated death in a Deptford lodging house in May 1593 — has generated as much scholarly fascination as his plays. Marlowe's direct influence on Shakespeare, in verse style, morally ambiguous protagonists, and specific dramatic material, makes his brief career foundational for the achievements treated in the following chapter.

## Glossary

- **Baines Note** — the May 1593 informer's report accusing Marlowe of blasphemous and heretical statements.
- **Blank verse** — unrhymed iambic pentameter; see also Chapter 2 (Surrey) and Chapter 4.
- **Overreacher** — a critical term for Marlowe's characteristic tragic protagonist type, defined by transgressive, ultimately self-destructive ambition.
- **"Mighty line"** — Ben Jonson's phrase (from his prefatory verses to Shakespeare's First Folio) for Marlowe's powerful blank verse style.
