## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Edmund Spenser and Sir Philip Sidney were close contemporaries and friends, moving in the same literary circles at Elizabeth I's court. They wrote at a genuinely confident moment in English history: the religious upheavals of Chapter 1 had settled into a consolidated Protestant order, the sonnet innovations of Wyatt and Surrey from Chapter 2 had already proven English poetry could match Italian sophistication, and England was expanding its colonial reach into Ireland, where Spenser himself spent much of his career.

Both poets' work is bound up with Elizabeth I herself as a literary and political icon, though in different ways. Spenser does it directly: *The Faerie Queene* allegorically celebrates the queen as "Gloriana." Sidney does it more indirectly, by embodying — and, after his early death, being mythologized as — the ideal Renaissance courtier, someone who could unite soldierly valor, courtly service, and genuine literary accomplishment all in one person.

## Literary Movements

When Sidney's *Astrophil and Stella* was finally published in 1591, five years after his death, it triggered something literary historians call the **Elizabethan sonnet sequence vogue** — a wave of Petrarch-influenced sonnet cycles, each loosely tracing a speaker's psychological progress through love for a usually unattainable beloved, that dominated English lyric poetry for roughly the next two decades. You'll meet several of these sequences: Samuel Daniel's *Delia* (1592), Michael Drayton's *Idea's Mirror* (1594), Spenser's own *Amoretti* (1595), and eventually, the most famous of them all, Shakespeare's *Sonnets* in Chapter 6.

Spenser's other great achievement runs on a completely different scale: *The Faerie Queene*, this period's supreme work of sustained allegorical epic. It combines Arthurian romance material — echoing the medieval "Matter of Britain" you learned about back in Part II, Chapter 2 — with moral and political allegory honouring Elizabeth I, all delivered in a deliberately archaic, Chaucer-inflected style of language.

## Author Profiles

**Edmund Spenser** (c. 1552–1599) — London-born poet, educated at the Merchant Taylors' School and Cambridge; entered the service of the Earl of Leicester before, in 1580, becoming secretary to Arthur Grey, 14th Baron Grey de Wilton, newly appointed Lord Deputy of Ireland. Spenser spent most of the remainder of his career in Ireland, acquiring the Kilcolman estate in County Cork (c. 1587–89), where he composed much of *The Faerie Queene*; his Irish estate was burned during the Nine Years' War in 1598, and he died in London the following year. Later admired as "the poets' poet" (an epithet coined by the essayist Charles Lamb) for his profound formal and stylistic influence on subsequent generations of poets.

**Sir Philip Sidney** (1554–1586) — Aristocratic courtier, soldier, diplomat, and poet-critic, nephew of the Earl of Leicester; composed his major works — the *Old Arcadia* (prose romance, begun 1580 for his sister Mary, Countess of Pembroke), *Astrophil and Stella* (sonnet sequence, c. 1582), and *The Defence of Poesy* (critical treatise, c. 1579–80) — within a remarkably compressed period, none published in his lifetime. Mortally wounded at the Battle of Zutphen in the Netherlands (22 September 1586) fighting on behalf of Protestant forces against Spain, he died three weeks later at thirty-one, and was subsequently mythologized as the ideal Renaissance figure combining martial valor, courtly grace, and literary genius.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***The Shepheardes Calender*** (1579). Twelve pastoral eclogues, one for each month, all framed by a recurring shepherd-poet persona named "Colin Clout" — Spenser's own poetic self-projection. It combines pastoral convention, following Virgil's classical model, with contemporary religious and political satire, delivered in a deliberately archaic style borrowed from Chaucer. Many scholars mark this poem as the true beginning of the mature English literary Renaissance.
- ***The Faerie Queene*** (Books I–III, 1590; Books I–VI, 1596). Spenser planned this allegorical epic in twelve books; he completed six. In it, knights representing particular virtues — Redcrosse Knight for Holiness, Sir Guyon for Temperance, Britomart for Chastity, and others — go on quests in service of the Faerie Queene herself, Gloriana, an explicit stand-in for Elizabeth I. Spenser invented a new stanza form specifically for this poem, now called the **Spenserian stanza**: nine lines, eight of them in iambic pentameter followed by one longer closing line (an alexandrine), rhyming ababbcbcc. Later poets, including Keats and Byron in Part VI, would return to this exact stanza form generations later.
- ***Astrophil and Stella*** (Sidney, composed c. 1582, published 1591). This is the first major English sonnet sequence — 108 sonnets and 11 songs tracing the emotional journey of "Astrophil" ("star-lover") through his unrequited love for "Stella" ("star"), a figure widely identified with Penelope Devereux, later Lady Rich. What sets it apart is its psychological immediacy and self-aware wit: Sidney takes Petrarchan convention and gives it a distinctly English, dramatically voiced quality that feels genuinely spoken rather than merely borrowed.
- ***The Defence of Poesy*** (Sidney, composed c. 1579–80, published 1595). This critical treatise responds directly to Puritan attacks on poetry's moral legitimacy. Sidney's argument: poetry combines philosophy's power to instruct with history's grounding in concrete particulars, which makes it, in his view, actually superior to both as a vehicle for moral education. It's a foundational text in the history of English literary criticism.
- ***Amoretti* and *Epithalamion*** (Spenser, 1595). *Amoretti* is a sequence of 89 sonnets recording Spenser's courtship of Elizabeth Boyle — and here's what makes it unusual: unlike the standard Petrarchan sequence, which stays permanently unrequited, this one culminates in marriage, celebrated in the accompanying *Epithalamion*, a formal wedding ode tracing the couple's wedding day from dawn all the way to the wedding night. Together, they give you a genuinely unusual Elizabethan sonnet sequence: one built around fulfilled, marital love rather than endless longing.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Why did Spenser write in such deliberately old-fashioned language?** Critics have long debated this. Was his archaic, Chaucer-derived vocabulary a nationalist assertion of a native English poetic lineage — comparable to how Part II treated Chaucer's own foundational status? A scholarly antiquarian affectation? Or a deliberate strategy for creating distance and enchantment? All three readings have real supporters.
- **How political is *The Faerie Queene*, really?** The poem's celebration of Elizabeth I as Gloriana is explicit and unmissable. But scholars keep debating the finer political detail of individual episodes — including how the poem treats Ireland and colonial policy, an issue sharpened by Spenser's own controversial prose tract *A View of the Present State of Ireland*. Don't read the poem as simple, untroubled royal praise; it's more complicated than that.
- **How much did Sidney's death shape his reputation?** Sidney's death at Zutphen, and the probably embellished legend of his last gesture — giving his water to a wounded soldier, saying "thy necessity is yet greater than mine" — turned him almost instantly into an idealized icon of Protestant chivalric sacrifice. Critics note that this legend has sometimes overshadowed close attention to what actually makes his poetry good: its psychological complexity and formal innovation.
- **Fashion or genuine innovation? Probably both.** The sonnet sequences that followed *Astrophil and Stella*'s 1591 publication — Daniel's *Delia*, Drayton's *Idea*, and others — can be read two ways at once: as evidence the form had genuine expressive power, and as a case study in Elizabethan literary fashion, where one prestigious model (Sidney's, itself building on Wyatt and Surrey from Chapter 2) quickly generated a crowded field of imitators of wildly varying quality.
- **What does it mean that Spenser's *Amoretti* ends in marriage?** Because it culminates in marriage rather than sustaining perpetual Petrarchan longing, critics read *Amoretti* as a deliberate, theologically informed revision of the sonnet sequence convention — consistent with something you'll also see in *The Faerie Queene*: Spenser's broader interest in reconciling romantic and erotic experience with Christian and marital order.

## Chapter Summary

Edmund Spenser and Philip Sidney, close contemporaries at the height of Elizabeth I's reign, produced the two most significant bodies of Elizabethan poetry. Spenser's *Shepheardes Calender* and his unfinished epic *The Faerie Queene* — composed in his own invented Spenserian stanza, and built as an elaborate allegorical celebration of Elizabeth I — earned him the title "the poets' poet," while his *Amoretti and Epithalamion* gave the period an unusual sonnet sequence, one culminating in marital fulfillment rather than perpetual longing. Sidney's entire major output was published only after his early death at the Battle of Zutphen in 1586: *Astrophil and Stella* became the first major English sonnet sequence, launching a vogue that would dominate English lyric poetry through the 1590s and beyond, and *The Defence of Poesy* became one of the foundational works of English literary criticism. Between them, Spenser and Sidney take the sonnet and allegorical-epic traditions inherited from Wyatt and Surrey in Chapter 2 and bring them to full maturity — the great flowering of Elizabethan literature.

## Glossary

- **Eclogue** — a short pastoral poem, typically structured as a dialogue between shepherds, following classical (Virgilian) convention.
- **Sonnet sequence** — a series of linked sonnets, typically tracing the psychological progress of a speaker's love for a beloved.
- **Spenserian stanza** — a nine-line stanza (eight iambic pentameter lines plus a concluding alexandrine, rhyming ababbcbcc) invented by Spenser for *The Faerie Queene*.
