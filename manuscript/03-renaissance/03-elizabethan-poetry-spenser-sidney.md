## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Edmund Spenser and Sir Philip Sidney, close contemporaries and friends within the Elizabethan court's literary circles, wrote during the height of Elizabeth I's reign, a period of consolidated Protestant settlement following the religious upheavals of Chapter 1, renewed literary confidence following the sonnet innovations of Wyatt and Surrey (Chapter 2), and English colonial expansion into Ireland, where Spenser spent much of his career. Both poets' work is deeply engaged with the cult of Elizabeth I as a literary and political icon — Spenser directly, through *The Faerie Queene*'s allegorical celebration of the queen as "Gloriana," and Sidney indirectly, through his embodiment (and, after his early death, mythologization) of the ideal Renaissance courtier uniting soldierly valor, courtly service, and literary accomplishment.

## Literary Movements

The 1591 posthumous publication of Sidney's *Astrophil and Stella* is conventionally credited with launching the **Elizabethan sonnet sequence vogue**, a wave of Petrarch-influenced sonnet cycles — narrating, in loosely linked sequence, the psychological progress of a speaker's love for an often unattainable beloved — that dominated English lyric poetry for roughly two decades, including Samuel Daniel's *Delia* (1592), Michael Drayton's *Idea's Mirror* (1594, later revised as *Idea*), Spenser's own *Amoretti* (1595), and eventually Shakespeare's *Sonnets* (Chapter 6). Spenser's parallel achievement, *The Faerie Queene*, represents the period's supreme achievement in sustained allegorical epic, combining Arthurian romance material (echoing the medieval Matter of Britain surveyed in Part II, Chapter 2), moral and political allegory honouring Elizabeth I, and a deliberately archaic, Chaucer-inflected poetic diction.

## Author Profiles

**Edmund Spenser** (c. 1552–1599) — London-born poet, educated at the Merchant Taylors' School and Cambridge; entered the service of the Earl of Leicester before, in 1580, becoming secretary to Arthur Grey, 14th Baron Grey de Wilton, newly appointed Lord Deputy of Ireland. Spenser spent most of the remainder of his career in Ireland, acquiring the Kilcolman estate in County Cork (c. 1587–89), where he composed much of *The Faerie Queene*; his Irish estate was burned during the Nine Years' War in 1598, and he died in London the following year. Later admired as "the poets' poet" (an epithet coined by the essayist Charles Lamb) for his profound formal and stylistic influence on subsequent generations of poets.

**Sir Philip Sidney** (1554–1586) — Aristocratic courtier, soldier, diplomat, and poet-critic, nephew of the Earl of Leicester; composed his major works — the *Old Arcadia* (prose romance, begun 1580 for his sister Mary, Countess of Pembroke), *Astrophil and Stella* (sonnet sequence, c. 1582), and *The Defence of Poesy* (critical treatise, c. 1579–80) — within a remarkably compressed period, none published in his lifetime. Mortally wounded at the Battle of Zutphen in the Netherlands (22 September 1586) fighting on behalf of Protestant forces against Spain, he died three weeks later at thirty-one, and was subsequently mythologized as the ideal Renaissance figure combining martial valor, courtly grace, and literary genius.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***The Shepheardes Calender*** (1579) — Twelve pastoral eclogues, one per month, framed by the recurring shepherd-poet persona "Colin Clout" (a Spenserian self-projection), combining pastoral convention (following Virgil's model) with contemporary religious and political satire and a deliberately archaic diction drawing on Chaucer; widely regarded as marking the beginning of the mature English literary Renaissance.
- ***The Faerie Queene*** (Books I–III, 1590; Books I–VI, 1596) — An unfinished allegorical epic (Spenser planned twelve books; six were completed) in which knights representing particular virtues — Redcrosse Knight (Holiness), Sir Guyon (Temperance), Britomart (Chastity), and others — undertake quests in service of the Faerie Queene, Gloriana, an explicit allegorical figure for Elizabeth I. Composed in the **Spenserian stanza** (nine lines: eight in iambic pentameter followed by a concluding alexandrine, rhyming ababbcbcc), a form Spenser invented specifically for the poem and which later poets (including Keats and Byron in Part VI) would revive.
- ***Astrophil and Stella*** (Sidney, composed c. 1582, published 1591) — The first major English sonnet sequence, comprising 108 sonnets and 11 songs tracing the emotional progress of "Astrophil" ("star-lover") in his unrequited love for "Stella" ("star"), widely identified with Penelope Devereux (later Lady Rich); notable for its psychological immediacy, self-aware wit, and formal virtuosity in adapting Petrarchan convention to a distinctly English, dramatically voiced idiom.
- ***The Defence of Poesy*** (Sidney, composed c. 1579–80, published 1595) — A critical treatise responding to Puritan and other attacks on poetry's moral legitimacy, arguing that poetry, uniquely combining philosophy's instructive purpose with history's concrete particularity, offers a superior vehicle for moral education; a foundational text of English literary criticism.
- ***Amoretti* and *Epithalamion*** (Spenser, 1595) — A sequence of 89 sonnets (*Amoretti*) recording Spenser's courtship of Elizabeth Boyle, unusually culminating (rather than remaining permanently unrequited, as in the Petrarchan convention) in the marriage celebrated in the accompanying *Epithalamion*, a formal wedding ode tracing the couple's wedding day from dawn to wedding night — together representing an unusual Elizabethan sonnet sequence built around fulfilled, marital love rather than perpetual unattainable longing.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Spenser's archaism.** Critics have long debated Spenser's deliberate use of archaic, Chaucer-derived vocabulary and spelling in *The Shepheardes Calender* and *The Faerie Queene* — read variously as a nationalist assertion of a native English poetic lineage (comparable to Part II's account of Chaucer's foundational status), a scholarly antiquarian affectation, or a deliberate strategy of poetic distancing and enchantment.
- **The politics of allegory in *The Faerie Queene*.** While the poem's celebration of Elizabeth I as Gloriana is explicit, scholars continue to debate the precise political valence of individual episodes — including its treatment of Ireland and colonial policy, informed by Spenser's own controversial prose tract *A View of the Present State of Ireland* — complicating any simple reading of the poem as untroubled royal panegyric.
- **Sidney's biographical mythologization.** Sidney's death at Zutphen, and the (probably later-embellished) legend of his gesture giving his water to a wounded soldier ("thy necessity is yet greater than mine"), rapidly transformed him into an idealized cultural icon of Protestant chivalric sacrifice, a reputation critics note sometimes overshadowed close attention to the psychological complexity and formal innovation of his actual poetry.
- **Sonnet sequence as fashion and as innovation.** The rapid proliferation of sonnet sequences following *Astrophil and Stella*'s 1591 publication (Daniel's *Delia*, Drayton's *Idea*, and others) is read by literary historians both as evidence of the form's genuine expressive power and as a case study in Elizabethan literary fashion, in which a prestigious model (Sidney's, itself building on Wyatt and Surrey, Chapter 2) rapidly generated a crowded field of imitators of widely varying quality and originality.
- **Spenser's *Amoretti* as generic departure.** Because the *Amoretti* sequence culminates in marriage rather than sustaining perpetual Petrarchan longing, critics read it as a deliberate, theologically inflected revision of the sonnet sequence convention, consistent with Spenser's broader interest (visible also in *The Faerie Queene*) in reconciling romantic and erotic experience with Christian and marital order.

## Chapter Summary

Edmund Spenser and Philip Sidney, close contemporaries at the height of Elizabeth I's reign, produced the two most significant bodies of Elizabethan poetry. Spenser's *Shepheardes Calender* and unfinished epic *The Faerie Queene* — composed in the poet's own invented Spenserian stanza and structured as an elaborate allegorical celebration of Elizabeth I — established him as "the poets' poet," while his *Amoretti and Epithalamion* offered an unusual sonnet sequence culminating in marital fulfillment rather than perpetual longing. Sidney, whose entire major literary output was published only after his early death at the Battle of Zutphen (1586), produced in *Astrophil and Stella* the first major English sonnet sequence, launching a vogue for sonnet sequences that would dominate English lyric poetry through the 1590s and 1600s, and in *The Defence of Poesy* one of the foundational works of English literary criticism. Together, their achievements mark the full maturation of the sonnet and allegorical/epic traditions inherited from Wyatt and Surrey (Chapter 2) into the great flowering of Elizabethan literature.

## Glossary

- **Eclogue** — a short pastoral poem, typically structured as a dialogue between shepherds, following classical (Virgilian) convention.
- **Sonnet sequence** — a series of linked sonnets, typically tracing the psychological progress of a speaker's love for a beloved.
- **Spenserian stanza** — a nine-line stanza (eight iambic pentameter lines plus a concluding alexandrine, rhyming ababbcbcc) invented by Spenser for *The Faerie Queene*.
