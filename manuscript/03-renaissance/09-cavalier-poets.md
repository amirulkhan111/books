## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The poets in this chapter wrote as self-consciously loyal courtiers of Charles I. Their name, "Cavalier," comes from the French word *chevalier*, and it names the same royalist military affiliation that would define one side of the English Civil War (1642–51) against the parliamentary "Roundheads." Their poetic sensibility descends directly from Ben Jonson's neoclassical wit and formal discipline, which you met in Chapter 7 — many of them gathered, during Jonson's lifetime and after, in an informal circle called the "Tribe of Ben" or "Sons of Ben." But their historical moment looked nothing like Jonson's own comparatively secure position at the Jacobean court. These poets lived through escalating political crisis, civil war, the king's execution in 1649, and, for several of them, personal ruin, imprisonment, or exile — all of it building toward the theatre closures and broader cultural rupture you already learned about in Chapter 4.

## Literary Movements

The **"Tribe of Ben"** — sometimes narrowed to "Sons of Ben" specifically for Jonson's dramatist followers — names the loose circle of younger poets who gathered around Ben Jonson, absorbing his neoclassical wit, Horatian restraint, and formal discipline, which you met in Chapter 7. The **Cavalier poets** took that stylistic inheritance and fused it with explicit royalist political loyalty and the courtly themes of love, honour, leisure, and — as the 1640s crisis escalated — impending loss. Expect Cavalier verse to be graceful, technically polished, direct and often playful in address, with a recurring **carpe diem** ("seize the day") sentiment: an urgency toward pleasure and love, justified by how inevitable mortality and the passage of time are. That urgency owes something to the metaphysical tradition you studied in Chapter 8, but it's expressed here with a lighter, more socially assured surface wit.

## Author Profiles

**Robert Herrick** (1591–1674) — London-born, educated at Cambridge; ordained in the Church of England in 1623 and appointed vicar of Dean Prior, Devon, in 1629, a rural post from which he was ejected in 1647 for refusing the parliamentary Solemn League and Covenant, returning to London to depend on friends' and family's charity until the 1660 Restoration allowed his return to Dean Prior. His major collection, *Hesperides* (1648, published together with *His Noble Numbers*), gathers over a thousand of his roughly 2,500 poems and includes his most celebrated carpe diem lyric, "To the Virgins, to Make Much of Time" ("Gather ye rosebuds while ye may") — a notably secular, pleasure-affirming sentiment for a clergyman, exemplifying the Cavalier fusion of classical (Horatian/Jonsonian) restraint with sensuous, pastoral English subject matter.

**Sir John Suckling** (1609–1641/42) — Courtier, poet, and noted wit, reputed inventor of the card game cribbage; author of witty lyrics including "Why So Pale and Wan, Fond Lover?" (from his play *Aglaura*). In May 1641, Suckling was implicated in the "First Army Plot," a scheme to rescue the imprisoned Earl of Strafford from the Tower of London and bring in French military support for the king; exposed, he fled to France, was convicted of high treason in absentia by Parliament (13 August 1641), and died in exile shortly afterward under obscure circumstances — most commonly attributed, following the antiquarian John Aubrey's account, to suicide by poison amid fear of poverty, though other accounts (including Alexander Pope's) offer differing explanations.

**Richard Lovelace** (1617/18–1657/58) — Kentish gentleman-soldier and poet, generally regarded as the archetypal Cavalier figure; served in the king's forces during the Bishops' Wars (1639–40) before, in 1642, presenting a Royalist petition to a hostile House of Commons, for which he was imprisoned in London's Gatehouse — an experience that produced "To Althea, from Prison" ("Stone walls do not a prison make, / Nor iron bars a cage"). Imprisoned again for nearly a year in 1648, he published his collection *Lucasta* in 1649, shortly after Charles I's execution had rendered the Royalist cause defeated; his most celebrated poem, "To Lucasta, Going to the Wars," balances romantic devotion against martial honour ("I could not love thee, dear, so much, / Loved I not honour more"). He spent his final years in poverty, dying in 1657 or 1658.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"To the Virgins, to Make Much of Time"** (Herrick). This compact carpe diem lyric urges young women to marry and embrace pleasure "while ye may," building its argument around the image of a rose that fades quickly. Critics have long noticed a real tension worth thinking about: how does this purely secular, pleasure-affirming poem sit alongside the fact that its author was an ordained clergyman?
- **"Why So Pale and Wan, Fond Lover?"** (Suckling). A brisk, cynically witty address to a despondent lover, mocking romantic self-pity with the detached, socially assured ease that defines Cavalier tone. Set it next to the metaphysical poets' more anguished treatments of love from Chapter 8, and you'll hear immediately how much lighter this register is.
- **"To Lucasta, Going to the Wars"** (Lovelace). The speaker weighs his genuine devotion to his beloved against something he claims to value even more: "honour." That balance articulates a central Cavalier ideal — martial loyalty itself understood as a form of love — addressed to a beloved figure, "Lucasta," who may be fictionalized or composite rather than one specific real woman.
- **"To Althea, from Prison"** (Lovelace). Written during Lovelace's actual 1642 imprisonment, this poem turns physical confinement into an occasion for affirming a deeper, inward spiritual and political freedom: "Stone walls do not a prison make." That paradox is exactly what gives the poem its lasting resonance, well beyond its original royalist occasion.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Cavalier lightness escapism, or defiance?** Critics increasingly read the characteristic lightness and wit of Cavalier verse not as indifference to the political catastrophe surrounding it, but as a deliberate, even defiant, aesthetic response to that catastrophe — grace and pleasure asserted precisely because political and personal security had become so uncertain. This reading gains real weight once you look at these poets' actual lives: Lovelace's imprisonments, Suckling's exile and likely suicide, Herrick's professional ruin, Lovelace's eventual poverty.
- **How should we read Herrick's clerical/secular tension?** Scholars continue to debate how to reconcile Herrick's substantial body of frankly sensuous, pleasure-affirming secular lyric with his simultaneous clerical vocation and devotional poetry, collected as *His Noble Numbers* alongside *Hesperides*. Are these two separate, compartmentalized modes? Or evidence of a more integrated Renaissance sensibility, genuinely comfortable holding sacred and secular impulses together at once?
- **Why is Suckling's death still uncertain?** Contemporary accounts of Suckling's death conflict — the antiquarian John Aubrey claims suicide by poison, while other accounts, including Alexander Pope's, point to death from infection. This uncertainty is a useful example of a broader problem: several Cavalier poets' biographies are genuinely hard to pin down, made worse by the political chaos and displacement of the early 1640s.
- **What makes "To Althea, from Prison" resonate beyond its original moment?** Lovelace's poem articulates a durable literary paradox: that true liberty is a state of mind, not a matter of physical circumstance. That idea has an obvious appeal well beyond its immediate royalist context, which is exactly why the poem keeps showing up in general anthologies today, long after its Civil War origins have faded from most readers' minds.

## Chapter Summary

The Cavalier poets — Robert Herrick, Sir John Suckling, and Richard Lovelace among them — took Ben Jonson's neoclassical wit and formal discipline, absorbed as members or associates of the "Tribe of Ben," and turned it into a distinctively royalist courtly verse tradition marked by graceful lyricism and carpe diem urgency, written against the escalating crisis of Charles I's reign and the English Civil War. Herrick's *Hesperides* (1648) combined frankly sensuous secular lyric with his own clerical vocation. Suckling's witty, socially assured verse ended in his likely suicide, following his exposure in the 1641 Army Plot. And Lovelace's *Lucasta* (1649), composed amid repeated royalist imprisonment, produced two of the era's most enduring lyrics on love, honour, and the paradox of inward freedom. Their biographies of imprisonment, exile, and poverty tell you something important: this poetry's characteristic grace didn't evade the surrounding political catastrophe — it responded to it directly. By 1642, that catastrophe had closed the London theatres, as you learned in Chapter 4; by 1649, it had executed the king to whom these poets' loyalty was pledged.

## Glossary

- **Carpe diem** — Latin, "seize the day"; a literary theme urging the embrace of pleasure given time's and mortality's inevitability.
- **Cavalier poets** — a group of royalist courtier poets (including Herrick, Suckling, Lovelace) active before and during the English Civil War.
- **"Tribe of Ben" / "Sons of Ben"** — the informal circle of poets influenced by Ben Jonson's neoclassical style (Chapter 7).
