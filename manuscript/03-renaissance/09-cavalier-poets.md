## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The Cavalier poets wrote as self-consciously loyal courtiers of Charles I, taking their name (from the French *chevalier*) from the same royalist military affiliation that would define one side of the English Civil War (1642–51) against the parliamentary "Roundheads." Their poetic sensibility descended directly from Ben Jonson's neoclassical wit and formal discipline (Chapter 7) — many gathered, in Jonson's lifetime and after, in an informal circle known as the "Tribe of Ben" or "Sons of Ben" — but their historical moment, unlike Jonson's own more secure Jacobean court position, was one of escalating political crisis, civil war, regicide (1649), and, for several of these poets, personal ruin, imprisonment, or exile, culminating in the theatre closures and broader cultural rupture discussed in Chapter 4.

## Literary Movements

The **"Tribe of Ben"** (sometimes distinguished as "Sons of Ben" for Jonson's dramatist followers specifically) denotes the loose circle of younger poets who gathered around Ben Jonson, absorbing his neoclassical wit, Horatian restraint, and formal discipline (Chapter 7) — a stylistic inheritance the **Cavalier poets** subsequently fused with explicit royalist political loyalty and the courtly themes of love, honour, leisure, and (given the escalating crisis of the 1640s) impending loss. Cavalier verse is typically characterized by graceful, technically polished lyricism, direct and often playful address, and recurring **carpe diem** ("seize the day") sentiment — an urgency toward pleasure and love justified by mortality's and time's inevitability — inherited in part from the metaphysical tradition (Chapter 8) but expressed with a lighter, more socially assured surface wit.

## Author Profiles

**Robert Herrick** (1591–1674) — London-born, educated at Cambridge; ordained in the Church of England in 1623 and appointed vicar of Dean Prior, Devon, in 1629, a rural post from which he was ejected in 1647 for refusing the parliamentary Solemn League and Covenant, returning to London to depend on friends' and family's charity until the 1660 Restoration allowed his return to Dean Prior. His major collection, *Hesperides* (1648, published together with *His Noble Numbers*), gathers over a thousand of his roughly 2,500 poems and includes his most celebrated carpe diem lyric, "To the Virgins, to Make Much of Time" ("Gather ye rosebuds while ye may") — a notably secular, pleasure-affirming sentiment for a clergyman, exemplifying the Cavalier fusion of classical (Horatian/Jonsonian) restraint with sensuous, pastoral English subject matter.

**Sir John Suckling** (1609–1641/42) — Courtier, poet, and noted wit, reputed inventor of the card game cribbage; author of witty lyrics including "Why So Pale and Wan, Fond Lover?" (from his play *Aglaura*). In May 1641, Suckling was implicated in the "First Army Plot," a scheme to rescue the imprisoned Earl of Strafford from the Tower of London and bring in French military support for the king; exposed, he fled to France, was convicted of high treason in absentia by Parliament (13 August 1641), and died in exile shortly afterward under obscure circumstances — most commonly attributed, following the antiquarian John Aubrey's account, to suicide by poison amid fear of poverty, though other accounts (including Alexander Pope's) offer differing explanations.

**Richard Lovelace** (1617/18–1657/58) — Kentish gentleman-soldier and poet, generally regarded as the archetypal Cavalier figure; served in the king's forces during the Bishops' Wars (1639–40) before, in 1642, presenting a Royalist petition to a hostile House of Commons, for which he was imprisoned in London's Gatehouse — an experience that produced "To Althea, from Prison" ("Stone walls do not a prison make, / Nor iron bars a cage"). Imprisoned again for nearly a year in 1648, he published his collection *Lucasta* in 1649, shortly after Charles I's execution had rendered the Royalist cause defeated; his most celebrated poem, "To Lucasta, Going to the Wars," balances romantic devotion against martial honour ("I could not love thee, dear, so much, / Loved I not honour more"). He spent his final years in poverty, dying in 1657 or 1658.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"To the Virgins, to Make Much of Time"** (Herrick) — A compact carpe diem lyric urging young women to marry and embrace pleasure "while ye may," using the swiftly fading rose as its central image; notable for the tension, much remarked by critics, between its purely secular hedonistic argument and its clergyman author.
- **"Why So Pale and Wan, Fond Lover?"** (Suckling) — A brisk, cynically witty address to a despondent lover, mocking romantic self-pity with characteristic Cavalier detachment and social ease, markedly lighter in tone than the metaphysical poets' more anguished treatments of love (Chapter 8).
- **"To Lucasta, Going to the Wars"** (Lovelace) — Balances the speaker's genuine devotion to his beloved against his greater devotion to "honour," articulating the Cavalier ideal of martial loyalty as itself a form of love, addressed to a fictionalized or composite beloved figure ("Lucasta").
- **"To Althea, from Prison"** (Lovelace) — Written during Lovelace's 1642 imprisonment, the poem transforms physical confinement into an occasion for affirming inward, spiritual and political freedom ("Stone walls do not a prison make"), a paradox that gives the poem enduring resonance beyond its immediate royalist occasion.

---

## Critical Perspectives

- **The Cavalier poets and political crisis.** Critics increasingly read Cavalier verse's characteristic lightness and wit not as escapist indifference to the surrounding political catastrophe but as a deliberate, even defiant, aesthetic response to it — grace and pleasure asserted precisely because political and personal security had become so uncertain, a reading that gains force from these poets' actual biographies of imprisonment (Lovelace, Suckling implicitly), professional ruin (Herrick), and early or violent death (Suckling, and Lovelace's eventual poverty).
- **Herrick's clerical/secular tension.** Scholars continue to debate how to reconcile Herrick's substantial body of frankly sensuous, pleasure-affirming secular lyric with his simultaneous clerical vocation and devotional poetry (collected as *His Noble Numbers* alongside *Hesperides*) — whether as compartmentalized, generically distinct modes, or as evidence of a more integrated Renaissance sensibility comfortable holding sacred and secular impulses together.
- **Suckling's death and its uncertainty.** The conflicting contemporary accounts of Suckling's death (Aubrey's suicide-by-poison narrative against Pope's account of death from infection) exemplify the broader evidentiary uncertainty surrounding several Cavalier poets' biographies, complicated further by the political chaos and displacement of the early 1640s.
- **"To Althea, from Prison" and the paradox of confined freedom.** Lovelace's poem is frequently read as a foundational articulation of a durable literary paradox — that true liberty is a condition of mind rather than circumstance — with obvious appeal beyond its immediate royalist political context, explaining its persistence in general anthologies independent of its Civil War origins.

## Chapter Summary

The Cavalier poets — Robert Herrick, Sir John Suckling, and Richard Lovelace among them — extended Ben Jonson's neoclassical wit and formal discipline (absorbed as members or associates of the "Tribe of Ben") into a distinctively royalist courtly verse tradition, marked by graceful lyricism and carpe diem urgency, written against the escalating crisis of Charles I's reign and the English Civil War. Herrick's *Hesperides* (1648) combined frankly sensuous secular lyric with his clerical vocation; Suckling's witty, socially assured verse ended in his likely suicide following his exposure in the 1641 Army Plot; and Lovelace's *Lucasta* (1649), composed amid repeated royalist imprisonment, produced two of the era's most enduring lyrics on love, honour, and the paradox of inward freedom. Their biographies of imprisonment, exile, and poverty underscore how directly this poetry's characteristic grace responded to — rather than simply evaded — the political catastrophe that would, by 1642, close the London theatres (Chapter 4) and, by 1649, execute the king to whom their loyalty was pledged.

## Glossary

- **Carpe diem** — Latin, "seize the day"; a literary theme urging the embrace of pleasure given time's and mortality's inevitability.
- **Cavalier poets** — a group of royalist courtier poets (including Herrick, Suckling, Lovelace) active before and during the English Civil War.
- **"Tribe of Ben" / "Sons of Ben"** — the informal circle of poets influenced by Ben Jonson's neoclassical style (Chapter 7).
