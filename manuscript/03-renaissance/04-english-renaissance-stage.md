## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The commercial theatre that produced Marlowe, Shakespeare, and Jonson emerged only a generation or two after the Reformation-driven suppression of the medieval mystery and morality play tradition (Part II, Chapter 7): as guild-sponsored religious cycle drama was progressively suppressed from the 1560s–70s, a new, secular, commercially organized theatrical industry rapidly filled the resulting cultural space, centred not in guild halls or church precincts but in purpose-built playhouses constructed just outside the City of London's jurisdiction, where civic authorities (often hostile to public performance on moral and public-order grounds) held no direct power. This chapter traces that transition and the institutional infrastructure — playhouses, companies, and royal licensing — that made the achievements of Chapters 5–7 possible.

## Literary Movements

The **University Wits** — a term coined only in the nineteenth century by the critic George Saintsbury, not used in the playwrights' own lifetimes — denotes a loose grouping of university-educated writers (Marlowe, Greene, and Nashe from Cambridge; Lyly, Lodge, and Peele from Oxford, with the non-university-trained Thomas Kyd often included) who, in the 1580s, brought classical learning and rhetorical sophistication to bear on the new commercial stage, decisively raising English drama's literary ambition beyond its medieval and popular-entertainment roots. Marlowe's mastery of blank verse (introduced by Surrey, Chapter 2) gave the public stage its characteristic dramatic voice for the following half-century (Chapter 5), while Kyd's *The Spanish Tragedy* (c. 1587) established the **revenge tragedy** as a major and enduring dramatic genre, directly influencing later works including Shakespeare's *Titus Andronicus* and *Hamlet*.

## Author Profiles

**James Burbage** (c. 1531–1597) — Actor and entrepreneur who, in 1576, built The Theatre in Shoreditch — sited deliberately outside the City of London's jurisdiction, where hostile civic authorities held no direct regulatory power — the first purpose-built commercial playhouse in London and a direct model for the amphitheatre-style playhouses that followed.

**The University Wits** — A retrospectively named group including **Christopher Marlowe** (treated fully in Chapter 5), **Robert Greene**, **Thomas Nashe**, **John Lyly**, **George Peele**, and **Thomas Lodge**, alongside the non-university **Thomas Kyd**; collectively responsible for professionalizing English commercial drama in the 1580s through classically informed plotting, rhetoric, and (in Marlowe's case above all) verse form.

**Thomas Kyd** (1558–1594) — Author of *The Spanish Tragedy* (composed c. 1587, first printed 1592), whose enormous popular success established the revenge tragedy as a dominant Elizabethan and Jacobean dramatic genre, introducing conventions (a ghost demanding vengeance, a delaying revenger-protagonist, a Machiavellian villain, a self-reflexive play-within-the-play) that would echo through the period's drama for decades.

**Philip Henslowe** (c. 1550–1616) — Theatrical entrepreneur who built the Rose theatre (1587) on the Bankside in Southwark and, through his detailed surviving business diary (*Henslowe's Diary*), provides historians with an invaluable record of Elizabethan theatrical finance, repertory, and practice.

**Edmund Tilney** — Master of the Revels under Elizabeth I for much of the period, responsible for licensing playing companies, playhouses, and individual plays for performance, exercising both quality control and political/religious censorship over the era's drama.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **The Theatre (1576) and the Globe (1599)** — The Theatre's 1598 dismantling and the reuse of its timbers to build the Globe on the South Bank (completed by summer 1599, at a cost of roughly £700) exemplifies both the practical, businesslike character of Elizabethan theatrical enterprise and the physical continuity of London's commercial playhouse tradition across a quarter-century — the Globe becoming, from 1599, the primary home of Shakespeare's company and plays.
- ***The Spanish Tragedy*** (Kyd) — A revenge tragedy in which the protagonist Hieronimo, seeking justice for his murdered son, ultimately achieves revenge through a play-within-the-play device; its combination of a ghost, delayed revenge, feigned or genuine madness, and metatheatrical structure established a durable template subsequently reworked by Shakespeare and others.
- **The playhouse structure** — The characteristic Elizabethan public playhouse (The Theatre, the Rose, the Swan, the Globe) was a roofless, polygonal or circular timber amphitheatre with tiered covered galleries surrounding an open standing-room "yard" (for the cheapest "groundling" admission) and a raised, partly covered stage projecting into the yard, backed by a "tiring house" for costume changes and entrances — a design enabling large, socially mixed audiences and flexible staging without elaborate scenery.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Continuity and rupture with medieval drama.** Scholars debate how sharply to distinguish the new commercial theatre from the medieval dramatic tradition it succeeded (Part II, Chapter 7): while institutionally and religiously distinct — secular, commercial, professionally acted, licensed by royal rather than ecclesiastical or guild authority — Elizabethan drama retained certain structural and thematic inheritances (moral allegory's residual influence, for instance, on morality-play-descended figures like the Vice) even as it developed a wholly new professional and commercial infrastructure.
- **The economics and geography of the playhouses.** Historians emphasize that the playhouses' location outside City jurisdiction (Shoreditch, and later Bankside south of the Thames) was a deliberate response to sustained civic hostility toward public theatre on grounds of morality, public order, and (particularly) plague-contagion risk — a hostility that anticipates, and helps explain the eventual success of, the 1642 Parliamentary closure.
- **All-male performance and boy actors.** The convention of boy actors performing female roles — a cultural rather than strictly legal prohibition on women appearing on the public stage, which persisted until the Restoration in 1660 — has generated extensive modern critical and performance-historical interest, particularly regarding its effects on the period's dramatic treatment of gender, disguise, and desire (a concern directly relevant to several Shakespeare comedies discussed in Chapter 6).
- **Censorship and the Master of the Revels.** While the Master of the Revels' role is often characterized primarily as "quality control" ensuring court-standard entertainment, his licensing authority also functioned as a genuine mechanism of political and religious censorship, shaping what could be staged — a regulatory apparatus that makes the eventual, far more sweeping 1642 closure a difference of degree as much as of kind from ongoing Elizabethan and Jacobean theatrical regulation.
- **1642 as this Part's dramatic endpoint.** The Long Parliament's 2 September 1642 ordinance, citing the need for national "humiliation" amid civil war and characterizing theatre as incompatible with pious solemnity, closed London's theatres for eighteen years (1642–1660) — providing, alongside the political/religious upheavals treated throughout this Part, a decisive institutional close to the commercial dramatic tradition inaugurated by Burbage in 1576, matching this Part's overall 1500–1660 chronological frame.

## Chapter Summary

English commercial theatre emerged rapidly in the wake of medieval religious drama's Reformation-era suppression (Part II, Chapter 7), centred from 1576 on purpose-built playhouses — beginning with James Burbage's Theatre in Shoreditch and culminating in the Globe (1599), built from the Theatre's own reused timbers by Shakespeare's company. The "University Wits" of the 1580s, above all Marlowe and Kyd, professionalized English drama through classical learning, blank verse mastery, and (in Kyd's *Spanish Tragedy*) the enormously influential revenge tragedy genre. This flourishing commercial theatre operated under the licensing authority of the Master of the Revels and the all-male performance convention (boy actors in female roles) until the Long Parliament's 1642 ordinance closed London's theatres for eighteen years, providing a decisive institutional endpoint — matching this Part's 1500–1660 chronological frame — to the dramatic tradition whose greatest achievements, Marlowe's and Shakespeare's, are treated in the following two chapters.

## Glossary

- **Groundling** — a spectator who paid the cheapest admission to stand in the open yard of an Elizabethan playhouse.
- **Master of the Revels** — the royal official responsible for licensing plays, playing companies, and playhouses, and for court entertainment.
- **Revenge tragedy** — a dramatic genre, established by Kyd's *The Spanish Tragedy*, centred on a protagonist's pursuit of vengeance, typically featuring a ghost, delay, and a climactic violent reckoning.
- **Tiring house** — the structure behind an Elizabethan playhouse stage used for costume changes, storage, and entrances/exits.
- **University Wits** — a retrospective term for a group of university-educated 1580s playwrights (Marlowe, Greene, Nashe, Lyly, Peele, Lodge) plus Thomas Kyd.
