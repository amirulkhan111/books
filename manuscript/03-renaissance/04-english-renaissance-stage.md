## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Think back to Part II, Chapter 7, where you learned that the Reformation shut down England's guild-sponsored mystery and morality plays from the 1560s–70s onward. This chapter picks up the story only a generation or two later, and shows you something striking: the cultural space those plays left empty didn't stay empty for long. A new, secular, commercially organized theatre industry rushed in to fill it — the very same industry that would go on to produce Marlowe, Shakespeare, and Jonson.

Notice where this new theatre actually set up shop: not in guild halls or church precincts, but in purpose-built playhouses constructed just outside the City of London's legal jurisdiction, specifically so that civic authorities — who were often hostile to public performance on moral and public-order grounds — had no direct power to shut them down. This chapter traces exactly how that transition happened, and the institutional machinery — playhouses, companies, royal licensing — that made the achievements you'll read about in Chapters 5 through 7 possible in the first place.

## Literary Movements

You'll often see the term **University Wits** applied to this generation of playwrights — but be aware it's a label historians invented later, coined in the nineteenth century by the critic George Saintsbury; none of these writers used it themselves. It refers to a loose group of university-educated writers — Marlowe, Greene, and Nashe from Cambridge; Lyly, Lodge, and Peele from Oxford, plus the non-university-trained Thomas Kyd, usually grouped in with them anyway — who in the 1580s brought real classical learning and rhetorical sophistication to the new commercial stage. Their impact was decisive: English drama's literary ambition jumped well beyond its medieval and popular-entertainment roots. Two contributions matter above all others. Marlowe mastered blank verse (which you first met through Surrey, back in Chapter 2), and that mastery gave the public stage its characteristic dramatic voice for the next half-century — you'll see this fully in Chapter 5. And Kyd's *The Spanish Tragedy* (c. 1587) established an entire new genre, the **revenge tragedy**, whose influence you can trace directly into later plays, including Shakespeare's *Titus Andronicus* and *Hamlet*.

## Author Profiles

**James Burbage** (c. 1531–1597) was an actor and entrepreneur who, in 1576, built The Theatre in Shoreditch, deliberately sited outside the City of London's jurisdiction, exactly where hostile civic authorities had no direct power to interfere. It was London's first purpose-built commercial playhouse, and it became the direct model for every amphitheatre-style playhouse that followed.

**The University Wits** is a name later historians gave, not a name these writers used for themselves. It groups together Christopher Marlowe, whom you'll meet fully in Chapter 5, along with Robert Greene, Thomas Nashe, John Lyly, George Peele, and Thomas Lodge, plus the non-university-trained Thomas Kyd, who's usually included with them anyway. Together, they professionalized English commercial drama in the 1580s through classically informed plotting and rhetoric — and, above all in Marlowe's case, verse form.

**Thomas Kyd** (1558–1594) wrote *The Spanish Tragedy*, composed around 1587 and first printed in 1592. Its enormous popular success established revenge tragedy as a dominant genre across the Elizabethan and Jacobean periods, and it introduced conventions — a ghost demanding vengeance, a hero who delays his revenge, a Machiavellian villain, a self-aware play-within-the-play — that would echo through English drama for decades afterward.

**Philip Henslowe** (c. 1550–1616) was a theatrical entrepreneur who built the Rose theatre in 1587, on the Bankside in Southwark. His detailed business diary survives today, and it gives historians an invaluable record of how Elizabethan theatre actually worked financially, day to day.

**Edmund Tilney** served as Master of the Revels under Elizabeth I for much of this period, responsible for licensing playing companies, playhouses, and individual plays before they could be performed — a role that combined genuine quality control with political and religious censorship of the era's drama.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **The Theatre (1576) and the Globe (1599)**. Watch the practical, almost businesslike logic here: when The Theatre was dismantled in 1598, its timbers were physically carried across the Thames and used to build the Globe on the South Bank, completed by summer 1599 for roughly £700. This single act of reuse captures both the hard-nosed commercial character of Elizabethan theatrical enterprise and the physical continuity of London's playhouse tradition across a quarter-century. From 1599 onward, the Globe became the primary home of Shakespeare's own company and plays.
- ***The Spanish Tragedy*** (Kyd). In this revenge tragedy, the protagonist Hieronimo seeks justice for his murdered son, and ultimately achieves his revenge through a play-within-the-play. Notice the ingredients: a ghost, delayed revenge, feigned or genuine madness, and a self-aware, "theatre within theatre" structure. That combination became a durable template, reworked again and again by Shakespeare and others after Kyd.
- **The playhouse structure**. Picture the typical Elizabethan public playhouse — The Theatre, the Rose, the Swan, the Globe — as a roofless, polygonal or circular timber amphitheatre. Tiered, covered galleries ring an open standing-room area called the "yard," where the cheapest "groundling" tickets got you in. A raised, partly covered stage projects out into that yard, backed by a "tiring house" used for costume changes and entrances. This design let large, socially mixed audiences gather cheaply, and it supported flexible staging without needing elaborate scenery.

## Critical Perspectives

- **How sharp was the break from medieval drama, really?** Scholars debate how cleanly to separate this new commercial theatre from the medieval tradition it replaced, which you studied in Part II, Chapter 7. Institutionally and religiously, the break is real: secular, commercial, professionally acted, licensed by royal rather than church or guild authority. But certain structural and thematic threads survived — you can still see moral allegory's residual influence, for instance, in morality-play-descended stock figures like the Vice.
- **Why did location matter so much?** Historians stress that building playhouses outside City jurisdiction — first Shoreditch, later Bankside, south of the Thames — was a deliberate response to sustained civic hostility toward public theatre, grounded in worries about morality, public order, and, especially, the risk of spreading plague in crowded gatherings. That same hostility helps explain why the 1642 Parliamentary closure, discussed below, succeeded so completely when it finally came.
- **What did all-male performance actually mean for the drama itself?** Boy actors performed all female roles — a cultural convention, not strictly a legal prohibition, and one that lasted until the Restoration in 1660. Modern critics and theatre historians have taken real interest in what this convention did to the period's dramatic treatment of gender, disguise, and desire — a question directly relevant to several of Shakespeare's comedies, which you'll meet in Chapter 6.
- **Was the Master of the Revels a quality inspector, or a censor?** Both, really. His role is often described mainly as "quality control," ensuring entertainment met court standards — but his licensing power also functioned as genuine political and religious censorship, shaping what could and couldn't be staged. Seen this way, the much more sweeping 1642 closure looks less like a sudden rupture and more like an intensification of controls that were already in place.
- **Why does 1642 make such a fitting endpoint for this chapter?** On 2 September 1642, the Long Parliament ordered London's theatres closed, citing the need for national "humiliation" during the civil war and calling theatre incompatible with proper solemnity. That closure lasted eighteen years, until 1660. Combined with the religious and political upheavals running through this whole Part, it gives the commercial dramatic tradition Burbage started in 1576 a clean institutional endpoint — one that lines up neatly with this Part's own 1500–1660 chronological frame.

## Chapter Summary

English commercial theatre emerged rapidly once medieval religious drama was suppressed in the Reformation era, as you learned in Part II, Chapter 7. From 1576 onward it centred on purpose-built playhouses, beginning with James Burbage's Theatre in Shoreditch and culminating in the Globe (1599), built from the Theatre's own reused timbers by Shakespeare's company. The "University Wits" of the 1580s, above all Marlowe and Kyd, professionalized English drama through classical learning, mastery of blank verse, and, in Kyd's *Spanish Tragedy*, the enormously influential revenge tragedy genre. This flourishing commercial theatre operated under the licensing authority of the Master of the Revels, and under the all-male performance convention of boy actors in female roles, until the Long Parliament's 1642 ordinance closed London's theatres for eighteen years — a decisive institutional endpoint, matching this Part's own 1500–1660 frame, for the dramatic tradition whose greatest achievements, Marlowe's and Shakespeare's, you'll meet in the next two chapters.

## Glossary

- **Groundling** — a spectator who paid the cheapest admission to stand in the open yard of an Elizabethan playhouse.
- **Master of the Revels** — the royal official responsible for licensing plays, playing companies, and playhouses, and for court entertainment.
- **Revenge tragedy** — a dramatic genre, established by Kyd's *The Spanish Tragedy*, centred on a protagonist's pursuit of vengeance, typically featuring a ghost, delay, and a climactic violent reckoning.
- **Tiring house** — the structure behind an Elizabethan playhouse stage used for costume changes, storage, and entrances/exits.
- **University Wits** — a retrospective term for a group of university-educated 1580s playwrights (Marlowe, Greene, Nashe, Lyly, Peele, Lodge) plus Thomas Kyd.
