## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Two new reigns give this chapter its two period names. The **Jacobean** period covers the reign of James I, 1603–25; the **Caroline** period covers Charles I, 1625–42, ending exactly where the theatre closures from Chapter 4 begin. Together, these two periods make up the second major phase of English Renaissance drama, following directly on from Marlowe's and Shakespeare's Elizabethan achievements in Chapters 5 and 6.

What actually changes, dramatically speaking? Broadly, tone darkens. Compared with Elizabethan comedy and tragedy, this period turns toward moral corruption, court intrigue, and violent, often Italian-set stories. Ben Jonson offers a genuine alternative to that darkening trend: a disciplined, neoclassical, satirical mode of his own, running alongside a parallel career supplying the Stuart monarchy with elaborate court masques — entertainments combining poetry, music, dance, and, through his collaboration with the designer Inigo Jones, increasingly spectacular staging.

## Literary Movements

Ben Jonson invented something he called the **comedy of humours**: a satirical dramatic mode where each character is dominated by a single exaggerated trait, or "humour." The idea draws on old classical medical theory, which held that four bodily fluids — blood, phlegm, black bile, yellow bile — determined a person's temperament. Jonson used this framework to expose greed, vanity, and folly through sharp moral and social satire, deliberately unlike Shakespeare's more romantically resolved comic mode from Chapter 6. Alongside his public-theatre comedies, Jonson also supplied the Stuart court with elaborate **masques** — courtly entertainments combining allegorical poetry, music, dance, and, through his long collaboration with the designer Inigo Jones, increasingly sophisticated staged spectacle. Between his public comedies and his court masques, Jonson became the era's dominant literary figure both at court and in the public playhouse.

Webster and Middleton pulled in a different direction, working within — and deepening — the darker Jacobean dramatic modes. Webster wrote **revenge tragedy**, the genre descended from Kyd back in Chapter 4, but intensified into a distinctively bleak, morally corrosive vision of court corruption and violence. Middleton worked across an unusually wide range: **city comedy**, satirizing London's mercantile and social life, alongside domestic and political tragedy — and, as was common practice for the period, he also worked as a collaborative playwright and adapter of other writers' work, including Shakespeare's.

## Author Profiles

**Ben Jonson** (1572–1637) — Educated at Westminster School before working briefly as a bricklayer (his stepfather's trade) and serving as a soldier in the Netherlands; established himself as a major dramatic and poetic voice second only to Shakespeare's in his own lifetime. In 1598 he killed a fellow actor, Gabriel Spencer, in a duel on Hoxton Fields, escaping execution by pleading "benefit of clergy" (demonstrating literacy) but being branded on the thumb. His major satirical comedies — *Every Man in His Humour* (1598), *Volpone* (1605–06), *The Alchemist* (1610), and *Bartholomew Fair* (1614) — established the comedy of humours as a distinct dramatic mode, while his long-running collaboration with Inigo Jones (beginning with *The Masque of Blackness*, 1605) made him the dominant supplier of Stuart court masques. Granted an annual royal pension in 1616, he is regarded as England's first (unofficial) poet laureate, and his later followers among younger poets were known as the "Tribe of Ben" or "Sons of Ben" (Chapter 9).

**John Webster** (c. 1578/80–c. 1632/34) — Little is documented of his life; probably London-born, son of a coach-maker. Author of two major Jacobean tragedies, *The White Devil* (performed and printed 1612, initially a poor stage failure) and *The Duchess of Malfi* (composed 1612–13, first performed at the intimate indoor Blackfriars theatre before a larger Globe audience in 1613–14), both revenge tragedies set in an intensely corrupt Italianate court world; widely regarded as the Jacobean dramatist with the single darkest vision of human nature among his contemporaries.

**Thomas Middleton** (1580–1627) — Prolific London-born playwright working across an unusually wide range of genres and frequently in collaboration with other dramatists (notably William Rowley); author of the city comedy *A Chaste Maid in Cheapside* (c. 1613) and the tragedies *Women Beware Women* (c. 1621) and, with Rowley, *The Changeling* (1622). Modern textual scholarship has also identified Middleton as the likely adapter responsible for revisions to Shakespeare's *Macbeth* (probably for a King's Men revival after Shakespeare's retirement or death), incorporating witch-songs from his own tragicomedy *The Witch* — a striking instance of the collaborative and adaptive textual culture that also affected several plays in the Shakespeare canon (Chapter 6).

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Volpone* (or, *The Fox*)** (Jonson, 1605–06). In this savage satire, the wealthy, childless Venetian Volpone feigns terminal illness in order to extract gifts from greedy legacy-hunters, aided by his parasite-servant Mosca. Most critics regard it as Jonson's finest and most enduringly performed play — a perfect display of the comedy of humours' moral and satirical bite.
- ***The Alchemist*** (Jonson, 1610). Set inside a single London house whose owner has fled a plague outbreak, this play follows a trio of confidence tricksters exploiting one gullible victim after another with fraudulent promises of alchemical and astrological power. It shows off Jonson's mastery of tightly unified neoclassical comic structure — one place, one day, one continuous action.
- **Court masques** (Jonson and Inigo Jones). Starting with *The Masque of Blackness* in 1605, Jonson's collaboration with Jones combined his own allegorical verse with Jones's increasingly elaborate scenic and mechanical stage design, producing spectacular, one-night-only court entertainments. It's worth knowing that this partnership eventually soured into a documented rivalry, each man convinced his own contribution — words or spectacle — mattered more.
- ***The Duchess of Malfi*** (Webster, 1612–13). A widowed Italian duchess secretly remarries her steward, Antonio, against her tyrannical brothers' wishes. What follows is imprisonment, psychological torment, and eventual murder, then an escalating spiral of further revenge and death. Critics celebrate the play for its unflinching, almost nihilistic treatment of cruelty and mortality, balanced against passages of real poetic and psychological intensity — especially in the Duchess's own defiant characterization.
- **Middleton's *Macbeth* adaptation**. Textual scholars, notably Gary Taylor, have found verbal parallels between the witch material in the surviving *Macbeth* text — the version printed in the 1623 First Folio you met in Chapter 6 — and Middleton's own tragicomedy *The Witch*. The likely explanation: Middleton reworked parts of Shakespeare's play, most visibly by inserting two witch-songs, for a later King's Men revival. This gives you a documented, concrete case of just how non-static "Shakespearean" printed texts actually were.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Jonson against Shakespeare.** Contemporaries, and later critics, have long contrasted Jonson's classically disciplined, satirically moralizing "comedy of humours" with Shakespeare's more fluid, romantically resolved comic mode. Jonson himself captured this contrast, and complicated it, in his own prefatory poem to Shakespeare's First Folio — "He was not of an age, but for all time" — a tribute that carries both genuine admiration and evident personal and professional rivalry.
- **Is Jacobean tragedy's darkness a deepening, or a decline?** Critics debate whether the violence-saturated tone of plays like *The Duchess of Malfi* and *The Changeling* represents a genuine artistic and moral deepening of the revenge tragedy tradition Kyd established back in Chapter 4, or instead reflects declining Jacobean court culture and an audience taste drifting toward sensationalism. How you answer this shapes how you value the whole Jacobean period relative to the Elizabethan era before it.
- **What does Middleton's collaborative practice tell us about Renaissance authorship?** Because Middleton worked so extensively in collaboration — with Rowley on *The Changeling*, and as an adapter of Shakespeare's *Macbeth* — his career has become central to how modern textual scholarship now understands Renaissance dramatic authorship: as something normally collaborative, not the work of a single isolated genius. You saw a version of this same shift in Chapter 6's discussion of Shakespeare's own collaboratively written plays.
- **Were the masques just lavish entertainment, or something more?** Scholars increasingly read the Jonson-Jones court masques as significant vehicles of Stuart political ideology, celebrating royal power, harmony, and order through allegorical spectacle — not merely disposable entertainment for one night. Ironically, that same one-night-only quality means most masques survive today only as printed texts, stripped of the visual and theatrical impact that was arguably their whole point.

## Chapter Summary

The Jacobean and Caroline periods extended English Renaissance drama beyond its Elizabethan foundations from Chapters 4 through 6, along two broadly contrasting paths. Ben Jonson's neoclassically disciplined, satirical "comedy of humours" — *Volpone*, *The Alchemist* — ran alongside his parallel career as the Stuart court's leading masque author, working with designer Inigo Jones, and together these made him England's first unofficial poet laureate. John Webster, meanwhile, pushed the revenge tragedy tradition Kyd had established into a darker, more psychologically and morally corrosive register in *The Duchess of Malfi* and *The White Devil*. Thomas Middleton's exceptionally wide generic range — city comedy, tragedy, and, as modern scholarship has established, adaptive work on Shakespeare's own *Macbeth* — shows you the collaborative, non-static textual culture that underlies much Renaissance drama. Together, these three dramatists kept English theatre vital through the reigns of James I and Charles I, until the Parliamentary closure of 1642, from Chapter 4, brought the era to its institutional close.

## Glossary

- **Caroline** — pertaining to the reign of Charles I (1625–1642).
- **City comedy** — a comic genre satirizing contemporary urban (chiefly London) mercantile and social life, exemplified by Middleton's *A Chaste Maid in Cheapside*.
- **Comedy of humours** — Jonson's satirical comic mode in which characters are dominated by a single exaggerated trait or "humour."
- **Jacobean** — pertaining to the reign of James I (1603–1625).
- **Masque** — an elaborate courtly entertainment combining poetry, music, dance, and spectacular staged design, associated above all with Jonson and Inigo Jones.
