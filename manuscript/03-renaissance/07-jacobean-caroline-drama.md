## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The **Jacobean** period (the reign of James I, 1603–25) and the **Caroline** period (the reign of Charles I, 1625–42, ending with the theatre closures discussed in Chapter 4) together comprise the second major phase of English Renaissance drama, following directly on the Elizabethan achievements of Marlowe and Shakespeare (Chapters 5–6). Dramatically, this period is generally characterized by a darkening of tone relative to Elizabethan comedy and tragedy — a turn toward moral corruption, court intrigue, and violent, often Italianate settings — alongside Ben Jonson's distinct neoclassical, satirical alternative and his parallel career supplying elaborate court masques for the Stuart monarchy, a form combining poetry, music, dance, and (via his collaborator Inigo Jones) increasingly spectacular stage design.

## Literary Movements

Ben Jonson developed the **comedy of humours**, a satirical dramatic mode in which characters are dominated by a single exaggerated trait or "humour" (drawing on the classical medical theory of four bodily fluids — blood, phlegm, black bile, yellow bile — determining temperament), deployed to expose greed, vanity, and folly with sharp moral and social satire, in explicit contrast to the more romantically resolved Shakespearean comic mode (Chapter 6). Alongside his public-theatre comedies, Jonson supplied the Stuart court with elaborate **masques** — courtly entertainments combining allegorical poetry, music, dance, and (via his long collaboration with the architect and designer Inigo Jones) increasingly sophisticated staged spectacle — establishing him as the era's dominant literary figure at court as well as in the public playhouse. Webster and Middleton, by contrast, worked chiefly within and extended the darker Jacobean dramatic modes: Webster in **revenge tragedy** descended from Kyd (Chapter 4), intensified into a distinctively bleak, morally corrosive vision of court corruption and violence; Middleton across an unusually wide generic range including **city comedy** (satirizing London's mercantile and social life) and domestic/political tragedy, while also working, as was common practice, as a collaborative playwright and adapter of others' work, including Shakespeare's.

## Author Profiles

**Ben Jonson** (1572–1637) — Educated at Westminster School before working briefly as a bricklayer (his stepfather's trade) and serving as a soldier in the Netherlands; established himself as a major dramatic and poetic voice second only to Shakespeare's in his own lifetime. In 1598 he killed a fellow actor, Gabriel Spencer, in a duel on Hoxton Fields, escaping execution by pleading "benefit of clergy" (demonstrating literacy) but being branded on the thumb. His major satirical comedies — *Every Man in His Humour* (1598), *Volpone* (1605–06), *The Alchemist* (1610), and *Bartholomew Fair* (1614) — established the comedy of humours as a distinct dramatic mode, while his long-running collaboration with Inigo Jones (beginning with *The Masque of Blackness*, 1605) made him the dominant supplier of Stuart court masques. Granted an annual royal pension in 1616, he is regarded as England's first (unofficial) poet laureate, and his later followers among younger poets were known as the "Tribe of Ben" or "Sons of Ben" (Chapter 9).

**John Webster** (c. 1578/80–c. 1632/34) — Little is documented of his life; probably London-born, son of a coach-maker. Author of two major Jacobean tragedies, *The White Devil* (performed and printed 1612, initially a poor stage failure) and *The Duchess of Malfi* (composed 1612–13, first performed at the intimate indoor Blackfriars theatre before a larger Globe audience in 1613–14), both revenge tragedies set in an intensely corrupt Italianate court world; widely regarded as the Jacobean dramatist with the single darkest vision of human nature among his contemporaries.

**Thomas Middleton** (1580–1627) — Prolific London-born playwright working across an unusually wide range of genres and frequently in collaboration with other dramatists (notably William Rowley); author of the city comedy *A Chaste Maid in Cheapside* (c. 1613) and the tragedies *Women Beware Women* (c. 1621) and, with Rowley, *The Changeling* (1622). Modern textual scholarship has also identified Middleton as the likely adapter responsible for revisions to Shakespeare's *Macbeth* (probably for a King's Men revival after Shakespeare's retirement or death), incorporating witch-songs from his own tragicomedy *The Witch* — a striking instance of the collaborative and adaptive textual culture that also affected several plays in the Shakespeare canon (Chapter 6).

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Volpone* (or, *The Fox*)** (Jonson, 1605–06) — A savage satire in which the wealthy, childless Venetian Volpone feigns terminal illness to extract gifts from greedy legacy-hunters, aided by his parasite-servant Mosca; widely regarded as Jonson's finest and most enduringly performed play, exemplifying the comedy of humours' moral and satirical bite.
- ***The Alchemist*** (Jonson, 1610) — Set within a single London house during a period of plague-driven absence of its owner, the play follows a trio of confidence tricksters exploiting a stream of gullible victims through fraudulent alchemical and astrological promises, demonstrating Jonson's mastery of tightly unified (in time, place, and action) neoclassical comic structure.
- **Court masques** (Jonson and Inigo Jones) — Beginning with *The Masque of Blackness* (1605), Jonson's collaboration with Jones combined his own allegorical verse with Jones's increasingly elaborate scenic and mechanical stage design, producing spectacular, ephemeral court entertainments that also became a site of documented creative rivalry between poet and designer over which contribution mattered more.
- ***The Duchess of Malfi*** (Webster, 1612–13) — Dramatizes the secret remarriage of a widowed Italian duchess to her steward Antonio, against her tyrannical brothers' wishes, leading to her imprisonment, psychological torment, and eventual murder, followed by an escalating spiral of further revenge and death; celebrated for its unflinching, almost nihilistic treatment of cruelty and mortality alongside passages of striking poetic and psychological intensity, particularly in the Duchess's own defiant characterization.
- **Middleton's *Macbeth* adaptation** — Textual scholars (notably Gary Taylor) have identified verbal parallels between the Hecate/witch material in the surviving *Macbeth* text (as printed in the 1623 First Folio, Chapter 6) and Middleton's own tragicomedy *The Witch*, suggesting Middleton reworked portions of Shakespeare's play — most visibly by inserting two witch-songs — for a King's Men revival, providing a documented case study in the collaborative and non-static nature of "Shakespearean" printed texts.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Jonson versus Shakespeare.** Contemporaries and later critics have long contrasted Jonson's classically disciplined, satirically moralizing "comedy of humours" with Shakespeare's more fluid, romantically resolved comic mode — a contrast Jonson himself, in his prefatory poem to the First Folio ("He was not of an age, but for all time"), both acknowledged and complicated through evident personal and professional rivalry alongside genuine admiration.
- **The "decadence" question in Jacobean tragedy.** Critics have debated whether the darkened, violence-saturated tone of plays like *The Duchess of Malfi* and *The Changeling* represents a genuine artistic and moral deepening of the revenge tragedy tradition established by Kyd (Chapter 4), or a symptom of declining Jacobean court culture and audience taste for sensationalism — a debate with implications for how the entire Jacobean dramatic period should be periodized and valued relative to the preceding Elizabethan era.
- **Middleton's collaborative and adaptive practice.** Because Middleton worked so extensively in collaboration (with Rowley on *The Changeling*, and as adapter of Shakespeare's *Macbeth*), his case has become central to modern textual scholarship's broader reassessment of single, unified authorship as the normal condition of Renaissance dramatic text production — a scholarly shift also visible in Chapter 6's discussion of Shakespeare's own collaborative plays.
- **The masque as ephemeral art and political statement.** Scholars increasingly read the Jonson-Jones court masques not merely as lavish but disposable entertainment but as significant vehicles of Stuart political ideology (celebrating royal power, harmony, and order through allegorical spectacle), even as the genre's material ephemerality (elaborate sets and costumes built for a single performance) means most masques survive only as printed texts, largely stripped of their original visual and theatrical impact.

## Chapter Summary

The Jacobean and Caroline periods extended English Renaissance drama beyond its Elizabethan foundations (Chapters 4–6) along two broadly contrasting paths: Ben Jonson's neoclassically disciplined, satirical "comedy of humours" (*Volpone*, *The Alchemist*) and his parallel career, with designer Inigo Jones, as the Stuart court's leading masque author, made him England's first unofficial poet laureate; while John Webster's *The Duchess of Malfi* and *The White Devil* pushed the revenge tragedy tradition established by Kyd into a darker, more psychologically and morally corrosive register. Thomas Middleton's exceptionally wide generic range — city comedy, tragedy, and, as modern scholarship has established, adaptive work on Shakespeare's own *Macbeth* — exemplifies the collaborative, non-static textual culture underlying much Renaissance drama. Together these three dramatists' achievements sustained English theatre's vitality through the reigns of James I and Charles I until the Parliamentary closure of 1642 (Chapter 4) brought the era to its institutional close.

## Glossary

- **Caroline** — pertaining to the reign of Charles I (1625–1642).
- **City comedy** — a comic genre satirizing contemporary urban (chiefly London) mercantile and social life, exemplified by Middleton's *A Chaste Maid in Cheapside*.
- **Comedy of humours** — Jonson's satirical comic mode in which characters are dominated by a single exaggerated trait or "humour."
- **Jacobean** — pertaining to the reign of James I (1603–1625).
- **Masque** — an elaborate courtly entertainment combining poetry, music, dance, and spectacular staged design, associated above all with Jonson and Inigo Jones.
