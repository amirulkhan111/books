## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Welcome to Part III. Part II ended with William Caxton's printing press and the Battle of Bosworth Field — a technological revolution and a political one, arriving almost together around 1485. This chapter opens on the far side of both changes, and shows you what they made possible.

Two overlapping upheavals define the opening of this new period. The first is intellectual: a movement called Christian humanism arrives and takes root in England, carried by a small circle of Oxford- and London-based scholars in close contact with Europe's wider community of scholars — above all with one towering figure, Erasmus of Rotterdam. The second is institutional and political: the English Reformation, triggered directly by Henry VIII's break with Rome over his marriage, but drawing on the very same scholarly methods humanism had already been refining — especially a renewed, careful attention to the original biblical languages, rather than relying on inherited Latin translations and commentary.

Neither of these upheavals could have moved as fast as they did without one enabling technology: the printing press Caxton brought to England, discussed in Part II, Chapter 9, and rapidly expanded by his successors. Printed texts could circulate at a speed and scale manuscript culture, which you spent all of Parts I and II inside, simply never allowed.

## Literary Movements

Start with the term **Christian humanism**, sometimes called Northern humanism to distinguish it from its Italian predecessor. It took the classical philological and rhetorical methods Renaissance Italy had already developed — close, careful study of ancient texts in their original languages — and pointed them at a distinctly religious goal: applying that same rigorous scholarship, above all direct study of Greek and Hebrew biblical and patristic sources, to renewing Christian life and institutions, rather than simply relying on the Latin Vulgate and medieval commentary tradition.

In England, this movement is embodied by a group historians call the "Oxford Reformers" — Grocyn, Linacre, Colet, and their close associate Thomas More — working in constant correspondence with their great continental friend, Erasmus. Between them, they produced both satirical and utopian literature (Erasmus's *Folly*, More's *Utopia*) and a new emphasis on translating Scripture into the vernacular, which fed directly into the Reformation itself. The resulting flood of English religious prose — biblical translation, polemical tracts, sermons — combined with the continued growth of the print trade Caxton started, built much of the textual infrastructure, and the reading public, that the poetry and drama of the rest of Part III would depend on.

## Author Profiles

**William Grocyn** (c. 1446–1519) and **Thomas Linacre** (c. 1460–1524) studied Greek together in Florence, around 1488–90, under Angelo Poliziano and Demetrios Chalkokondyles. When they returned to England, Grocyn delivered Oxford's first Greek lectures ever given by an Englishman, and both men became central figures in a humanist circle that included the younger John Colet and Thomas More — establishing Greek studies as a permanent feature of English higher education for the first time.

**John Colet** (1467–1519) was a theologian and educational reformer, the son of a prosperous London merchant, educated at Oxford and then in France and Italy. He became Dean of St Paul's Cathedral in 1504, and in 1509 he founded St Paul's School, London — a landmark institution of humanist education built around direct study of classical and Christian texts.

**Desiderius Erasmus** (c. 1466–1536), sometimes called the "Prince of Humanists," was a Dutch scholar whose repeated visits to England from 1499 onward brought him into close friendship with both Colet and More. You already know two of his major achievements from above: *In Praise of Folly*, which he composed in a single week while staying at More's house, and his 1516 scholarly edition of the Greek New Testament, which became foundational for the biblical humanism and translation work that followed, Tyndale's included.

**Thomas More** (1478–1535) was an English lawyer, statesman, and humanist, educated at Oxford, where he studied Greek with Linacre. His *Utopia* (1516) established the "utopia" genre, as you read above. More rose all the way to Lord Chancellor of England under Henry VIII — but he refused to accept the Act of Supremacy and the annulment of Henry's marriage to Catherine of Aragon, and for that refusal he was tried for treason and beheaded on 6 July 1535.

**William Tyndale** (c. 1494–1536) was an English biblical scholar and linguist who produced the first English New Testament translated directly from Greek, and, for the Pentateuch, from Hebrew, rather than from the Latin Vulgate — printed at Worms in 1526. His distinctive vocabulary and rhythm passed substantially into later English Bibles, including the 1611 King James Version, where scholars estimate his wording survives in roughly 76 to 83 percent of some passages. Convicted of heresy, he was executed — strangled, then burned — in the Low Countries in 1536.

**Myles Coverdale** (1488–1569), an English clergyman and translator, published the first complete printed English Bible in 1535, drawing on Tyndale's earlier work, the Latin Vulgate, and Martin Luther's German Bible. He was later involved in preparing the officially authorized Great Bible of 1539 as well.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***In Praise of Folly*** (Erasmus, 1509/1511). Picture a satirical speech, delivered in the voice of Folly herself, personified and speaking in her own defence. She mocks the vanity and corruption of contemporary institutions — pointedly including the Church — while the piece quietly pivots toward a genuinely orthodox religious purpose underneath the comedy. Erasmus wrote it at More's house and dedicated it to him — the Latin title, *Moriae Encomium*, is a pun on More's own name. It's a perfect example of humanism's signature combination: classical rhetorical wit paired with real religious seriousness.
- ***Utopia*** (More, 1516). This Latin work comes in two parts: first a dialogue on the social and political problems of contemporary England, then a description of the imaginary island of Utopia — a name that literally means "No-place." Utopia's rationally ordered society works as both an implicit critique of European institutions and a genuinely ambiguous model, one More never quite lets you take entirely at face value. Structurally, it owes something to Erasmus's *Folly*. Its lasting legacy is enormous: it gave the world the word "utopia" as a literary genre, and it remains a foundational text of humanist political thought.
- **Tyndale's New Testament** (1526). This is the first English biblical translation made directly from the Greek text, rather than from the Latin Vulgate everyone had relied on before. Its plain, rhythmically powerful vernacular prose shaped every English Bible translation that followed, and more broadly shaped the development of English prose style itself — which is why scholars rank Tyndale, alongside Chaucer from Part II, Chapter 3, among the single most consequential influences on English's literary register.
- **Coverdale's Bible (1535) and the Great Bible (1539)**. Coverdale's 1535 translation leaned heavily on earlier work — Tyndale's, the Vulgate, Luther's German Bible — but it still holds a real distinction: it's the first complete printed English Bible. The 1539 Great Bible, built substantially on Tyndale's and Coverdale's phrasing, went a step further: it became the first English Bible officially authorized for use in parish churches, putting vernacular Scripture at the centre of English religious and literary life on an institutional scale for the first time.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was the Reformation a natural extension of humanism, or a real break from it?** Scholars debate this closely. The Oxford Reformers themselves generally wanted reform within the existing Church, not a split from it — and Erasmus notably stayed a Catholic critic rather than joining the Lutheran or English Reformations, even though his own philological methods and his Greek New Testament directly made reformist Bible translation possible. Hold both facts together: humanism enabled the Reformation without every humanist endorsing it.
- **Did More actually believe in Utopia?** Critics still argue over this. Was Utopian society meant as a genuine ideal? An ironic critique exposing how cold and impractical purely rational social planning can be? Or a deliberately unresolved provocation, designed to make readers argue exactly the way we still do? The debate gets more interesting once you remember More's later career: as Lord Chancellor, his own political and religious positions grew considerably more conservative and orthodox than *Utopia* might suggest.
- **Why does Tyndale matter so much to the history of English?** So much of Tyndale's phrasing survived, via Coverdale and the Great Bible, all the way into the King James Bible of 1611 (Chapter 10) that scholars rank him among the single most influential shapers of English literary prose — on a scale genuinely comparable to Chaucer's in Part II. It's worth sitting with what that means: Tyndale shaped the language mainly through translation, not original composition, which is a useful reminder that translation can be just as powerful a literary act as writing something wholly new.
- **How much did print culture actually drive the Reformation?** Historians generally treat the rapid multiplication of printed Bibles, tracts, and humanist texts, following on from Caxton in Part II, Chapter 9, as a genuine precondition for the Reformation's speed and scale in England — a pattern that echoes print's role in the wider European Reformation too. By this period, the question of "the Bible in English" had become inseparable from questions of print access, censorship, and royal permission.

## Chapter Summary

English Christian humanism, carried by Grocyn, Linacre, Colet, and Thomas More, and closely tied to their continental friend Erasmus, applied rigorous classical scholarship to religious and educational renewal, producing landmark works like *In Praise of Folly* and *Utopia*. That scholarship fed directly into the far more institutionally decisive English Reformation, triggered by Henry VIII's 1534 Act of Supremacy and reaching its most visible consequence in the 1536–41 dissolution of the monasteries. William Tyndale's pioneering English New Testament (1526) cost him his life in 1536, but supplied much of the language later absorbed into the officially authorized Great Bible (1539) and, eventually, the King James Bible. None of this could have moved at the speed it did without the print culture Caxton started, discussed in Part II, Chapter 9. That combination — humanist scholarship, reformist urgency, and printed dissemination — sets the institutional, linguistic, and religious stage for the poetry and drama of the sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries you'll meet in the rest of Part III.

## Glossary

- **Christian/Northern humanism** — a Renaissance intellectual movement applying classical philological method to biblical and patristic texts in service of religious and educational renewal.
- **Dissolution of the monasteries** — the 1536–41 process by which Henry VIII disbanded England's monastic institutions and seized their property.
- **Royal Supremacy** — the doctrine, established by the 1534 Act of Supremacy, that the English monarch, not the Pope, is supreme head of the Church of England.
- **Vulgate** — the Latin translation of the Bible, traditionally attributed to St Jerome, that was the standard biblical text of the medieval Western Church.
