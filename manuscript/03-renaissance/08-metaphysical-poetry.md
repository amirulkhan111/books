# Chapter 8: Metaphysical Poetry — Donne, Herbert, Marvell, Vaughan

*Part III: The Renaissance / Early Modern Period, 1500–1660*

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Define the "metaphysical conceit" and explain Samuel Johnson's later, largely critical coinage of the term "metaphysical poets."
2. Analyze John Donne's poetic career across its secular *Songs and Sonnets* and later devotional *Holy Sonnets*, in relation to his conversion and ordination.
3. Discuss George Herbert's *The Temple* and its distinctive devotional and formal techniques, including pattern poetry.
4. Situate Andrew Marvell's political and lyric poetry, including his career alongside Milton, within the metaphysical tradition.
5. Explain Henry Vaughan's relationship to Herbert's influence in *Silex Scintillans*.
6. Assess T. S. Eliot's 1921 essay as the decisive twentieth-century revival of critical interest in metaphysical poetry.

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The four poets in this chapter span the turbulent final decades of this Part's timeframe: Donne's career runs through the Elizabethan and Jacobean periods, Herbert's through Caroline devotional retirement, and Marvell's straight into the mid-century political and religious crises — including active government service alongside Milton — that led to the theatre closures and civil wars you met in Chapter 4. Don't expect a tight-knit literary circle, though. Only Vaughan explicitly modelled himself on another poet in this group, Herbert. What actually unites these four is a shared formal and intellectual style — one that later critics named, and at first criticized rather than praised, only in the following century.

## Literary Movements

The single feature that defines **metaphysical poetry** is the **metaphysical conceit**: an elaborate, deliberately startling comparison that yokes together ideas you wouldn't normally put in the same sentence — often reaching for scientific, philosophical, or technical vocabulary (compasses, alchemy, cartography) to explore love, mortality, or religious devotion through argumentative, often paradoxical wit.

Here's something worth knowing before you use the label "metaphysical poet" yourself: it's not a term these poets ever applied to themselves. Samuel Johnson coined it in 1781, in his *Life of Cowley*, naming "a race of writers that may be termed the metaphysical poets" — and he meant it largely as criticism, accusing them of over-intellectualized, unmusical difficulty. The label stuck as an insult for well over a century. It took T. S. Eliot's 1921 essay "The Metaphysical Poets," framed as a review of an anthology of the tradition, to decisively flip the verdict. Eliot argued Donne and his contemporaries possessed a "unified sensibility" — thought and feeling working as one — that later English poetry, in his own controversial phrase, suffered a "dissociation of sensibility" and lost.

## Author Profiles

**John Donne** (1572–1631) was born into a Roman Catholic family in London — his mother descended from Thomas More's sister, whom you met back in Chapter 1 — and abandoned Catholicism for the Church of England in the 1590s under mounting religious and family pressure. His secret 1601 marriage to Anne More effectively ended his secular career ambitions and briefly landed him in prison; the couple had twelve children in sixteen years before Anne died in 1617. His earlier poems, mostly circulated only in manuscript, treat erotic and romantic love with the argumentative wit and physical, almost spiritual conceits you saw above; his later *Holy Sonnets*, begun by 1609 and continuing after his wife's death, turn that same intellectual intensity toward mortality, sin, and divine grace. Ordained an Anglican priest in 1615, at James I's own insistence, Donne became Dean of St Paul's Cathedral in 1621, where his sermons and devotional prose — including *Devotions upon Emergent Occasions* (1624), the source of the famous lines "no man is an island" and "for whom the bell tolls" — became just as celebrated as his poetry.

**George Herbert** (1593–1633) had a distinguished early career, including a term as Public Orator of Cambridge University, before he was ordained and, in 1630, became rector of the rural parish of Bemerton, near Salisbury. There, he revised and completed the poetry collection *The Temple: Sacred Poems and Private Ejaculations*, published only after his death, in 1633. His devotional lyrics combine plain, direct language with real formal ingenuity — you already met his "pattern" poem "Easter Wings" above, and his poem "The Collar" dramatizes a speaker's rebellion against faith, with a title that puns on both a clerical collar and "choler," meaning anger.

**Andrew Marvell** (1621–1678) was a poet and, later, a politician. He composed much of his celebrated lyric poetry, including "To His Coy Mistress," probably around 1650–52, while living at Nun Appleton, the Yorkshire estate of the retired parliamentary general Thomas Fairfax. His "An Horatian Ode upon Cromwell's Return from Ireland" (1650) offers an unusually balanced, formally classical meditation on Cromwell's rise — one that acknowledges both Cromwell's political ruthlessness and Charles I's own dignified death. In 1657, Marvell joined the blind John Milton as Latin Secretary to Cromwell's Council of State, and he later used his political standing to help secure Milton's release from imprisonment at the Restoration, a story you'll read in full in Chapter 11. Most of Marvell's poetry remained unpublished until after his death, in 1681.

**Henry Vaughan** (1621–1695) was a Welsh poet and physician who openly credited reading Herbert's devotional poetry with turning him away from "idle verse" — his own earlier, more secular writing — and toward religious composition, addressing Herbert directly as his poetic "master." His major work, *Silex Scintillans* ("The Flashing Flint," published in two parts, 1650 and 1655), extends Herbert's devotional lyric mode with the distinctive mystical, nature-oriented sensibility you saw above in "The Retreat."

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"The Flea" and "The Sun Rising"** (Donne, *Songs and Sonnets*). These poems show Donne's characteristic method at its most audacious: a flea's shared bite of two lovers' blood becomes proof of their union; the rising sun gets scolded for interrupting lovers in bed. Both conceits are pushed with argumentative, dramatic immediacy and colloquial directness, a world away from the smoother, more conventionally Petrarchan lyric of the Elizabethan sonneteers you met in Chapter 3.
- **"Death, be not proud" and "Batter my heart"** (Donne, *Holy Sonnets*). Here Donne turns the same conceited, argumentative intensity from his secular love poems toward mortality and divine grace. "Death, be not proud" directly challenges and shrinks Death through paradox — "Death, thou shalt die." "Batter my heart" pleads for violent divine intervention — "Batter my heart, three-personed God" — in language that's startlingly physical, even erotically charged.
- ***The Temple*** (Herbert, 1633). This collection is carefully organized almost like architecture — its very title evokes a physical church — and it moves through a range of devotional lyric forms. "Easter Wings" is visually shaped on the page to resemble wings, a striking example of the period's interest in "pattern poetry." "The Collar" dramatizes a speaker's rebellion against religious constraint, then resolves it — notice the title's pun, working as both a clerical collar and "choler," meaning anger.
- **"To His Coy Mistress"** (Marvell). This carpe diem poem is structured like a formal logical argument, in three parts: "Had we but world enough and time . . . But at my back I always hear . . . Now therefore . . ." It combines seductive urgency with strikingly grim memento-mori imagery — worms, dust, the grave — a fusion of eros and mortality typical of metaphysical wit at its sharpest.
- ***Silex Scintillans*** (Vaughan, 1650, 1655). Vaughan takes Herbert's devotional mode and adds his own distinctive emphasis: nature as a vehicle for spiritual insight, and a recurring, mystically tinged longing for childhood innocence and a purity that existed before birth. You can hear this clearly in "The Retreat," where the speaker longs to return to a state "before I understood this place."

## Critical Perspectives

- **How did Johnson's hostility toward these poets get reversed?** Samuel Johnson's 1781 coinage of "metaphysical poets" was genuinely disapproving — he accused the group of forced, unmusical "wit" that displayed learning rather than achieving real poetry — and that negative judgment dominated critical opinion for well over a century. T. S. Eliot's 1921 essay reversed the verdict completely, arguing that the metaphysicals' capacity to fuse "thought" and "feeling" represented a lost ideal that modern poetry should strive to recover.
- **What is Eliot's "dissociation of sensibility," and should you trust it?** Eliot's influential thesis holds that a "dissociation of sensibility" set in after the metaphysical period, splitting intellect from emotion in the English poetry that followed. This is a sweeping historical claim, and later scholars — including, eventually, Eliot himself — have qualified and debated it extensively. Even so, it remains the single most influential framework for understanding how modern readers came to value this chapter's poets.
- **How should we relate Donne's two poetic voices?** Critics continue to debate how the erotic, often cynical wit of Donne's *Songs and Sonnets* relates to the penitential intensity of his later *Holy Sonnets* and sermons. Is this one coherent sensibility, simply redirected by conversion and ordination? Or does it reveal a deeper, unresolved tension between secular and sacred impulses running through his whole career? Both readings have real support.
- **Is Herbert's formal experimentation decoration, or devotion itself?** Scholars generally read *The Temple*'s careful structural organization, and Herbert's recurring formal experiments — pattern poems, dialogue structures — as integral to its devotional purpose, not decorative extras. For Herbert, form itself becomes an act of worship and spiritual discipline.
- **Whose side was Marvell really on?** "An Horatian Ode"'s famously balanced treatment of both Cromwell and the executed Charles I has generated real debate over where Marvell's own sympathies actually lay, and what stance the poem ultimately takes. That very ambiguity is a fitting emblem for the period itself — political allegiances during the civil wars and Interregnum were often just as fraught and shifting.

## Chapter Summary

The poets conventionally grouped together as "metaphysical" — Donne, Herbert, Marvell, and Vaughan — share a distinctive style built on the elaborate, intellectually argumentative "conceit." Samuel Johnson first named the group, largely disapprovingly, in 1781, and it took T. S. Eliot's influential 1921 essay to decisively rehabilitate their reputation. Donne's own career moved from witty, physically audacious secular love poetry to an equally intense religious devotion following his ordination and his deanship at St Paul's. Herbert's posthumously published *The Temple* combined plain devotional diction with real formal ingenuity, including shaped "pattern" poems. Marvell's lyric and political poetry, written against the backdrop of civil war and Commonwealth, and alongside his colleague Milton as Latin Secretary, combined seductive wit with genuine political complexity. And Vaughan explicitly extended Herbert's devotional mode into a more mystically tinged register of his own. Their shared formal signature — argumentative wit fusing intellect and feeling — sets up this book's later treatment of Milton in Chapters 11 and 12, and the seventeenth century's wider religious and political crisis.

## Glossary

- **Conceit (metaphysical)** — an elaborate, intellectually startling comparison yoking together disparate ideas, the defining formal feature of metaphysical poetry.
- **Dissociation of sensibility** — T. S. Eliot's 1921 term for a supposed later split between thought and feeling in English poetry, following the metaphysical period.
- **Metaphysical poets** — a term coined (largely disapprovingly) by Samuel Johnson in 1781 for Donne, Herbert, Marvell, Vaughan, and related poets.
- **Pattern poem** — a poem whose visual layout on the page forms a shape relevant to its content, as in Herbert's "Easter Wings."
