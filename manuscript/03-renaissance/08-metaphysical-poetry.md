# Chapter 8: Metaphysical Poetry — Donne, Herbert, Marvell, Vaughan

*Part III: The Renaissance / Early Modern Period, 1500–1660*

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Define the "metaphysical conceit" and explain Samuel Johnson's later, largely critical coinage of the term "metaphysical poets."
2. Analyze John Donne's poetic career across its secular *Songs and Sonnets* and later devotional *Holy Sonnets*, in relation to his conversion and ordination.
3. Discuss George Herbert's *The Temple* and its distinctive devotional and formal techniques, including pattern poetry.
4. Situate Andrew Marvell's political and lyric poetry, including his career alongside Milton, within the metaphysical tradition.
5. Explain Henry Vaughan's relationship to Herbert's influence in *Silex Scintillans*.
6. Assess T. S. Eliot's 1921 essay as the decisive twentieth-century revival of critical interest in metaphysical poetry.

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The poets treated in this chapter wrote across the turbulent final decades of this Part's timeframe — from Donne's Elizabethan and Jacobean career through Herbert's Caroline devotional retirement, to Marvell's active participation in the mid-century political and religious crises (Commonwealth service, alongside Milton, as Latin Secretary) that would culminate in the theatre closures and civil wars discussed in Chapter 4. Their poetry is united less by direct personal association (though Vaughan explicitly modelled himself on Herbert) than by a shared formal and intellectual style, retrospectively named and largely first negatively characterized only in the following century.

## Literary Movements

**Metaphysical poetry** is characterized by the **metaphysical conceit** — an elaborate, intellectually startling comparison yoking together disparate or "heterogeneous" ideas (in Johnson's later, disapproving phrase), often drawing on scientific, philosophical, or technical vocabulary (compasses, alchemy, cartography) to explore love, mortality, and religious devotion with argumentative, often paradoxical wit. The label itself is a later critical construction: Samuel Johnson, in his 1781 *Life of Cowley*, first named "a race of writers that may be termed the metaphysical poets," intending the term largely as criticism of their perceived over-intellectualized, unmusical difficulty rather than as praise. It was T. S. Eliot's 1921 essay "The Metaphysical Poets" — framed as a review of H. J. C. Grierson's anthology of the tradition — that decisively rehabilitated the group's critical reputation, proposing that Donne and his contemporaries possessed a "unified sensibility" (integrating thought and feeling) that later English poetry, in Eliot's controversial formulation, suffered a "dissociation of sensibility" and lost.

## Author Profiles

**John Donne** (1572–1631) — Born to a Roman Catholic family in London (his mother a descendant of Thomas More's sister, Part III Chapter 1), Donne abandoned Catholicism for the Church of England in the 1590s under mounting religious and family pressure. His secret 1601 marriage to Anne More effectively ended his secular career ambitions and led to brief imprisonment; the couple had twelve children in sixteen years before Anne's death in 1617. Donne's earlier, largely manuscript-circulated *Songs and Sonnets* (composed variously from the 1590s to c. 1617) treat erotic and romantic love with characteristic argumentative wit and physical/spiritual conceits; his later *Holy Sonnets* (begun by 1609, continuing after his wife's death) turn the same intellectual intensity toward mortality, sin, and divine grace. Ordained an Anglican priest in 1615 at James I's insistence, Donne became Dean of St Paul's Cathedral in 1621, where his sermons and devotional prose (including *Devotions upon Emergent Occasions*, 1624, source of the famous "no man is an island" and "for whom the bell tolls" passages) achieved comparable renown to his poetry.

**George Herbert** (1593–1633) — English poet, orator, and priest; after a distinguished early career (including service as Public Orator of Cambridge University), Herbert was ordained and, in 1630, became rector of the rural parish of Bemerton, near Salisbury, where he revised and completed the poetry collection *The Temple: Sacred Poems and Private Ejaculations*, published posthumously in 1633. Herbert's devotional lyrics combine plain, direct diction with formal ingenuity, including "pattern" or shaped poems (*Easter Wings*, its lines visually arranged to resemble wings) and psychologically dramatic negotiations with faith and rebellion (*The Collar*, whose title puns on clerical collar and "choler"/anger).

**Andrew Marvell** (1621–1678) — Poet and politician; composed much of his celebrated lyric poetry, including "To His Coy Mistress" (a carpe diem seduction poem structured as a formal logical argument) probably around 1650–52 while resident at Nun Appleton, the Yorkshire estate of the retired parliamentary general Thomas Fairfax. His "An Horatian Ode upon Cromwell's Return from Ireland" (1650) offers an unusually balanced, formally classical meditation on Cromwell's rise that acknowledges both his political ruthlessness and Charles I's dignified death. In 1657 Marvell joined the blind John Milton as Latin Secretary to Cromwell's Council of State, later using his political influence to help secure Milton's release from imprisonment at the Restoration (Chapter 11); most of Marvell's poetry remained unpublished until after his death (1681).

**Henry Vaughan** (1621–1695) — Welsh poet and physician; explicitly credited his reading of Herbert's devotional poetry with turning him from "idle verse" (his own earlier, more secular writing) toward religious composition, addressing Herbert directly as his poetic "master." His major work, *Silex Scintillans* ("The Flashing Flint," Parts I and II, 1650 and 1655), extends Herbert's devotional lyric mode with a distinctive mystical and nature-oriented sensibility, as in his celebrated poem "The Retreat."

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"The Flea," "The Sun Rising"** (Donne, *Songs and Sonnets*) — Exemplify Donne's characteristic method: an outrageous, logically extended conceit (a flea's shared bite of two lovers' blood as proof of their union; the rising sun rebuked for interrupting lovers in bed) deployed with argumentative, dramatic immediacy and colloquial directness markedly different from the smoother, more conventionally Petrarchan lyric of Elizabethan sonneteers (Chapter 3).
- **"Death, be not proud" and "Batter my heart"** (Donne, *Holy Sonnets*) — Apply the same conceited, argumentative intensity of the secular love poems to confrontations with mortality and divine grace, the former directly challenging and diminishing Death through paradox ("Death, thou shalt die"), the latter pleading for violent divine intervention ("Batter my heart, three-personed God") in a startlingly physical, even erotically inflected register.
- ***The Temple*** (Herbert, 1633) — A carefully architecturally organized collection (its very title evoking a physical church structure) moving through varied devotional lyric forms; "Easter Wings," visually shaped on the page to resemble wings, exemplifies the period's interest in "pattern poetry," while "The Collar" dramatizes, then resolves, a speaker's rebellious resistance to religious constraint.
- **"To His Coy Mistress"** (Marvell) — A carpe diem poem structured as a three-part logical syllogism ("Had we but world enough and time . . . But at my back I always hear . . . Now therefore . . ."), combining seductive urgency with strikingly grim, memento-mori imagery (worms, dust, the grave) characteristic of metaphysical wit's fusion of eros and mortality.
- ***Silex Scintillans*** (Vaughan, 1650, 1655) — Extends Herbert's devotional mode with a distinctive emphasis on nature as a vehicle for spiritual insight and a recurring, mystically inflected longing for childhood innocence and pre-existent purity, as in "The Retreat"'s desire to return to a state "before I understood this place."

## Critical Perspectives

- **Johnson's critical hostility and its reversal.** Samuel Johnson's 1781 coinage of "metaphysical poets" was substantially disapproving — accusing the group of forced, unmusical "wit" that merely displayed learning rather than achieving true poetry — and this negative assessment dominated critical opinion for well over a century until T. S. Eliot's 1921 essay reversed the verdict, arguing that the metaphysicals' capacity to fuse "thought" and "feeling" represented a lost ideal for which modern poetry should strive.
- **Eliot's "dissociation of sensibility."** Eliot's influential, though later self-qualified, thesis holds that a "dissociation of sensibility" set in after the metaphysical period, separating intellect from emotion in subsequent English poetry — a sweeping historical claim that has been extensively debated and qualified by later scholars, but which remains the single most influential critical framework for understanding this chapter's poets' modern reputation.
- **Donne's biographical duality.** Critics continue to debate how to relate the erotic, often cynical wit of Donne's *Songs and Sonnets* to the penitential intensity of his later *Holy Sonnets* and sermons — whether as a coherent single sensibility redirected by conversion and ordination, or as evidence of a more fundamental, unresolved tension between secular and sacred impulses throughout his career.
- **Herbert's architectural and formal design.** Scholars read *The Temple*'s careful structural organization and Herbert's recurring formal experimentation (pattern poems, dialogic structures) as integral to, rather than merely decorative additions to, its devotional purpose — form itself becoming an act of worship and intellectual/spiritual discipline.
- **Marvell's political ambiguity.** "An Horatian Ode"'s famously balanced treatment of both Cromwell and the executed Charles I has generated extensive debate over Marvell's own political sympathies and the poem's ultimate stance — a debate emblematic of the period's genuinely fraught and shifting political allegiances during the civil wars and Interregnum.

## Chapter Summary

The poets conventionally grouped as "metaphysical" — Donne, Herbert, Marvell, and Vaughan — share a distinctive style built on the elaborate, intellectually argumentative "conceit," first named (largely disapprovingly) by Samuel Johnson in 1781 and only decisively rehabilitated by T. S. Eliot's influential 1921 essay. Donne's career moved from witty, physically audacious secular love poetry to an equally intense religious devotion following his ordination and deanship at St Paul's; Herbert's posthumously published *The Temple* combined plain devotional diction with formal ingenuity, including shaped "pattern" poems; Marvell's lyric and political poetry, composed against the backdrop of civil war and Commonwealth (and alongside his colleague Milton as Latin Secretary), combined seductive wit with genuine political complexity; and Vaughan explicitly extended Herbert's devotional mode into a more mystically inflected register. Their shared formal signature — argumentative wit fusing intellect and feeling — anticipates this book's later treatment of Milton (Chapters 11–12) and the seventeenth century's broader religious and political crisis.

## Glossary

- **Conceit (metaphysical)** — an elaborate, intellectually startling comparison yoking together disparate ideas, the defining formal feature of metaphysical poetry.
- **Dissociation of sensibility** — T. S. Eliot's 1921 term for a supposed later split between thought and feeling in English poetry, following the metaphysical period.
- **Metaphysical poets** — a term coined (largely disapprovingly) by Samuel Johnson in 1781 for Donne, Herbert, Marvell, Vaughan, and related poets.
- **Pattern poem** — a poem whose visual layout on the page forms a shape relevant to its content, as in Herbert's "Easter Wings."
