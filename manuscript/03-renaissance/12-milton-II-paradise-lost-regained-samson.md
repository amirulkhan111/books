## Historical and Socio-Political Context

This is the final chapter of Part III, and it belongs to the poet you already met in Chapter 11 — now blind, politically disgraced, and only narrowly escaped from execution. Milton composed his three major late poems — *Paradise Lost* (1667, expanded 1674), *Paradise Regained*, and *Samson Agonistes* (both 1671) — after everything Chapter 11 described: blindness since 1652, disgrace and brief imprisonment at the 1660 Restoration. He produced his greatest poetry, in other words, from a position of profound private and public loss.

You might notice something odd about the dates: this chapter's poems were all published after 1660, technically past Part III's own 1500–1660 frame. So why include them here rather than in Part IV? Because Milton's intellectual formation, his poetic technique, and his theological concerns all belong entirely to the world you've traced across this Part — the humanist and biblical scholarship of Chapter 1, the sonnet and blank verse traditions of Chapters 2 and 5, and his own earlier career from Chapter 11. This book, like most standard literary histories, treats Milton as the culminating figure of English Renaissance literature, rather than reclassifying his late work into the period that follows.

## Literary Movements

*Paradise Lost* is the culminating achievement of the **English epic** tradition within this Part. It draws on classical epic convention — invoking the muse, starting partway through the action (*in medias res*), extended epic similes — established by Homer and Virgil, while choosing **blank verse** as its narrative medium. Remember where that form came from: Surrey introduced it in Chapter 2, and Marlowe and Shakespeare mastered it dramatically in Chapters 5 and 6. Milton defends the choice explicitly in his prefatory note, calling rhyme "the invention of a barbarous age" and asserting blank verse as the metrically and philosophically fitting vehicle for serious epic ambition.

*Paradise Regained* takes a deliberately different approach: a more restrained, spare style, suited to its subject (Christ's temptation) and to its implicit argument that inward spiritual victory, not external epic grandeur, is what true heroism actually looks like. *Samson Agonistes* turns to a different classical model again — Greek tragic closet drama, meaning a play written to be read rather than staged. (Notice the timing: the commercial stage had only just reopened in 1660, the event you learned about in Chapter 4, yet Milton deliberately writes for the page instead.) The play applies Sophoclean and Aeschylean tragic structure to the biblical story of Samson.

## Author Profiles

**John Milton** (1608–1674) — see Chapter 11 for his biography through 1660. In the years following his Restoration-era imprisonment and release, now blind, politically marginalized, and reduced in circumstance, Milton dictated *Paradise Lost* to a series of amanuenses (including his daughters) over roughly 1658–63, signing a publishing contract with Samuel Simmons on 27 April 1667 that paid him £5 immediately and, ultimately across subsequent editions, a total of only £28 shared between Milton and his widow for what is now regarded as the supreme achievement of English epic poetry. He published *Paradise Regained* and *Samson Agonistes* together in 1671, and oversaw the expanded, twelve-book second edition of *Paradise Lost* in 1674, the year of his death.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Paradise Lost*** (1667; rev. 1674). This blank-verse epic — ten books in 1667, reorganized into twelve in 1674 — retells the biblical Fall of Man: Satan's rebellion and expulsion from Heaven, his temptation of Eve, and, through her, Adam, in Eden, and humanity's resulting loss of paradise and mortality. Milton frames all of it around a stated purpose, announced in the poem's opening invocation: "to justify the ways of God to men." Pay close attention to Satan, especially in the poem's early books — he's eloquent, charismatic, and psychologically compelling, and he has generated the field's single most enduring critical controversy, which you'll meet below. The poem's blank verse also achieves a sustained rhetorical and structural sophistication that settled, once and for all, whether the form could carry large-scale English epic.
- ***Paradise Regained*** (1671). This four-book sequel narrates Christ's temptation in the wilderness by Satan, and it's structured around dialogue and rhetorical contest rather than *Paradise Lost*'s cosmic action and spectacle. Many critics read its comparatively restrained scale and style as a deliberate formal argument: spiritual steadfastness under temptation, not martial or cosmic grandeur, is Milton's truest heroic ideal — an implicit correction of *Paradise Lost*'s more ambivalently magnetic Satan.
- ***Samson Agonistes*** (1671). This closet tragedy, not intended for stage performance, dramatizes the biblical judge Samson's final days: blind, enslaved, and humiliated among the Philistines, he moves through despair toward one final act of destructive, self-sacrificing strength, pulling down the temple of Dagon. The play borrows its form directly from Greek tragedy — a chorus, unity of time and place, violence reported rather than staged. And its material — a blind, politically defeated former champion of his people, brought low yet capable of one last devastating act — has long struck critics as strikingly, if not straightforwardly, autobiographical.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Milton secretly on Satan's side?** William Blake's celebrated, much-debated claim that Milton "was of the Devil's party without knowing it" argues that Milton wrote with greater imaginative freedom and power about Hell and Satan than about Heaven and God. That observation launched a long critical tradition — running through Blake himself (Part V, Chapter 6) and the Romantic poets (Part VI), and beyond — reading Satan as *Paradise Lost*'s most compelling character, in productive tension with the poem's explicit theological purpose of justifying divine providence. Other critics, notably C. S. Lewis in the twentieth century, pushed back hard against this "Satanist" reading, arguing that the poem's structure deliberately exposes Satan's eloquence as self-deceiving rhetorical corruption, not genuine heroism.
- **What does it mean that Milton was paid so little for his masterpiece?** Milton received a strikingly small sum for *Paradise Lost* — ultimately £28 in total, shared with his widow. Literary historians often cite this figure as a vivid index of two things at once: the era's still-underdeveloped literary marketplace, and Milton's own diminished, politically marginal standing at the time he wrote and sold the poem. The contrast with the poem's later, enduring canonical status is sharp.
- **Should we read *Samson Agonistes* as Milton's own story?** The parallels between Samson's blindness, captivity, and political humiliation and Milton's own circumstances are impossible to miss, and widely noted. But scholars caution against too simple an equation between poet and biblical subject — the play's theological and dramatic concerns (divine providence, the line between appropriate and excessive violence, true versus false strength) operate on their own terms, independent of any strict autobiographical match.
- **Do these three works form a single, connected statement?** Increasingly, critics read *Paradise Lost*, *Paradise Regained*, and *Samson Agonistes* together, as a deliberately connected sequence exploring genuine heroism, obedience, and providence from three complementary angles: cosmic Fall, spiritual temptation resisted, and tragic, destructive redemption. Read this way, the three works form a sustained final meditation, composed in blindness and political defeat, on the theological and political convictions that had shaped Milton's entire career since the early poetry and prose you met in Chapter 11.

## Chapter Summary

Blind, politically disgraced, and materially diminished after the Restoration crisis you read about in Chapter 11, Milton produced in his final years the supreme achievement of English epic poetry: *Paradise Lost* (1667, expanded to twelve books in 1674), a blank-verse retelling of the Fall of Man explicitly intended "to justify the ways of God to men," whose magnetically eloquent Satan has generated centuries of critical debate since William Blake's provocative claim that Milton was unknowingly "of the Devil's party." Its 1671 companion volume, pairing the restrained *Paradise Regained* with the Greek-tragic closet drama *Samson Agonistes*, extends Milton's late meditation on heroism, temptation, and providence, with Samson's blind, captive, yet ultimately destructively redemptive figure widely, if cautiously, read against Milton's own life. Though published after 1660, these works properly belong to the culminating achievement of everything you've traced across this entire Part — the blank verse of Surrey and Marlowe, the biblical scholarship of the Reformation and the King James Bible, and Milton's own decades of poetic and political engagement — closing Part III's account of English Renaissance and early modern literature on its single most consequential individual achievement.

That closes Part III. You've now traced English literature from humanist scholarship and the Reformation, through Shakespeare's stage, to Milton's blind, defiant epic — a period-long argument about language, faith, and power carried out almost entirely in verse and print. Part IV picks the story up in Restoration England, where a very different literary culture, marked by wit, satire, and a newly reopened public stage, takes shape.

## Glossary

- **Amanuensis** — a person who writes or types from dictation; Milton, blind from 1652, dictated his late poetry to several amanuenses, including his daughters.
- **Closet drama** — a play written for reading rather than stage performance, as with *Samson Agonistes*.
- **In medias res** — Latin, "into the middle of things"; the classical epic convention of beginning a narrative partway through its action, employed in *Paradise Lost*.
