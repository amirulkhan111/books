## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Milton composed his three major late poems — *Paradise Lost* (1667; expanded 1674), *Paradise Regained*, and *Samson Agonistes* (both 1671) — after the political defeat, personal danger, and narrow survival recounted in Chapter 11: blind since 1652, politically disgraced and briefly imprisoned at the 1660 Restoration, Milton produced his greatest poetry from a position of profound private and public loss. Although this composition and publication falls after Part III's nominal 1500–1660 chronological frame, Milton's intellectual formation, poetic technique, and theological concerns belong entirely to the world traced across this Part — from the humanist and biblical scholarship of Chapter 1, through the sonnet and blank verse traditions of Chapters 2 and 5, to his own earlier career (Chapter 11) — and this book, like most standard literary histories, treats him as the culminating figure of English Renaissance literature rather than reclassifying his late work into the following period.

## Literary Movements

*Paradise Lost* represents the culminating achievement of the **English epic** tradition within this Part, drawing on classical epic convention (invocation of the muse, in medias res structure, epic similes) established by Homer and Virgil while adapting **blank verse** — introduced by Surrey (Chapter 2) and mastered dramatically by Marlowe and Shakespeare (Chapters 5–6) — as its narrative medium; Milton's prefatory note defends this choice explicitly against rhyme, which he calls "the invention of a barbarous age," asserting blank verse as the metrically and philosophically appropriate vehicle for serious epic ambition. *Paradise Regained*, by contrast, adopts a deliberately more restrained, spare style suited to its subject (Christ's temptation) and its implicit argument that inward spiritual victory, not external epic grandeur, constitutes true heroism; *Samson Agonistes* returns to a wholly different classical model, the Greek tragic closet drama (intended for reading rather than the Restoration-era commercial stage, itself just reopened after the 1660 revival discussed in Chapter 4), applying Sophoclean and Aeschylean tragic structure to the biblical story of Samson.

## Author Profiles

**John Milton** (1608–1674) — see Chapter 11 for his biography through 1660. In the years following his Restoration-era imprisonment and release, now blind, politically marginalized, and reduced in circumstance, Milton dictated *Paradise Lost* to a series of amanuenses (including his daughters) over roughly 1658–63, signing a publishing contract with Samuel Simmons on 27 April 1667 that paid him £5 immediately and, ultimately across subsequent editions, a total of only £28 shared between Milton and his widow for what is now regarded as the supreme achievement of English epic poetry. He published *Paradise Regained* and *Samson Agonistes* together in 1671, and oversaw the expanded, twelve-book second edition of *Paradise Lost* in 1674, the year of his death.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Paradise Lost*** (1667; rev. 1674) — An epic in blank verse (ten books in 1667, reorganized into twelve in 1674) retelling the biblical Fall of Man: Satan's rebellion and expulsion from Heaven, his temptation of Eve (and, through her, Adam) in Eden, and humanity's resulting loss of paradise and mortality, framed by Milton's stated purpose, announced in the opening invocation, "to justify the ways of God to men." The poem's Satan — eloquent, charismatic, and psychologically compelling in the poem's early books — has generated the field's most enduring critical controversy (discussed below), while its blank verse achieves a sustained rhetorical and structural sophistication that decisively established the form as viable for large-scale English epic composition.
- ***Paradise Regained*** (1671) — A four-book sequel narrating Christ's temptation in the wilderness by Satan, structured around dialogue and rhetorical contest rather than *Paradise Lost*'s cosmic action and spectacle; its comparatively restrained scale and style have been read as a deliberate formal argument that spiritual steadfastness under temptation, rather than martial or cosmic grandeur, constitutes Milton's truest heroic ideal — an implicit correction of *Paradise Lost*'s more ambivalently magnetic Satan.
- ***Samson Agonistes*** (1671) — A closet tragedy (not intended for stage performance) dramatizing the biblical judge Samson's final days: blind, enslaved, and humiliated among the Philistines, Samson moves through despair toward a final act of destructive, self-sacrificing strength, pulling down the temple of Dagon. Its formal debt to Greek tragedy (a chorus, unity of time and place, reported rather than staged violence) combines with material — a blind, politically defeated former champion of his people, brought low yet capable of a final devastating act — that critics have long read as strikingly, if not necessarily straightforwardly, autobiographical.

## Critical Perspectives

- **The Satan controversy.** William Blake's celebrated (and much-debated) observation that Milton "was of the Devil's party without knowing it" — because Milton, in Blake's reading, wrote with greater imaginative freedom and power about Hell and Satan than about Heaven and God — inaugurated a long critical tradition (extending through the Romantic poets, Chapter 6 of the following Part, and beyond) reading Satan as *Paradise Lost*'s most compelling character, in productive tension with the poem's explicit theological purpose of justifying divine providence; other critics (notably C. S. Lewis, in the twentieth century) have pushed back forcefully against this "Satanist" reading, arguing that the poem's structure deliberately exposes Satan's eloquence as self-deceiving rhetorical corruption rather than genuine heroism.
- **The economics of Milton's masterpiece.** The strikingly small sum (ultimately £28, shared with his widow) Milton received for *Paradise Lost* is frequently cited by literary historians as a vivid index of both the era's underdeveloped literary marketplace and Milton's own diminished, politically marginal standing at the time of the poem's composition and sale — a sharp contrast to the poem's subsequent, and enduring, canonical status.
- **Reading *Samson Agonistes* autobiographically.** While the parallels between Samson's blindness, captivity, and political humiliation and Milton's own circumstances are widely noted, scholars caution against a too-simple equation of poet and biblical subject, noting that the play's theological and dramatic concerns (divine providence, appropriate versus excessive violence, the nature of true versus false strength) operate on their own terms independent of strict autobiographical correspondence.
- **The three works as a triptych.** Critics increasingly read *Paradise Lost*, *Paradise Regained*, and *Samson Agonistes* together as a deliberately connected sequence exploring, from complementary angles (cosmic Fall, spiritual temptation resisted, tragic destructive redemption), the nature of genuine heroism, obedience, and providence — a sustained final meditation, composed in blindness and political defeat, on the theological and political convictions that had shaped Milton's entire career since Chapter 11's early poetry and prose.

## Chapter Summary

Blind, politically disgraced, and materially diminished following the Restoration crisis recounted in Chapter 11, Milton produced in his final years the supreme achievement of English epic poetry: *Paradise Lost* (1667; expanded to twelve books in 1674), a blank-verse retelling of the Fall of Man explicitly intended "to justify the ways of God to men," whose magnetically eloquent Satan has generated centuries of critical debate since William Blake's provocative claim that Milton was unknowingly "of the Devil's party." Its 1671 companion volume, pairing the restrained *Paradise Regained* with the Greek-tragic closet drama *Samson Agonistes*, extends Milton's late meditation on heroism, temptation, and providence, with Samson's blind, captive, yet ultimately destructively redemptive figure widely (if cautiously) read against Milton's own biography. Though published after 1660, these works are properly understood as the culminating achievement of the literary, linguistic, and theological traditions traced across this entire Part — the blank verse of Surrey and Marlowe, the biblical scholarship of the Reformation and the King James Bible, and Milton's own decades of poetic and political engagement — closing Part III's account of English Renaissance and early modern literature on its single most consequential individual achievement.

## Glossary

- **Amanuensis** — a person who writes or types from dictation; Milton, blind from 1652, dictated his late poetry to several amanuenses, including his daughters.
- **Closet drama** — a play written for reading rather than stage performance, as with *Samson Agonistes*.
- **In medias res** — Latin, "into the middle of things"; the classical epic convention of beginning a narrative partway through its action, employed in *Paradise Lost*.
