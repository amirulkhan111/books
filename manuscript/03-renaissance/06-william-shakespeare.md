## Historical and Socio-Political Context

William Shakespeare wrote within the commercial theatre infrastructure and playing-company system established in Chapter 4, as a working shareholder of the Lord Chamberlain's Men (from 1594; renamed the King's Men under James I's patronage from 1603) rather than solely as an independent literary figure — a professional position that shaped his prolific output across roughly two decades of sustained, commercially oriented dramatic writing. His career spans the transition from Elizabeth I's reign to James I's, and his plays absorb and transform the dramatic innovations of Marlowe (Chapter 5) and the broader University Wits (Chapter 4) while developing a psychological, linguistic, and structural range that has made his canon the most studied body of work in English literary history.

## Literary Movements

Shakespeare's canon is conventionally organized, following the tripartite division established by the 1623 First Folio itself (*Comedies, Histories, & Tragedies*), into four categories — the fourth, the **late romances**, being a later critical addition. **Comedies** (e.g., *A Midsummer Night's Dream*, *Twelfth Night*, *Much Ado About Nothing*, *As You Like It*, *The Merchant of Venice*) typically combine mistaken identity, wit, and romantic intrigue resolved in marriage or reconciliation. **Histories** (e.g., the two Henry IV plays, *Henry V*, *Richard II*, *Richard III*) dramatize English royal succession and civil conflict, extending the history-play tradition Marlowe had helped establish with *Edward II* (Chapter 5). **Tragedies** (e.g., *Hamlet*, *Othello*, *King Lear*, *Macbeth*, *Romeo and Juliet*) explore catastrophic individual and political downfall through psychologically complex protagonists, building on and substantially exceeding the tragic model established by Marlowe's overreachers. The **late romances** (*Pericles*, *Cymbeline*, *The Winter's Tale*, *The Tempest*) — a category first named by the critic Edward Dowden in his 1875 study *Shakspere: A Critical Study of His Mind and Art* — combine tragic and comic elements with fantastical, far-flung settings, supernatural intervention, and themes of loss, separation, and improbable, redemptive reunion.

## Author Profiles

**William Shakespeare** (1564–1616) — Born and baptized in Stratford-upon-Avon, Warwickshire, son of a glover and local official; married Anne Hathaway in 1582 (their first child, Susanna, baptized 1583; twins Hamnet and Judith, 1585). Nothing certain is documented of his activities during the "lost years" (1585–92); by 1592 he was sufficiently established as a London playwright to draw a jealous printed attack (the "upstart crow" reference in *Greenes, Groats-worth of Witte*). A founding shareholder of the Lord Chamberlain's Men (1594, later the King's Men), Shakespeare wrote and performed within this single company for the bulk of his career, achieving sufficient prosperity to purchase New Place, Stratford's second-largest house, in 1597, following his son Hamnet's death the previous year. He largely retired to Stratford after 1613 (the year the Globe burned down during a performance of *Henry VIII*) and died on 23 April 1616. His surviving canon comprises some 37–39 plays (the range reflecting disputes over attribution and collaborative authorship), 154 sonnets, and several narrative poems (*Venus and Adonis*, *The Rape of Lucrece*).

**John Heminges** and **Henry Condell** — Fellow actor-shareholders in Shakespeare's company who, seven years after his death, compiled and published the First Folio (1623), *Mr. William Shakespeares Comedies, Histories, & Tragedies*, using prompt-books, earlier quarto editions, and their own theatrical knowledge to assemble the most authoritative early collection of his plays — an editorial act without which eighteen plays, including *Macbeth*, *Julius Caesar*, *Twelfth Night*, *The Tempest*, and *Antony and Cleopatra*, would very likely have been lost entirely.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***A Midsummer Night's Dream* and *Twelfth Night*** (Comedies) — Exemplify Shakespeare's comic mastery of intersecting romantic plots, disguise, mistaken identity, and (in *Twelfth Night*, via the cross-dressed Viola) gender play enabled by the all-male performance convention (Chapter 4), resolved in the genre's characteristic movement toward marriage and social reintegration.
- ***Richard II*, the *Henry IV* plays, and *Henry V*** (Histories) — Together form a linked tetralogy dramatizing the fifteenth-century crisis of royal legitimacy from Richard II's deposition through Prince Hal's transformation into the martial King Henry V, extending the psychologically interiorized treatment of kingship Marlowe pioneered in *Edward II* (Chapter 5) into Shakespeare's most sustained historical sequence.
- ***Hamlet*, *Othello*, *King Lear*, and *Macbeth*** (Tragedies) — Shakespeare's four major tragedies, probing (respectively) delayed revenge and existential doubt, sexual jealousy and racial otherness, familial betrayal and cosmic/political disorder, and unchecked ambition and moral self-destruction, each combining Marlovian tragic scale with a psychological interiority — most famously realized in the soliloquy form — that decisively exceeds Marlowe's own dramatic achievement.
- ***The Tempest*** (Late Romance) — Widely (though not universally) regarded as Shakespeare's final solely authored play, staging the exiled magician Prospero's reconciliation with those who wronged him on a remote island, and frequently read (whether or not intended by Shakespeare as deliberate farewell) as a meditation on artistic power, forgiveness, and theatrical illusion itself.
- ***Shake-speares Sonnets*** (1609) — A sequence of 154 sonnets published by Thomas Thorpe, conventionally divided into poems addressed to a "Fair Youth" (sonnets 1–126, urging marriage and procreation before shifting into more complex meditations on love, time, and poetic immortality) and to a "Dark Lady" (sonnets 127–152, addressing a more sexually charged and morally ambivalent relationship); the volume's dedication to a mysterious "Mr. W.H." — variously identified with candidates including Henry Wriothesley, third Earl of Southampton, and William Herbert, third Earl of Pembroke — remains unresolved.

## Critical Perspectives

- **The "lost years" and biographical speculation.** The absence of any documentary record for 1585–92 has generated extensive, largely unverifiable speculation (schoolmaster, soldier, legal clerk, traveler) about Shakespeare's activities before his emergence as a London playwright; scholars generally treat such theories as suggestive rather than established.
- **Authorship of the "upstart crow" attack.** While traditionally attributed to Robert Greene, recent stylometric analysis has suggested the *Groatsworth* pamphlet, or at least its Shakespeare-attacking passage, may have been substantially the work of Thomas Nashe — a reminder, relevant throughout this Part, of the collaborative and sometimes disputed authorship common in Elizabethan print and theatrical culture.
- **Generic boundary-crossing.** Modern criticism increasingly questions the neatness of the comedy/history/tragedy/romance division, noting substantial genre-mixing within individual plays (the comic gravediggers in the tragic *Hamlet*; the tragicomic tonal instability of the "problem plays," such as *Measure for Measure* and *All's Well That Ends Well*, which resist easy placement in any of the four categories).
- **Collaborative authorship.** Modern textual scholarship, increasingly informed by stylometric analysis, has identified substantial collaborative authorship in several plays long attributed solely to Shakespeare — including *Henry VIII* and *The Two Noble Kinsmen* (both with John Fletcher) and, per recent scholarship, portions of *Titus Andronicus* (with George Peele) and the *Henry VI* plays — complicating any simple notion of a single, unmediated authorial canon.
- **The Shakespeare authorship question.** A persistent, non-academic tradition of skepticism — proposing alternative authors such as Edward de Vere, 17th Earl of Oxford (the "Oxfordian" theory) or Francis Bacon (the "Baconian" theory) — has circulated since the nineteenth century. The overwhelming scholarly consensus rejects these theories as lacking documentary support and resting on circumstantial or demonstrably flawed reasoning (the Oxfordian theory, for instance, requires explaining plays scholars date after Oxford's 1604 death); this book follows that consensus in treating Shakespeare of Stratford as the author of the canon bearing his name, while noting the persistence of the controversy as a notable, if scholarly marginal, feature of his cultural reception.
- **The First Folio's editorial significance.** Because Heminges and Condell's 1623 compilation preserved eighteen plays otherwise unpublished in Shakespeare's lifetime, textual scholars treat the Folio (alongside the earlier individual "quarto" editions of some plays, which sometimes differ substantially from Folio texts, as with the divergent Quarto and Folio texts of *King Lear*) as an indispensable but not unproblematic witness, requiring the same kind of careful textual-critical attention given to *Doctor Faustus*'s A- and B-texts (Chapter 5).

## Chapter Summary

William Shakespeare, a working shareholder in the Lord Chamberlain's/King's Men from 1594, produced across roughly two decades a canon of some 37–39 plays spanning comedy, history, tragedy, and the late romances (a category named only in 1875 by Edward Dowden), alongside the 154 sonnets published in 1609. Documentary gaps — the "lost years" of 1585–92 and the disputed authorship of the 1592 "upstart crow" attack that first notices him as a playwright — leave substantial biographical uncertainty, while modern scholarship increasingly recognizes collaborative authorship in several plays. The 1623 First Folio, compiled posthumously by his fellow actors Heminges and Condell, preserved eighteen plays that would otherwise have been lost, cementing Shakespeare's canon in the form through which most subsequent readers have encountered it. Despite a persistent, scholarly marginal "authorship question," the overwhelming academic consensus affirms Shakespeare of Stratford as author of the canon bearing his name — a canon that, building on the dramatic and formal innovations of Marlowe (Chapter 5) and the broader Renaissance stage (Chapter 4), constitutes the central achievement of English Renaissance literature.

## Glossary

- **First Folio** — the 1623 posthumous collected edition of 36 Shakespeare plays, compiled by John Heminges and Henry Condell.
- **Late romances** — Shakespeare's final plays (*Pericles*, *Cymbeline*, *The Winter's Tale*, *The Tempest*), a category named by Edward Dowden in 1875.
- **"Lost years"** — the undocumented period of Shakespeare's life, c. 1585–92.
- **Quarto** — a smaller, individually printed edition of a single play, several of which preceded and sometimes differ from the First Folio's texts.
