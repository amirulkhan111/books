## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Everything you've read so far in this Part — the commercial theatre infrastructure of Chapter 4, Marlowe's mastery of blank verse and the overreacher protagonist in Chapter 5 — was building toward this chapter. William Shakespeare wrote inside that same commercial system, not as an independent literary figure working alone, but as a working shareholder of the Lord Chamberlain's Men from 1594 (renamed the King's Men once James I became their royal patron in 1603). That professional, financial stake in his own company shaped how prolifically he wrote across roughly two decades of sustained, commercially minded output.

His career spans the handover from Elizabeth I's reign to James I's, and his plays absorb and transform everything Marlowe and the wider University Wits had already achieved — while developing a psychological, linguistic, and structural range that has made his body of work the single most studied in the whole history of English literature.

## Literary Movements

You'll find Shakespeare's plays sorted into four categories, and it's worth knowing where that scheme comes from. Three of the categories go straight back to the 1623 First Folio itself, whose title page reads *Comedies, Histories, & Tragedies*. The fourth, the **late romances**, is a later critical addition.

**Comedies** — think *A Midsummer Night's Dream*, *Twelfth Night*, *Much Ado About Nothing*, *As You Like It*, *The Merchant of Venice* — typically combine mistaken identity, wit, and romantic intrigue, resolved by marriage or reconciliation. **Histories** — the two *Henry IV* plays, *Henry V*, *Richard II*, *Richard III* — dramatize English royal succession and civil conflict, extending the history-play tradition Marlowe had already established with *Edward II* in Chapter 5. **Tragedies** — *Hamlet*, *Othello*, *King Lear*, *Macbeth*, *Romeo and Juliet* — explore catastrophic individual and political downfall through psychologically complex protagonists, building on Marlowe's overreacher model and pushing well past it. And the **late romances** — *Pericles*, *Cymbeline*, *The Winter's Tale*, *The Tempest*, a category the critic Edward Dowden first named in 1875 — blend tragic and comic elements together, set in fantastical, far-flung places, with supernatural intervention and a recurring pattern of loss, separation, and improbable, redemptive reunion.

## Author Profiles

**William Shakespeare** (1564–1616) was born and baptized in Stratford-upon-Avon, Warwickshire, the son of a glover and local official. He married Anne Hathaway in 1582; their first child, Susanna, was baptized in 1583, followed by twins Hamnet and Judith in 1585. What happened next is a genuine gap in the record — nothing certain survives documenting his activities during the so-called "lost years," 1585–92 — but by 1592 he was established enough as a London playwright to draw a jealous printed attack, the "upstart crow" reference you'll meet in Critical Perspectives below. He became a founding shareholder of the Lord Chamberlain's Men in 1594 (later the King's Men), and he wrote and performed within that single company for the bulk of his career, growing prosperous enough to buy New Place, Stratford's second-largest house, in 1597, the year after his son Hamnet died. He largely retired to Stratford after 1613, the year the Globe burned down during a performance of *Henry VIII*, and died on 23 April 1616. His surviving body of work comes to some 37–39 plays — the range reflects ongoing disputes over attribution and collaboration — plus 154 sonnets and several narrative poems, including *Venus and Adonis* and *The Rape of Lucrece*.

**John Heminges** and **Henry Condell** were fellow actor-shareholders in Shakespeare's company. Seven years after his death, they compiled and published the First Folio (1623), *Mr. William Shakespeares Comedies, Histories, & Tragedies*, drawing on prompt-books, earlier quarto editions, and their own theatrical knowledge to assemble the most authoritative early collection of his plays. Without that editorial act, eighteen plays — including *Macbeth*, *Julius Caesar*, *Twelfth Night*, *The Tempest*, and *Antony and Cleopatra* — would very likely have been lost to us entirely.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***A Midsummer Night's Dream* and *Twelfth Night*** (Comedies). These two plays show off Shakespeare's comic mastery: intersecting romantic plots, disguise, mistaken identity, and, in *Twelfth Night*, gender play built directly on the all-male performance convention from Chapter 4 — Viola disguises herself as a man, played, remember, by a boy actor playing a woman playing a man. Both plays resolve the way comedies of this period typically do: through marriage and social reintegration.
- ***Richard II*, the *Henry IV* plays, and *Henry V*** (Histories). Read together, these four plays form a linked sequence dramatizing the fifteenth-century crisis of royal legitimacy — starting with Richard II's deposition and ending with Prince Hal's transformation into the martial King Henry V. This is Shakespeare's most sustained historical sequence, and it extends the psychologically interior treatment of kingship Marlowe had already pioneered in *Edward II*, back in Chapter 5.
- ***Hamlet*, *Othello*, *King Lear*, and *Macbeth*** (Tragedies). These are Shakespeare's four major tragedies, and each probes something different: *Hamlet* explores delayed revenge and existential doubt, *Othello* sexual jealousy and racial otherness, *King Lear* familial betrayal alongside cosmic and political disorder, *Macbeth* unchecked ambition and moral self-destruction. All four combine Marlowe's sense of tragic scale with something Marlowe never quite achieved — a psychological interiority, most famously delivered through the soliloquy, that lets you hear a character actually thinking.
- ***The Tempest*** (Late Romance). Most scholars regard this as Shakespeare's final solely authored play, though not everyone agrees. It stages the exiled magician Prospero's reconciliation with those who wronged him, on a remote island — and whether or not Shakespeare intended it as his own farewell to the stage, readers have long taken it as a meditation on artistic power, forgiveness, and theatrical illusion itself.
- ***Shake-speares Sonnets*** (1609). This sequence of 154 sonnets, published by Thomas Thorpe, conventionally splits into two groups: poems addressed to a "Fair Youth" (sonnets 1–126, which start out urging marriage and procreation before shifting into more complex meditations on love, time, and poetic immortality), and poems addressed to a "Dark Lady" (sonnets 127–152, a more sexually charged and morally ambivalent relationship). The volume is dedicated to a mysterious "Mr. W.H.," and nobody has ever settled who that actually was — candidates include Henry Wriothesley, third Earl of Southampton, and William Herbert, third Earl of Pembroke.

## Critical Perspectives

- **What really happened in the "lost years"?** We have no documentary record at all for 1585–92, and that gap has produced endless, largely unverifiable speculation — was Shakespeare a schoolmaster? A soldier? A legal clerk? A traveler? Treat these theories as suggestive guesses, not established fact.
- **Who actually wrote the "upstart crow" attack?** Tradition credits Robert Greene, but recent stylometric analysis (computer-assisted comparison of writing style) suggests the attack on Shakespeare may actually have been substantially written by Thomas Nashe. It's a useful reminder, and one that applies throughout this Part: Elizabethan print and theatrical authorship was often collaborative and sometimes genuinely disputed.
- **Do Shakespeare's plays really fit neatly into four categories?** Increasingly, modern criticism says no. Notice the comic gravediggers inside the tragic *Hamlet*, or the tonal instability of the so-called "problem plays" — *Measure for Measure*, *All's Well That Ends Well* — which resist easy placement in any of the four categories at all.
- **Did Shakespeare write everything attributed to him alone?** Not always. Modern textual scholarship, increasingly informed by stylometric analysis, has identified real collaborative authorship in several plays long thought to be solely Shakespeare's — *Henry VIII* and *The Two Noble Kinsmen*, both co-written with John Fletcher, and, according to recent scholarship, parts of *Titus Andronicus* (with George Peele) and the *Henry VI* plays. This complicates any simple picture of a single author working entirely alone.
- **What about the Shakespeare authorship question?** Since the nineteenth century, a persistent, non-academic tradition has proposed alternative authors for Shakespeare's plays — Edward de Vere, 17th Earl of Oxford (the "Oxfordian" theory), or Francis Bacon (the "Baconian" theory). The overwhelming scholarly consensus rejects both: they lack documentary support and rest on circumstantial or demonstrably flawed reasoning — the Oxfordian theory, for instance, has to somehow explain plays that scholars confidently date after Oxford's death in 1604. This book follows that scholarly consensus, treating Shakespeare of Stratford as the author of the canon bearing his name, while noting that the controversy persists as a notable feature of his popular, if not scholarly, reception.
- **Why does the First Folio matter so much?** Heminges and Condell's 1623 compilation preserved eighteen plays that had never been published in Shakespeare's lifetime — without it, they would very likely be lost to us entirely. Textual scholars treat the Folio, alongside the earlier individual "quarto" printings of some plays (which sometimes differ substantially, as with *King Lear*'s two versions), as an indispensable but genuinely imperfect witness — deserving the same careful textual-critical attention you already gave to *Doctor Faustus*'s A- and B-texts in Chapter 5.

## Chapter Summary

William Shakespeare, a working shareholder in the Lord Chamberlain's/King's Men from 1594, produced across roughly two decades a canon of some 37–39 plays spanning comedy, history, tragedy, and the late romances — a fourth category named only in 1875, by the critic Edward Dowden — alongside the 154 sonnets published in 1609. Real documentary gaps remain: the "lost years" of 1585–92, and the disputed authorship of the 1592 "upstart crow" attack that first notices him as a working playwright. Modern scholarship also increasingly recognizes genuine collaborative authorship in several plays once assumed to be his alone. The 1623 First Folio, compiled after his death by his fellow actors Heminges and Condell, preserved eighteen plays that would otherwise very likely have been lost, and it fixed Shakespeare's canon into the form through which most later readers have encountered it. Despite a persistent but scholarly marginal "authorship question," the overwhelming academic consensus affirms Shakespeare of Stratford as the author of the canon bearing his name — a canon that, building directly on Marlowe's innovations from Chapter 5 and the wider Renaissance stage from Chapter 4, stands as the central achievement of English Renaissance literature.

## Glossary

- **First Folio** — the 1623 posthumous collected edition of 36 Shakespeare plays, compiled by John Heminges and Henry Condell.
- **Late romances** — Shakespeare's final plays (*Pericles*, *Cymbeline*, *The Winter's Tale*, *The Tempest*), a category named by Edward Dowden in 1875.
- **"Lost years"** — the undocumented period of Shakespeare's life, c. 1585–92.
- **Quarto** — a smaller, individually printed edition of a single play, several of which preceded and sometimes differ from the First Folio's texts.
