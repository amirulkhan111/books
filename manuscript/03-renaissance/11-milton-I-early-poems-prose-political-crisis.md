## Historical and Socio-Political Context

John Milton's career spans, more completely than any other figure treated in this Part, the full arc of England's mid-seventeenth-century political and religious crisis: from Charles I's personal rule and the Bishops' Wars, through the outbreak of civil war (1642), the execution of the king (1649) and the establishment of the Commonwealth (in which Milton served as a senior government official), to the Restoration of Charles II (1660), which briefly threatened Milton's life for his prominent defence of regicide. This chapter treats Milton's early poetic career and his extensive political prose writing; Chapter 12 treats the great poetry — above all *Paradise Lost* — composed after 1660, in blindness and political defeat.

## Literary Movements

Milton's early poetry moves across several distinct modes inherited from earlier chapters of this Part: pastoral and companion-poem convention ("L'Allegro"/"Il Penseroso"), the courtly masque form (*Comus*, following the Jonson-Jones tradition of Chapter 7, though composed for a private aristocratic rather than royal occasion), and pastoral elegy (*Lycidas*, working within a classical tradition stretching back through Spenser, Chapter 3, to Virgil). His subsequent prose career belongs to the flourishing, often furiously contested pamphlet culture of the Civil War decades, in which print (building on the expanding print infrastructure traced since Caxton, Part II Chapter 9, and accelerated by the humanist and Reformation printing discussed in Chapter 1) became the primary battleground for political and religious argument.

## Author Profiles

**John Milton** (1608–1674) — London-born son of a scrivener; educated at St Paul's School and Christ's College, Cambridge, where his fastidious manner earned him the nickname "the Lady of Christ's." After Cambridge, he spent six years (1632–38) at his family's Horton estate in intensive private study and poetic composition, producing "L'Allegro," "Il Penseroso," *Comus* (1634), and "Lycidas" (1637, mourning his drowned Cambridge contemporary Edward King). A fifteen-month tour of France and Italy (1638–39) brought him into contact with Galileo, then under house arrest near Florence for his heliocentric astronomy; Milton cut the tour short on news of England's deepening political crisis. Returning to England, he devoted the 1640s substantially to prose pamphleteering: attacks on episcopal church government, four controversial tracts defending the legality of divorce (provoked by his first wife Mary Powell's abrupt 1642 departure), and *Areopagitica* (1644), his celebrated defence of a free press against government licensing. Appointed Latin Secretary to Cromwell's Council of State in 1649, Milton became the Commonwealth's leading polemical defender, publishing *The Tenure of Kings and Magistrates* and *Eikonoklastes* (both 1649) and the Latin *Pro Populo Anglicano Defensio* (1651) — the last written even as his eyesight, failing since the mid-1640s, deteriorated toward the total blindness he reached by 1652. At the 1660 Restoration, Milton's prominent defence of the regicide made him a marked man; he went into hiding, saw his books publicly burned, and was briefly arrested and imprisoned before the intercession of his friend and former Latin Secretary colleague Andrew Marvell (Chapter 8), by then a sitting MP, helped secure his release and spare him near-certain execution.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"L'Allegro" and "Il Penseroso"** (c. 1631–32) — Companion poems contrasting the pleasures of cheerful, sociable life ("L'Allegro," Italian for "the happy man") with those of solitary, scholarly contemplation ("Il Penseroso," "the thoughtful man"), demonstrating Milton's early formal virtuosity and his engagement with classical and English pastoral convention.
- ***Comus*** (1634) — A masque (Chapter 7) performed at Ludlow Castle, dramatizing a virtuous lady's resistance to the seductive, morally corrupting enchanter Comus; notable for adapting the courtly masque form to serious moral and philosophical argument about chastity and virtue rather than purely celebratory royal spectacle.
- **"Lycidas"** (1637) — A pastoral elegy mourning Edward King, drowned at sea, that moves beyond personal grief into pointed contemporary criticism of clerical corruption within the English Church — an early instance of Milton's characteristic fusion of high classical/biblical poetic form with urgent contemporary political and religious argument.
- ***The Doctrine and Discipline of Divorce*** (1643/44) — Argues, on grounds of personal liberty and spiritual/emotional compatibility rather than merely physical consummation, for the legality of divorce — a position provoked by Milton's own failed first marriage and considered radically controversial in its own time.
- ***Areopagitica*** (1644) — A prose oration (its title alluding to the Areopagus, the ancient Athenian council) opposing Parliament's licensing order requiring pre-publication government approval of printed works; argues that truth emerges more reliably through open contest among competing ideas than through suppression, in language ("as good almost kill a man as kill a good book . . . he who destroys a good book, kills reason itself") that remains a foundational text in the history of arguments for freedom of the press.
- ***The Tenure of Kings and Magistrates* and *Eikonoklastes*** (1649) — Written in the immediate aftermath of Charles I's execution, the former argues on political-theoretical grounds for a people's right to depose and execute a tyrannical ruler; the latter directly rebuts the enormously popular *Eikon Basilike* (a purported royal memoir, published immediately after Charles I's execution, that cast the king as a Christian martyr), attempting to dismantle its powerful royalist propaganda effect.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Milton's early poetry and later prose: continuity or rupture?** Critics debate how directly the aesthetic and moral concerns of Milton's early poetry (chastity and virtue in *Comus*; clerical corruption in "Lycidas") anticipate his subsequent turn to explicitly political prose polemics, or whether the 1640s crisis represents a genuine redirection of a poet's career toward more immediately urgent public argument.
- **The divorce tracts and Milton's personal investment.** Scholars generally read the divorce tracts as substantially, though not exclusively, motivated by Milton's own marital crisis, while noting that his arguments extend well beyond personal grievance into a broader, genuinely radical theory of marriage grounded in companionship and mutual consent rather than canon-law technicality.
- ***Areopagitica*'s limits and legacy.** While celebrated as a foundational free-press text, critics note that Milton's argument was not an unlimited defence of free expression — he explicitly excluded Catholic and certain other texts from his proposed toleration — complicating any simple, anachronistic reading of the tract as a modern liberal free-speech manifesto.
- **Milton as regicide's defender.** Milton's willingness to serve the Commonwealth as its leading polemical voice, defending the execution of a king on both political-theoretical and providential religious grounds, made him uniquely exposed at the Restoration; critics read his subsequent, celebrated turn to epic poetry (Chapter 12) partly against this background of personal danger, political defeat, and — following his 1652 blindness — profound physical vulnerability.
- **The role of personal networks in Milton's survival.** That Milton's escape from probable execution depended substantially on Marvell's personal and political intercession underscores how contingent, rather than simply due to abstract legal process, survival through the volatile transition of 1660 could be — a reminder of the personal stakes underlying the literary-historical narrative traced throughout this Part.

## Chapter Summary

John Milton's early career spans an unusually complete range of literary and political engagement: pastoral, masque, and elegiac poetry composed during his years of private study at Horton; a formative encounter with Galileo during his 1638–39 Italian tour, cut short by news of England's deepening crisis; a decade of urgent prose pamphleteering addressing episcopal reform, divorce, and (in *Areopagitica*) press freedom; and, from 1649, service as the Commonwealth's leading polemical voice as Cromwell's Latin Secretary, defending the regicide against domestic and European criticism even as his eyesight failed toward total blindness by 1652. The 1660 Restoration exposed Milton to genuine mortal danger for this record of service, from which he escaped only through the intercession of his friend Andrew Marvell — setting the stage for the blind, politically defeated poet's turn, treated in Chapter 12, to the composition of his greatest poetry.

---

## Glossary

- ***Areopagitica*** — Milton's 1644 prose oration opposing government licensing and censorship of the press.
- ***Eikon Basilike*** — a purported royal memoir published after Charles I's execution, presenting him as a Christian martyr; the target of Milton's *Eikonoklastes*.
- **Latin Secretary** — Milton's official post (Secretary for Foreign Tongues) under Cromwell's Council of State, 1649–53.
- **Masque** — see Chapter 7; the courtly entertainment form Milton adapted for *Comus*.
