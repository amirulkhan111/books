## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Of every writer in this Part, John Milton lived through the fullest arc of England's mid-seventeenth-century political and religious crisis. His life runs from Charles I's personal rule and the Bishops' Wars, through the outbreak of civil war in 1642, the execution of the king in 1649 and the establishment of the Commonwealth — where Milton himself served as a senior government official — all the way to the Restoration of Charles II in 1660, which briefly put Milton's own life in real danger for his prominent defence of the regicide.

This chapter covers Milton's early poetic career and his extensive political prose writing. Chapter 12 will take you into the great poetry — above all *Paradise Lost* — that he composed after 1660, in blindness and political defeat. Keep that split in mind: the Milton of this chapter is a young poet and a fiercely engaged political pamphleteer, not yet the blind epic poet you'll meet next.

## Literary Movements

Milton's early poetry moves across several modes you've already met earlier in this Part. He writes in the pastoral and companion-poem convention, in "L'Allegro" and "Il Penseroso." He works in the courtly masque form in *Comus*, following the Jonson-Jones tradition from Chapter 7, though his masque was composed for a private aristocratic occasion rather than a royal one. And he writes pastoral elegy in "Lycidas," working within a classical tradition that stretches back through Spenser in Chapter 3 all the way to Virgil.

His prose career, which follows, belongs to something quite different: the flourishing, often furiously contested pamphlet culture of the Civil War decades. Print — building on the expanding infrastructure you've traced since Caxton in Part II, Chapter 9, and accelerated by the humanist and Reformation printing you studied in Chapter 1 — had become, by this point, the primary battleground for political and religious argument.

## Author Profiles

**John Milton** (1608–1674) — London-born son of a scrivener; educated at St Paul's School and Christ's College, Cambridge, where his fastidious manner earned him the nickname "the Lady of Christ's." After Cambridge, he spent six years (1632–38) at his family's Horton estate in intensive private study and poetic composition, producing "L'Allegro," "Il Penseroso," *Comus* (1634), and "Lycidas" (1637, mourning his drowned Cambridge contemporary Edward King). A fifteen-month tour of France and Italy (1638–39) brought him into contact with Galileo, then under house arrest near Florence for his heliocentric astronomy; Milton cut the tour short on news of England's deepening political crisis. Returning to England, he devoted the 1640s substantially to prose pamphleteering: attacks on episcopal church government, four controversial tracts defending the legality of divorce (provoked by his first wife Mary Powell's abrupt 1642 departure), and *Areopagitica* (1644), his celebrated defence of a free press against government licensing. Appointed Latin Secretary to Cromwell's Council of State in 1649, Milton became the Commonwealth's leading polemical defender, publishing *The Tenure of Kings and Magistrates* and *Eikonoklastes* (both 1649) and the Latin *Pro Populo Anglicano Defensio* (1651) — the last written even as his eyesight, failing since the mid-1640s, deteriorated toward the total blindness he reached by 1652. At the 1660 Restoration, Milton's prominent defence of the regicide made him a marked man; he went into hiding, saw his books publicly burned, and was briefly arrested and imprisoned before the intercession of his friend and former Latin Secretary colleague Andrew Marvell (Chapter 8), by then a sitting MP, helped secure his release and spare him near-certain execution.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"L'Allegro" and "Il Penseroso"** (c. 1631–32). These companion poems contrast two ways of living: the pleasures of cheerful, sociable life ("L'Allegro," Italian for "the happy man") against those of solitary, scholarly contemplation ("Il Penseroso," "the thoughtful man"). Together they show off Milton's early formal skill and his deep engagement with classical and English pastoral convention.
- ***Comus*** (1634). This is a masque, the courtly form you learned about in Chapter 7, performed at Ludlow Castle. It dramatizes a virtuous lady resisting the seductive, morally corrupting enchanter Comus. What's notable is what Milton does with the form: instead of purely celebratory royal spectacle, he adapts the masque toward serious moral and philosophical argument about chastity and virtue.
- **"Lycidas"** (1637). A pastoral elegy mourning Edward King, a Cambridge contemporary who drowned at sea. The poem moves beyond personal grief into pointed criticism of clerical corruption within the English Church — an early example of something you'll see again and again in Milton: the fusion of high classical and biblical poetic form with urgent, contemporary political and religious argument.
- ***The Doctrine and Discipline of Divorce*** (1643/44). This tract argues for the legality of divorce, grounded in personal liberty and spiritual and emotional compatibility, not merely physical consummation. Milton's own failed first marriage provoked this position directly, and it was considered radically controversial at the time.
- ***Areopagitica*** (1644). This prose oration — its title alludes to the Areopagus, the ancient Athenian council — opposes Parliament's licensing order, which required government approval before anything could be printed. Milton's central argument: truth emerges more reliably through open contest among competing ideas than through suppression. His language still gets quoted today: "as good almost kill a man as kill a good book . . . he who destroys a good book, kills reason itself." It remains a foundational text in the history of arguments for a free press.
- ***The Tenure of Kings and Magistrates* and *Eikonoklastes*** (1649). Milton wrote both in the immediate aftermath of Charles I's execution. The first argues, on political-theoretical grounds, that a people has the right to depose and execute a tyrannical ruler. The second directly rebuts *Eikon Basilike*, an enormously popular purported royal memoir published right after the execution that portrayed Charles as a Christian martyr — Milton wrote *Eikonoklastes* specifically to dismantle its powerful royalist propaganda effect.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does Milton's early poetry anticipate his later political prose, or break from it?** Critics debate how directly the aesthetic and moral concerns of Milton's early poetry — chastity and virtue in *Comus*, clerical corruption in "Lycidas" — anticipate his later turn to explicit political polemic. Or does the crisis of the 1640s represent a genuine redirection, a poet's career pulled toward more urgent, immediate public argument?
- **How personal were the divorce tracts, really?** Scholars generally read the divorce tracts as substantially, though not entirely, motivated by Milton's own marital crisis. But notice that his arguments reach well beyond personal grievance, into a genuinely radical theory of marriage grounded in companionship and mutual consent rather than technical points of canon law.
- **Was *Areopagitica* really a free-speech manifesto?** Not quite, and it's worth knowing the limits. Milton's argument, celebrated as a foundational free-press text, was not an unlimited defence of free expression — he explicitly excluded Catholic texts, among others, from his proposed toleration. Be careful not to read the tract anachronistically, as if it were a modern liberal free-speech document.
- **Why does Milton's defence of regicide matter for how we read his later poetry?** Milton's willingness to serve the Commonwealth as its leading polemical voice, defending the king's execution on both political-theoretical and providential religious grounds, made him uniquely exposed once the Restoration arrived. Critics read his later turn to epic poetry, which you'll meet in Chapter 12, partly against this background — personal danger, political defeat, and, after his 1652 blindness, real physical vulnerability.
- **How much did personal connections matter to Milton's survival?** Milton's escape from probable execution depended substantially on Marvell's personal and political intercession. That's a useful reminder: surviving the volatile transition of 1660 was often a matter of who you knew, not simply abstract legal process — a reminder of the real personal stakes sitting underneath this whole Part's literary history.

## Chapter Summary

John Milton's early career covers an unusually complete range of literary and political engagement: pastoral, masque, and elegiac poetry composed during his years of private study at Horton; a formative encounter with Galileo during his 1638–39 Italian tour, cut short by news of England's deepening crisis; a decade of urgent prose pamphleteering on episcopal reform, divorce, and, in *Areopagitica*, press freedom; and, from 1649 onward, service as the Commonwealth's leading polemical voice, as Cromwell's Latin Secretary, defending the regicide against domestic and European criticism even as his eyesight failed toward total blindness by 1652. The 1660 Restoration put Milton in genuine mortal danger for this record of service, and he escaped only through the intercession of his friend Andrew Marvell — setting the stage for the blind, politically defeated poet's turn, which Chapter 12 covers, toward composing his greatest poetry.

## Glossary

- ***Areopagitica*** — Milton's 1644 prose oration opposing government licensing and censorship of the press.
- ***Eikon Basilike*** — a purported royal memoir published after Charles I's execution, presenting him as a Christian martyr; the target of Milton's *Eikonoklastes*.
- **Latin Secretary** — Milton's official post (Secretary for Foreign Tongues) under Cromwell's Council of State, 1649–53.
- **Masque** — see Chapter 7; the courtly entertainment form Milton adapted for *Comus*.
