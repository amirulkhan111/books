## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The two poets in this chapter, Sir Thomas Wyatt and Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey, were both courtiers, and both lived and wrote right inside the volatile politics of Henry VIII's court — the very same court whose matrimonial and religious crises drove the Reformation you studied in Chapter 1. That court could be lethal, and both poets felt it directly. Wyatt moved in Anne Boleyn's circle, and when she fell from favour and was executed in 1536, he was imprisoned in the Tower of London — he survived, but several men around him did not. Surrey, a generation younger, was executed for treason in January 1547, just days before Henry VIII himself died, another casualty of the same court's deadly factional infighting.

Despite writing under this kind of pressure, both poets changed English poetry permanently. Their two great innovations — the sonnet, and blank verse — arrived in English through close, direct engagement with Italian Renaissance models, continuing exactly the kind of cross-fertilization with continental learning you saw humanism driving in Chapter 1. And here's a detail worth remembering as you read this chapter: almost none of either poet's work was printed during their own lifetime.

## Literary Movements

You'll often see Wyatt and Surrey called jointly the "fathers of the English sonnet" — but their actual contributions are quite different from each other, and it's worth keeping them distinct. **Wyatt** brought the **Petrarchan (Italian) sonnet** into English: an octave, eight lines typically rhyming abbaabba, followed by a sestet, six lines in a more variable rhyme pattern. He built this mostly through close translation and looser adaptation of the fourteenth-century Italian poet Petrarch, alongside experiments with other imported forms (the rondeau, terza rima, ottava rima) and homegrown satire and epigram. **Surrey**, a generation younger, reshaped the sonnet into a new structure: three independently rhymed quatrains (typically abab cdcd efef) followed by a closing couplet (gg). This is what we now call the **English, or "Shakespearean," sonnet form** — and notice why it caught on: English simply doesn't have as many available rhymes as Italian, so a structure needing fewer matching rhymes per section suited the language better. This form would go on to dominate the great Elizabethan sonnet sequences of Chapter 3, and above all Shakespeare's own sonnets in Chapter 6.

Surrey has a second major innovation to his name, arguably even more consequential: **blank verse**, meaning unrhymed iambic pentameter, which he introduced in his translation of the *Aeneid*. Blank verse would go on to become the dominant verse form of English Renaissance drama, shaping everything Marlowe (Chapter 5) and Shakespeare would later achieve on stage.

## Author Profiles

**Sir Thomas Wyatt** (1503–1542) — Kent-born courtier and diplomat, educated at St John's College, Cambridge; entered Henry VIII's diplomatic service around 1526. Moved in the same court circle as Anne Boleyn, with whom he was rumoured (without firm evidence of a physical relationship) to have been romantically involved before Henry VIII's interest in her became apparent; imprisoned in the Tower of London in 1536 amid Anne Boleyn's fall, he was released without execution, unlike several other men associated with her. Wyatt is credited with introducing the Petrarchan sonnet, and several other Italian and French verse forms, into English poetry, alongside a body of satires, epigrams, and lyrics notable for their psychological complexity and courtly wariness.

**Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey** (c. 1516/1517–1547) — Aristocratic courtier-poet, a generation younger than Wyatt; developed the distinctively English sonnet form (three quatrains and a couplet) that would later be used by Shakespeare, and, in his translation of Books II and IV of Virgil's *Aeneid* (*Certain Bokes of Virgiles Aenaeis*, composed c. 1540, published 1557), introduced blank verse into English — a self-conscious effort to create an English vernacular equivalent to the unrhymed dactylic hexameter of classical epic. Executed for treason on 19 January 1547, in the final volatile weeks of Henry VIII's reign, on charges centred on his quartering of the royal arms.

**Richard Tottel** (d. 1594) — London printer and publisher, based at Temple Bar on Fleet Street, whose main business was law-book printing; his 5 June 1557 publication of *Songes and Sonettes* ("Tottel's Miscellany") — featuring 40 poems by Surrey, 96 by Wyatt, 40 by Nicholas Grimald, and 95 by unknown authors, none previously printed — was the first printed anthology of English poetry and the primary means by which Wyatt's and Surrey's work reached a wide readership, a full decade or more after both poets' deaths.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"Whoso List to Hunt"** (Wyatt). This poem is part translation, part imitation of Petrarch's *Rima* 190, and it recasts Petrarch's original conceit — hunting a deer — as an extended metaphor for pursuing a courtly woman who is unattainable because she already belongs to someone else. Read the ending carefully: the deer wears a collar reading "Noli me tangere . . . for Caesar's I am," echoing the old tradition of imperial deer marked as royal property. Most readers take this as a discreet, genuinely dangerous allusion to Anne Boleyn's position as Henry VIII's ("Caesar's") claimed possession, likely written in the late 1520s or early 1530s while the king was courting her.
- **Surrey's translation of the *Aeneid*, Books II and IV**. By rendering Virgil's Latin epic in unrhymed iambic pentameter, instead of any rhymed English form, Surrey effectively invented blank verse as an English equivalent to classical unrhymed metre. He composed it around 1540, but it wasn't published until 1557, after his death, alongside Tottel's *Miscellany*. Small as this translation looks on its own, its formal innovation became foundational for practically the whole later tradition of English dramatic and epic verse.
- ***Songes and Sonettes* (Tottel's Miscellany)**. Tottel printed Wyatt's and Surrey's poems, often quietly smoothing their metre to suit contemporary taste, alongside other courtly verse of the period. In doing so, he created the first substantial printed readership for English lyric poetry, and directly shaped the Elizabethan sonnet trend you'll meet in the next chapter.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Wyatt's roughness against Surrey's smoothness.** Critics have long contrasted Wyatt's often metrically irregular, psychologically compressed, guarded verse with Surrey's more regular, "smoother" style. Don't read this purely as a difference in personal talent — it may also reflect their different positions at court: Wyatt navigating direct, immediate political danger, Surrey enjoying a more secure aristocratic status, even though it ultimately proved just as fatal.
- **How far should we trust the biographical reading of "Whoso List to Hunt"?** Reading this poem as coded commentary on Anne Boleyn is a strong, well-established tradition — and the Caesar allusion makes it genuinely plausible. But it's also a useful case study in a recurring temptation when reading courtier poetry from this period: treating every Petrarchan adaptation as veiled political autobiography. Be cautious about extending this kind of reading to all of Wyatt's work.
- **Can you trust Tottel's printed text of Wyatt?** Not entirely. Textual scholars have shown Tottel frequently smoothed Wyatt's characteristically irregular metre into something more conventional for contemporary taste. That means readers for centuries were actually reading a normalized, "Tottelized" Wyatt, not his original, rougher verse — a caution about editorial mediation you've already met once before, with Caxton's editorial interventions in Part II, Chapter 9.
- **Why does Surrey's blank verse matter so much, given how minor the translation itself seems?** Because blank verse went on to become the standard medium of English Renaissance drama — you'll see it at work in Marlowe (Chapter 5) and Shakespeare (Chapter 6) — literary historians rank Surrey's translation among the single most consequential formal innovations in the whole history of English verse, even though the translation itself was a comparatively minor, belatedly published work.

## Chapter Summary

Sir Thomas Wyatt and Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey, both court poets whose lives ended in the lethal politics of Henry VIII's reign, together brought the sonnet into English poetry through direct engagement with Italian models. Wyatt adapted Petrarch's octave-sestet form, as in "Whoso List to Hunt," widely read as coded commentary on Anne Boleyn; Surrey reshaped it into the three-quatrain-and-couplet structure Shakespeare would later use. Surrey also introduced blank verse into English, through his *Aeneid* translation — a formal innovation of foundational importance for all the English drama and epic poetry that followed. Neither poet saw his work substantially printed in his own lifetime; both reached a wide readership only through Richard Tottel's 1557 *Songes and Sonettes*, the first printed anthology of English poetry, which directly shaped the Elizabethan sonnet tradition you'll explore in the next chapter.

## Glossary

- **Blank verse** — unrhymed iambic pentameter, introduced into English by Surrey's translation of the *Aeneid*.
- **English (Shakespearean) sonnet** — a fourteen-line sonnet form of three quatrains and a concluding couplet, developed by Surrey.
- **Petrarchan (Italian) sonnet** — a fourteen-line sonnet form of an octave and a sestet, associated with the Italian poet Petrarch and introduced into English by Wyatt.
- **Volta** — the "turn" in a sonnet's argument or tone, typically marking the transition between its structural sections.
