## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Sir Thomas Wyatt and Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey, both wrote as courtiers directly enmeshed in the volatile politics of Henry VIII's court — the same court whose matrimonial and religious crises drove the Reformation surveyed in Chapter 1. Wyatt's career intersected directly with the fall of Anne Boleyn, in whose circle he moved and whose 1536 execution he witnessed (or narrowly avoided sharing the fate of) from imprisonment in the Tower of London; Surrey, a generation younger, was executed for treason in January 1547, days before Henry VIII's own death, a victim of the same court's lethal factional politics. Both poets' literary innovations — the sonnet and blank verse, respectively — entered English poetry through direct, close engagement with Italian Renaissance models, continuing the humanist cross-fertilization with continental learning traced in Chapter 1, but almost none of their work was printed in their own lifetimes.

## Literary Movements

Wyatt and Surrey are jointly credited as the "fathers of the English sonnet," but their formal contributions differ significantly. **Wyatt** introduced the **Petrarchan (Italian) sonnet** into English — an octave (eight lines, typically rhyming abbaabba) followed by a sestet (six lines, in varying rhyme patterns) — through close translation and looser adaptation of poems by the fourteenth-century Italian poet Petrarch, alongside experiments in other imported forms (the rondeau, terza rima, ottava rima) and native satirical and epigrammatic modes. **Surrey**, a generation younger, reshaped the sonnet into what became the **English (or "Shakespearean") sonnet form** — three quatrains (each rhyming independently, typically abab cdcd efef) followed by a concluding couplet (gg) — a structure better suited to English's comparative scarcity of rhyme compared to Italian, and one that would dominate the great Elizabethan sonnet sequences of Chapter 3 and, above all, Shakespeare's own sonnets (Chapter 6). Surrey's second major formal innovation, **blank verse** (unrhymed iambic pentameter), introduced in his translation of the *Aeneid*, would become the dominant verse form of English Renaissance drama, decisively shaping the achievements of Marlowe (Chapter 5) and Shakespeare.

## Author Profiles

**Sir Thomas Wyatt** (1503–1542) — Kent-born courtier and diplomat, educated at St John's College, Cambridge; entered Henry VIII's diplomatic service around 1526. Moved in the same court circle as Anne Boleyn, with whom he was rumoured (without firm evidence of a physical relationship) to have been romantically involved before Henry VIII's interest in her became apparent; imprisoned in the Tower of London in 1536 amid Anne Boleyn's fall, he was released without execution, unlike several other men associated with her. Wyatt is credited with introducing the Petrarchan sonnet, and several other Italian and French verse forms, into English poetry, alongside a body of satires, epigrams, and lyrics notable for their psychological complexity and courtly wariness.

**Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey** (c. 1516/1517–1547) — Aristocratic courtier-poet, a generation younger than Wyatt; developed the distinctively English sonnet form (three quatrains and a couplet) that would later be used by Shakespeare, and, in his translation of Books II and IV of Virgil's *Aeneid* (*Certain Bokes of Virgiles Aenaeis*, composed c. 1540, published 1557), introduced blank verse into English — a self-conscious effort to create an English vernacular equivalent to the unrhymed dactylic hexameter of classical epic. Executed for treason on 19 January 1547, in the final volatile weeks of Henry VIII's reign, on charges centred on his quartering of the royal arms.

**Richard Tottel** (d. 1594) — London printer and publisher, based at Temple Bar on Fleet Street, whose main business was law-book printing; his 5 June 1557 publication of *Songes and Sonettes* ("Tottel's Miscellany") — featuring 40 poems by Surrey, 96 by Wyatt, 40 by Nicholas Grimald, and 95 by unknown authors, none previously printed — was the first printed anthology of English poetry and the primary means by which Wyatt's and Surrey's work reached a wide readership, a full decade or more after both poets' deaths.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"Whoso List to Hunt"** (Wyatt) — A partial translation, partial imitation of Petrarch's *Rima* 190 ("Una candida cerva sopra l'erba"), recasting the Petrarchan hind-hunt conceit as an extended metaphor for courtly pursuit of an unattainable, already-claimed woman; the poem's closing allusion to a collar reading "Noli me tangere . . . for Caesar's I am" (echoing the ancient tradition of imperial deer marked as royal property) is widely read as a discreet, dangerous allusion to Anne Boleyn's position as Henry VIII's ("Caesar's") claimed possession, composed probably in the late 1520s or early 1530s during her courtship by the king.
- **Surrey's translation of the *Aeneid*, Books II and IV** — By rendering Virgil's Latin epic in unrhymed iambic pentameter rather than a rhymed English form, Surrey created blank verse as a deliberate vernacular equivalent to classical unrhymed quantitative metre; though composed around 1540, the translation was not published until 1557 (posthumously, alongside Tottel's *Miscellany*), and its formal innovation would prove foundational for the entire subsequent tradition of English dramatic and epic verse.
- ***Songes and Sonettes* (Tottel's Miscellany)** — By printing Wyatt's and Surrey's poems (often silently regularizing their metre to contemporary taste) alongside other courtly verse, Tottel's anthology created the first substantial printed readership for English lyric poetry and directly shaped the Elizabethan sonnet vogue of the following decades (Chapter 3).

## Critical Perspectives

- **Wyatt's roughness versus Surrey's smoothness.** Critics have traditionally contrasted Wyatt's often metrically irregular, psychologically compressed, and tonally guarded verse with Surrey's more metrically regular, "smoother" style — a contrast sometimes read as reflecting their different court positions (Wyatt's precarious survival amid direct political danger versus Surrey's more secure, if ultimately equally fatal, aristocratic status) as much as differing poetic temperament.
- **Biographical reading and its limits.** The strong biographical tradition reading "Whoso List to Hunt" as coded commentary on Anne Boleyn exemplifies a recurring critical temptation — and risk — in reading courtier poetry of this period as veiled political/personal allegory; while the Caesar allusion makes such a reading plausible and widely accepted, scholars caution against treating all of Wyatt's Petrarchan adaptations as straightforward biographical code.
- **Tottel's editorial intervention.** Textual scholars have shown that Tottel's printed texts frequently smoothed Wyatt's characteristically irregular metre to conform to more regular contemporary taste, meaning that readers for centuries encountered a normalized "Tottelized" Wyatt rather than his original, rougher verse — a caution about editorial mediation directly comparable to Caxton's editorial interventions in medieval texts (Part II, Chapter 9).
- **Surrey's blank verse as a hinge innovation.** Because blank verse became the standard medium of English Renaissance drama (Marlowe, Chapter 5; Shakespeare, Chapter 6), Surrey's translation — though a comparatively minor and belatedly published work in itself — is regarded by literary historians as one of the single most consequential formal innovations in the history of English verse.

## Chapter Summary

Sir Thomas Wyatt and Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey, both court poets whose lives ended in the lethal politics of Henry VIII's reign, jointly introduced the sonnet into English poetry through direct engagement with Italian models: Wyatt adapting Petrarch's octave-sestet form (as in "Whoso List to Hunt," widely read as coded commentary on Anne Boleyn) and Surrey reshaping it into the three-quatrain-and-couplet structure later used by Shakespeare. Surrey's translation of the *Aeneid* additionally introduced blank verse into English, a formal innovation of foundational importance for later English drama and epic. Neither poet saw his work substantially printed in his lifetime; both reached a wide readership only through Richard Tottel's 1557 *Songes and Sonettes*, the first printed anthology of English poetry, which directly shaped the Elizabethan sonnet tradition explored in the following chapter.

## Glossary

- **Blank verse** — unrhymed iambic pentameter, introduced into English by Surrey's translation of the *Aeneid*.
- **English (Shakespearean) sonnet** — a fourteen-line sonnet form of three quatrains and a concluding couplet, developed by Surrey.
- **Petrarchan (Italian) sonnet** — a fourteen-line sonnet form of an octave and a sestet, associated with the Italian poet Petrarch and introduced into English by Wyatt.
- **Volta** — the "turn" in a sonnet's argument or tone, typically marking the transition between its structural sections.
