## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The prose treated in this chapter developed alongside, and in significant tension with, the poetic and dramatic achievements of Chapters 2–9: Francis Bacon's programme for a new empirical science, Thomas Browne's idiosyncratic meditations on faith and mortality, and the King James Bible's monumental collaborative translation project together represent English prose's growing capacity, by the early seventeenth century, to carry philosophical, scientific, and theological argument of the highest sophistication — continuing a vernacular expansion of English prose traceable back through Tyndale's Bible translation (Chapter 1) and, more distantly, the Alfredian and Ælfrician prose traditions of Part I.

## Literary Movements

This chapter's prose spans three distinct registers: Bacon's **aphoristic, plain-style essay**, developing a compact, pointed English prose deliberately suited to practical worldly wisdom and, in his scientific writing, to methodical philosophical argument; Browne's **ornate, Latinate meditative prose**, characterized by elaborate periodic sentences, learned vocabulary (Browne is credited with coining or popularizing numerous English words from Latin roots), and a distinctive fusion of scientific curiosity with religious wonder; and the **collaborative, deliberately dignified translation prose** of the King James Bible, produced by committee yet achieving a rhythmic, quotable register that would prove the single most influential body of English prose for the following three centuries.

---

## Author Profiles

**Francis Bacon** (1561–1626) — Statesman, philosopher, and essayist; rose to Lord Chancellor of England under James I before his 1621 conviction on bribery charges (a common contemporary judicial practice, though Bacon's prosecution is sometimes read as also politically motivated) ended his public career, after which he devoted his remaining years to philosophical and scientific writing. His *Essays* (1597; expanded 1612 and 1625) established a compact, aphoristic English prose style addressing practical and moral topics ("Of Truth," "Of Studies," "Of Death"), while *The Advancement of Learning* (1605) and *Novum Organum* (1620) laid out his programme for a new, empirically grounded natural philosophy based on systematic, inductive observation — a foundational contribution to the methodology of modern science, developed further in his posthumously published utopian work *New Atlantis* (1627), which imagines a scientifically organized ideal society.

**Sir Thomas Browne** (1605–1682) — London-born physician, educated at Winchester, Oxford, and Leiden (M.D. 1633); practised medicine in Norwich from 1637 until his death, and was knighted in 1671. His *Religio Medici* ("The Religion of a Doctor," 1643) meditates personally and discursively on the relationship between Christian faith and scientific/medical knowledge, achieving wide European readership (and, notably, placement on the Catholic Church's *Index of Prohibited Books*); *Pseudodoxia Epidemica* (1646), subtitled *Enquiries into Very Many Received Tenets and Commonly Presumed Truths*, systematically examines and debunks popular misconceptions through empirical investigation; and *Hydriotaphia, Urn Burial* (1658), prompted by the discovery of ancient burial urns near Norwich, offers an elaborate meditation on mortality, memory, and the human desire for immortality, widely regarded as a high point of English baroque prose style.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Essays*** (Bacon, 1597–1625) — Compact, epigrammatic prose pieces on practical and moral subjects, developed across three progressively expanded editions; Bacon's plain, pointed style ("Some books are to be tasted, others to be swallowed, and some few to be chewed and digested") established a new English model for concise, worldly-wise essayistic prose, markedly distinct from the more elaborate Ciceronian or Senecan rhetorical traditions.
- ***Novum Organum*** ("New Instrument," Bacon, 1620) — Proposes a systematic method of inductive reasoning — patient accumulation and comparison of empirical evidence, organized through "tables of discovery," to identify underlying causes and natural "forms" — explicitly positioned as a replacement for Aristotelian deductive logic; also introduces Bacon's influential concept of the "idols of the mind" (systematic sources of human error, including linguistic, individual, and institutional biases), a foundational contribution to the philosophy and methodology of the coming scientific revolution.
- ***Religio Medici*** (Browne, 1643) — A personal, digressive meditation reconciling Browne's simultaneous commitments to Christian faith and scientific/medical rationalism, notable for its tolerant, exploratory tone and its willingness to acknowledge doubt and paradox within orthodox belief.
- ***Hydriotaphia, Urn Burial*** (Browne, 1658) — Structured around the discovery of ancient burial urns, the work moves from historical and archaeological discussion of funerary customs into an increasingly elaborate, elegiac meditation on time, oblivion, and mortality, culminating in prose widely regarded as among the most sonorous and philosophically resonant in the English language.
- **The King James Bible** (1611) — Produced by forty-seven scholars organized into six translation committees (two each at Westminster, Oxford, and Cambridge), drawing extensively on Tyndale's earlier translation (Chapter 1) — an estimated 83 percent of the KJV New Testament retains Tyndale's wording — alongside the Bishops' Bible (1568) and other prior versions; intended by James I as an authoritative, unifying translation superseding both the more overtly Puritan Geneva Bible and the officially sanctioned but less popular Bishops' Bible, and achieving, through its deliberately dignified, rhythmically cadenced prose, unparalleled and enduring influence on subsequent English literary style.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Bacon: philosopher of science or fallen statesman?** Critics and historians continue to weigh how far Bacon's public disgrace (the 1621 bribery conviction) should affect assessment of his philosophical achievement; the now-common view treats his scientific and literary legacy as substantially independent of, though inevitably shadowed by, his compromised public career.
- **Browne's stylistic extravagance.** Browne's elaborate, Latinate sentence structures and extensive neologistic vocabulary have drawn both admiration (for their sonorous, meditative power, especially in *Urn Burial*) and criticism (for occasional obscurity and self-indulgent ornamentation) — a stylistic debate directly relevant to broader period-wide questions about "plain" versus "ornate" prose styles, of which Bacon and Browne represent near-opposite poles.
- **Committee translation and literary unity.** A recurring critical question about the King James Bible concerns how a text produced by a committee of forty-seven scholars, working across six separate translation groups, nonetheless achieved such remarkable stylistic consistency and rhythmic power — generally explained by the shared translation principles and extensive cross-checking process established at Hampton Court, and by the translators' common debt to Tyndale's already highly accomplished earlier prose.
- **The King James Bible's afterlife.** Because the King James Bible became, for over three centuries, the single most widely read and memorized English text, its phrasing, rhythms, and vocabulary are widely regarded by literary historians as one of the most pervasive influences on subsequent English literature — a linguistic legacy comparable in scale, though different in character (collective and institutional rather than individually authored), to Chaucer's (Part II) and Tyndale's (Chapter 1) contributions.

## Chapter Summary

English prose achieved new philosophical, scientific, and theological sophistication in this period through three distinct traditions: Francis Bacon's plain, aphoristic *Essays* and his methodologically foundational *Novum Organum*, establishing both a durable model of English essayistic style and the empirical scientific method later developed by the Royal Society; Sir Thomas Browne's elaborately Latinate, meditative prose, reconciling Christian faith with scientific curiosity across *Religio Medici*, *Pseudodoxia Epidemica*, and *Hydriotaphia*; and the collaboratively produced King James Bible (1611), which, building substantially on Tyndale's earlier translation (Chapter 1), achieved a stylistic unity and lasting linguistic influence disproportionate to its committee origins. Together, alongside the poetic and dramatic achievements of the preceding chapters, these prose traditions demonstrate the full range and maturity English literary language had achieved by the mid-seventeenth century, on the eve of the political and cultural rupture — civil war, regicide, and the 1642 theatre closures (Chapter 4) — that closes this Part.

## Glossary

- **Authorized Version** — an alternative name for the King James Bible (1611), reflecting its royal commissioning.
- **Idols of the mind** — Bacon's term (in *Novum Organum*) for systematic sources of human intellectual error.
- **Inductive reasoning** — Bacon's proposed scientific method of drawing general conclusions from accumulated empirical observation.
