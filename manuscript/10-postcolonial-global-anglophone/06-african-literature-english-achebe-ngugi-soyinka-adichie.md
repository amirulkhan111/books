## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The 1884–85 Berlin Conference, at which European powers divided Africa among themselves with little regard for existing ethnic, linguistic, or political boundaries, created the arbitrary colonial borders that would become independent nations decades later — nations frequently containing multiple, sometimes historically rival ethnic groups within a single artificial state, a lasting source of postcolonial political instability this chapter's writers confront directly. British colonial rule in West and East Africa produced, among other territories, Nigeria (independent in 1960), Kenya (independent in 1963, following the Mau Mau uprising of the 1950s), and Ghana — already met in Part IX, Chapter 1, as the first sub-Saharan African colony to gain independence, in 1957.

Independence, though, did not automatically bring writers freedom from repression. As you'll see below, both Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o and Wole Soyinka were imprisoned not by colonial authorities but by their own postcolonial governments — a sobering reminder that the struggle these writers describe did not end neatly with formal political independence.

## Literary Movements

This chapter's central theoretical debate concerns the very question Raja Rao's preface to *Kanthapura* raised in Chapter 5: what should African writers do with English, the colonizer's language? **Chinua Achebe** argued that English could be reshaped and "made to bear the weight" of African experience, deliberately incorporating Igbo proverbs, vocabulary, and speech rhythms into his own English prose. **Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o** reached the opposite conclusion: in *Decolonising the Mind* (1986), discussed fully below, he argued that language itself is inseparable from cultural imperialism, meaning genuine decolonization requires African writers to abandon European languages altogether in favor of their own — a conviction he acted on directly by ending his own career as an English-language novelist to write instead in his native Gikuyu. This unresolved disagreement between two of African literature's most significant figures remains one of postcolonial literary theory's defining debates.

Wole Soyinka's drama pursues a different but related project: blending Yoruba mythology and ritual performance traditions directly with Western dramatic form, refusing to present African cultural material as simply exotic local color within an otherwise conventional Western play. Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie, writing a generation later, extended Edward Said's critique of Orientalism (Chapter 1) into a widely influential contemporary formulation: her concept of **"the danger of a single story"** warns against reducing any culture or people to one flattening narrative, whether pitying, exoticizing, or otherwise reductive — a caution as relevant to how the West has represented Africa as Said's original argument was to its representation of the Middle East and Asia.

## Author Profiles

**Chinua Achebe** (1930–2013), already met in Part VIII, Chapter 4, for his influential critique of Conrad's *Heart of Darkness*, established his own reputation as a novelist with *Things Fall Apart* (1958), discussed fully below, followed by *No Longer at Ease* (1960), *Arrow of God* (1964), and *A Man of the People* (1966), together tracing Nigerian society across the colonial period and its uneasy transition to independence.

**Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o** (b. 1938) began his career writing in English, publishing *Weep Not, Child* (1964) and *A Grain of Wheat* (1967), before his co-authored Gikuyu-language play *Ngaahika Ndeenda* ("I Will Marry When I Want"), performed by ordinary villagers rather than professional actors, led the Kenyan government to detain him without trial in 1977–78. He reportedly wrote his prison memoir and his first Gikuyu-language novel on toilet paper during this imprisonment, and afterward committed permanently to writing fiction in Gikuyu rather than English, setting out the theoretical case for this decision in *Decolonising the Mind* (1986), discussed fully below.

**Wole Soyinka** (b. 1934) became the first African, and the first Black writer of any nationality, to win the Nobel Prize in Literature, in 1986, recognized for a body of dramatic work blending Yoruba ritual and mythology with Western theatrical form, most famously in *Death and the King's Horseman* (1975), discussed fully below. During the Nigerian Civil War, Soyinka was imprisoned for nearly two years, largely in solitary confinement, for attempting to broker peace between the warring sides, an experience he documented in the poetry collection *A Shuttle in the Crypt* and the memoir *The Man Died* (both 1972).

**Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie** (b. 1977) represents a younger generation of Nigerian writers, achieving acclaim with *Purple Hibiscus* (2003), *Half of a Yellow Sun* (2006), discussed fully below, and *Americanah* (2013), which explores race, immigration, and identity through a Nigerian woman's experience in the United States. Her 2009 talk "The Danger of a Single Story," discussed above, and her later feminist writing have made her one of contemporary African literature's most publicly influential voices.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Achebe, *Things Fall Apart* (1958).** Following Okonkwo, a proud, respected leader in the fictional Igbo village of Umuofia whose rigid, hypermasculine values and fear of weakness precipitate his own tragic downfall as British colonial administration and Christian missionaries arrive and disrupt traditional Igbo society, the novel takes its title directly from a line in Yeats's "The Second Coming" (Part VIII, Chapter 2) — a deliberate, pointed act of literary reclamation, using a canonical Irish modernist poem about civilizational collapse to frame an African novel about the collapse a very different colonial encounter actually caused.
- **Ngũgĩ, *Decolonising the Mind* (1986).** Arguing that European languages carry European cultural assumptions inseparably within them, the book makes the theoretical case for Ngũgĩ's own practical decision to write exclusively in Gikuyu from this point onward, insisting that genuine African literary decolonization requires reclaiming African languages themselves, not merely African subject matter expressed in a European tongue.
- **Soyinka, *Death and the King's Horseman* (1975).** Based on a real 1946 colonial Nigerian incident, the play follows a Yoruba chief's horseman, traditionally expected to take his own life to accompany his king into death, whose ritual suicide is instead prevented by British colonial officials — an intervention that, within the play's own Yoruba cosmology, disrupts a necessary metaphysical transition rather than simply "saving a life" in straightforwardly humanitarian terms. Soyinka himself has insisted the play should not be read as a simple "clash of cultures" morality tale, but as a serious meditation on cosmic duty and transition that colonial ignorance catastrophically misunderstands.
- **Adichie, *Half of a Yellow Sun* (2006).** Set during the Nigerian Civil War that also shaped Soyinka's own imprisonment, the novel follows several characters' intertwined lives through the Biafran secession and its devastating human cost, bringing a later generation's narrative skill to a conflict still shaping Nigerian society and literature decades afterward.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Achebe or Ngũgĩ right about English?** This chapter's central debate remains genuinely unresolved: Achebe's approach has produced internationally influential literature reaching a vast global readership while retaining distinctly African rhythms and content, while Ngũgĩ's position insists that reach itself may come at the cost of true linguistic and cultural decolonization — a tension between international visibility and linguistic authenticity that recurs, in different forms, across nearly every literature studied in this Part.
- **Is *Death and the King's Horseman* a "clash of cultures" tragedy?** Despite Soyinka's own explicit objection to this reading, many productions and critical discussions have nonetheless framed the play in exactly these terms; the tension between the author's stated intention and how audiences actually receive the work raises a genuine question about how much authority an author's own commentary should hold over a play's public interpretation.
- **Does "the danger of a single story" risk becoming its own flattening slogan?** Adichie's concept has proven enormously influential and widely quoted, but some critics note that its very popularity risks reducing a nuanced argument about representation into a simplified catchphrase — an irony worth noticing in a concept specifically about the danger of oversimplification.
- **What does the imprisonment of both Ngũgĩ and Soyinka by their own governments suggest about postcolonial African literature?** Both writers faced genuine danger not from colonial authorities but from the independent African governments that succeeded them, a sobering reminder that formal political independence did not automatically guarantee writers' freedom of expression, and that postcolonial literature's engagement with power remains an ongoing, unfinished project rather than one resolved by independence alone.

## Chapter Summary

African literature in English emerged from the arbitrary colonial borders the Berlin Conference drew across the continent, and its writers have confronted not only colonial disruption but also, in Ngũgĩ's and Soyinka's cases, imprisonment by their own postcolonial governments. Chinua Achebe's *Things Fall Apart*, its title drawn directly from Yeats, dramatized traditional Igbo society's collapse under colonial pressure while reshaping English to carry that specifically African story. Ngũgĩ's dramatic rejection of English for Gikuyu, argued fully in *Decolonising the Mind*, stands as the sharpest possible counter-position to Achebe's approach, and this chapter's central, still-unresolved debate. Wole Soyinka's *Death and the King's Horseman* blends Yoruba ritual with Western dramatic form to dramatize colonial interference in indigenous cosmology, while Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's concept of "the danger of a single story" extends Said's critique of Orientalism into a widely influential contemporary warning against flattening any culture into a single narrative. Chapter 7 now turns to Caribbean literature, through Walcott, Naipaul, and Kincaid.
