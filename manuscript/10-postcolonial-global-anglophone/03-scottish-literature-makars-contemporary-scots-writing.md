## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Scottish literature works across three genuinely distinct language traditions, and keeping them separate matters for everything in this chapter. **Scottish Gaelic**, a Celtic language related to Irish Gaelic (Chapter 2), was historically dominant in the Highlands and Islands. **Scots**, by contrast, is a Germanic language descended from Northern Middle English, historically dominant in the Lowlands and at the medieval Scottish royal court — the language of the Makars, discussed below, and later of Robert Burns, already studied in Part V, Chapter 7. And **Scottish English** refers to the standard English spoken and written in Scotland today, itself inflected by both earlier traditions. This chapter follows Scots and Scottish English as its primary threads, while keeping Scottish Gaelic's parallel, equally significant history in view.

Scotland's political relationship to England took a genuinely different path from Ireland's. The 1603 Union of the Crowns joined the two kingdoms under a single monarch, James VI of Scotland (also James I of England), without merging their parliaments; full political union came only in 1707, when the Acts of Union merged the Scottish and English parliaments into a single Parliament of Great Britain — a union Scotland's own Parliament formally voted for, under real economic pressure following the catastrophic failure of the Darien scheme, a Scottish colonial venture in Panama that had bankrupted much of the Scottish nobility. This negotiated, if economically coerced, path to union — and Scots' own substantial subsequent participation in administering, trading with, and profiting from the British Empire — creates a genuine critical question this chapter will return to directly: does Scotland fit the postcolonial framework introduced in Chapter 1 in the same way Ireland does, or does its history demand a more qualified application of that same theory?

That said, Scotland's own internal history includes a genuinely devastating episode: the **Highland Clearances** (roughly 1750–1860), in which landlords forcibly evicted Highland tenant farmers to convert land to more profitable sheep farming, causing mass displacement and emigration that devastated Scottish Gaelic culture and language in ways that parallel, in outline, the Irish Great Famine's effect on Irish Gaelic (Chapter 2). As you'll see below, this destruction unfolded at almost exactly the same historical moment that Walter Scott's fiction was romanticizing and popularizing Highland culture for a mass British readership — a genuine tension worth holding in mind.

## Literary Movements

The **Makars** — Scots for "makers," the term used for the poets writing at the Scottish royal court in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries — are sometimes called "Scottish Chaucerians," a label worth using cautiously: while poets like Robert Henryson, William Dunbar, and Gavin Douglas clearly knew and admired Chaucer's work (Part II, Chapter 3), they developed a genuinely distinct literary tradition in Middle Scots rather than simply imitating an English model. Henryson's *Testament of Cresseid* extends Chaucer's own *Troilus and Criseyde* with an entirely original, harsher conclusion; Douglas's *Eneados*, a complete Scots translation of Virgil's *Aeneid*, was the first complete translation of a major classical epic into any form of English or Scots, a genuine landmark predating comparable English translations.

Centuries later, the twentieth-century **Scottish Renaissance**, led by the poet Hugh MacDiarmid, pursued a strikingly similar project to the Irish Literary Revival you met in Chapter 2, but aimed at reviving Scots itself rather than English: MacDiarmid championed what he called **"synthetic Scots"** or **Lallans** — a literary Scots deliberately assembled from historical dictionaries and older texts rather than simply transcribing contemporary spoken dialect, used to prove Scots could carry modernist literary ambition fully as well as English. Contemporary Scots writing, discussed below through Irvine Welsh and James Kelman, takes yet another approach, rendering actual spoken working-class Scots dialect directly and often phonetically on the page, provoking real debate about dialect's literary legitimacy within mainstream British literary institutions.

## Author Profiles

**William Dunbar** (c. 1459–1530) served as a court poet under King James IV, producing an unusually wide range of work — from devotional religious poetry to bawdy satire — that showcases Middle Scots's full expressive range. His "Lament for the Makaris," discussed fully below, remains his most enduringly studied poem.

**Walter Scott** (1771–1832) transformed the historical novel into a major literary form with *Waverley* (1814), discussed fully below, and its many successors, including *Rob Roy* (1817), which, alongside popularizing Highland Scotland for a mass British and international readership, also helped shape lasting, sometimes oversimplified popular images of Scottish identity — tartan, clan loyalty, romantic rebellion — that later critics have examined with real skepticism, particularly given their timing alongside the Highland Clearances' actual, contemporaneous devastation of the same culture.

**Hugh MacDiarmid** (1892–1978), the pen name of Christopher Murray Grieve, led the twentieth-century Scottish Renaissance's revival of literary Scots, discussed above, most ambitiously in his long poem *A Drunk Man Looks at the Thistle* (1926), discussed fully below.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Dunbar, "Lament for the Makaris."** Listing generations of dead poets, including Chaucer and Gower alongside earlier Scottish makars, the poem repeats the Latin refrain *timor mortis conturbat me* ("the fear of death disturbs me") after each stanza, building a genuinely moving meditation on mortality's universal reach across even the most celebrated poetic achievement.
- **Scott, *Waverley* (1814).** Following a young English officer's involvement in the 1745 Jacobite Rising through the Scottish Highlands, the novel essentially invented the historical novel as a genre, combining careful period research with romantic adventure — a combination immensely influential on later fiction throughout Europe, even as its romanticized Highland setting arrived just as the Highland Clearances were actively displacing the real communities it depicted.
- **MacDiarmid, *A Drunk Man Looks at the Thistle* (1926).** Following a drunk man's rambling, philosophically ambitious meditation on Scotland, whisky, and national identity, written entirely in MacDiarmid's own "synthetic Scots," the poem demonstrates that Scots could sustain genuinely modernist literary ambition — a direct, deliberate answer to any assumption that Scots was merely a quaint regional dialect unsuited to serious contemporary literature.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does Scotland genuinely fit the postcolonial framework introduced in Chapter 1?** Ireland's colonial history involved conquest, dispossession, and the systematic suppression of a subject population; Scotland's 1707 union was formally negotiated by its own Parliament, and Scots subsequently played a substantial, active role administering and profiting from the British Empire elsewhere — facts that lead some critics to treat Scotland's relationship to England as fundamentally different from a straightforward colonial one, even as others argue the Highland Clearances and Gaelic culture's suppression represent a genuinely internal colonial dynamic within Scotland itself.
- **Did Scott's fiction preserve Highland culture, or romantically tame it?** *Waverley* and its successors brought Highland Scotland to an enormous international audience with real narrative sympathy, but critics have long noted the uncomfortable timing: Scott's romantic vision of clan loyalty and rebellion reached its height of popularity exactly as the Highland Clearances were destroying the actual communities and Gaelic culture his fiction celebrated, effectively replacing a living culture with a marketable, safely historical image of it.
- **Is MacDiarmid's "synthetic Scots" a legitimate literary language, or an artificial invention?** Because MacDiarmid assembled his literary Scots partly from historical dictionaries rather than transcribing any single contemporary spoken dialect, some critics have questioned whether "synthetic Scots" represents a living language tradition at all; MacDiarmid's defenders argue that all literary languages involve some degree of deliberate artistic construction, and that his achievement lies precisely in proving Scots's continued capacity for serious literary ambition.
- **Did the Booker Prize controversy over Kelman's win reveal snobbery toward working-class dialect?** James Kelman's 1994 Booker Prize win for a novel rendering Glaswegian working-class speech and stream-of-consciousness technique directly provoked strong objections from some judges and critics, in terms some observers read as reflecting genuine class and dialect prejudice within Britain's literary establishment rather than purely aesthetic disagreement.

## Chapter Summary

Scottish literature works across three distinct language traditions — Scottish Gaelic, Scots, and Scottish English — and its political relationship to England, formalized through the 1707 Acts of Union, took a genuinely different, more negotiated path than Ireland's, complicating any simple application of Chapter 1's postcolonial framework, even as the Highland Clearances' devastation of Gaelic culture suggests an internal colonial dynamic within Scotland itself. The medieval Makars, particularly William Dunbar, established a distinct Middle Scots literary tradition alongside their acknowledged debt to Chaucer. Walter Scott's invention of the historical novel brought Highland Scotland to a mass international audience even as the Clearances were destroying the real culture his fiction romanticized. Hugh MacDiarmid's twentieth-century Scottish Renaissance revived literary Scots through deliberately constructed "synthetic Scots," while contemporary writers like Irvine Welsh and James Kelman render actual spoken dialect directly, provoking real debate, exemplified by Kelman's controversial Booker win, about dialect's literary legitimacy. Chapter 4 now turns to a third distinct tradition within these islands: Welsh writing in English, from Dylan Thomas and his successors.

## Glossary

- **Makar** — Scots for "maker" or poet; historically the term for court poets like Dunbar and Henryson, and revived in 2004 as the title for Scotland's national poet.
- **Middle Scots** — the distinct Germanic literary language, descended from Northern Middle English, used by the medieval Makars and later Robert Burns.
- **Synthetic Scots (Lallans)** — Hugh MacDiarmid's deliberately constructed literary Scots, assembled from historical dictionaries rather than transcribed contemporary dialect.
- **Highland Clearances** — the forced eviction of Highland tenant farmers (roughly 1750–1860) to convert land to sheep farming, devastating Scottish Gaelic culture.
