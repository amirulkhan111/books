## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Australia and Canada belong to a category postcolonial theory calls the **settler colony**, and this chapter asks you to hold in mind a genuine complication that this category introduces to everything you have studied so far in this Part. In Ireland, India, Africa, and the Caribbean, a relatively small number of British colonizers ruled over a large indigenous or enslaved population that remained, demographically, the overwhelming majority. In a settler colony, by contrast, European colonizers arrived in large numbers, displaced or killed the indigenous population through violence and disease, and became the demographic majority themselves. This produces a doubled postcolonial situation found nowhere else in this Part: the settler-descended majority has its own postcolonial relationship to Britain, developing a distinct national literature and identity independent of the "mother country" — while the surviving Indigenous population has an entirely different, and in crucial ways more urgent, postcolonial relationship to the settlers who now govern the land as their own. Both stories belong in this chapter, and neither can stand in for the other.

Britain's colonization of Australia began in 1788, when the First Fleet established a penal colony at Botany Bay, transporting convicted criminals to serve their sentences on the other side of the world. British colonizers claimed the continent under the legal fiction of **terra nullius** — "land belonging to no one" — treating Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples, whose ancestors had lived on the continent for over 60,000 years, as though they had no prior claim to the land at all. The consequences were catastrophic: frontier violence, introduced disease, and dispossession devastated Aboriginal populations, and government policy through much of the twentieth century forcibly removed Aboriginal children from their families to be raised in white institutions or foster families — the **Stolen Generations**, a policy that continued in various forms until the 1970s. Australia federated as an independent nation in 1901, immediately adopting the restrictive White Australia Policy, which was only fully dismantled in the 1970s. Legal recognition of Aboriginal land rights came far later still: the High Court's 1992 *Mabo* decision formally overturned terra nullius, and Prime Minister Kevin Rudd delivered a national apology to the Stolen Generations only in 2008.

Canada's colonial history layers a second complication on top of the settler-colonial pattern: Canada is a doubly colonial settler nation, founded on both French and British colonization. France established New France along the St. Lawrence River in the early seventeenth century; Britain took control of it under the 1763 Treaty of Paris, and the 1774 Quebec Act preserved French civil law and the Catholic Church's position in Quebec, embedding a lasting English-French duality into Canadian identity that has no equivalent elsewhere in this Part. Canada became a self-governing dominion in 1867. As in Australia, the arrival of European settlers devastated Indigenous peoples — First Nations, Métis, and Inuit — through violence, disease, and dispossession, and the Canadian government operated a residential school system from the 1880s until the last school closed in 1996, forcibly removing Indigenous children from their families and communities in a deliberate policy of cultural assimilation whose survivors and their descendants live with its consequences today. Canada's Truth and Reconciliation Commission, reporting in 2015, formally documented this history as **cultural genocide**.

## Literary Movements

Australian literature's earliest distinct national voice emerged from the **bush ballad** tradition of the late nineteenth century, when writers like Henry Lawson and Banjo Paterson turned to the harsh rural interior — "the bush" — as the setting for a specifically Australian identity, told in a plain-spoken, often wryly humorous vernacular voice that deliberately distanced itself from genteel English literary models. This nation-building project succeeded remarkably well: Paterson's "Waltzing Matilda" remains Australia's best-known unofficial anthem. Twentieth-century Australian fiction then moved toward international modernist ambition, above all in Patrick White's densely symbolic, psychologically searching novels, which won Australia's first Nobel Prize in Literature in 1973. Since the 1980s, Aboriginal Australian writers have produced a literature of their own that directly challenges the bush-ballad tradition's settler-centered national story, recovering suppressed history and asserting Aboriginal perspectives and storytelling traditions within English-language literary form.

Canadian literature's foundational modern gesture was critical rather than creative: Margaret Atwood's *Survival: A Thematic Guide to Canadian Literature* (1972) argued that Canadian literature, unlike the American frontier narrative of conquest, was organized around a central theme of **survival** — victims and survivors rather than conquerors — a claim that, like Chinua Achebe's and Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o's arguments about African literature (Chapter 6), sought to name and legitimate a national literary tradition on its own terms rather than by comparison to a dominant neighbor's. Canadian fiction's international reputation subsequently rested heavily on realist and modernist short-story and novel writing — above all Alice Munro's precisely observed short fiction, which won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 2013 — alongside Atwood's own genre-spanning career, which extends from realist fiction into speculative dystopia. As in Australia, Indigenous Canadian writers, including Thomas King and Tomson Highway, have built a literature directly engaging Indigenous storytelling traditions, the residential school legacy, and First Nations survival and resistance.

## Author Profiles

**Henry Lawson** (1867–1922) wrote short stories and ballads capturing the harshness and mateship of rural Australian bush life, including "The Drover's Wife," a foundational text of Australian short fiction.

**Banjo Paterson** (1864–1941), a contemporary and friendly rival of Lawson's, wrote the bush ballads "The Man from Snowy River" and "Waltzing Matilda" (1895), the latter of which became Australia's best-known unofficial national song.

**Patrick White** (1912–1990) won the 1973 Nobel Prize in Literature, becoming the first Australian to do so, for densely symbolic novels including *The Tree of Man* (1957) and *Voss* (1957), discussed fully below.

**Sally Morgan** (b. 1951), an Aboriginal Australian writer, established Aboriginal life-writing as a major literary form with *My Place* (1987), discussed fully below.

**Alexis Wright** (b. 1950), a Waanyi writer, has become Aboriginal Australian fiction's leading contemporary voice with *Carpentaria* (2006), discussed fully below.

**Margaret Atwood** (b. 1939) is Canada's most internationally prominent living writer, both as a novelist — *The Handmaid's Tale* (1985), discussed fully below — and as a critic, through *Survival* (1972), discussed above.

**Alice Munro** (1931–2024) won the 2013 Nobel Prize in Literature for a body of short fiction, discussed fully below, that established the short story as a form fully capable of sustaining a major literary career.

**Michael Ondaatje** (b. 1943), born in Sri Lanka and immigrated to Canada as a teenager, won the Booker Prize for *The English Patient* (1992), discussed fully below, and his career previews Chapter 9's diaspora and migrant writing.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Paterson, "Waltzing Matilda" (1895).** Opening "Once a jolly swagman camped by a billabong," the ballad follows an itinerant worker who drowns himself rather than be arrested for stealing a sheep — a story of resistance to authority told in deliberately Australian vernacular vocabulary (*swagman*, *billabong*, *jumbuck*) that helped establish a distinctly national poetic voice.
- **White, *Voss* (1957).** Loosely based on the historical explorer Ludwig Leichhardt's disappearance in the Australian interior, the novel follows an obsessive expedition leader's psychological and near-mystical confrontation with the vast, indifferent Australian landscape, using dense, symbolically layered modernist prose that helped win White his Nobel Prize.
- **Morgan, *My Place* (1987).** A memoir recovering the author's own Aboriginal heritage, long hidden by her family out of fear and shame under assimilationist pressure, the book became a landmark text for bringing Aboriginal family history and the Stolen Generations' effects to a mass Australian readership.
- **Wright, *Carpentaria* (2006).** Set in a fictional Gulf of Carpentaria town, the novel blends Aboriginal oral storytelling traditions with ambitious, non-linear narrative form to depict Aboriginal community life, environmental struggle, and conflict with a mining company — extending, in an Australian context, the postcolonial concern with land and dispossession introduced in Chapter 1.
- **Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale* (1985).** Imagining a future totalitarian regime that strips women of legal and reproductive autonomy, the novel stands in the dystopian tradition of George Orwell's *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (Part IX, Chapter 1), using speculative fiction to warn against real, observable political tendencies rather than predicting a specific future.
- **Munro, *Lives of Girls and Women* (1971).** A linked story cycle following a girl's coming of age in small-town Ontario, the book exemplifies Munro's characteristic technique: precisely observed realist detail revealing sudden, often unsettling emotional depths beneath ordinary domestic surfaces.
- **Ondaatje, *The English Patient* (1992).** Set in an Italian villa at the close of the Second World War, the novel weaves together the memories of a badly burned, unidentified patient with the stories of the nurse, thief, and sapper who care for him, using a fragmented, achronological structure to explore displacement, identity, and the shifting boundaries of national belonging.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does the settler-colony category genuinely fit within Chapter 1's postcolonial framework, or does it demand its own separate theory?** Some critics argue that settler-descended Australian and Canadian literatures are properly postcolonial, having developed distinct national identities in reaction against British cultural dominance; others insist that because the same settler societies became colonizers of Indigenous peoples in their own right, applying "postcolonial" to settler-descended literature without centering Indigenous experience risks obscuring an ongoing colonial relationship rather than describing a completed one.
- **Is Atwood's "survival" thesis a genuine account of Canadian literature, or a reductive one?** *Survival*'s argument that Canadian literature centers on victims and survivors rather than conquerors successfully named a distinct national literary identity, in a gesture Chapter 6 showed African writers also making for their own literatures, but later critics have questioned whether the thesis flattens a much more varied national literature, including Indigenous writing, into a single settler-focused pattern.
- **Can Aboriginal and Indigenous literatures be read simply as versions of settler-descended national literature, or do they demand different critical tools?** Wright's and Morgan's work draws on oral storytelling traditions and historical experience — including the Stolen Generations and residential schools — that settler-descended literary criticism was not originally built to address, a tension similar in kind, though not in degree, to the debate over Chinua Achebe's and Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o's African-language writing in Chapter 6.
- **Does *The Handmaid's Tale*'s status as a dystopian warning risk being read too narrowly as commentary on one political moment?** As with Orwell's *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (Part IX, Chapter 1), Atwood's novel has been read against a range of shifting contemporary political contexts since its 1985 publication, a testament to its designed openness but also a caution against treating any one reading as final.

## Chapter Summary

Australia and Canada belong to postcolonial theory's distinct **settler colony** category, in which European colonizers displaced Indigenous populations and became the demographic majority themselves, producing a doubled postcolonial story found nowhere else in this Part: a settler-descended national literature developing its own identity apart from Britain, and an entirely separate Indigenous literature of survival and resistance against the settlers who now govern the land. Australian literature moved from the nation-building bush ballads of Lawson and Paterson through Patrick White's Nobel Prize-winning modernism to the emergence of Aboriginal writers like Sally Morgan and Alexis Wright, whose work confronts the Stolen Generations and land dispossession directly. Canadian literature's founding critical gesture, Margaret Atwood's *Survival*, named survival rather than conquest as the national literary theme, while Alice Munro's Nobel-winning short fiction and Atwood's own dystopian *The Handmaid's Tale* established Canada's international reputation, alongside Indigenous Canadian writers confronting the residential school legacy. Michael Ondaatje's immigrant career, threaded through this chapter, previews Chapter 9's turn to diaspora and migrant writing.

## Glossary

- **Settler colony** — a colony in which European colonizers displaced or killed the Indigenous population through violence and disease and became the demographic majority themselves, distinct from colonies where colonizers remained a small ruling minority.
- **Terra nullius** — "land belonging to no one," the legal fiction used to justify British claims to Australia without recognizing prior Aboriginal ownership, overturned by the 1992 Mabo decision.
- **Stolen Generations** — Aboriginal Australian children forcibly removed from their families under government assimilation policy, continuing in various forms until the 1970s.
- **Residential school system** — the Canadian government policy, operating from the 1880s until 1996, of forcibly removing Indigenous children from their families for assimilationist education, documented as cultural genocide by the 2015 Truth and Reconciliation Commission.
