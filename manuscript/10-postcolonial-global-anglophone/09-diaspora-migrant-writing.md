# Chapter 9: Diaspora and Migrant Writing

*Part X: Postcolonial and Global Anglophone Literatures*

---

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Distinguish "migrant writing," produced by first-generation writers who themselves crossed borders, from "diaspora writing," produced by their British-born descendants negotiating an inherited, rather than directly experienced, sense of displacement.
2. Explain Stuart Hall's account of diasporic cultural identity as a continual process of production rather than a fixed, recoverable essence, and Paul Gilroy's related concept of "the Black Atlantic."
3. Analyze Sam Selvon's *The Lonely Londoners* (1956) as a landmark formal innovation, narrating the Windrush generation's experience in modified Caribbean creole prose.
4. Trace the shift from first-generation Windrush-era writing to second- and third-generation British Asian and Black British writing, through Hanif Kureishi, Monica Ali, and Bernardine Evaristo.
5. Synthesize this Part's recurring language debate — from Scots (Chapter 3) through Raja Rao (Chapter 5), Achebe and Ngũgĩ (Chapter 6), and Brathwaite's "nation language" (Chapter 7) — as it reaches a final form in migrant and diasporic writers' own reshaping of English prose.

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Part IX, Chapter 1 traced the *Empire Windrush*'s 1948 arrival at Tilbury Docks as the beginning of large-scale Commonwealth immigration to Britain, and Chapter 6 of that same Part showed Zadie Smith's *White Teeth* giving that demographic transformation comic, multi-generational fictional form. This chapter goes further back to the literature the Windrush generation itself produced, and further forward to the British-born generations that followed it. Caribbean migration in the 1950s was followed by substantial South Asian migration across the same decades, intensified by a further, distinct wave in 1972, when Ugandan dictator Idi Amin expelled the country's entire South Asian population, prompting tens of thousands of Ugandan Asians to resettle in Britain. Over the following decades, Britain's Caribbean- and South Asian-descended population increasingly consisted not of migrants who had themselves crossed an ocean, but of their children and grandchildren, born and raised in Britain, whose relationship to an ancestral "homeland" was inherited rather than personally remembered — a genuinely different experience this chapter asks you to distinguish carefully from the first generation's.

This distinction matters for the theoretical vocabulary this chapter introduces. The Jamaican-born British cultural theorist **Stuart Hall**, writing from within the Black British experience he helped analyze, argued in his influential essay "Cultural Identity and Diaspora" (1990) that diasporic identity should be understood not as a fixed essence waiting to be rediscovered in an ancestral homeland, but as something continually produced anew through memory, narrative, and the very displacement that seems to threaten it. The British sociologist **Paul Gilroy** extended this thinking in *The Black Atlantic* (1993), arguing that Black identity and culture in Britain, the Caribbean, and the Americas is best understood not through any single national tradition but as a genuinely transnational formation, circulating across the ocean crossings of slavery and subsequent migration alike. Both arguments give this chapter's writers a vocabulary for describing an identity built from movement and mixture rather than rooted stability — directly extending Homi Bhabha's concepts of hybridity and mimicry, introduced in Chapter 1, from colonial contact generally to the specific, lived experience of migration to Britain itself.

## Literary Movements

The Windrush generation's own writing, above all Sam Selvon's, made a formal breakthrough beyond simply depicting migrant experience as subject matter: Selvon narrated *The Lonely Londoners* not just in Caribbean creole dialogue but in a modified creole narrative voice throughout, refusing standard English prose as the default vehicle for serious literary narration. This gesture belongs squarely within this Part's recurring language debate: like the Scots writers of Chapter 3, Raja Rao's preface to *Kanthapura* in Chapter 5, the Achebe-Ngũgĩ debate of Chapter 6, and Kamau Brathwaite's "nation language" in Chapter 7, Selvon asserted that a colonized people's own spoken English carried literary authority in its own right, rather than serving only as dialect color within a standard-English frame. Later British Asian and Black British writing, emerging from the 1980s onward through Hanif Kureishi, Monica Ali, Andrea Levy, and Bernardine Evaristo, shifted the central question from the migrant's arrival and adjustment to the British-born descendant's negotiation of a doubled, hyphenated identity — British and Caribbean, British and Bangladeshi, British and Nigerian — lived from birth rather than crossed into.

## Author Profiles

**Sam Selvon** (1923–1994), a Trinidadian writer who moved to London in 1950, established Windrush-generation fiction's landmark text with *The Lonely Londoners* (1956), discussed fully below.

**George Lamming** (1927–2022), a Barbadian writer and Selvon's close contemporary, explored colonial Caribbean childhood and subsequent emigration to Britain in *In the Castle of My Skin* (1953) and *The Emigrants* (1954).

**Hanif Kureishi** (b. 1954), born in London to an English mother and Pakistani father, established British Asian fiction's comic, sexually frank voice with *The Buddha of Suburbia* (1990), discussed fully below.

**Andrea Levy** (1956–2019), born in London to Jamaican parents who had arrived on the *Empire Windrush*'s era of migration, gave the Windrush generation's own arrival its fullest fictional treatment from a second-generation perspective in *Small Island* (2004), discussed fully below.

**Monica Ali** (b. 1967), born in Dhaka to a Bangladeshi father and English mother and raised in England, depicted London's Bangladeshi community in *Brick Lane* (2003), discussed fully below.

**Bernardine Evaristo** (b. 1959), born in London to an English mother and Nigerian father, became the first Black woman to win the Booker Prize for *Girl, Woman, Other* (2019), discussed fully below.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Selvon, *The Lonely Londoners* (1956).** Following a loosely connected group of Caribbean migrants navigating 1950s London's poverty, loneliness, and casual racism, the novel's real innovation lies in its narrative voice: Selvon renders even the third-person narration in a modified, rhythmically distinctive Caribbean creole, insisting that the migrants' own spoken English could carry a serious novel's full narrative weight.
- **Levy, *Small Island* (2004).** Alternating narration between a Jamaican couple who migrate to London around the *Empire Windrush*'s arrival and the English couple whose house they come to share, the novel gives full human interiority to both the migrants' hopeful expectations and the English characters' often ungenerous responses, refusing to let either side of the encounter stand as a simple villain or victim.
- **Kureishi, *The Buddha of Suburbia* (1990).** Following Karim Amir, the mixed English-Pakistani son of a suburban London family, through comic misadventures in 1970s counterculture and the theatre world, the novel treats hyphenated identity not as a source of tragic division but as a resource for wit, performance, and reinvention.
- **Ali, *Brick Lane* (2003).** Following Nazneen, a young Bangladeshi woman brought to London for an arranged marriage, from isolated domestic confinement toward a hard-won independence, the novel depicts London's Bangladeshi community from a distinctly female perspective largely absent from earlier migrant fiction's more male-centered narratives.
- **Evaristo, *Girl, Woman, Other* (2019).** Following twelve interconnected characters, mostly Black British women, across generations and geographies in a distinctive, punctuation-light verse-like prose style, the novel's structure itself enacts Stuart Hall's account of diasporic identity as multiple and continually produced rather than singular and fixed.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does the migrant/diaspora distinction risk erasing continuity between generations, or does it capture a genuine difference in experience?** Selvon's and Lamming's first-generation characters directly remember an ancestral home and the specific act of crossing to Britain, while Kureishi's, Ali's, and Evaristo's British-born characters relate to that same heritage only through inherited memory and family narrative — a difference in kind, not merely degree, that this chapter's vocabulary, drawn from Hall and Gilroy, is specifically built to describe.
- **Did Evaristo's shared 2019 Booker Prize win, alongside Margaret Atwood (Chapter 8), reflect genuine parity of achievement or the judging panel's discomfort with choosing between them?** The Booker judges' decision to split the prize, against the competition's own rules, drew criticism for potentially diminishing Evaristo's landmark achievement as the first Black woman to win the prize outright, even as it also brought her novel wider attention.
- **Does Selvon's creole narrative voice risk being read as merely picturesque dialect, or does it constitute the same serious linguistic argument found elsewhere in this Part?** Read alongside the Scots (Chapter 3), Kanthapura (Chapter 5), and nation-language (Chapter 7) debates, Selvon's formal choice makes the same claim those other writers made: that a colonized people's spoken English is a full literary language in its own right, not a lesser variant of a metropolitan standard.

## Chapter Summary

This chapter distinguished first-generation migrant writing, exemplified by Sam Selvon's landmark creole-narrated *The Lonely Londoners* and George Lamming's fiction, from the diaspora writing of British-born descendants — Andrea Levy, Hanif Kureishi, Monica Ali, and Bernardine Evaristo — whose relationship to ancestral heritage is inherited rather than directly experienced. Stuart Hall's account of diasporic identity as continually produced, and Paul Gilroy's transnational "Black Atlantic," gave this shift a theoretical vocabulary directly extending Chapter 1's Bhabha-derived concepts of hybridity and mimicry from colonial contact generally to migration specifically. Selvon's creole narrative voice brought this Part's recurring language debate — traced from Scots (Chapter 3) through Raja Rao (Chapter 5), Achebe and Ngũgĩ (Chapter 6), and Brathwaite (Chapter 7) — to a final, London-set form. With this chapter, Part X's survey of postcolonial and global Anglophone literatures closes; Part XI now turns from literature itself to the formal history of literary criticism and theory, including a dedicated chapter on postcolonial theory that revisits Said, Bhabha, Spivak, and Fanon from Chapter 1 in fuller critical-historical context.

## Glossary

- **Migrant writing** — literature by first-generation writers who themselves crossed borders, directly remembering and narrating the experience of migration.
- **Diaspora writing** — literature by migrants' descendants, negotiating an inherited rather than personally experienced relationship to ancestral heritage.
- **The Black Atlantic** — Paul Gilroy's term for a transnational Black cultural identity circulating across Britain, the Caribbean, and the Americas, rather than belonging to any single national tradition.
