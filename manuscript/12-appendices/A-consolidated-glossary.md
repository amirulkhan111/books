# Appendix A: Consolidated Glossary of Literary Terms

*Part XII: Appendices*

---

This glossary gathers every term defined across the book's eleven Parts into one alphabetically organized reference, arranged Part by Part in the order you met them. Each entry names the chapter where the term was first explained in full; where a later chapter revisits or extends the same term, that chapter is noted too. Use this appendix for quick review — for the full teaching context behind any term, return to the chapter cited.

---

## Part I: Old English (Anglo-Saxon) Literature

- **Adventus Saxonum** — Latin, "the coming of the Saxons"; the traditional term for the Germanic settlement of Britain. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Caesura** — the metrical pause dividing an Old English poetic line into two half-lines. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Comitatus** — the bond of mutual loyalty and obligation between a lord and his retainers, central to Anglo-Saxon heroic culture. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Danelaw** — the area of eastern and northern England under Scandinavian settlement and legal custom following the Alfred–Guthrum treaty. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Eardstapa** — "earth-stepper," the term used for the speaker of *The Wanderer*. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Editio princeps** — the first printed edition of a text. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Elegy (Old English)** — a modern critical term for a cluster of Exeter Book poems sharing a meditative first-person voice and themes of loss, exile, and transience. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Exile theme** — the recurring Old English motif of a speaker cut off from lordship, kinship, and hall-community. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Flashback/flashforward (digression)** — *Beowulf*'s technique of interweaving legendary or historical episodes into the main narrative for thematic effect. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Formula** — a recurring, metrically fitted word-group used by oral or oral-derived poets to meet compositional needs in performance. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Geong hæleð** — "young hero," the epithet applied to Christ in *The Dream of the Rood*. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Gleeman** — an Old English term for an itinerant entertainer/performer, sometimes distinguished from, sometimes overlapping with, the court-attached scop. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Heptarchy** — a traditional (and imprecise) term for the seven principal Anglo-Saxon kingdoms: Kent, Northumbria, Mercia, East Anglia, Essex, Sussex, Wessex. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Homily** — a sermon or didactic religious prose text intended for instruction, often for oral delivery. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Kenning** — a compressed, often compound metaphorical expression standing in for a more ordinary noun (e.g., "whale-road" for the sea). *[Ch. 2]*
- **Litotes** — ironic understatement, frequent in Old English verse, in which something is affirmed by negating its opposite. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Nowell Codex** — the manuscript, part of British Library Cotton MS Vitellius A.xv, containing the unique surviving copy of *Beowulf*. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Peregrinus** — Latin, "wanderer/pilgrim"; in Whitelock's reading of *The Seafarer*, a monk undertaking voluntary religious exile. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Prosopopoeia** — a rhetorical device in which an inanimate object or abstraction is given a human voice; central to *The Dream of the Rood*. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Rood** — an Old English word for a cross or crucifix. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Runic signature** — Cynewulf's practice of encoding his name in runic characters within the closing lines of his poems. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Scop** — an Anglo-Saxon poet-singer, typically attached to a lord's court. *[Ch. 1–2]*
- **Scriptorium** — a monastic room dedicated to the copying and production of manuscripts. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Sermo Lupi ad Anglos** — Latin, "The Sermon of the Wolf to the English"; Wulfstan's title, playing on his own name. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Thula** — an Old English catalogue-poem or list-poem, as exemplified by much of *Widsith*. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Ubi sunt** — Latin, "where are they?"; a rhetorical formula lamenting the disappearance of past glory, people, or joys. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Written-formulaic** — Larry Benson's term for literate Old English poetry that employs a traditional formulaic style without necessarily being orally composed. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Æstel** — a valuable reading-pointer that Alfred reportedly sent with each copy of the *Pastoral Care* distributed to his bishops. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Alliterative Revival** — the fourteenth-century Middle English resurgence of alliterative verse composition. *[Ch. 7; see also Part II, Ch. 2]*
- **ISSEME** — International Society for the Study of Early Medieval England, renamed (2019) from the International Society of Anglo-Saxonists (ISAS). *[Ch. 7]*
- **Philology** — the historical and comparative study of language, the primary discipline through which Old English literature was edited and studied. *[Ch. 7]*

## Part II: Middle English Literature

- **Anchoress/anchorite** — a woman/man permanently enclosed in a small cell attached to a church, devoted to prayer and contemplation; Julian of Norwich's vocation. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Anglo-Norman** — the variety of Old French used in England following the Norman Conquest, the prestige language of the court and aristocracy. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Ballad stanza** — a four-line quatrain, typically alternating four-stress and three-stress lines, rhyming ABCB. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Bob and wheel** — the five-line rhymed unit concluding each stanza of *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Breton lai** — a short narrative verse genre, associated with Celtic folkloric material, exemplified by Marie de France. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Broadside** — a single printed sheet, a common early modern medium for disseminating ballad texts. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Burden/refrain** — a repeated line or phrase recurring throughout a ballad or carol. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Chancery Standard** — the London-based written English dialect used in royal administrative documents, increasingly adopted as a de facto written standard from the fifteenth century. *[Ch. 9]*
- **"Child number"** — the standard reference number assigned to a traditional ballad per Francis James Child's catalogue. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Colophon** — a printer's statement at the end of a book, giving details of printing, date, and place. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Confessio Amantis** — "The Lover's Confession"; the title and organizing frame of Gower's major English poem. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Discernment of spirits** — the practice of assessing whether a claimed visionary or spiritual experience is of divine, natural, or diabolical origin. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Do-Well, Do-Bet, Do-Best** — the escalating ethical/spiritual stages sought by the dreamer in *Piers Plowman*'s later sections. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Domesday Book** — the Latin-language land and resource survey of England commissioned by William the Conqueror, completed 1086. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Dream vision** — a medieval narrative genre in which the main action is presented as occurring within the narrator's dream. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Estates satire** — a medieval literary tradition cataloguing social types according to their conformity or failure to conform to idealized roles. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Fabliau** — a short, often comic and bawdy narrative genre, exemplified by the Miller's and Reeve's Tales. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Frame narrative** — a narrative structure in which an outer story contains and organizes a series of inner, told stories. *[Ch. 3; see also Part VII, Ch. 8, and Part VIII, Ch. 4]*
- **Incremental repetition** — a ballad technique in which a phrase or stanza pattern recurs with small, cumulative variation. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Incunabulum** (pl. *incunabula*) — a book printed in Europe before 1501. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Lady Meed** — an allegorical figure in *Piers Plowman* personifying reward, payment, or bribery. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Lollardy** — the unorthodox religious movement associated with John Wyclif's teachings. *[Ch. 6]*
- **"Matter of Britain"** — the legendary historical/Arthurian material descending from Geoffrey of Monmouth's *Historia Regum Britanniae*. *[Ch. 1–2]*
- **Merciless Parliament** — the 1388 Parliament that prosecuted and executed a number of Richard II's associates. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Miracle play** — a play dramatizing the life or miracles of a saint. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Morality play** — an allegorical drama depicting the struggle between virtue and vice for an individual human soul. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Movable type** — individually cast letter blocks arranged and rearranged to print text, the core technology of Gutenberg's printing method. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Mystery (cycle) play** — a guild-produced play dramatizing an episode of biblical history, performed as part of a coordinated sequence. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Older Scots** — the medieval form of the Scots language, in which John Barbour composed *The Bruce*. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Pentangle** — the five-pointed star symbol on Gawain's shield, representing interlocking sets of chivalric and religious virtues. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Quem Quaeritis** — Latin, "Whom do you seek?"; the earliest identifiable liturgical dramatic trope. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Recension** — a distinct revised edition or version of a text. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Rhyme royal** — a seven-line iambic pentameter stanza rhyming ababbcc, introduced into English by Chaucer. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Romance (medieval)** — a narrative genre of chivalric adventure, courtly love, and the marvellous. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Shewings** — Julian of Norwich's own term for the sixteen visions underlying *Revelations of Divine Love*. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Trilingualism (medieval England)** — the coexistence of Latin, Anglo-Norman French, and English as the three literary/administrative languages of medieval England, c. 1066–c. 1500. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Visio** — the term commonly used for the earlier, more socially satirical section of *Piers Plowman*. *[Ch. 4]*

## Part III: The Renaissance / Early Modern Period

- **Amanuensis** — a person who writes or types from dictation; the blind Milton dictated his late poetry to several amanuenses. *[Ch. 12]*
- **Areopagitica** — Milton's 1644 prose oration opposing government licensing and censorship of the press. *[Ch. 11]*
- **Authorized Version** — an alternative name for the King James Bible (1611). *[Ch. 10]*
- **Baines Note** — the May 1593 informer's report accusing Marlowe of blasphemous and heretical statements. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Blank verse** — unrhymed iambic pentameter, introduced into English by Surrey's translation of the *Aeneid*. *[Ch. 2; see also Ch. 5, and Part V, Ch. 2]*
- **Caroline** — pertaining to the reign of Charles I (1625–1642). *[Ch. 7]*
- **Carpe diem** — Latin, "seize the day"; a literary theme urging the embrace of pleasure given time's and mortality's inevitability. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Cavalier poets** — a group of royalist courtier poets (including Herrick, Suckling, Lovelace) active before and during the English Civil War. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Christian/Northern humanism** — a Renaissance intellectual movement applying classical philological method to biblical and patristic texts. *[Ch. 1]*
- **City comedy** — a comic genre satirizing contemporary urban mercantile and social life, exemplified by Middleton. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Closet drama** — a play written for reading rather than stage performance, as with *Samson Agonistes*. *[Ch. 12]*
- **Comedy of humours** — Jonson's satirical comic mode in which characters are dominated by a single exaggerated trait. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Conceit (metaphysical)** — an elaborate, intellectually startling comparison yoking together disparate ideas, the defining formal feature of metaphysical poetry. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Dissociation of sensibility** — T. S. Eliot's 1921 term for a supposed later split between thought and feeling in English poetry, following the metaphysical period. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Dissolution of the monasteries** — the 1536–41 process by which Henry VIII disbanded England's monastic institutions and seized their property. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Eikon Basilike** — a purported royal memoir published after Charles I's execution, the target of Milton's *Eikonoklastes*. *[Ch. 11]*
- **English (Shakespearean) sonnet** — a fourteen-line sonnet form of three quatrains and a concluding couplet, developed by Surrey. *[Ch. 2]*
- **First Folio** — the 1623 posthumous collected edition of 36 Shakespeare plays, compiled by John Heminges and Henry Condell. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Groundling** — a spectator who paid the cheapest admission to stand in the open yard of an Elizabethan playhouse. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Idols of the mind** — Bacon's term (in *Novum Organum*) for systematic sources of human intellectual error. *[Ch. 10]*
- **In medias res** — Latin, "into the middle of things"; the classical epic convention of beginning a narrative partway through its action. *[Ch. 12]*
- **Inductive reasoning** — Bacon's proposed scientific method of drawing general conclusions from accumulated empirical observation. *[Ch. 10]*
- **Jacobean** — pertaining to the reign of James I (1603–1625). *[Ch. 7]*
- **Late romances** — Shakespeare's final plays (*Pericles*, *Cymbeline*, *The Winter's Tale*, *The Tempest*), a category named by Edward Dowden in 1875. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Latin Secretary** — Milton's official post (Secretary for Foreign Tongues) under Cromwell's Council of State, 1649–53. *[Ch. 11]*
- **"Lost years"** — the undocumented period of Shakespeare's life, c. 1585–92. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Masque** — an elaborate courtly entertainment combining poetry, music, dance, and spectacular staged design, associated above all with Jonson and Inigo Jones. *[Ch. 7; see also Ch. 11]*
- **Master of the Revels** — the royal official responsible for licensing plays, playing companies, and playhouses. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Metaphysical poets** — a term coined (largely disapprovingly) by Samuel Johnson in 1781 for Donne, Herbert, Marvell, Vaughan, and related poets. *[Ch. 8]*
- **"Mighty line"** — Ben Jonson's phrase for Marlowe's powerful blank verse style. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Overreacher** — a critical term for Marlowe's characteristic tragic protagonist type. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Pattern poem** — a poem whose visual layout on the page forms a shape relevant to its content, as in Herbert's "Easter Wings." *[Ch. 8]*
- **Petrarchan (Italian) sonnet** — a fourteen-line sonnet form of an octave and a sestet, introduced into English by Wyatt. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Quarto** — a smaller, individually printed edition of a single play. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Revenge tragedy** — a dramatic genre, established by Kyd's *The Spanish Tragedy*, centred on a protagonist's pursuit of vengeance. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Royal Supremacy** — the doctrine, established by the 1534 Act of Supremacy, that the English monarch is supreme head of the Church of England. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Sonnet sequence** — a series of linked sonnets, typically tracing the psychological progress of a speaker's love for a beloved. *[Ch. 3; see also Part VII, Ch. 4]*
- **Spenserian stanza** — a nine-line stanza invented by Spenser for *The Faerie Queene*. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Tiring house** — the structure behind an Elizabethan playhouse stage used for costume changes, storage, and entrances/exits. *[Ch. 4]*
- **"Tribe of Ben" / "Sons of Ben"** — the informal circle of poets influenced by Ben Jonson's neoclassical style. *[Ch. 9]*
- **University Wits** — a retrospective term for a group of university-educated 1580s playwrights (Marlowe, Greene, Nashe, Lyly, Peele, Lodge) plus Thomas Kyd. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Volta** — the "turn" in a sonnet's argument or tone. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Vulgate** — the Latin translation of the Bible, traditionally attributed to St Jerome. *[Ch. 1]*

## Part IV: The Restoration and Augustan Age

- **Allegory** — a narrative in which characters and events systematically represent other, usually more abstract or historically specific, meanings. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Amatory fiction** — prose fiction centered on passionate romantic and sexual intrigue. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Anglo-Irish** — describing people of English descent, usually of the established Protestant church, born or settled in Ireland during English/British rule. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Augustan** — a term applied to English literature of the Restoration and early eighteenth century, describing its self-conscious imitation of Roman classical models. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Bathos** — an abrupt, often comic descent from the elevated to the trivial, a term Pope popularized. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Clarendon Code** — a series of laws passed 1661–1665 re-establishing the Church of England and restricting Nonconformists' rights. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Closed (heroic) couplet** — a pair of rhymed lines whose grammar and sense are self-contained; Pope's technical signature. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Coffeehouse culture** — the social world of London's coffeehouses, flourishing after the Restoration. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Comedy of manners** — a comic genre satirizing the social codes and sexual intrigues of a fashionable upper-class world. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Comic epic in prose** — Fielding's term for a new kind of extended comic fiction modeled on epic structure and Cervantes. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Coverture** — the legal doctrine under which a married woman's property and legal identity were absorbed into her husband's. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Epic machinery** — the supernatural apparatus traditionally used in epic poetry; Pope's sylphs are a mock-heroic version. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Epistolary novel** — a novel told entirely through the letters its characters write to one another. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Exclusion Crisis** — the political conflict of 1679–1681 over attempts to exclude the Catholic James, Duke of York, from the English succession. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Formal realism** — Ian Watt's term for the technique of building fictional credibility through detailed, everyday circumstantial detail. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Heroic couplet** — a pair of rhymed, metrically regular lines, the dominant verse form of heroic tragedy and Restoration/Augustan satire. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Heroic tragedy (heroic play)** — a grandiose Restoration tragic form, written in rhymed heroic couplets. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Horatian satire** — gentle, good-humored satire that mocks folly with tolerance, named for the Roman poet Horace. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Interregnum** — the period between 1649 and 1660 when England had no monarch. *[Ch. 1]*
- **"Johnsonese"** — a term for Johnson's characteristic prose style: long, balanced, Latinate sentences built on careful antithesis. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Juvenalian satire** — angry, morally urgent satire that attacks vice and corruption, named for the Roman poet Juvenal. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Lexicography** — the practice and craft of compiling dictionaries. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Licensing Act** — a law requiring government approval before publication, in force in England from 1662 until it lapsed in 1695. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Mock-heroic (mock-epic)** — a satirical mode that applies the grand conventions of epic poetry to a deliberately trivial subject. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Nonconformist** — a Protestant who, after the Restoration, would not accept the doctrine or discipline of the re-established Church of England. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Novel** — an extended prose narrative, generally realistic in setting and detail, centered on individual characters' developing lives. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Patent (theatrical)** — a royal license granting exclusive legal right to stage spoken drama. *[Ch. 2; see also Part VII, Ch. 13]*
- **Patronage** — financial and social support of a writer by a wealthy or aristocratic sponsor. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Penal Laws** — English (later British) legislation restricting the civil, political, and educational rights of Roman Catholics. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Periodical essay** — a short prose essay published at regular intervals in a dedicated paper. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Persona** — a fictional narrating identity adopted by an essayist, distinct from the author's own voice. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Picaresque** — an episodic narrative tradition following a resourceful, roguish protagonist through loosely connected adventures. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Plain style** — the ideal, promoted by the Royal Society, of clear, unornamented English prose. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Poet Laureate** — an official royal appointment recognizing a nation's leading poet. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Politeness** — an early eighteenth-century social and literary ideal combining refined manners, moral seriousness, and sociable wit. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Popish Plot** — a fabricated 1678 conspiracy story invented by Titus Oates. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Proviso scene** — a scene, most famously in Congreve's *The Way of the World*, negotiating the terms of a future marriage. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Quotation-illustration method** — Johnson's technique of illustrating a word's meaning with quotations drawn from real literary usage. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Rake** — the stock male protagonist of comedy of manners: witty, sexually predatory, socially skilled. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Restoration** — the return of the monarchy to England in 1660 with Charles II. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Roman à clef** — a novel whose fictional characters and events are meant to be decoded as real people and events. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Royal Society** — the scientific society founded in 1660 to promote experimental, evidence-based "natural knowledge." *[Ch. 1]*
- **Scriblerus Club** — a literary group founded in 1714 by Swift, Pope, John Gay, and John Arbuthnot. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Secret history (scandal fiction)** — a genre of fiction thinly fictionalizing real contemporary political and social scandal. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Stamp Act (1712)** — a tax on newspapers and periodicals. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Subscription publishing** — a method of funding a book's production by having readers pay in advance for a copy. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Sustained ironic persona** — a satirical technique in which a writer maintains a fictional narrating voice without breaking character. *[Ch. 5]*
- **"Writing to the moment"** — Richardson's term for narrating events and emotions as they happen, through letters written in the heat of experience. *[Ch. 7]*

## Part V: The Age of Sensibility

- **Blank verse** (recap) — unrhymed, metrically regular verse, revived by Young and Blair. *[Ch. 2; see Part III, Ch. 2]*
- **Digression** — the technique, used most radically by Sterne, of interrupting a narrative's forward movement. *[Ch. 5]*
- **The doomed genius myth** — the cultural narrative, consolidated after Chatterton's death, of a brilliant young artist destroyed by poverty and an indifferent literary establishment. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Elegiac stanza** — a four-line stanza rhymed ABAB, used by Gray in his "Elegy." *[Ch. 2; distinct from the "Elegiac Sonnets" title in Part VI, Ch. 11]*
- **Elegy** — a poem of serious reflection, traditionally mourning the dead or meditating on loss. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Fenian Cycle** — the body of Gaelic oral legend concerning the hero Fionn mac Cumhaill (Fingal) and his warriors. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Fragmentary (found manuscript) frame** — a narrative device presenting a story as an incomplete or damaged document. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Graveyard School (graveyard poetry)** — a retrospective label for mid-eighteenth-century poets writing meditative poetry set among tombs and ruins. *[Ch. 2]*
- **"Heaven-taught ploughman"** — Henry Mackenzie's phrase for Burns, celebrating him as a natural, untutored genius. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Illuminated printing (relief etching)** — Blake's invented method of etching text and image together onto a copper plate. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Laughing comedy** — Goldsmith's term for comedy designed to provoke genuine laughter, as a corrective to sentimental comedy's moralizing. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Literary forgery** — a work presented as genuinely older, or by a different author, than it actually is. *[Ch. 3–4]*
- **Lowland Scots** — a Germanic language closely related to English, historically spoken across Lowland Scotland. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Moral sense** — the theory, developed by Shaftesbury and Hutcheson, that humans possess an innate faculty for perceiving moral goodness. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Muniment Room** — the chamber at St Mary Redcliffe Church, Bristol, where genuine medieval documents were stored. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Novel of sensibility** — a genre of eighteenth-century fiction structured around emotionally charged scenes designed to provoke sympathy. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Ossianic** — describing the melancholy, sublime, Highland-inflected literary style associated with Macpherson's Ossian poems. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Primitivism** — the eighteenth-century belief that older, less "civilized" cultures preserved a raw emotional and poetic power lost to modern society. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Pseudo-medieval language** — Chatterton's invented approximation of fifteenth-century English spelling and vocabulary. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Rowley poems** — the body of poetry and prose Chatterton wrote under the invented identity of a fifteenth-century monk. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Sensibility** — a refined capacity for sympathetic emotional response, understood as rooted in the body's nervous responsiveness. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Sentiment / sentimental** — refined, tender moral emotion. *[Ch. 1]*
- **The sublime** — in Burke's aesthetic theory, the pleasurable terror produced by encountering vastness, obscurity, or power. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Sympathy** — in Adam Smith's usage, the imaginative capacity to place oneself in another's situation and share their feeling. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Impartial spectator** — Adam Smith's term for an imagined, disinterested observer by whose standards we judge our own conduct. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Vernacular** — the everyday spoken language of a particular place or people, as opposed to a formal standard language. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Visionary poetry** — poetry that claims to draw on genuine visionary or mystical experience as its source. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Contrary states** — Blake's term for innocence and experience as two equally real, coexisting ways of perceiving the world. *[Ch. 6]*

## Part VI: The Romantic Period

- **"A man speaking to men"** — Wordsworth's democratic description of the poet as sharing fundamental human nature with readers. *[Ch. 2]*
- **The "Elia" persona** — Charles Lamb's pseudonym for his essays. *[Ch. 8]*
- **The Annuals** — lavishly illustrated literary gift-books, published yearly from the 1820s, marketed toward women readers. *[Ch. 11]*
- **Byronic hero** — a brooding, charismatic, morally ambiguous literary character type originating in Byron's poetry. *[Ch. 5; see also Part VII, Ch. 8]*
- **Conversation poem** — a meditative blank-verse poem, developed by Coleridge, addressed to a silent listener. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Elegiac Sonnets** — Charlotte Smith's title for her influential 1784 collection; "elegiac" here describes mood, not a distinct stanza form. *[Ch. 11; distinct from the "elegiac stanza" of Part V, Ch. 2]*
- **Explained supernatural** — Ann Radcliffe's technique of building suspense around apparently supernatural events later revealed to have natural causes. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Familiar essay** — a personal, digressive prose form making the author's own memory and temperament its central subject. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Fancy** — in Coleridge's theory, a lower mental faculty that merely combines and rearranges existing images without genuine creation. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Free indirect discourse** — a narrative technique blending a character's private thoughts into the third-person narrative voice. *[Ch. 10]*
- **Gothic novel** — a genre, originating with Walpole's *The Castle of Otranto* (1764), centered on ruined settings and supernatural or apparent supernatural events. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Gusto** — Hazlitt's critical term for intense, vivid expressive vitality in art and writing. *[Ch. 8]*
- **"Great year"** — the informal term for Keats's extraordinarily productive period, roughly 1818–1819. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Irony** — a mode of expression in which the intended meaning differs from, or comments critically on, the literal statement. *[Ch. 10]*
- **"L.E.L."** — Letitia Elizabeth Landon's publishing byline. *[Ch. 11]*
- **Lyrical drama** — verse drama, like *Prometheus Unbound*, written primarily to be read and imagined rather than staged. *[Ch. 6]*
- **"Modern Prometheus"** — Mary Shelley's subtitle for *Frankenstein*. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Negative capability** — Keats's term for the capacity to remain in uncertainty and doubt without forcing premature resolution. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Novel of manners** — a genre of fiction centered on the customs, conversation, and social codes of a particular class. *[Ch. 10]*
- **Ode** — a formal, often elevated lyric poem, typically meditating on a serious subject. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Organic form** — Coleridge's concept of artistic form growing according to a work's own inner law, like a living organism. *[Ch. 4; recapped in Part XI, Ch. 3]*
- **Ottava rima** — an eight-line Italian stanza form (rhymed ABABABCC), used by Byron in *Don Juan*. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Pantisocracy** — Coleridge and Robert Southey's unrealized 1794 plan for a utopian, egalitarian community in America. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Peterloo Massacre** — the 16 August 1819 killing and wounding of peaceful pro-reform demonstrators by cavalry in Manchester. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Philhellenism** — a European movement of sympathy and support for Greek independence and classical Greek culture. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Poetic diction** — the elevated, specialized vocabulary eighteenth-century poetry treated as proper to serious verse; rejected by Wordsworth. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Primary imagination** — the basic, universal, largely unconscious power by which the mind shapes sensory experience into perception. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Reign of Terror** — the period (1793–1794) of mass executions in Revolutionary France under Robespierre. *[Ch. 1]*
- **The Recluse** — Wordsworth's planned, never-completed large-scale philosophical epic. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Romanticism / Romantic** — a retrospective critical label for a loosely connected group of English writers, roughly 1798–1837, united by a shared value placed on individual imagination and feeling. *[Ch. 1]*
- **The Romantic imagination** — the conviction that individual creative and visionary power offers privileged access to truth and meaning. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Secondary imagination** — the poet's conscious, deliberate echo of primary imagination, recreating perceptions into new organic wholes. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Spots of time** — Wordsworth's term, from *The Prelude*, for especially vivid childhood memories that retain a lasting restorative power. *[Ch. 3]*
- **"Spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings"** — Wordsworth's definition of poetry's origin. *[Ch. 2]*
- **"Unacknowledged legislators of the world"** — Shelley's claim, in "A Defence of Poetry," that poets shape moral and political progress as significantly as lawmakers. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Willing suspension of disbelief** — Coleridge's term for a reader's voluntary acceptance of a supernatural or fantastic premise as emotionally real. *[Ch. 2]*

## Part VII: The Victorian Age

- **Actor-manager** — a leading actor who also managed and directed their own theatre company, exemplified by Henry Irving. *[Ch. 13]*
- **Aestheticism ("art for art's sake")** — the movement, associated with Pater and Wilde, holding that art's value lies in beauty and sensation rather than moral instruction. *[Ch. 11]*
- **Arts and Crafts Movement** — a design movement founded substantially by William Morris, promoting handcrafted goods against industrial mass production. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Barbarians, Philistines, Populace** — Arnold's typology of English social classes in *Culture and Anarchy*. *[Ch. 5]*
- **"The best that has been thought and said"** — Matthew Arnold's definition of culture, from *Culture and Anarchy* (1869). *[Ch. 5; challenged by Raymond Williams in Part XI, Ch. 8]*
- **Bildungsroman** — a novel tracing a protagonist's psychological and moral development from childhood to maturity, exemplified by *Jane Eyre*. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Byronic hero** (recap) — exemplified here by Heathcliff and, more gently, Mr. Rochester. *[Ch. 8; see Part VI, Ch. 5]*
- **Chartism** — a mass working-class political movement (1838–1857) demanding the People's Charter's democratic reforms. *[Ch. 1]*
- **"Condition of England question"** — Thomas Carlyle's 1839 term for the urgent social crisis of industrial poverty and class conflict. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Corn Laws** — tariffs on imported grain, in force 1815–1846, that kept bread prices artificially high. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Crisis of faith** — the widespread Victorian struggle to reconcile traditional Christian belief with new scientific knowledge. *[Ch. 1]*
- **"Cup-and-saucer" drama** — T. W. Robertson's naturalistic domestic staging and dialogue style. *[Ch. 13]*
- **Curtal sonnet** — Hopkins's own invented, compressed sonnet form, used in "Pied Beauty." *[Ch. 6]*
- **Disinterestedness** — Arnold's principle that criticism should pursue objective understanding free from partisan pressure. *[Ch. 5; developed further in Part XI, Ch. 4]*
- **Dramatic irony** — the gap between what a speaker believes their words reveal and what those words actually expose. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Dramatic monologue** — a poem spoken entirely in the voice of a specific character, addressed to an implied listener. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Fatalism (determinism)** — the view, central to Hardy's mature fiction, that human lives are shaped by indifferent chance rather than moral order. *[Ch. 10]*
- **Fin de siècle** — French for "end of century"; the late-nineteenth-century mood of cultural uncertainty. *[Ch. 11]*
- **"Fleshly"** — Robert Buchanan's critical term, from his 1871 attack on Rossetti, for poetry he considered excessively sensuous. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Frame narrative** (recap) — used in both *Wuthering Heights* and *The Tenant of Wildfell Hall*. *[Ch. 8; see Part II, Ch. 3]*
- **Harm principle** — John Stuart Mill's argument, from *On Liberty*, that individual freedom should be restricted only to prevent harm to others. *[Ch. 12]*
- **Higher Criticism** — a nineteenth-century German scholarly movement examining Christian scripture through historical analysis. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Idyll** — a short poem, often narrative or pastoral, describing a particular scene or incident. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Immanent Will** — Hardy's own term for the impersonal, purposeless cosmic force he believed governs events. *[Ch. 10]*
- **"In Memoriam stanza"** — the rhymed ABBA quatrain form Tennyson used throughout *In Memoriam*. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Lord Chamberlain's licensing** — the legal power, dating to 1737, to license and censor all publicly performed stage plays in Britain. *[Ch. 13]*
- **"The madwoman in the attic"** — Sandra Gilbert and Susan Gubar's phrase, from their 1979 study, for Bertha Mason as Jane Eyre's symbolic double. *[Ch. 8; extended into "anxiety of authorship" in Part XI, Ch. 10]*
- **Melodrama** — a popular Victorian theatrical genre built around clear moral resolution and stock characters. *[Ch. 13]*
- **Music hall** — a distinct Victorian popular entertainment venue offering variety programs, separate from legitimate stage drama. *[Ch. 13]*
- **Oxford Movement (Tractarianism)** — a High Church revival within the Church of England in the 1830s–40s, led in part by John Henry Newman. *[Ch. 12]*
- **Patent theatre** (recap) — Drury Lane and Covent Garden's exclusive legal monopoly on spoken drama, ended by the Theatres Act (1843). *[Ch. 13; see Part IV, Ch. 2]*
- **Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood** — a group of English painters and, later, poets, founded 1848, reacting against Royal Academy convention. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Ratiocination** — confident, systematic reasoning from small observed details to a conclusion, the method of Sherlock Holmes. *[Ch. 11]*
- **Realism** — the Victorian literary movement, closely associated with Eliot, holding that fiction should represent ordinary lives with unsparing seriousness. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Risorgimento** — the nineteenth-century movement for Italian political unification and independence. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Sage writing** — a Victorian mode of first-person non-fiction prose combining social diagnosis, moral argument, and personal vision. *[Ch. 12]*
- **Sensation novel** — a genre of Victorian fiction, pioneered by Wilkie Collins, built around crime, scandal, and hidden secrets. *[Ch. 11]*
- **Serialization** — the Victorian practice of publishing a novel in monthly or weekly instalments. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Social-problem novel (Condition-of-England novel, industrial novel)** — a genre of Victorian fiction dramatizing industrial poverty and class conflict, descending from Carlyle's 1839 phrase. *[Ch. 1, fully treated Ch. 7]*
- **Sonnet sequence** (recap) — a connected series of sonnets exploring a single overarching theme. *[Ch. 4; see Part III, Ch. 3]*
- **Sprung rhythm** — Hopkins's metrical system, counting only stressed syllables per line. *[Ch. 6]*
- **"Sweetness and light"** — a phrase Arnold borrowed from Swift's *Battle of the Books* (Part IV, Ch. 5) to describe culture's civilizing effect. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Triple-decker** — the standard three-volume format in which many Victorian novels were published. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Utilitarianism** — a philosophy judging actions by measurable outcomes, critiqued directly in Dickens's *Hard Times*. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Verse novel** — an extended narrative poem taking on the social scope of prose fiction. *[Ch. 3]*
- **"Web" (of interconnected lives)** — a term describing Eliot's technique, especially in *Middlemarch*, of interweaving multiple characters' plots. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Wessex** — Thomas Hardy's fictionalized version of Dorset and the surrounding West Country. *[Ch. 10]*
- **Inscape** — Hopkins's term for the unique, individual essential pattern that makes each thing distinctively itself. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Instress** — the perceptual and spiritual energy that allows an attentive observer to apprehend a thing's inscape. *[Ch. 6]*

## Part VIII: The Modern Period

- **Anglo-American Modernism** — the transatlantic literary movement uniting American-born and British/Irish poets, chiefly based in London. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Avant-garde** — French for "advanced guard"; art or writing that deliberately breaks new ground for a narrow, specialized audience. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Blood consciousness** — D. H. Lawrence's term for an instinctive, bodily form of knowledge suppressed by industrial civilization's rational intellect. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Bloomsbury Group** — a circle of early twentieth-century London writers, artists, and intellectuals, including Virginia Woolf, Lytton Strachey, and Roger Fry. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Cambridge Apostles** — a secret Cambridge University discussion society whose membership shaped several central Bloomsbury figures. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Drama of ideas** — Shaw's dramatic mode, built around structured argument about a genuine social or moral question. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Epiphany** — James Joyce's term for a sudden, quietly devastating moment of psychological or spiritual revelation. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Fabian Society** — a British socialist organization, founded 1884, advocating gradual, democratic social reform. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Frame narrative** (recap) — used by Conrad's recurring narrator Marlow. *[Ch. 4; see Part II, Ch. 3, and Part VII, Ch. 8]*
- **Free verse** — poetry without a fixed, regular metrical pattern or rhyme scheme. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Georgian poetry** — a body of English poetry, collected in Edward Marsh's anthology series from 1912, favoring traditional form over modernist experimentation. *[Ch. 3]*
- **The great tradition** — F. R. Leavis's critical category placing Lawrence alongside Austen, Eliot, James, and Conrad. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Gyre** — W. B. Yeats's term, from *A Vision*, for the interlocking, cone-shaped historical cycles he believed governed civilizations. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Imagism** — an early modernist poetic movement, led chiefly by Ezra Pound, insisting on a single, sharply observed image. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Life Force** — Shaw's philosophy of an evolutionary, creative energy driving living things toward greater self-awareness. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Little magazine** — a small-circulation avant-garde periodical publishing experimental modernist writing. *[Ch. 1]*
- **"MacSpaunday"** — a mocking portmanteau nickname for Auden, Spender, MacNeice, and Day-Lewis. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Modernism** — the retrospective critical label for early twentieth-century writers who broke with Victorian literary form. *[Ch. 1]*
- **New biography** — Lytton Strachey's ironic, psychologically probing biographical method. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Obscenity trial (literary censorship)** — legal proceedings, such as the 1921/1933 rulings on *Ulysses*, judging a work's sexual content against its literary merit. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Poetic impersonality** — T. S. Eliot's theory that a poem's power comes from the poet's mind fusing tradition and experience, independent of personal expression. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Post-Impressionism** — a term coined by Roger Fry to describe the radical French painters, including Cézanne and Van Gogh. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Pylon poetry** — 1930s poetry embracing modern industrial imagery as legitimate poetic subject matter. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Shell shock** — the era's term for the severe psychological trauma many soldiers developed after sustained combat. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Pararhyme (slant rhyme)** — a near-rhyme in which line endings share consonant sounds but not matching vowel sounds. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Spanish Civil War** — the 1936–1939 conflict between Spain's Republican government and Franco's fascist-backed Nationalist forces. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Stream of consciousness** — a narrative technique rendering a character's continuous, associative flow of thought directly. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Theatre of the Absurd** — a dramatic movement dramatizing existence's fundamental meaninglessness through fragmented plot and language. *[previewed Ch. 6; fully defined Part IX, Ch. 3]*
- **Vorticism** — a short-lived, aggressive modernist movement, led by Pound and Wyndham Lewis around 1914. *[Ch. 2]*

## Part IX: Postwar, Postmodern, and Contemporary British Literature

- **Angry Young Men** — a press-coined label for John Osborne and similarly disaffected mid-1950s British writers. *[Ch. 3]*
- **"Bog poems"** — Seamus Heaney's poems, chiefly collected in *North* (1975), using preserved Iron Age bog bodies as a frame for sectarian violence. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Comedy of ideas** — Tom Stoppard's dramatic mode, combining intellectual wordplay with serious philosophical engagement. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Comedy of menace** — Harold Pinter's dramatic mode, combining naturalistic dialogue with unexplained, unsettling threat. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Decolonization** — the process by which Britain's colonial territories gained independence, beginning rapidly after 1945. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Historiographic metafiction** — Linda Hutcheon's term for fiction combining a historical setting with a self-consciously modern, questioning narrator. *[Ch. 4]*
- **In-yer-face theatre** — Aleks Sierz's term for a wave of deliberately confrontational 1990s British playwrights, including Sarah Kane. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Kitchen sink realism** — postwar drama set in ordinary, often shabby domestic interiors. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Metafiction** — fiction that draws deliberate attention to its own status as a constructed, authored text. *[Ch. 4]*
- **The Movement** — a loose group of 1950s British poets, defined by plain diction and ironic detachment. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Neo-Romanticism** — the emotionally heightened 1940s poetic style, epitomized by Dylan Thomas, against which the Movement reacted. *[Ch. 2]*
- **New Lines (1956)** — Robert Conquest's defining anthology of Movement poetry. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Overlapping dialogue** — Caryl Churchill's scripting technique, in which characters' lines are written to interrupt and talk over one another. *[Ch. 7]*
- **"Pinter pause"** — Harold Pinter's characteristic use of silence within dialogue. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Postmodernism** — a contested, retrospective critical label for literature following modernism. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Speculative fiction** — fiction that imagines an alternative or future reality to explore ideas about identity, mortality, or society. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Theatre of the Absurd** (full definition) — named by critic Martin Esslin and founded by Samuel Beckett, dramatizing existence's fundamental lack of guaranteed meaning. *[Ch. 3; previewed Part VIII, Ch. 6]*
- **The Troubles** — the sectarian conflict in Northern Ireland (roughly 1968–1998). *[Ch. 5]*
- **Welfare state** — the comprehensive system of social insurance and public services established by Attlee's postwar Labour government. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Windrush generation** — the Caribbean migrants who arrived in Britain from 1948 onward. *[Ch. 1]*

## Part X: Postcolonial and Global Anglophone Literatures

- **Anglo-Welsh literature** — literature written in English by Welsh writers, distinct from the continuing Welsh-language tradition. *[Ch. 4]*
- **The Black Atlantic** — Paul Gilroy's term for a transnational Black cultural identity circulating across Britain, the Caribbean, and the Americas. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Creole** — a language that develops from sustained contact between different linguistic communities. *[Ch. 7]*
- **"The danger of a single story"** — Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's term for the risk of reducing any culture to one flattening narrative. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Diaspora writing** — literature by migrants' descendants, negotiating an inherited relationship to ancestral heritage. *[Ch. 9]*
- **The empire writes back** — Salman Rushdie's phrase naming postcolonial writers' strategy of reclaiming narrative authority in the colonizer's own language. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Eisteddfod** — a competitive Welsh festival of poetry, music, and performance. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Gaelic Revival** — the movement to revive the Irish language and Gaelic culture, tied to Yeats's Celtic Revival. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Highland Clearances** — the forced eviction of Highland tenant farmers (roughly 1750–1860) to convert land to sheep farming. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Hybridity** — Homi Bhabha's term for the new, mixed cultural identities produced by colonial contact. *[Ch. 1; extended in Part XI, Ch. 11]*
- **Laws in Wales Acts (1535, 1542)** — legislation formally annexing Wales into England's legal and administrative system. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Magical realism** — a literary mode blending everyday realistic detail with genuinely magical elements. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Makar** — Scots for "maker" or poet. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Middle Scots** — the distinct Germanic literary language used by the medieval Makars and later Robert Burns. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Migrant writing** — literature by first-generation writers who themselves crossed borders. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Mimicry** — Homi Bhabha's term for colonized subjects' partial adoption of the colonizer's culture, "almost the same, but not quite." *[Ch. 1; extended in Part XI, Ch. 11]*
- **Nation language** — Kamau Brathwaite's term for Caribbean Creole speech patterns as a legitimate literary language. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Orientalism** — Edward Said's term for the way Western scholarship constructed "the Orient" as an exotic Other. *[Ch. 1; extended in Part XI, Ch. 11]*
- **Partition** — the 1921 division of Ireland into the Irish Free State and Northern Ireland. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Postcolonialism (post-colonialism)** — a contested term describing any critical perspective examining or resisting colonial power and its lasting effects. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Protestant Ascendancy** — the Anglo-Irish Protestant landowning class that dominated Irish political and economic life. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Residential school system** — the Canadian government policy of forcibly removing Indigenous children from their families for assimilationist education. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Settler colony** — a colony in which European colonizers displaced the Indigenous population and became the demographic majority. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Stolen Generations** — Aboriginal Australian children forcibly removed from their families under government assimilation policy. *[Ch. 8]*
- **The subaltern** — a term, borrowed from Antonio Gramsci, for subordinated groups without access to power or representation. *[Ch. 1; the Gramscian source discussed in Part XI, Ch. 8]*
- **Synthetic Scots (Lallans)** — Hugh MacDiarmid's deliberately constructed literary Scots. *[Ch. 3]*
- **Terra nullius** — "land belonging to no one," the legal fiction used to justify British claims to Australia. *[Ch. 8]*

## Part XI: Literary Criticism and Theory

- **Affective fallacy** — Wimsatt and Beardsley's term for the error of judging a poem's meaning by its emotional effect on a reader. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Anagnorisis** — a moment of critical recognition or discovery within a tragic plot. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Ancients versus Moderns** — the sustained European quarrel over whether classical literature represented an unsurpassable standard. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Anthropocene** — a proposed geological epoch defined by irreversible human impact on the planet. *[Ch. 12]*
- **Anxiety of authorship** — Sandra Gilbert and Susan Gubar's term for women writers' foundational doubt about whether a woman could be an author at all. *[Ch. 10]*
- **Anxiety of influence** — Harold Bloom's term for a poet's anxious, competitive struggle with a powerful precursor. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Author-function** — Michel Foucault's term for authorship as a historically constructed category regulating discourse. *[Ch. 7; extended into "discourse" in Ch. 11]*
- **Base / superstructure** — Marx and Engels's model in which economic base shapes ideological superstructure. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Catharsis** — Aristotle's term for tragedy's purging or purification of pity and fear. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Close reading** — slow, careful attention to a text's own words, images, and structure, founded by Richards. *[Ch. 5]*
- **The common reader** — Johnson's proposed standard of literary judgment: sustained, uncorrupted general readership response. *[Ch. 2]*
- **Cultural materialism** — Raymond Williams's extension of Marxist criticism, treating culture as an entire "whole way of life." *[Ch. 8]*
- **Deconstruction** — Derrida's practice of reading a text to reveal how its own internal logic undermines its hierarchical oppositions. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Defamiliarization (*ostranenie*)** — Viktor Shklovsky's term for art's function of disrupting habitual perception. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Différance** — Derrida's coined term for meaning's endless deferral along a chain of signifiers. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Discourse** — Michel Foucault's term for a body of knowledge inseparable from the power relations that produce it. *[Ch. 11]*
- **Distant reading** — Franco Moretti's method of analyzing literary patterns across large corpora of texts computationally. *[Ch. 12]*
- **Ecocriticism** — the study of literature's relationship to the physical environment. *[Ch. 12]*
- **Ecofeminism** — Val Plumwood's critique connecting the domination of nature to the domination of women. *[Ch. 12]*
- **Écriture féminine** — Hélène Cixous's term for a mode of writing rooted in bodily experience that disrupts phallogocentric binary logic. *[Ch. 10]*
- **Epochs of concentration / epochs of expansion** — Arnold's distinction between periods of creative action and idea-generating criticism. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Expressive theory** — the view that literature's value lies in expressing the writer's own inner feeling; Abrams's "lamp." *[Ch. 3]*
- **Feminine / Feminist / Female phases** — Showalter's three-phase model of women's literary history. *[Ch. 10]*
- **Gynocriticism** — Elaine Showalter's term for feminist criticism focused on constructing women's own literary tradition. *[Ch. 10]*
- **Hamartia** — a tragic protagonist's error or flaw that precipitates their downfall. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Hegemony** — Antonio Gramsci's term for a dominant class's ability to secure cultural consent rather than mere coercion. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Intentional fallacy** — Wimsatt and Beardsley's term for the error of judging a poem's meaning by its author's intention. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Langue / parole** — Saussure's distinction between a language's whole underlying system and any individual act of speech. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Logocentrism** — Derrida's term for Western philosophy's assumption that a stable foundation grounds and fixes meaning. *[Ch. 7]*
- **Mimesis** — Aristotle's term for imitation, the fundamental activity he takes all poetry to perform. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Mimetic theory** — the view that literature's value lies in accurately reflecting an external reality; Abrams's "mirror." *[Ch. 3]*
- **Narrative function** — Vladimir Propp's term for a recurring plot event appearing in a fixed sequence across a genre. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Neoclassicism** — the critical assumption that literature should be judged against classical models and rules. *[Ch. 1]*
- **New Criticism** — the mid-twentieth-century American movement treating the literary text as an autonomous structure. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Objective correlative** — Eliot's term for the set of objects or events that must be present in a work to evoke a specific emotion reliably. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Organic form** (recap) — Coleridge's concept of artistic form growing from a work's own inner law. *[Ch. 3; see Part VI, Ch. 4]*
- **Pastoral** — the literary tradition idealizing rural life and landscape, critiqued by Raymond Williams. *[Ch. 12]*
- **Peripeteia** — a sudden reversal of fortune within a tragic plot. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Phallogocentrism** — Cixous's term combining phallocentrism and Derrida's logocentrism. *[Ch. 10]*
- **"Poetry is a criticism of life"** — Arnold's definition, from "The Study of Poetry" (1880), of poetry's function. *[Ch. 4]*
- **Power/knowledge** — Foucault's compound term for the inseparability of authorized knowledge from the power structures producing it. *[Ch. 11]*
- **Sign** — in Saussure's linguistics, the arbitrary union of a signifier and a signified. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Stock response** — Richards's term for a reflexive, pre-formed reaction to a poem's subject matter. *[Ch. 5]*
- **Structuralism** — the critical movement analyzing literature and culture as systems of underlying relations, drawing on Saussurean linguistics. *[Ch. 6]*
- **Structures of feeling** — Williams's term for the lived, still-emerging quality of a generation's actual experience. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Stylometry** — computational, statistical analysis of an author's characteristic word choices, used to resolve authorship disputes. *[Ch. 12]*
- **The three unities** — the rule that drama should observe unity of action, time, and place; only unity of action is substantially Aristotle's own. *[Ch. 1]*
- **Totality** — Georg Lukács's term for the coherent underlying structure of an era's social relations. *[Ch. 8]*
- **Touchstone method** — Arnold's technique of testing a poem's quality by comparison against short passages from acknowledged great poets. *[Ch. 4]*
- **"The unconscious is structured like a language"** — Lacan's structuralist reformulation of Freud. *[Ch. 9]*
- **The uncanny (*das Unheimliche*)** — Freud's term for the dread produced when something familiar and repressed returns in unfamiliar form. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Oedipus complex** — Freud's theory of a child's unconscious desire for the opposite-sex parent and rivalry with the same-sex parent, named for Sophocles's tragedy. *[Ch. 9]*
- **Wit** (in Pope's sense) — apt, memorable expression of an already-recognized truth, central to neoclassical literary value. *[Ch. 2]*
