## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Tennyson (Chapter 2) and Arnold (Chapter 5) both wrestled with religious doubt from within a shaken but still recognizably Anglican framework. Gerard Manley Hopkins takes this Part's exploration of Victorian faith in a genuinely different direction: not doubt gradually eroding belief, but intense, sometimes agonized devotion within a faith he never abandoned, even at its most desolate.

Hopkins was born in 1844 to a devout, artistically talented middle-class Anglican family, and at Balliol College, Oxford, from 1863, he came under the influence of the **Oxford Movement** (also called the Tractarian Movement), a High Church Anglican revival emphasizing Catholic tradition and ritual within the Church of England. Several of the movement's leading figures, most famously John Henry Newman — whom you'll meet fully in Chapter 12 — eventually converted to Roman Catholicism outright, and in 1866 Hopkins followed the same path, received into the Catholic Church by Newman himself. This was a socially costly decision in Victorian England, where Catholicism still carried real suspicion, and it caused a painful rift with his own Anglican family. Two years later, in 1868, Hopkins joined the Jesuit order to train for the priesthood — and, in an act of dramatic religious self-renunciation he called "the slaughter of the innocents" in a letter to a friend, burned all his existing poems, resolving to write no more, out of concern that poetic ambition might compromise his religious vocation. For roughly seven years, he kept this resolution completely.

## Literary Movements

Hopkins's most significant technical innovation is **sprung rhythm**, a metrical system he believed he had rediscovered from older English poetic traditions — Anglo-Saxon accentual verse and popular folk rhythms — rather than simply invented from nothing. Where conventional English meter, like iambic pentameter, counts a fixed number of both stressed and unstressed syllables per line, sprung rhythm counts only the **stressed** syllables, allowing a highly variable number of unstressed syllables between them. The result is an unusually vigorous, irregular rhythm that Hopkins felt captured natural speech and intense emotional or spiritual energy far better than smoother conventional meter — a genuinely significant innovation that anticipates the free-verse experimentation of the twentieth-century modernist poets you'll meet in Part VIII.

Hopkins's other major contribution is a pair of terms drawn partly from his study of the medieval theologian and philosopher Duns Scotus: **inscape** and **instress**. Inscape names the unique, individual essential pattern that makes each thing distinctively and irreducibly itself — related to Scotus's philosophical concept of "haecceitas," or "thisness." Instress names the perceptual and spiritual energy that lets an attentive observer actually apprehend a thing's inscape. For Hopkins, this was not merely an aesthetic theory but a devout theological claim: each created thing, by fully being itself, gives glory to God — a principle that underlies the confident nature poems discussed below.

This gives Hopkins's poetry a genuinely distinct place in this Part's ongoing exploration of Victorian religious experience. Where Tennyson's *In Memoriam* moves through doubt toward hard-won, incomplete consolation, and Arnold's "Dover Beach" accepts faith's retreat and seeks comfort in human fidelity alone, Hopkins remains, throughout his life, a believer — but one whose faith, as his later "terrible sonnets" show, could still open onto genuine desolation, without that desolation ever amounting to actual disbelief.

## Author Profiles

**Gerard Manley Hopkins** (1844–1889) broke his seven years of poetic silence only when his religious superior encouraged him to write about a specific tragedy: the December 1875 wreck of the German ship the *Deutschland*, which sank in a storm near the mouth of the Thames, killing many aboard, including five Franciscan nuns exiled from Germany under Bismarck's anti-Catholic laws. "The Wreck of the Deutschland" (1876), Hopkins's first mature poem, wrestles directly with why God permits such suffering, and it introduced sprung rhythm in its full, mature form — so unconventional that the Jesuit journal *The Month* rejected it as unpublishable. Hopkins was ordained a Jesuit priest in 1877, the same year he composed several of his most celebrated shorter poems, including "God's Grandeur," "Pied Beauty," and, around this same period, "As Kingfishers Catch Fire," all expressing his theology of inscape and instress with real confidence and joy. His later career took him through pastoral and teaching postings across England, Wales — where he learned Welsh and absorbed its alliterative poetic tradition into his own dense, musical technique — and Scotland, before a final, difficult posting as Professor of Greek at University College Dublin from 1884, where heavy workloads, professional isolation, and homesickness for England produced the genuinely anguished "terrible sonnets" of around 1885. Hopkins died of typhoid fever in Dublin in 1889, having published almost none of his mature poetry during his lifetime; he shared it privately, chiefly with his friend the poet Robert Bridges (later himself Poet Laureate), who preserved the manuscripts and published the first collected edition only in 1918 — nearly thirty years after Hopkins's death, and squarely within the modernist literary period rather than the Victorian one that had actually produced the poems.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"The Wreck of the Deutschland" (1876).** Read this long, formally radical ode as Hopkins's first full deployment of sprung rhythm, wrestling with the theological problem of suffering through the specific, historically real tragedy of the shipwrecked nuns, and ultimately finding meaning rather than simply cataloguing loss.
- **"God's Grandeur" (1877).** This sonnet directly engages the industrialization Chapter 1 introduced — "generations have trod, have trod, have trod" and left the "soil... bare" — yet concludes in confident hope, with the Holy Spirit still "brood[ing]" over "the bent / World... with warm breast and with ah! bright wings," offering a strikingly different response to industrial modernity than the darker anxieties of Tennyson or Arnold.
- **"Pied Beauty" (1877).** Composed in Hopkins's own invented compressed sonnet form, the "curtal sonnet," this brief poem's opening line — "Glory be to God for dappled things" — gives his inscape theology its most concise, joyful statement: variety, irregularity, and imperfection in nature as themselves evidence of divine glory.
- **"As Kingfishers Catch Fire" (c. 1877–1882).** This poem states Hopkins's inscape philosophy most directly: every creature "Selves—goes itself; myself it speaks and spells," and by extension, Hopkins argues, Christ "plays in ten thousand places" through the distinctive being of every just individual thing.
- **The "terrible sonnets" (c. 1885).** Poems like "Carrion Comfort" and "No worst, there is none" present raw, unflinching spiritual desolation and near-despair, written during Hopkins's isolated final years in Dublin — proof that his faith, however confidently expressed elsewhere, was not immune to genuine suffering and doubt about God's presence, even if it never broke into outright disbelief.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Do the confident nature poems and the "terrible sonnets" contradict each other?** Rather than treating "God's Grandeur" and "Carrion Comfort" as inconsistent, most critics read both as honest expressions of the same lived faith at different, genuinely difficult moments — proof that devout belief doesn't require constant, unbroken confidence.
- **Was Hopkins's poem-burning and seven-year silence healthy humility or troubling self-suppression?** Reasonable readers disagree: some see genuine, admirable religious discipline in Hopkins's willingness to subordinate personal ambition to his vocation; others see a talented writer needlessly silencing himself for years over anxieties his own later work suggests were never fully resolved.
- **Is "sprung rhythm" truly a rediscovery, or a personal invention framed as tradition?** Hopkins himself insisted he was recovering an authentic older English practice rather than inventing something wholly new, but most metrical historians treat this claim as, at best, a partial truth — sprung rhythm's actual effect in Hopkins's hands is distinctively his own, whatever older precedents he drew on.
- **What does Hopkins's posthumous, essentially modernist reception suggest about literary periodization?** Hopkins wrote entirely within the Victorian period but had almost no Victorian readers and no Victorian influence; his real critical home is the twentieth century, a genuinely useful reminder that a writer's own lifetime and their actual literary-historical impact don't always align.

## Chapter Summary

Converting to Roman Catholicism under John Henry Newman's influence and entering the Jesuit order, Gerard Manley Hopkins burned his early poems and kept a seven-year poetic silence before returning to writing with "The Wreck of the Deutschland" (1876), introducing his invented "sprung rhythm" metrical system. His mature poems, including "God's Grandeur" and "Pied Beauty" (both 1877), express a confident theology of inscape and instress — the conviction that each created thing's distinctive individuality gives glory to God — while his later "terrible sonnets" (c. 1885), written during a difficult final posting in Dublin, show that same devout faith tested by genuine spiritual desolation. Almost none of Hopkins's mature poetry was published in his lifetime; his friend Robert Bridges preserved and eventually published it in 1918, meaning Hopkins's real literary influence belongs to the twentieth-century modernist period covered in Part VIII, not to the Victorian age that actually produced his work. Chapter 7 now turns from poetry to prose, and the social-problem novel of Dickens and Gaskell.

## Glossary

- **Sprung rhythm** — Hopkins's metrical system, counting only stressed syllables per line and allowing a variable number of unstressed syllables between them, producing an unusually vigorous, irregular rhythm.
- **Inscape** — Hopkins's term for the unique, individual essential pattern that makes each thing distinctively itself.
- **Instress** — the perceptual and spiritual energy that allows an attentive observer to apprehend a thing's inscape.
- **Curtal sonnet** — Hopkins's own invented, compressed sonnet form, roughly three-quarters the length of a standard sonnet, used in "Pied Beauty."
