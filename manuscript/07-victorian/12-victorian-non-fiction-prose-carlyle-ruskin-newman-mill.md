# Chapter 12: Victorian Non-Fiction Prose: Carlyle, Ruskin, Newman, Mill

*Part VII: The Victorian Age, 1837–1901*

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Define "sage writing" as a distinct Victorian mode of non-fiction prose and explain why it flourished during this period.
2. Trace Thomas Carlyle's career in full, connecting his coinage of the "Condition of England question" (Chapter 1) to his wider body of work and its troubling later politics.
3. Explain John Ruskin's art and social criticism, and connect his praise of Gothic craftsmanship to William Morris and the Pre-Raphaelites (Chapter 4).
4. Explain John Henry Newman's role in the Oxford Movement and his conversion to Roman Catholicism, connecting it to Gerard Manley Hopkins's own conversion (Chapter 6).

6. Analyze Mill's *On Liberty* and *The Subjection of Women* as foundational texts of liberal and early feminist political thought.
7. Evaluate the sage writers collectively as figures using prose, rather than poetry or the novel, to diagnose and respond to the era's central crises.

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

You have already met Thomas Carlyle once in this Part, in Chapter 1, as the writer whose 1839 pamphlet *Chartism* named "the Condition of England question" and gave the social-problem novel of Chapter 7 its defining vocabulary. This chapter gives Carlyle, along with three other major prose writers of the period, the fuller biographical and critical treatment their influence deserves. Critics sometimes group Carlyle, Ruskin, Newman, and Mill together under the label **"sage writing"** — a mode of serious, often prophetic non-fiction prose in which a single distinctive authorial voice diagnoses what has gone wrong with contemporary society and proposes, often forcefully, how it should be set right. Where a novelist like Dickens dramatizes social problems through invented characters and plot, the Victorian sage addresses the reader directly, arguing a position with the full force of personal conviction.

It's worth asking why this mode of writing flourished so vigorously in the Victorian period specifically. Partly, it reflects the same expanding periodical culture and reading public that made serialized fiction possible in Chapter 7: essays and treatises, like novels, could now reach a genuinely mass audience rather than a narrow circle of specialists. Partly, it reflects the era's genuine hunger for authoritative guidance amid rapid, disorienting change — industrialization, religious doubt following Darwin, and shifting political structures all created real appetite for writers willing to argue, with confidence, what should be believed and done about it. The four writers in this chapter address different aspects of that disorientation: Carlyle and Ruskin respond above all to industrial society's effects on human dignity and craftsmanship; Newman responds to a crisis of religious authority; and Mill responds to fundamental questions of individual liberty and social justice, including, in a genuinely pioneering way, the position of women.

## Literary Movements

**Sage writing**, introduced above, is best understood not as a single movement with shared beliefs but as a shared *method*: a first-person, often highly stylized prose voice combining social diagnosis, moral argument, and a strong personal vision of how things ought to be. This method allows genuinely opposed conclusions — Carlyle's growing distrust of democracy sits uneasily beside Mill's passionate defense of individual liberty — while sharing a common confidence that prose itself, argued forcefully enough, can change how readers see their own society.

Within this shared mode, this chapter's four writers cluster into two related but distinct concerns. Carlyle and Ruskin both respond to industrialization's effect on human dignity, but from different angles: Carlyle focuses on leadership and the need for strong, capable direction of society (a concern that darkens, as you'll see below, into troubling territory later in his career), while Ruskin focuses more specifically on labor and craftsmanship, arguing that mechanized industrial work strips workers of the creative dignity that skilled handwork once gave them — an argument that directly shaped William Morris's turn toward handcrafted design, discussed in Chapter 4. Newman and Mill, meanwhile, both address questions of authority and conscience, though from opposite directions: Newman defends the claims of institutional religious authority against a skeptical age, while Mill defends the individual conscience and free thought against any authority, religious or social, that would suppress it.

## Author Profiles

**Thomas Carlyle** (1795–1881), born in rural Scotland and originally intended for the ministry, turned instead toward literature and philosophy, developing an intense interest in German Romantic thought that shaped his own famously idiosyncratic prose style — dense, exclamatory, and often deliberately strange. His early masterpiece, *Sartor Resartus* ("The Tailor Re-Tailored," 1833–34), presents itself as a commentary on the philosophy of a fictional German professor obsessed with the relationship between spiritual truth and the external "clothing" — customs, institutions, appearances — through which societies express it. Carlyle's 1839 *Chartism* and his lecture series *On Heroes, Hero-Worship, and the Heroic in History* (1841) developed his conviction that history is driven above all by exceptional individuals — heroes, prophets, strong leaders — capable of cutting through social confusion, a conviction he extended in *Past and Present* (1843) by contrasting a well-ordered medieval monastery with the directionless chaos he saw in contemporary industrial society. It's important to read this admiration for strong leadership honestly rather than sentimentally: in his later career, Carlyle's growing impatience with democracy shaded into genuinely indefensible territory, including writings defending forced labor and expressing openly racist views about people of African descent — a reminder that the same writer who named the Condition of England question and demanded sympathy for industrial suffering could also hold views modern readers rightly find shameful, and serious study of Carlyle has to hold both facts at once rather than smoothing over the second.

**John Ruskin** (1819–1900) built his early reputation as an art critic, defending the painter J. M. W. Turner against hostile critics across the five volumes of *Modern Painters* (1843–60) and developing an influential theory that great art requires unflinching "truth to nature" combined with real moral seriousness. He extended this thinking to architecture in *The Stones of Venice* (1851–53), whose chapter "The Nature of Gothic" praises medieval Gothic buildings precisely because their irregular, imperfect ornamentation reflects the genuine creative freedom of the individual craftsmen who made them — a sharp contrast, in Ruskin's view, to the soul-crushing uniformity of mechanized industrial labor. This argument directly shaped William Morris's own turn toward handcrafted design and decorative art, discussed in Chapter 4, and helped inspire the broader Arts and Crafts movement that followed. Ruskin was also an early, outspoken public defender of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood (also Chapter 4) against hostile early reviews. Later in his career, Ruskin turned toward direct economic and social criticism in *Unto This Last* (1860), attacking laissez-faire economics for treating human wellbeing as secondary to wealth accumulation — a work whose influence reached remarkably far beyond Britain, including the Indian independence leader Mohandas Gandhi, who later credited it with transforming his own thinking about economic justice. Ruskin's personal life was markedly less settled than his public reputation: his marriage to Effie Gray was annulled, unconsummated, in 1854, after which she married the Pre-Raphaelite painter John Everett Millais.

**John Henry Newman** (1801–1890) began his career as an Anglican clergyman and became the central figure of the **Oxford Movement** (also called Tractarianism, after the *Tracts for the Times* the group published), a High Church revival within the Church of England during the 1830s and 1840s that sought to recover the church's older Catholic-influenced liturgical and theological traditions. Newman's own religious searching eventually carried him further than most of his fellow Tractarians: in 1845, in an act that scandalized a Victorian public deeply suspicious of Roman Catholicism, he formally converted to the Catholic Church, later becoming a cardinal in 1879. When the novelist and clergyman Charles Kingsley publicly questioned Newman's honesty about this conversion, Newman responded with *Apologia Pro Vita Sua* (1864), a spiritual autobiography defending the sincerity of his religious journey that remains one of the great achievements of English autobiographical prose. His earlier lecture series *The Idea of a University* (1852) set out an influential vision of liberal education as the cultivation of the whole mind, not merely vocational or narrowly specialized training. You have already met Newman once before in this Part: he is the same figure whose influence drew Gerard Manley Hopkins toward his own Catholic conversion in Chapter 6, making Newman one of the few writers in this chapter to shape another writer in this book directly and personally, not merely through his ideas.

**John Stuart Mill** (1806–1873), raised under an extraordinarily demanding program of education designed by his philosopher father, James Mill, became the leading philosophical voice of nineteenth-century liberalism. Mill refined the utilitarian philosophy of his father and of Jeremy Bentham — the same "greatest good for the greatest number" calculus Dickens satirized through Gradgrind in *Hard Times* (Chapter 7) — by insisting that not all pleasures are equal, and that intellectual and moral pleasures rank above merely physical ones, a distinction he set out fully in *Utilitarianism* (1863). His *On Liberty* (1859) remains a foundational text of liberal political philosophy, arguing that individual freedom of thought, expression, and action should be restricted only to prevent genuine harm to others — a principle now often called the "harm principle." In *The Subjection of Women* (1869), Mill made a rigorous, sustained argument for women's full legal and social equality, remarkably progressive for its time; he credited much of his thinking on the subject to his intellectual partnership with his wife, Harriet Taylor Mill, whose influence on his work he described as profound and, by his own account, under-acknowledged during her lifetime.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Carlyle, *Past and Present* (1843).** Contrasting an orderly, purposeful medieval monastery with the drifting, leaderless chaos Carlyle saw in industrial Britain, the book argues that modern society has lost the capacity for genuine authority and heroic leadership that earlier ages, in Carlyle's idealized reading of them, once possessed.
- **Ruskin, "The Nature of Gothic," from *The Stones of Venice* (1851–53).** This chapter argues that medieval Gothic architecture's irregularity and imperfection are not flaws but evidence of the individual craftsman's creative freedom, in sharp contrast to the mechanized uniformity of industrial manufacture — an argument with direct, traceable influence on William Morris and the Arts and Crafts movement (Chapter 4).
- **Ruskin, *Unto This Last* (1860).** Attacking classical political economy's treatment of human beings as purely self-interested economic units, the essays insist that a just economy must be grounded in ethical concern for human wellbeing, not merely in efficient wealth production — an argument whose influence reached well beyond Victorian Britain, notably shaping Gandhi's later economic thought.
- **Newman, *Apologia Pro Vita Sua* (1864).** Written rapidly in response to a public attack on his honesty, this spiritual autobiography traces Newman's religious development from his Anglican upbringing through the Oxford Movement to his Catholic conversion, defending the sincerity and coherence of a journey his contemporaries frequently viewed with suspicion.
- **Mill, *On Liberty* (1859).** Arguing that individual liberty of thought and action should be restricted only to prevent harm to others, the essay defends free expression even of unpopular or apparently false opinions, on the grounds that untested beliefs, however true, harden into empty dogma without the constant challenge of open debate.
- **Mill, *The Subjection of Women* (1869).** Systematically dismantling contemporary arguments for women's legal and social subordination, the essay insists that the supposed natural differences between men and women cannot be verified while women remain denied equal education and opportunity — an argument that anticipates, and directly influenced, later feminist political thought.

## Critical Perspectives

- **How should readers hold Carlyle's genuine insight and his later politics together?** Carlyle's Condition of England writing did real work in forcing Victorian readers to confront industrial suffering, yet his later hero-worship curdled into open contempt for democracy and, in his most notorious writings, into explicit racism. Responsible criticism doesn't excuse the second by pointing to the first, nor does it dismiss his earlier social criticism simply because of what came later — it holds both as part of a single, genuinely troubling intellectual career.
- **Is Ruskin's praise of medieval craftsmanship nostalgic escapism, or serious social criticism?** Some critics have read Ruskin's admiration for Gothic imperfection as an impractical, backward-looking fantasy, unable to offer industrial Britain any real alternative to mechanized labor. Others argue that his underlying point — that a society's economic system should be judged by its effect on human dignity, not merely its output — remains a serious and durable critique, one that found genuinely practical expression in Morris's Arts and Crafts movement and in Gandhi's economic thought.
- **Does Newman's conversion narrative fully persuade?** *Apologia Pro Vita Sua* is widely admired as a masterpiece of sincere self-examination, but some critics have noted that autobiography, however honest in intention, inevitably shapes a life's messier realities into a more coherent narrative than they possessed at the time — a caution worth applying to any spiritual autobiography read as straightforward historical record.
- **How radical was Mill's feminism, really?** *The Subjection of Women* was strikingly progressive for 1869, but some later feminist critics have noted its arguments still largely assume a companionate, respectable middle-class marriage as the ideal, rather than questioning the institution of marriage itself — a limitation worth weighing against the essay's genuinely pioneering insistence on women's fundamental equality.

## Chapter Summary

Carlyle, Ruskin, Newman, and Mill together represent the Victorian sage's characteristic mode of prose: a confident, personally engaged voice diagnosing the era's central crises and arguing forcefully for how they should be addressed. Carlyle, already met in Chapter 1 as the coiner of the Condition of England question, extended his concern with leadership and social order across a career that, read honestly, combines genuine insight with a later descent into indefensible politics. Ruskin's defense of Gothic craftsmanship against industrial uniformity directly shaped William Morris's work in Chapter 4 and reached remarkably far beyond Britain through *Unto This Last*'s influence on Gandhi. Newman, whose own conversion narrative in the *Apologia* remains a landmark of English autobiography, is the same figure whose influence drew Gerard Manley Hopkins toward Catholicism in Chapter 6. And Mill's *On Liberty* and *The Subjection of Women* gave Victorian liberalism, and early feminist thought, some of its most enduring foundational arguments. Chapter 13, the final chapter of this Part, turns to Victorian drama and the theatre before the arrival of Wilde and Shaw.

## Glossary

- **Sage writing** — a Victorian mode of first-person non-fiction prose combining social diagnosis, moral argument, and a strong personal vision, exemplified by Carlyle, Ruskin, Newman, and Mill.
- **Oxford Movement (Tractarianism)** — a High Church revival within the Church of England in the 1830s and 1840s, led in part by John Henry Newman, seeking to recover older Catholic-influenced traditions.
- **Harm principle** — John Stuart Mill's argument, from *On Liberty*, that individual freedom should be restricted only to prevent harm to others.
