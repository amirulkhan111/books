## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Queen Victoria reigned from 1837, when she ascended the throne at eighteen, until her death in 1901 — over sixty-three years, the longest reign any British monarch had yet held. "Victorian" is a genuinely useful shorthand for this book's purposes, but it's worth stating the same caution you've met with earlier period labels like "Romanticism" (Part VI, Chapter 1): a monarch's reign is a convenient boundary, not a natural literary or cultural unit, and the developments this chapter describes didn't begin or end neatly at 1837 or 1901.

What genuinely does define this period is the full maturing of Britain's Industrial Revolution, already underway before Victoria's accession but reaching its most visible, most socially consequential stage during her reign. Factories drew millions of people from the countryside into rapidly growing industrial cities — Manchester, Birmingham, Leeds — where overcrowding, poor sanitation, and dangerous working conditions, including for children, became urgent public concerns. Parliament responded with a series of Factory Acts (1833, 1844, 1847) limiting working hours, and a Mines Act (1842) banning underground work for women and children under ten — evidence that the human costs of industrial growth were becoming impossible to ignore, even for a government generally reluctant to regulate private industry.

In 1839, the writer and social critic Thomas Carlyle — whom you'll meet in full in Chapter 12 — published a pamphlet titled *Chartism*, which gave this whole cluster of anxieties a lasting name: the **"Condition of England question."** Carlyle argued that Britain's political and intellectual leadership was failing to confront the real suffering industrial poverty and class conflict were producing, hidden behind the nation's growing wealth and confidence. The phrase caught on, and it names the concern that drives an entire genre of fiction you'll study directly in Chapter 7: the social-problem novel, through which writers like Dickens and Gaskell dramatized industrial poverty and class division for a middle-class reading public increasingly aware that something needed answering.

That same anxiety had a direct political outlet in **Chartism**, a mass working-class movement (1838–1857) built around the "People's Charter" (1838), which demanded universal male suffrage, secret ballots, and other democratic reforms; Parliament rejected the Chartists' mass petitions in 1839, 1842, and 1848, and full working-class enfranchisement remained decades away. A related struggle over the **Corn Laws** — tariffs that kept bread prices artificially high to protect landowners, at direct cost to industrial workers and urban consumers — ended in 1846, when Robert Peel's government repealed them, a landmark victory for free-trade liberalism that split the Conservative Party in the process. The devastating Irish Potato Famine (1845–1852), which killed roughly a million people and drove mass emigration, added real urgency to the debate over food prices and government responsibility.

Victorian Britain's confidence in its own industrial and commercial achievement found its most spectacular public expression in the **Great Exhibition of 1851**, housed in London's purpose-built Crystal Palace and organized substantially through Prince Albert's initiative — a celebration of British manufacturing prowess that drew millions of visitors, even as critics like Carlyle himself remained skeptical that industrial triumph alone had actually resolved the Condition of England question underneath it. This same period saw Britain's empire expand toward its greatest territorial extent: the 1857 Indian Rebellion led directly to the end of East India Company rule and the imposition of direct Crown government in 1858, and Victoria was declared Empress of India in 1876. Victorian literature's relationship to this empire is genuinely complicated — sometimes assumed quietly as background wealth or exotic setting, sometimes engaged with directly — and this chapter leaves fuller postcolonial critical treatment to Part X, much as the brief colonial reference in Austen's *Mansfield Park* (Part VI, Chapter 10) already previewed.

Finally, 1859 brought a genuine intellectual earthquake: Charles Darwin's *On the Origin of Species*, which proposed that species, including humans, developed through natural selection rather than direct divine creation. Combined with earlier challenges to traditional biblical chronology from geology, Darwin's theory intensified a widespread Victorian "crisis of faith" that runs directly through the poetry and prose of the chapters ahead — Tennyson's doubt in Chapter 2, Arnold's criticism in Chapter 5, and Hopkins's struggle to reconcile faith and modern knowledge in Chapter 6 all wrestle, in different ways, with exactly this disruption.

## Literary Movements

This chapter's key phrase, the **"Condition of England question,"** deserves careful attention because it shapes so much of what follows in Part VII. Carlyle's *Chartism* (1839) didn't just describe industrial poverty; it accused Britain's leadership of a failure of moral and imaginative attention, treating economic statistics as a substitute for genuine engagement with human suffering. Writers who took up this challenge directly — producing what critics now call the **social-problem novel** or **"Condition-of-England novel"** — used realistic fiction to make middle-class readers confront industrial poverty and class conflict they might otherwise never encounter firsthand. You'll study this genre fully through Dickens and Gaskell in Chapter 7, but it's worth knowing now that the genre's very name descends directly from Carlyle's 1839 phrase.

The other defining intellectual current this chapter introduces is the Victorian **crisis of faith**: a widespread, often painful struggle to reconcile traditional Christian belief with new scientific knowledge, especially following Darwin's 1859 theory of evolution by natural selection, though earlier geological findings (challenging a literal biblical timeline) had already begun unsettling traditional certainties. This crisis shows up across genres and writers with very different temperaments — you'll meet its effects on elegiac poetry in Chapter 2, on literary and social criticism in Chapter 5, and on devout religious poetry itself in Chapter 6 — proof that a single historical development can produce genuinely varied literary responses rather than one uniform reaction.

## Author Profiles

**Thomas Carlyle** (1795–1881) receives his full biographical and critical treatment in Chapter 12, alongside his fellow Victorian prose writers Ruskin, Newman, and Mill; here, know him specifically as the writer whose 1839 pamphlet *Chartism* gave this entire chapter, and much of the literature ahead, its defining vocabulary.

**Charles Darwin** (1809–1882), while not primarily a literary figure, produced in *On the Origin of Species* (1859) one of the single most literarily consequential works of the entire Victorian period. Trained originally for a career in the church before turning to natural history, Darwin spent decades gathering evidence — including from his 1831–1836 voyage aboard HMS *Beagle* — before publishing his theory of evolution by natural selection, which explained the diversity of living species without requiring direct, individual divine creation of each one. The theory's implications for traditional religious belief, and for humanity's own sense of its special place in creation, reverberate through Victorian literature for the rest of this Part.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Carlyle, *Chartism* (1839).** Read this pamphlet as the single text that gave the Victorian age its defining social anxiety a name, arguing that Britain's political class had failed to reckon honestly with the human costs of industrial progress — a challenge the social-problem novel of Chapter 7 takes up directly in fictional form.
- **The People's Charter (1838).** This foundational document of the Chartist movement — demanding universal male suffrage, secret ballots, and other democratic reforms — represents working-class political ambition given concrete, organized form, even though Parliament rejected its demands repeatedly across the following decade.
- **Darwin, *On the Origin of Species* (1859).** Beyond its scientific argument, the book's implications for religious belief and human self-understanding make it essential background for understanding the "crisis of faith" that runs through so much Victorian poetry and prose, discussed further in Chapters 2, 5, and 6.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is "Victorian" a coherent literary period, or a convenient label imposed after the fact?** Much as Part VI, Chapter 1, questioned "Romanticism" as a retrospective critical convenience, "Victorian" risks flattening genuinely disparate literary developments — from Tennyson's doubt-haunted elegy to Wilde's late-century wit — under a single monarch's name. Most literary historians now use the term cautiously, as a useful chronological marker rather than a claim about genuine artistic unity.
- **Was the Great Exhibition genuine achievement or triumphalist spectacle?** The Exhibition's celebration of British industrial prowess drew huge popular enthusiasm, but critics, Carlyle prominent among them, worried that its confident display of manufactured wealth distracted from the Condition of England question's continuing, unresolved urgency for the industrial poor.
- **How much did Darwin alone cause the Victorian crisis of faith?** *On the Origin of Species* is often treated as a single, decisive turning point, but earlier developments — particularly Charles Lyell's geological work in the 1830s, which had already challenged a literal biblical timeline — suggest Darwin accelerated and crystallized doubts that were already developing from multiple directions, rather than creating them from nothing.
- **How should Victorian literature's relationship to empire be read?** Much Victorian fiction treats imperial wealth and expansion as unremarkable background rather than a subject demanding direct scrutiny, a pattern you already glimpsed in Austen's *Mansfield Park* (Part VI, Chapter 10). Part X will take up this question in full through postcolonial criticism, but it's worth noticing, as you read the rest of Part VII, how often empire appears at the margins of texts that never make it their explicit subject.

## Chapter Summary

Queen Victoria's long reign (1837–1901) provides a convenient, if imperfect, boundary for a period defined above all by the full social maturation of Britain's Industrial Revolution: mass urbanization, dangerous factory conditions, and a legislative response that only partially addressed the human costs involved. Thomas Carlyle's 1839 pamphlet *Chartism* named this whole cluster of anxieties the "Condition of England question," giving later writers, especially the social-problem novelists of Chapter 7, a defining vocabulary and challenge. Chartism and the Corn Laws controversy gave this anxiety direct political expression, while the 1851 Great Exhibition displayed Victorian Britain's confident industrial achievement, not without contemporary skepticism about what that confidence obscured. The empire's expansion, culminating in Victoria's 1876 title of Empress of India, forms an often-unexamined backdrop to much Victorian literature, a question Part X will take up fully. And Darwin's 1859 *On the Origin of Species*, building on earlier geological challenges to biblical chronology, intensified a genuine crisis of religious faith that shapes the poetry and prose of nearly every chapter ahead. Chapter 2 now turns to the era's dominant poetic voice, Alfred, Lord Tennyson.

## Glossary

- **"Condition of England question"** — Thomas Carlyle's 1839 term for the urgent social crisis of industrial poverty and class conflict he argued Britain's leadership was failing to address.
- **Chartism** — a mass working-class political movement (1838–1857) demanding democratic reforms set out in the People's Charter (1838).
- **Corn Laws** — tariffs on imported grain, in force from 1815 until their repeal in 1846, that kept bread prices artificially high.
- **Social-problem novel (Condition-of-England novel)** — a genre of Victorian fiction, studied fully in Chapter 7, that dramatizes industrial poverty and class conflict for a middle-class readership.
- **Crisis of faith** — the widespread Victorian struggle to reconcile traditional Christian belief with new scientific knowledge, especially following Darwin's theory of evolution.
