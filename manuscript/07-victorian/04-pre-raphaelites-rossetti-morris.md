## Historical and Socio-Political Context

In 1848, three young painters — Dante Gabriel Rossetti, William Holman Hunt, and John Everett Millais — founded the **Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood**, a name that states their program directly. They believed contemporary Royal Academy painting, following conventions they traced back to Raphael and the High Renaissance, had grown formulaic, idealized, and generalized, losing the vivid color, careful natural detail, and psychological sincerity they admired in earlier, "pre-Raphael" Italian art. Their paintings, often drawing on medieval, biblical, or literary subjects, treated these with unusually intense, closely observed realism — every leaf, every fold of fabric rendered with near-photographic precision — combined with genuine moral and spiritual seriousness rather than mere decoration.

The critic John Ruskin, whom you'll meet fully in Chapter 12, became the Brotherhood's most important early public defender, writing letters to *The Times* against hostile reviewers and later incorporating Pre-Raphaelite principles into his own influential art criticism. The movement soon expanded beyond its original three founders to include Christina Rossetti (Dante Gabriel's sister) and, in a somewhat later, second wave, William Morris — whose remarkable range as poet, designer, and eventual committed socialist gives this chapter its final, and in some ways most far-reaching, figure.

## Literary Movements

Pre-Raphaelite poetry inherits directly from William Blake's example (Part V, Chapter 6): a genuine, sustained interest in combining visual and verbal art within a single creative practice, rather than treating them as separate disciplines. Dante Gabriel Rossetti painted and wrote about the same subjects — "The Blessed Damozel" exists as both an 1850 poem and a later painting — and his sister Christina's poetry was directly illustrated by fellow Pre-Raphaelite artists, most famously her brother's own designs for *Goblin Market*. The movement's characteristic combination of intense sensuous detail, medieval or biblical subject matter, and genuine moral seriousness runs through nearly everything this chapter studies.

Dante Gabriel Rossetti's major poetic project, *The House of Life*, is a **sonnet sequence** — following the Renaissance tradition you've now met revived and reworked several times across this book (Part III; Charlotte Smith, Part VI, Chapter 11; Elizabeth Barrett Browning, Chapter 3) — tracing love, desire, and mortality across 101 sonnets in its final 1881 form, in dense, richly symbolic language blending religious and frankly sensuous imagery. That sensuousness drew serious critical fire: in 1871, the critic Robert Buchanan published a hostile essay, "The Fleshly School of Poetry," attacking Rossetti and several contemporaries for what Buchanan considered excessive physical sensuality dressed up as serious art. The attack wounded Rossetti deeply, contributing to a later decline into paranoia, insomnia, and dependence on chloral hydrate and alcohol.

William Morris's contribution extends the Pre-Raphaelite sensibility well beyond poetry into design, craft, and eventually radical politics. His design firm, and the **Arts and Crafts Movement** it helped found, promoted handcrafted textiles, wallpaper, furniture, and stained glass explicitly against the perceived ugliness and dehumanization of industrial mass production — a direct aesthetic and moral response to exactly the Condition of England question Chapter 1 introduced. Morris's later socialism, culminating in his utopian romance *News from Nowhere* (1890), imagines a future England transformed into a decentralized, craft-based, ecologically harmonious society with industrial capitalism entirely abandoned — connecting the Pre-Raphaelite movement's aesthetic values directly to the political radicalism you've already met in Blake, Shelley, and Byron (Part VI).

## Author Profiles

**Dante Gabriel Rossetti** (1828–1882), a founding member of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood, worked simultaneously as painter and poet throughout his career, often returning to the same subject in both media. His marriage to Elizabeth Siddal, herself a talented painter and poet whose own work has only recently received serious critical attention, ended in 1862 with her death from a laudanum overdose, likely suicide, following the stillbirth of their child. In his grief, Rossetti buried the manuscript of his unpublished poems in her coffin — only to have her grave exhumed seven years later, in 1869, so the poems could be recovered and published, a decision that reveals something genuinely complicated about the relationship between his grief and his artistic ambition. *Poems* (1870) and the eventually expanded *House of Life* sonnet sequence (final form, 1881) show his mature style at its richest, though the "Fleshly School" controversy of 1871 cast a long shadow over his final decade.

**Christina Rossetti** (1830–1894), Dante Gabriel's younger sister, developed a poetic voice distinctly her own, shaped by a devout High Anglican faith that led her to decline at least two marriage proposals over religious differences and to spend years working at a Highgate refuge for former prostitutes. *Goblin Market* (1862), her most celebrated poem, tells the story of two sisters, Laura and Lizzie, and Laura's dangerous temptation by goblin merchants selling irresistible forbidden fruit; after Laura's obsessive craving nearly destroys her, Lizzie heroically confronts the goblins and lets her sister lick the fruit's juice from her own body, an inversion that breaks rather than feeds the addiction and saves Laura's life. The poem's richly sensuous imagery and its themes of temptation, sisterly sacrifice, and redemption have made it one of the most variously interpreted poems in Victorian literature, discussed further below. Christina Rossetti's devotional poetry, including the sonnet "Remember" and the Christmas carol "In the Bleak Midwinter" (1872), remains widely read and, in the carol's case, still sung today — evidence of a lasting popular legacy that complements her more recent critical rehabilitation as one of the greatest Victorian poets, a recovery echoing the reputation-history pattern you've met with several other women writers in this book.

**William Morris** (1834–1896) brought the Pre-Raphaelite sensibility into an unusually wide range of practical and political projects. His early poetry, including *The Defence of Guenevere and Other Poems* (1858) and the massive *The Earthly Paradise* (1868–1870), retold Arthurian, classical, and Norse material with real narrative richness and popular success. In 1861, he founded a design firm producing handcrafted textiles, wallpaper, and furniture based on medieval and Pre-Raphaelite aesthetic principles, becoming a founding figure of the Arts and Crafts Movement — a direct reaction against industrial mass production's perceived dehumanization of craft labor. Morris grew increasingly politically radical in his later years, founding the Socialist League in 1884 and writing *News from Nowhere* (1890), a utopian romance imagining a post-revolutionary England organized around decentralized, dignified craft labor rather than industrial capitalism. In 1891, he founded the Kelmscott Press, producing lavishly designed, hand-crafted books in medieval-inspired typography, most famously the 1896 Kelmscott Chaucer — a fitting final project connecting Morris's aesthetic philosophy back to Chaucer himself (Part II, Chapter 3).

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **D. G. Rossetti, "The Blessed Damozel" (1850).** This early poem, depicting a deceased woman in heaven longing for her earthly lover to join her, blends devotional imagery with intense romantic and sensuous longing — a combination that would remain characteristic of Rossetti's mature work.
- **D. G. Rossetti, *The House of Life* (final form, 1881).** Read this sonnet sequence's dense, symbol-laden language as Rossetti's most sustained attempt to fuse spiritual and physical experience of love into a single poetic vision, the very quality Buchanan's "Fleshly School" attack condemned as excessive.
- **Christina Rossetti, *Goblin Market* (1862).** The poem rewards multiple, coexisting readings: as a Christian allegory of temptation and redemptive sacrifice, as an exploration of female sexual desire and its dangers, and as a commentary on the seductive, potentially destructive logic of market exchange itself — none of which cancels out the others.
- **Christina Rossetti, devotional poetry.** "Remember," with its unusually generous request to be forgotten rather than mourned if remembering causes pain, and "In the Bleak Midwinter," still sung as a Christmas carol, show Rossetti's devotional voice at its most emotionally direct and enduringly popular.
- **Morris, *News from Nowhere* (1890).** This utopian romance imagines Morris's design philosophy carried to its full political conclusion: a future society that has abandoned industrial capitalism entirely in favor of decentralized, craft-based, genuinely dignified labor — connecting the aesthetic concerns of this chapter directly to the political radicalism of Blake, Shelley, and Byron in Part VI.

## Critical Perspectives

- **How should *Goblin Market* be read?** The poem supports genuinely multiple interpretations at once — religious allegory, exploration of female desire, economic critique — and most modern critics resist choosing a single "correct" reading, treating the poem's rich ambiguity as central to its lasting power rather than a problem to be resolved.
- **What does Rossetti's exhumation of Elizabeth Siddal's grave reveal?** Retrieving buried poems from a dead wife's coffin sits uneasily between grief, guilt, and genuine artistic ambition — a decision that resists any simple moral verdict, but that says something real about how completely Rossetti's identity was bound up in his own poetic legacy.
- **Was the "Fleshly School" attack fair criticism or prudish philistinism?** Buchanan's essay reflected genuine contemporary anxiety about sensuous poetic material, but later critical opinion has largely rehabilitated Rossetti's work, suggesting that what one generation condemns as excessive can look, to a later one, like genuine artistic seriousness about physical and spiritual experience together.
- **Is there a tension between Morris's socialism and his own luxury craft goods?** Morris's handcrafted textiles and furniture were, in practice, expensive items only wealthy patrons could typically afford — a real and often-noted contradiction with his stated socialist commitment to dignified labor and goods accessible to all, worth holding onto rather than resolving too neatly in his favor.

## Chapter Summary

Founded in 1848 as a reaction against formulaic Royal Academy convention, the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood brought a combined visual-verbal artistic practice, medieval and biblical subject matter, and genuine moral seriousness to both painting and poetry, extending the example William Blake had already set in Part V. Dante Gabriel Rossetti's dual painter-poet career, culminating in *The House of Life* (1881), drew both admiration and, in Robert Buchanan's 1871 "Fleshly School" attack, real hostility. His sister Christina Rossetti's *Goblin Market* (1862) remains one of the most richly interpretable poems of the Victorian period, supporting religious, sexual, and economic readings simultaneously, while her devotional poetry achieved a lasting popular legacy few other Victorian poets matched. William Morris extended the movement furthest, from Arthurian and classical narrative poetry into design, craft, and eventually committed socialist politics, directly connecting Pre-Raphaelite aesthetics to the Condition of England question introduced in Chapter 1. Chapter 5 now turns to Matthew Arnold, whose poetry and criticism engage many of the same Victorian anxieties from a more skeptical, less overtly medievalizing perspective.

## Glossary

- **Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood** — a group of English painters and, later, poets, founded in 1848, reacting against Royal Academy convention in favor of detailed naturalism, vivid color, and moral seriousness modeled on art before Raphael.
- **Sonnet sequence** — a connected series of sonnets, usually exploring a single overarching theme such as love, across many individual poems.
- **"Fleshly"** — the critical term Robert Buchanan used in his 1871 attack on Rossetti and his contemporaries, describing poetry he considered excessively sensuous or physical.
- **Arts and Crafts Movement** — a design movement founded substantially by William Morris, promoting handcrafted goods against industrial mass production.
