## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Matthew Arnold was born in 1822, the son of Thomas Arnold, the famous and hugely influential headmaster of Rugby School, whose reforms — emphasizing moral character alongside academic learning — shaped Victorian public-school education broadly and later inspired Thomas Hughes's popular novel *Tom Brown's School Days* (1857). Growing up as the son of such a prominent, morally serious educational reformer left Matthew Arnold with a lifelong preoccupation with culture, moral seriousness, and social reform that runs through both his poetry and his later criticism.

After Rugby and Balliol College, Oxford, Arnold spent thirty-five years, from 1851 until his death, working as a government Inspector of Schools — a demanding, unglamorous administrative career that, through extensive travel and reporting on European educational systems, gave him an unusually direct, comparative view of culture and society across national lines, grounding his later criticism in genuine practical experience rather than armchair theorizing. His close friendship with the poet Arthur Hugh Clough, whose death in 1861 prompted Arnold's pastoral elegy "Thyrsis" (itself following the tradition of Milton's "Lycidas," Part III), was another formative relationship of his early career.

Arnold's own literary career follows a distinctive arc: an intense but relatively compact period of poetic composition, largely complete by his mid-forties, followed by a long, prolific turn to prose criticism that occupied the rest of his life. This chapter studies both halves of that career, and the genuine continuity of concern running through them.

## Literary Movements

"Dover Beach" is Arnold's single most famous poem, and it belongs directly alongside Tennyson's *In Memoriam* (Chapter 2) as one of the two defining Victorian statements of religious doubt. Standing at a window overlooking the English Channel, the poem's speaker moves from calm natural beauty — "The sea is calm tonight" — to a devastating extended metaphor: the "Sea of Faith" that "was once, too, at the full" has retreated, leaving only its "melancholy, long, withdrawing roar." The poem's final image, a "darkling plain" where "ignorant armies clash by night," offers one of the bleakest visions of the modern condition in English poetry, though its closing appeal to human fidelity — "Ah, love, let us be true / To one another!" — offers a modest, human-scaled alternative to the lost cosmic order.

"The Scholar-Gipsy" (1853) approaches similar territory from a different angle, drawing on a seventeenth-century Oxford legend about a poor scholar who abandoned university life to live among gypsies and learn their secret lore, remaining, according to the story, unaged and occasionally sighted decades later. Arnold uses the legend to contrast the scholar-gipsy's single-minded, undistracted purpose against what he calls, in the poem's own memorable phrase, "this strange disease of modern life" — its "sick hurry, divided aims." Both poems share Arnold's characteristic diagnosis: a modern world that has lost the wholeness and certainty earlier ages, real or imagined, once possessed.

Arnold's later criticism develops this diagnosis into a full cultural program. *Culture and Anarchy* (1869) defines **culture** as **"the best that has been thought and said"** — a phrase that became enormously influential, and later contested, as a definition of the humanist liberal-arts ideal. Arnold argues that English society was dangerously fragmented into narrow class interests: the **Barbarians** (the aristocracy, preoccupied with manners and sport), the **Philistines** (the middle class, absorbed in business and material wealth, indifferent to higher culture), and the **Populace** (the as-yet-uneducated working class). Only a shared culture, offering what Arnold calls, borrowing the phrase from Jonathan Swift's *Battle of the Books* (Part IV, Chapter 5), **"sweetness and light,"** could unite these fragmented interests and prevent the "anarchy" of pure class self-assertion. Arnold's related theory of critical **disinterestedness** — the critic's obligation to pursue objective understanding free from practical, political, or partisan pressure — extends this same commitment to intellectual honesty into the practice of criticism itself, articulated most fully in "The Function of Criticism at the Present Time" (1864/1865).

## Author Profiles

**Matthew Arnold** (1822–1888) wrote nearly all his enduring poetry within roughly a decade, from *The Strayed Reveller* (1849) through *New Poems* (1867), before turning almost entirely to prose criticism for the rest of his career. A telling episode from this transition: his 1852 poem "Empedocles on Etna," dramatizing the Greek philosopher's suicide by leaping into the volcano, was withdrawn by Arnold himself from his 1853 collected *Poems*, with a preface explaining that the poem's unrelieved despair, lacking any genuine poetic "joy" or consolation, failed to justify the suffering it depicted — an early, explicit statement of the critical standards Arnold would spend the rest of his career developing. His later criticism, above all *Culture and Anarchy* (1869) and the essays collected across two series (1865, 1888), made him one of the most influential cultural critics of the Victorian age, and in "The Study of Poetry" (1880) he made an extraordinary prediction: that as scientific and critical doubt continued eroding religious dogma, "most of what now passes with us for religion and philosophy will be replaced by poetry" — poetry itself taking up the consolatory, interpretive cultural role religion could no longer reliably supply. This claim directly extends the anxiety of "Dover Beach" into a positive program for literature's expanding importance in a secularizing age.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"Dover Beach" (1867, likely composed 1851).** Read the poem's movement from sensory description to metaphysical despair to human consolation as a compressed version of the whole Victorian crisis of faith: beauty observed, certainty lost, and only human loyalty offered in its place.
- **"The Scholar-Gipsy" (1853).** The scholar-gipsy's legendary single-mindedness serves as a foil for Arnold's diagnosis of modern fragmentation — a wistful, elegiac contrast rather than a genuinely available alternative for the poem's own modern speaker.
- **"Empedocles on Etna" (1852, withdrawn 1853).** Arnold's own explanation for withdrawing this poem reveals his developing critical conviction that poetry has an obligation to offer readers something more than accurate depiction of despair — an early statement of concerns his mature criticism would develop at length.
- ***Culture and Anarchy* (1869).** Arnold's class typology and his definition of culture as "the best that has been thought and said" remain among the most influential, and most contested, statements of the humanist cultural ideal in English criticism.
- **"The Function of Criticism at the Present Time" (1864/1865).** Arnold's defense of criticism as a genuinely valuable, active cultural practice, guided by "disinterestedness," argues against treating criticism as merely secondary to creative literature.
- **"The Study of Poetry" (1880).** Arnold's prediction that poetry would increasingly assume religion's cultural function gives his entire career, poetry and criticism together, its clearest unifying statement of purpose.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does "Dover Beach" achieve genuine consolation?** The poem's closing appeal to human fidelity offers real, if modest, comfort, but many readers find it deliberately insufficient against the "darkling plain" that precedes it — a smaller, more human-scaled hope than the cosmic order the poem mourns losing, comparable to the qualified consolations you've already met in Tennyson's *In Memoriam* and Wordsworth's Immortality Ode (Part VI, Chapter 3).
- **Is "the best that has been thought and said" a universal ideal, or a particular one dressed as universal?** Later critics have questioned whether Arnold's culture, drawing heavily on classical and European literary tradition, quietly universalizes a specific, educated, upper-middle-class cultural standard as though it were culture itself — a critique with real implications for later debates about canon formation this book has touched on throughout.
- **Is Arnold's Barbarians/Philistines/Populace typology fair sociology or reductive caricature?** The framework offers a memorable, quotable way of describing English class culture, but critics have long debated whether it does genuine analytical work or simply repackages condescension toward each class in turn.
- **Did Arnold's prediction that poetry would replace religion come true?** Over a century later, this claim looks both prescient (in the seriousness with which secular readers often turn to literature for meaning) and overstated (organized religion has hardly disappeared) — a useful test case for how boldly literary critics can, and perhaps should, predict culture's future direction.

## Chapter Summary

Shaped by his father Thomas Arnold's educational reforms and grounded by decades of practical experience as a school inspector, Matthew Arnold wrote a compact but enduring body of poetry — above all "Dover Beach" (1867) and "The Scholar-Gipsy" (1853) — diagnosing modern spiritual doubt and fragmentation, before turning to criticism for the remainder of his career. *Culture and Anarchy* (1869) defined culture as "the best that has been thought and said" and offered a memorable, if contested, class typology of Barbarians, Philistines, and Populace; his essays on criticism argued for "disinterestedness" as the critic's proper discipline; and "The Study of Poetry" (1880) made his boldest claim of all, that poetry would increasingly assume the cultural functions religion could no longer securely provide. Arnold's career shows, more clearly than almost any other Victorian writer's, how directly poetry and criticism could serve the same underlying purpose: helping readers navigate a world in which inherited certainties, religious and otherwise, no longer held automatically. Chapter 6 now turns to Gerard Manley Hopkins, whose own struggle to reconcile faith and modern experience takes a very different, more explicitly devotional path than Arnold's.

## Glossary

- **"The best that has been thought and said"** — Matthew Arnold's definition of culture, from *Culture and Anarchy* (1869).
- **Barbarians, Philistines, Populace** — Arnold's typology of English social classes in *Culture and Anarchy*: the aristocracy, the middle class, and the working class, respectively.
- **"Sweetness and light"** — a phrase Arnold borrowed from Swift's *Battle of the Books* (Part IV, Chapter 5) to describe culture's civilizing, unifying effect.
- **Disinterestedness** — Arnold's principle that criticism should pursue objective understanding free from practical, political, or partisan pressure.
