## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Alfred Tennyson was born in 1809 in Somersby, Lincolnshire, one of twelve children of a clergyman whose own struggles with depression and alcoholism, compounded by a family history of epilepsy and mental instability affecting several of his siblings, made Tennyson's childhood home a genuinely difficult one — and left him with a lifelong private anxiety about inheriting the same instability. At Trinity College, Cambridge, from 1827, Tennyson joined an elite intellectual society called the Cambridge Apostles, where he formed the defining friendship of his life with Arthur Henry Hallam, a brilliant young man already engaged to marry Tennyson's own sister Emily. Hallam died suddenly in 1833, at just twenty-two, of a cerebral hemorrhage while traveling in Vienna — a loss that devastated Tennyson personally and struck his whole family doubly hard, and that directly produced his greatest and most sustained poetic achievement.

The year 1850 marks the pivot of Tennyson's career, arriving all at once: he finally published *In Memoriam A.H.H.*, the long elegy for Hallam he had been composing, on and off, for seventeen years; its enormous success led directly to his appointment as Poet Laureate, succeeding Wordsworth, who had died that same year; and Tennyson married Emily Sellwood, after a courtship delayed more than a decade by financial uncertainty and family disapproval. He held the Laureateship for the rest of his life — one of the longest tenures in the office's history — writing occasional poems to mark major national events, most famously "The Charge of the Light Brigade" (1854), discussed below.

## Literary Movements

Tennyson's "Ulysses" (1833, published 1842) is one of the earliest and finest examples of a form this Part will return to directly in Chapter 3: the **dramatic monologue** — a poem spoken entirely in the voice of a specific character, addressed to an implied listener, that reveals the speaker's own psychology through what they say, sometimes more fully than the speaker themselves realizes. In "Ulysses," the aged Homeric hero, restless after returning home from his long voyage, resolves to set out once more rather than settle into ordinary domestic rule — "To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield." Written in the immediate aftermath of Hallam's death, the poem reads naturally as Tennyson's own transmuted response to loss: a determination to keep purposefully moving forward rather than surrender to grief.

*In Memoriam A.H.H.* takes up grief far more directly and at far greater length, across 133 numbered lyric sections composed in a distinctive quatrain form — rhymed ABBA, in iambic tetrameter — now often called the **"In Memoriam stanza."** The poem traces an extended, genuinely evolving process: raw sorrow gradually giving way to searching doubt, engagement with the unsettling scientific findings of contemporary geology (which had already begun suggesting a natural world of vast timescales, extinction, and struggle rather than settled, providential order), and only a hard-won, qualified consolation by its end. Its most famous line — "Nature, red in tooth and claw" — captures exactly the crisis Chapter 1 introduced: a natural world that seemed, to increasingly many Victorian readers, indifferent or even hostile to the comforting religious order earlier generations had assumed. Published nine years before Darwin's *On the Origin of Species*, *In Memoriam* shows that Victorian anxiety about faith and nature was already fully underway before 1859 gave it its most famous scientific statement.

## Author Profiles

**Alfred, Lord Tennyson** (1809–1892) achieved a level of sustained institutional recognition and popular love that sets him apart from many of the writers this book has traced through earlier "doomed genius" patterns — Chatterton (Part V, Chapter 4), the myth built around Keats (Part VI, Chapter 7), even Blake's long posthumous obscurity (Part V, Chapter 6). Tennyson, by contrast, spent over forty years as Poet Laureate at the very center of Victorian public life, dying a celebrated national figure and receiving burial in Westminster Abbey. His early poetry, including "The Lady of Shalott" (1832, revised 1842), already shows his extraordinary technical gift: rich, musical language and precise sensory description in service of a story — a woman cursed to view the world only through a mirror's reflection, who breaks her curse by looking directly at Sir Lancelot and dies floating down the river toward Camelot — that later became one of the single most popular subjects for the Pre-Raphaelite painters you'll meet in Chapter 4. Tennyson's technical mastery of poetic sound is often ranked, by admiring critics, second only to Milton's in the whole English tradition, though some contemporaries and later critics have questioned whether that musical brilliance sometimes outran genuine intellectual substance — a tension worth weighing directly against *In Memoriam*'s real philosophical seriousness. Beyond his shorter lyrics, Tennyson devoted decades to *Idylls of the King* (1859–1885), an ambitious cycle retelling the Arthurian legend across connected narrative poems, exploring ideal kingship and the fragile order of Camelot's eventual fall — a project that occupied much of his later career, even if it is less widely read today than his shorter, more concentrated work.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***In Memoriam A.H.H.* (1850).** Read this not as a single, unified argument but as a genuine record of grief's actual movement over time — doubt, scientific anxiety, and consolation alternating and developing across its 133 sections, rather than resolving neatly into a single fixed position on faith.
- **"Ulysses" (1833/1842).** The poem's heroic determination to "strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield" has made it one of the most quoted expressions of persistent purpose in English poetry, though its speaker's willingness to abandon his kingdom and son for one final voyage complicates any simple reading of the poem as uncomplicated heroism.
- **"The Lady of Shalott" (1832/1842).** Often read as a meditation on the isolation of the artist, forced to experience the world only through mediated representation rather than direct engagement, the poem's rich symbolism made it an enduring source of inspiration for the visual artists of Chapter 4.
- **"The Charge of the Light Brigade" (1854).** Responding to a real, disastrous cavalry charge during the Crimean War's Battle of Balaclava, caused by a miscommunicated order, the poem honors the soldiers' disciplined courage — "Theirs not to reason why, / Theirs but to do and die" — while still openly acknowledging the command failure that doomed them: "someone had blundered."
- ***Idylls of the King* (1859–1885).** This sustained Arthurian cycle, exploring the rise and eventual fall of Camelot's moral order, reflects broader Victorian anxieties about whether the era's own rapid social change might likewise threaten a fragile civilizational order — a large-scale project demonstrating Tennyson's range well beyond his more concentrated shorter lyrics.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does *In Memoriam* achieve genuine consolation?** The poem's final sections move toward hope and acceptance, but many critics read this resolution as hard-won and provisional rather than fully settled — closer to Wordsworth's compensating "philosophic mind" in the Immortality Ode (Part VI, Chapter 3) than to any confident religious certainty.
- **Is "Ulysses" a celebration of heroic striving, or something more ambiguous?** Ulysses's decision to abandon his elderly kingdom and son Telemachus, whom he describes with a certain condescension, in favor of one more adventure raises real questions about whether the poem endorses his choice unreservedly or invites readers to notice what, and whom, he is leaving behind.
- **Does Tennyson's musical brilliance sometimes come at the expense of intellectual depth?** Critics both contemporary and later have voiced this concern, though *In Memoriam*'s genuine philosophical seriousness about faith, doubt, and scientific challenge makes a strong case against any simple dismissal of Tennyson as merely a master of pleasing sound.
- **How should "The Charge of the Light Brigade" be read: celebration or critique?** The poem honors the soldiers' obedience and courage without endorsing the decision that sent them to their deaths, holding admiration and implicit institutional criticism together in a way that resists any single, simple verdict on military heroism.

## Chapter Summary

Shaped decisively by the sudden death of his close friend Arthur Hallam in 1833, Alfred Tennyson spent seventeen years composing *In Memoriam A.H.H.* (1850), the Victorian age's defining poem of grief and religious doubt, directly engaging the same crisis of faith Chapter 1 introduced, years before Darwin gave it its most famous scientific statement. His dramatic monologue "Ulysses" (1833/1842) transmutes personal loss into a statement of persistent purpose, while "The Lady of Shalott" (1832/1842) explores artistic isolation in imagery that would prove enormously influential on the Pre-Raphaelite painters of Chapter 4. As Poet Laureate from 1850 until his death in 1892, Tennyson gave the Victorian age its most memorable public poem of military honor and institutional failure in "The Charge of the Light Brigade" (1854), and his long Arthurian cycle, *Idylls of the King*, meditated on the fragility of moral and civic order across three decades of sustained composition. Unlike so many writers this book has traced through patterns of neglect and posthumous rediscovery, Tennyson achieved and sustained enormous institutional recognition throughout his own lifetime. Chapter 3 now turns to his great poetic contemporaries, Robert and Elizabeth Barrett Browning, who develop the dramatic monologue Tennyson pioneered in "Ulysses" into their own distinctive achievements.

## Glossary

- **Dramatic monologue** — a poem spoken entirely in the voice of a specific character, addressed to an implied listener, revealing the speaker's psychology through their own words.
- **"In Memoriam stanza"** — the rhymed ABBA quatrain form, in iambic tetrameter, Tennyson used throughout *In Memoriam A.H.H.*
- **Idyll** — a short poem, often narrative or pastoral, describing a particular scene or incident; the basis for the title of Tennyson's *Idylls of the King*.
