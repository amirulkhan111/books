## Historical and Socio-Political Context

By the last two decades of Victoria's reign, a new mood had begun to unsettle the confident moral earnestness that had shaped so much of the literature you've studied so far in this Part. Critics now often call this mood the **fin de siècle** — French for "end of century" — a term capturing a widespread sense, among writers and artists across Europe, that an old order was closing and something more uncertain, more skeptical, and often more decadent was taking its place. Where Matthew Arnold, in Chapter 5, had argued that culture should pursue "sweetness and light" as a serious moral and civic good, a younger generation of writers began to ask why art needed any moral justification at all — why beauty, wit, and pleasure shouldn't be treated as valuable in themselves, independent of whether they improved anyone's character.

This shift also reflects a less comfortable set of anxieties. Darwin's theory of evolution (Chapter 1) had already unsettled religious certainty; by the 1880s and 1890s, some scientists and social critics had extended his ideas into more troubling territory, worrying that modern civilization itself might be capable of biological or moral "degeneration" — sliding backward rather than progressing. Fiction of this period repeatedly dramatizes a fear that respectable, civilized appearances might conceal something darker and more primitive underneath, whether inside a single divided human mind, inside a secret criminal underworld hidden beneath ordinary domestic life, or inside the outwardly respectable institutions of the empire.

Meanwhile, an expanding reading public, cheaper periodicals, and new mass-circulation magazines — like *The Strand Magazine*, launched in 1891 — created enormous appetite for gripping, plot-driven popular fiction, continuing the serialization culture you first met with Dickens in Chapter 7. Sensation fiction and, later, detective fiction answered that appetite directly, building entire novels and stories around crime, secrecy, and the thrill of discovery. This chapter studies four writers who, in different ways, all responded to this late-Victorian mood: Robert Louis Stevenson and Oscar Wilde, whose fiction and drama probe questions of hidden identity and the value of beauty and pleasure, and Wilkie Collins and Arthur Conan Doyle, whose sensation and detective fiction gave Victorian readers a thrilling, orderly way to confront the same anxieties about hidden crime and disorder.

## Literary Movements

**Aestheticism**, the guiding movement behind Wilde's work, holds that art's value lies in beauty and sensation, not in moral instruction or social usefulness — a position often summarized by the slogan "**art for art's sake**." The critic Walter Pater gave the movement one of its most influential statements in *Studies in the History of the Renaissance* (1873), urging readers to seek out intense, heightened aesthetic experience for its own sake rather than for any lesson it might teach. This stands in deliberate tension with the moral seriousness Matthew Arnold had championed in Chapter 5: where Arnold treated culture as a civic good meant to elevate and improve society, aestheticism insisted that demanding art justify itself morally missed the point of art altogether. Aestheticism shaded, in its more extreme and self-consciously provocative forms, into **decadence** — a related late-century movement celebrating artifice, excess, and sensation, often deliberately shocking conventional Victorian taste.

Stevenson's fiction belongs to a different but related current: the **Gothic double**, a figure you already met in the Gothic novel tradition of Part VI, Chapter 9. Where Mary Shelley's Frankenstein confronts a monstrous creation entirely separate from himself, Stevenson's *Jekyll and Hyde* internalizes that horror completely — the monster and the respectable gentleman turn out to be one and the same person, split into two physical bodies by scientific experiment. This is a distinctly late-Victorian variation on the Gothic: the threat isn't a foreign castle or ancient curse, but the divided, potentially uncontrollable nature hiding inside every outwardly civilized person.

The **sensation novel**, pioneered by Wilkie Collins in the 1860s, brought crime, scandal, bigamy, and hidden secrets into ordinary English domestic settings, deliberately designed to shock and thrill a respectable middle-class readership with the suggestion that such disorder might be lurking behind any comfortable household's front door. **Detective fiction**, which grew directly out of this sensation tradition, offered readers a reassuring counter-movement: rather than simply exposing hidden crime, it dramatized crime being solved, methodically and rationally, restoring order through a detective's superior powers of observation and deduction. Arthur Conan Doyle's Sherlock Holmes became the genre's defining figure, and the enormous, sustained popularity of the Holmes stories shows how powerfully this fantasy of rational order answered late-Victorian anxieties about hidden disorder.

## Author Profiles

**Robert Louis Stevenson** (1850–1894), born in Edinburgh, abandoned an initial engineering and law education for a literary career pursued partly in search of a healthier climate, since he suffered from chronic lung illness for most of his life; he eventually settled in Samoa, where he died in 1894. His most enduring works include the adventure novel *Treasure Island* (1883), which helped establish many enduring conventions of pirate fiction, and *Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde* (1886), discussed fully below, alongside the historical adventure novel *Kidnapped* (1886).

**Oscar Wilde** (1854–1900), born in Dublin and educated at Trinity College Dublin and then Oxford, became the most celebrated wit and aesthete of the late Victorian period, cultivating a public persona built around the **epigram** — a short, polished saying that turns a conventional idea neatly on its head — along with paradox and a deliberately provocative devotion to beauty over conventional morality. His major works include the novel *The Picture of Dorian Gray* (1890) and a sequence of brilliant society comedies culminating in *The Importance of Being Earnest* (1895). That same year, Wilde's private life collided catastrophically with Victorian public morality: after suing the Marquess of Queensberry for libel over an accusation related to Wilde's relationship with the marquess's son, Wilde lost the case and was himself prosecuted and convicted of "gross indecency" — the era's legal term criminalizing homosexual acts between men — and sentenced to two years of hard labor. He wrote a long, anguished letter later published as *De Profundis* and the poem *The Ballad of Reading Gaol* (1898) in the aftermath, but never recovered his health, reputation, or finances, and died in Paris in 1900, in poverty and exile.

**Wilkie Collins** (1824–1889), a close friend and sometime collaborator of Dickens, published his major sensation novels in Dickens's own periodical, *All the Year Round* — a direct continuation of the serialization culture from Chapter 7. His two most significant works, *The Woman in White* (1859–60) and *The Moonstone* (1868), are discussed fully below.

**Arthur Conan Doyle** (1859–1930), born in Edinburgh and trained as a physician, based his most famous creation, the detective Sherlock Holmes, partly on one of his own medical school teachers, Dr. Joseph Bell, whose remarkable skill at diagnosing patients through close observation of small physical details left a deep impression on the young Doyle. Beginning with *A Study in Scarlet* (1887), Doyle's Holmes stories, especially once serialized in *The Strand Magazine* from 1891, became a genuine popular phenomenon; when Doyle, tiring of the character and wanting to focus on other writing, killed Holmes off in 1893, public outcry eventually forced him to revive the detective in later stories, a striking early demonstration of how completely a fictional character could outgrow its own author's intentions.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Stevenson, *Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde* (1886).** Dr. Henry Jekyll, a respected physician, develops a potion that separates his personality into two distinct bodies: the outwardly respectable Jekyll and the violent, uninhibited Mr. Hyde, who commits increasingly serious crimes as Jekyll loses control over the transformation. The novella works powerfully as psychological allegory, suggesting that Victorian respectability itself depends on suppressing a darker, more instinctive side of human nature that can never be fully eliminated, only temporarily contained.
- **Wilde, *The Picture of Dorian Gray* (1890).** A beautiful young man, Dorian Gray, wishes that a portrait of himself might age and bear the marks of his sins instead of his own face — a wish that is granted, allowing Dorian to pursue increasingly corrupt pleasures while his painted image grows monstrous in a locked room. The novel's preface directly defends art's independence from conventional morality — asserting, in Wilde's own famous formulation, that books are simply "well written, or badly written," not moral or immoral — even as the novel's plot arguably punishes Dorian's amoral pursuit of pleasure in the end, a tension critics still debate.
- **Wilde, *The Importance of Being Earnest* (1895).** Subtitled *A Trivial Comedy for Serious People*, this society comedy follows two young men who each invent a fictitious alter ego to escape social obligations, entangling them in a plot built almost entirely around mistaken identity, absurd coincidence, and Wilde's characteristic epigrammatic wit. Beneath its farcical plot, the play relentlessly satirizes Victorian social hypocrisy, marriage as a social transaction, and the rigid earnestness Wilde's own aestheticism rejected.
- **Collins, *The Woman in White* (1859–60).** Told through multiple narrators' testimonies, as though assembling evidence for a legal case, the novel follows a plot involving mistaken identity, a wrongful confinement in a private asylum, and a scheme to seize an heiress's inheritance — a foundational work of sensation fiction, exposing the disorder and cruelty that could lurk behind respectable English domestic institutions.
- **Collins, *The Moonstone* (1868).** Following the theft of a valuable diamond looted from India and its mysterious disappearance from an English country house, the novel is often credited as the first great detective novel in English, featuring the methodical Sergeant Cuff and a similarly multi-narrator structure to *The Woman in White*; its opening backstory, involving the diamond's violent seizure during British military action in India, quietly links its English domestic mystery to the empire's own history, a connection Part X will take up more fully.
- **Conan Doyle, the Sherlock Holmes stories (1887 onward).** Narrated by Holmes's loyal companion Dr. Watson, the stories showcase Holmes's method of **ratiocination** — drawing confident, often startling conclusions from small physical details ordinary observers overlook — offering readers the satisfying spectacle of apparent chaos resolved through pure rational deduction.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does *Jekyll and Hyde* suggest that evil is a separate, external force, or an inseparable part of every civilized person?** Most modern critics read the novella as arguing the latter: Hyde isn't a foreign contamination introduced into Jekyll, but a genuine part of Jekyll's own nature that Victorian respectability had simply forced into hiding — a considerably more unsettling idea than a simple battle between an innocent man and an external monster.
- **How should Wilde's biography shape our reading of his fiction and drama?** For much of the twentieth century, Wilde's 1895 trials overshadowed serious critical attention to his work, reducing him in popular memory to scandal rather than artistic achievement. More recent criticism reads *The Picture of Dorian Gray* and Wilde's plays as engaging seriously, if often obliquely under the threat of prosecution, with questions of hidden identity, forbidden desire, and the gap between public respectability and private truth — questions that take on particular resonance given what happened to Wilde himself only a few years later.
- **Do genre fictions like the sensation novel and detective fiction deserve the same critical seriousness as Dickens, Eliot, or Hardy?** Sensation and detective fiction were both wildly popular but, for many decades, dismissed by critics as merely commercial entertainment rather than serious literature. More recent scholarship has pushed back strongly, arguing that Collins's exposure of institutional cruelty (private asylums, coercive marriage law) and Conan Doyle's engagement with new forensic science and empire deserve exactly the same sustained critical attention as any more conventionally "literary" Victorian fiction.
- **What does *The Moonstone*'s colonial backstory suggest about detective fiction's relationship to empire?** The diamond at the novel's center is itself stolen imperial property, and its disappearance in England echoes an earlier act of violence committed by Englishmen in India — a detail some critics read as detective fiction quietly working through anxieties about empire's own hidden costs, anticipating the fuller postcolonial critical treatment in Part X.

## Chapter Summary

The late Victorian period's fin-de-siècle mood produced fiction markedly more skeptical of earlier moral earnestness than anything studied earlier in this Part. Robert Louis Stevenson's *Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde* internalized the Gothic double from Part VI into a single divided self, suggesting that civilized respectability always conceals a more dangerous nature underneath. Oscar Wilde, building on Walter Pater's aestheticism, championed art's independence from conventional morality in *The Picture of Dorian Gray* and *The Importance of Being Earnest*, before his own 1895 trials and imprisonment exposed how little tolerance Victorian society actually extended to the private lives behind such art. Wilkie Collins's sensation fiction and Arthur Conan Doyle's Sherlock Holmes stories, meanwhile, gave Victorian readers two complementary ways of confronting hidden crime and disorder — one exposing it, the other triumphantly solving it — through immensely popular genre fiction whose critical reputation has only recently caught up with its cultural impact. Chapter 12 turns from fiction to the era's great essayists and social critics: Carlyle, Ruskin, Newman, and Mill.

## Glossary

- **Fin de siècle** — French for "end of century"; the late-nineteenth-century mood of cultural uncertainty and reaction against earlier Victorian moral earnestness.
- **Aestheticism ("art for art's sake")** — the movement, associated with Walter Pater and Oscar Wilde, holding that art's value lies in beauty and sensation rather than moral instruction.
- **Decadence** — a related late-century movement celebrating artifice, excess, and sensation, often deliberately provocative toward conventional Victorian taste.
- **Epigram** — a short, polished saying that turns a conventional idea neatly on its head, the signature device of Oscar Wilde's wit.
- **Sensation novel** — a genre of Victorian fiction, pioneered by Wilkie Collins, built around crime, scandal, and hidden secrets within ordinary domestic settings.
- **Ratiocination** — confident, systematic reasoning from small observed details to a conclusion, the defining method of Sherlock Holmes's detective work.
