## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Describe the major historical shocks of the early twentieth century — the First World War above all — and explain why they unsettled Victorian confidence in progress and order.

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Queen Victoria died in January 1901, closing a reign of over sixty-three years that had given the previous Part of this book its name and its rough shape. But as you've now seen repeated many times across this book — with "Romanticism" in Part VI and "Victorian" itself in Part VII — a monarch's death is a convenient marker, not a genuine turning point in literary history. The writers you'll meet across this Part didn't wake up on a single morning in 1901 and start writing differently. What actually changed English literature, over the following four decades, was a sequence of much deeper shocks to the confident, orderly Victorian worldview you've spent the last thirteen chapters studying — shocks severe enough that many writers of this period came to feel, quite consciously, that the old ways of telling a story or shaping a poem could no longer describe the world they were actually living in.

The single greatest of these shocks was the **First World War** (1914–1918). Earlier wars in the century — the Crimean War behind Tennyson's "The Charge of the Light Brigade" (Part VII, Chapter 2), or the colonial conflicts underlying Victorian imperial confidence — had done little to disturb Britain's broader faith that history moved, however unevenly, toward progress. The First World War was different in kind, not just scale: new industrial weapons — machine guns, poison gas, heavy artillery, and eventually tanks — combined with trench warfare to kill and maim on a scale no one had previously imagined, including roughly 900,000 British dead. For a generation raised to believe in steady human and moral improvement, the spectacle of Europe's most "civilized" nations spending four years methodically destroying an entire generation of young men was very difficult to reconcile with any confident Victorian story about progress. You'll meet the poets who wrote directly out of this experience — some still believing in the war's nobility, others shattered by what they witnessed — in Chapter 3.

Alongside the war, three intellectual developments unsettled Victorian certainties from a different direction entirely. The Austrian physician Sigmund Freud, whose *The Interpretation of Dreams* appeared in 1899 (dated 1900), argued that human beings are governed as much by an **unconscious** mind — a hidden layer of desire, memory, and conflict operating beneath ordinary awareness — as by the rational, self-aware mind Victorian thought had generally assumed was in charge. Albert Einstein's theories of relativity (1905 and 1915), confirmed dramatically to public attention by a 1919 solar eclipse observation, suggested that even time and space themselves were not the fixed, absolute background Victorian science had assumed, but something that shifted depending on an observer's own position and motion. And the French philosopher Henri Bergson distinguished between clock time — the uniform, mechanically measured time a Victorian railway timetable assumed — and **duration**, the flowing, unevenly stretched and compressed way time is actually *experienced* by a conscious mind. Together, these ideas suggested that the confident, orderly, external world Victorian realism had taken for granted might be far less stable, and far less knowable from the outside, than anyone had assumed — and, as you'll see below, they fed directly into how modernist writers chose to represent human consciousness on the page.

This period also saw real, hard-won social change. The **Women's Social and Political Union**, founded in 1903 by Emmeline Pankhurst, led an increasingly militant campaign for women's suffrage — including the death of the activist Emily Davison, who stepped in front of the King's horse at the 1913 Derby. The Representation of the People Act (1918) finally gave the vote to women over thirty who met a property qualification, with full equal voting rights following only in 1928. These campaigns, and the war itself, changed British society's sense of women's proper role well beyond politics — a change you'll see reflected directly in the fiction of Chapter 4.

## Literary Movements

**Modernism** is the name literary critics later gave to the loose, often fiercely disagreeing group of writers who, across roughly the 1910s to the 1930s, deliberately broke with the storytelling and verse conventions you've studied throughout Part VII. Like every period label in this book, it's worth treating cautiously: no single group of writers gathered under a banner reading "Modernism," and the term itself only became standard among critics well after the fact. What genuinely unites these writers is less a shared belief than a shared sense that Victorian literary form — confident omniscient narrators explaining a character's motives clearly, regular meter and rhyme, a story told start to finish in clear chronological order — could no longer honestly capture a world reshaped by war, a newly uncertain science, and a newly uncertain human mind.

The clearest technical expression of this belief is **stream of consciousness**, a term the American psychologist William James coined in 1890 to describe the mind's continuous, associative flow of thought, later adopted by literary critics to describe a narrative technique that tries to render a character's inner thoughts and perceptions directly on the page, without the tidy, grammatically ordered summary a conventional Victorian narrator would have provided. Where a Dickens narrator (Part VII, Chapter 7) reliably tells you what a character thinks and why, a stream-of-consciousness narrative lets you experience the raw, unfiltered, sometimes fragmented movement of thought itself — you'll study this technique fully through Joyce and Woolf in Chapter 4. Modernist poets pursued a parallel break with **free verse** — poetry that abandons a fixed, regular metrical pattern and rhyme scheme in favor of line breaks and rhythms shaped by the sense and feeling of the content itself, rather than a predetermined pattern. You already met an important precursor to this shift in Gerard Manley Hopkins's "sprung rhythm" (Part VII, Chapter 6) and in Hardy's late, formally traditional but startlingly modern poetry (Part VII, Chapter 10); modernist poets took the underlying impulse — that form should serve feeling rather than the reverse — much further, often abandoning regular meter altogether.

A closely related early movement, **Imagism**, led chiefly by the poet Ezra Pound from around 1912, insisted that poetry should present a single, sharply observed image with extreme economy of language, stripped of ornamental description or explicit commentary — you'll meet Pound and this movement fully in Chapter 2. Modernist writing also tends to prize **fragmentation** and difficulty as positive literary values in their own right: rather than explaining an idea clearly for a broad audience, as the Victorian social-problem novel (Part VII, Chapter 7) deliberately did, modernist texts often juxtapose disconnected images, voices, or time periods and expect the reader to do real interpretive work to assemble their meaning. This preference for difficulty over broad accessibility connects to a real shift in how modernist writing actually reached readers: rather than the mass-circulation serialized fiction and popular periodicals that carried Dickens and Gaskell to a wide public, much modernist writing first appeared in small **avant-garde** ("advanced guard") **little magazines** — low-circulation periodicals aimed at a narrow, specialized readership of fellow writers and critics rather than the broad middle-class audience Victorian serialization had cultivated.

## Author Profiles

**Sigmund Freud** (1856–1939), while not a literary writer himself, produced in his theory of the unconscious one of the single most literarily consequential ideas of the whole period. Trained as a neurologist in Vienna, Freud argued that much of human motivation, memory, and desire operates below conscious awareness, surfacing only indirectly — in dreams, slips of the tongue, or neurotic symptoms — and that a person's own account of their motives is therefore never fully reliable or complete. This idea, gradually absorbed into English intellectual and literary culture over the following decades, gave modernist writers a powerful justification for depicting characters whose inner lives are murkier, more contradictory, and less available to their own understanding than any Victorian narrator had generally allowed.

**Virginia Woolf** (1882–1941) receives her full treatment as a novelist in Chapter 4, alongside Joseph Conrad and James Joyce; here, know her specifically as the critic who put modernism's sense of a decisive break into its single most quoted sentence. In her 1924 essay "Mr. Bennett and Mrs. Brown," Woolf declared that "on or about December 1910, human character changed," arguing that the solidly detailed, externally observed fiction of immediately preceding novelists like Arnold Bennett could no longer capture how people actually experienced their own inner lives. Whether or not any single month in 1910 truly marks such a change, Woolf's essay remains modernism's clearest first-hand explanation of why its writers felt Victorian and Edwardian fictional form had become inadequate to their own historical moment.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Woolf, "Mr. Bennett and Mrs. Brown" (1924).** Read this essay as modernism's own manifesto for the break this chapter describes: Woolf argues directly that a new kind of fiction, attentive to the fleeting, fragmentary, inward texture of a single mind, is needed to replace novelists who, in her view, described a character's house, income, and social position in exhaustive detail while leaving the actual person inside all that detail curiously absent.
- **Pound, "In a Station of the Metro" (1913).** This extremely short Imagist poem compresses a crowd of faces glimpsed in a Paris underground station into a single, tightly juxtaposed image, offered without explanatory narrative or comment — a deliberate demonstration of Imagism's central principle that a single precise image, stripped of ornament, can carry more than pages of conventional description.
- **Eliot, *The Waste Land* (1922) and Joyce, *Ulysses* (1922), previewed.** Both landmark works appeared in the same year, and both will receive full treatment later in this Part (Chapter 2 and Chapter 4 respectively); know for now that their fragmented structures, mixture of voices and historical periods, and demanding, allusive difficulty made 1922 the year many literary historians treat as modernism's decisive arrival in English-language literature.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Did the First World War cause literary modernism, or only accelerate it?** The war's disillusioning effect on Victorian confidence is real and well documented, but Imagism, Freud's theory of the unconscious, and Einstein's early relativity papers all predate 1914 — suggesting the war intensified and gave urgent new subject matter to a formal break that was already gathering momentum, rather than creating that break from nothing.
- **Is "Modernism" a coherent movement, or a convenient label applied after the fact?** As with "Romanticism" (Part VI, Chapter 1) and "Victorian" (Part VII, Chapter 1), the writers this Part studies frequently disagreed sharply with one another in style and belief; treating them as a single unified "movement" risks flattening real differences for the sake of a tidy label, even though the term remains a genuinely useful shorthand for a real shared sense of formal crisis.
- **Was modernism's difficulty a genuine artistic necessity, or a form of elitism?** Modernist writers frequently argued that only fragmented, demanding form could honestly represent a genuinely fragmented modern experience. Critics have long countered that this same difficulty, and modernism's preference for small avant-garde little magazines over mass readership, effectively excluded the broad audience the Victorian social-problem novel (Part VII, Chapter 7) had deliberately tried to reach and educate.
- **How much did the war's disillusionment differ between poets who fought and those who did not?** This chapter previews a contrast Chapter 3 will develop fully: early war poetry (like Rupert Brooke's) could still frame the war in noble, patriotic terms, while poets who survived prolonged front-line combat (like Wilfred Owen and Siegfried Sassoon) produced a far darker, more disillusioned response — proof that "the war's effect on literature" was never a single, uniform experience.

## Chapter Summary

The Modern Period opens not with a single dated event but with a sequence of overlapping shocks to Victorian confidence: the unprecedented, industrialized slaughter of the First World War; Freud's argument that an unconscious mind governs much of human behavior beneath rational awareness; Einstein's dismantling of absolute time and space; and Bergson's distinction between mechanical clock time and the flowing, subjective experience of duration. Together, these developments convinced a loose, often disagreeing group of writers, later labeled "Modernist" by critics, that Victorian literary form — confident narration, clear chronology, regular verse — could no longer honestly represent a genuinely destabilized modern world. Techniques like stream of consciousness and free verse, and movements like Imagism, offered new formal tools for that task, while small avant-garde little magazines replaced mass serialization as modernism's characteristic mode of reaching an audience. Virginia Woolf's 1924 essay "Mr. Bennett and Mrs. Brown" remains the era's clearest first-hand statement of why this break felt necessary. Chapter 2 now turns to modernist poetry proper, through Yeats, Eliot, Pound, and the wider current of Anglo-American Modernism.

## Glossary

- **Modernism** — the retrospective critical label for the loose, often disagreeing group of early twentieth-century writers who broke with Victorian literary form in response to war, new science, and new ideas about the mind.
- **Stream of consciousness** — a narrative technique, adapting a term coined by the psychologist William James, that renders a character's continuous, associative flow of thought directly, without a conventional narrator's tidy summary.
- **Free verse** — poetry without a fixed, regular metrical pattern or rhyme scheme, shaped instead by the sense and feeling of its content.
- **Imagism** — an early modernist poetic movement, led chiefly by Ezra Pound, insisting on a single, sharply observed image presented with extreme economy of language.
- **Avant-garde** — French for "advanced guard"; art or writing that deliberately breaks new ground, typically for a narrow, specialized audience rather than a broad public.
- **Little magazine** — a small-circulation avant-garde periodical publishing experimental modernist writing, in contrast to the mass-circulation serialization of Part VII, Chapter 7.
