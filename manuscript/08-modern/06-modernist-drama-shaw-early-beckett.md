## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Part VII, Chapter 13 closed its survey of Victorian drama with a promise: that the serious literary theatre Oscar Wilde's society comedies had only begun to revive would find its fuller flowering in George Bernard Shaw. This chapter delivers on that promise. Shaw, an Irish-born socialist who moved to London in 1876, found his central influence not in Wilde's epigrammatic wit but in the Norwegian playwright Henrik Ibsen, whose "problem plays" tackled serious contemporary social issues directly rather than through melodrama's comfortable moral clarity (Part VII, Chapter 13). Shaw's 1891 study *The Quintessence of Ibsenism* championed exactly this example, and his own plays became a vehicle for the democratic socialist politics he championed as a leading member of the **Fabian Society**, an organization advocating gradual, reasoned social reform rather than revolutionary upheaval.

Shaw's career also demonstrates that the Lord Chamberlain's censorship powers, introduced in Part VII, Chapter 13, remained a genuine obstacle well into the twentieth century, not merely a Victorian relic. As you'll see below, his early play *Mrs Warren's Profession* was denied a public performance license for over three decades. Meanwhile, a younger Irish writer, Samuel Beckett, was forming the artistic sensibility that would eventually produce an entirely different kind of modern drama. Beckett's formative years, discussed at the end of this chapter, unfolded directly alongside James Joyce (Chapter 4) in Paris and through the genuine danger of Nazi-occupied France during the Second World War — but his own defining theatrical achievement, *Waiting for Godot*, belongs chronologically to the period covered fully in Part IX, Chapter 3, and this chapter accordingly previews rather than analyzes it.

## Literary Movements

Shaw's central contribution to English drama is best described as the **drama of ideas**: theatre built not around plot suspense or emotional catharsis, but around a genuine, structured argument about a real social or moral question, dramatized through characters who each embody a different position in that argument. This approach owes an explicit debt to Ibsen, and Shaw made the argumentative dimension of his plays almost as important as their performance by attaching long, discursive prefaces to their published editions — sometimes considerably longer than the plays themselves — in which he laid out his social and political reasoning directly to the reader.

Shaw also developed his own philosophical framework, which he called the **Life Force**: an evolutionary, creative energy driving living things toward ever-greater self-awareness and purpose, developed most fully in *Man and Superman* (1903) and drawing on the same broader intellectual current as Bergson's concept of duration, introduced in Chapter 1 — both thinkers, working independently, imagined life as a dynamic, forward-moving process resistant to static, mechanical explanation.

Samuel Beckett's early work, previewed at this chapter's end, belongs to a genuinely different current that would only fully emerge after the period this Part covers: what critics later named the **Theatre of the Absurd**, in which drama abandons clear argument, resolution, or even conventional plot altogether to dramatize existence's fundamental meaninglessness directly. You'll study this movement fully in Part IX, Chapter 3; for now, know that Beckett's formative apprenticeship under Joyce's radical linguistic experimentation, and his own wartime experience of danger and uncertainty, both fed directly into the starkly different, minimalist dramatic voice he would develop after 1945.

## Author Profiles

**George Bernard Shaw** (1856–1950) moved from Dublin to London as a young man, working first as a critic — of music, then of drama — before turning to playwriting himself in the 1890s, already a committed socialist and prominent Fabian Society lecturer and pamphleteer. His plays, discussed fully below, made him one of the most commercially successful and publicly recognized dramatists of the early twentieth century, and he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1925. Shaw is often noted, remarkably, as one of very few people to have won both a Nobel Prize and an Academy Award, the latter for his own screenplay adaptation of *Pygmalion* for the 1938 film version. He continued writing until his death in 1950, at ninety-four.

**Samuel Beckett** (1906–1989) was born in Foxrock, near Dublin, and educated at Trinity College Dublin before moving to Paris in 1928, where he formed a close working relationship with James Joyce (Chapter 4), assisting with research for what eventually became *Finnegans Wake*. Beckett's own early publications from this period — the poem *Whoroscope* (1930), the critical study *Proust* (1931), the story collection *More Pricks Than Kicks* (1934), and his first novel, *Murphy* (1938) — show real traces of Joyce's dense, allusive influence, even though Beckett's mature style would eventually move in almost the opposite direction, toward radical linguistic and dramatic minimalism. When Germany occupied France, Beckett joined a Paris Resistance cell as a courier and translator; after the cell was betrayed in 1942, he fled to unoccupied Vichy France, spending the rest of the war in hiding in the village of Roussillon, working as an agricultural laborer — an experience of danger, uncertainty, and survival that would echo, transformed, through his postwar theatrical work. That mature work, including *Waiting for Godot*, belongs to the period covered fully in Part IX, Chapter 3.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Shaw, *Mrs Warren's Profession* (written 1893).** Presenting a woman who built her fortune managing brothels across Europe not as a moral scandal but as a rational response to the limited economic options available to poor women, the play's frank treatment of prostitution as an economic rather than purely moral issue led the Lord Chamberlain to deny it a public performance license; it was performed privately in 1902 to sidestep this restriction and not licensed for public British performance until 1925, a censorship saga lasting well over three decades.
- **Shaw, *Pygmalion* (1912–1913).** Following the phonetics professor Henry Higgins's bet that he can transform Eliza Doolittle, a Cockney flower-seller, into someone who can pass for a duchess simply by changing her speech, the play uses this social experiment to probe class, language, and identity as constructed rather than innate. Shaw deliberately resisted a conventional romantic resolution between Higgins and Eliza, a choice later popular adaptations, including the musical *My Fair Lady*, have often reversed — a genuine point of ongoing critical and adaptive disagreement about what Shaw's ending actually means.
- **Shaw, *Saint Joan* (1923).** Widely considered Shaw's greatest achievement, this historical tragedy dramatizes Joan of Arc's trial and execution with genuine sympathy for both Joan's visionary conviction and the political and religious authorities who felt compelled, by their own institutional logic, to condemn her — refusing to reduce either side to simple villainy.
- **Beckett, *Murphy* (1938), previewed.** Beckett's first novel, following an alienated, passive protagonist who withdraws increasingly from the world around him, already shows the philosophical preoccupation with inaction, meaninglessness, and the limits of language that would eventually define his mature theatrical work — though its dense, allusive prose style, still closer to Joyce's influence, differs sharply from the spare minimalism of his later drama.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does Shaw's didacticism strengthen or weaken his plays as theatre?** Shaw's plays argue their social and political points with real clarity and force, but some critics have long objected that his characters can feel more like mouthpieces for a position than fully independent people — a genuine tension between drama as persuasive argument and drama as human portrait that Shaw himself never fully resolved.
- **What does *Pygmalion*'s ending actually mean?** Shaw's own stage directions and prefatory notes insist Eliza does not marry Higgins, yet audiences and later adaptations have repeatedly imposed a romantic conclusion the text itself resists — a striking case of a popular audience's expectations overriding an author's explicit intentions.
- **What does *Mrs Warren's Profession*'s censorship history reveal about theatrical control in the twentieth century?** The play's three-decade wait for a public license shows that the Lord Chamberlain's power, introduced as a Victorian institution in Part VII, Chapter 13, remained a genuinely active constraint on serious drama well into the 1920s, not a fading formality — a useful corrective to any assumption that theatrical censorship simply disappeared with the Victorian age.
- **How should Beckett's apprenticeship under Joyce be read, given how differently his mature work turned out?** Beckett's early fiction's dense allusiveness suggests he initially worked very much in Joyce's shadow; his later, radical turn toward silence, repetition, and minimalism can be read either as an eventual escape from that influence or as its own kind of response to the same modernist crisis of meaning Joyce's generation confronted, pursued to an even more austere conclusion.

## Chapter Summary

George Bernard Shaw fulfilled the revival of serious English drama Part VII, Chapter 13, had only begun to trace through Oscar Wilde, building on Ibsen's example to create a genuine "drama of ideas" argued through witty, structured dialogue and extensive published prefaces. *Mrs Warren's Profession*'s three-decade censorship saga shows the Lord Chamberlain's licensing power remained a real constraint well into the twentieth century, while *Pygmalion* and *Saint Joan* stand as his most enduring achievements, the former still debated for what its resisted romantic ending actually means. Meanwhile, a younger Samuel Beckett formed his own artistic sensibility in Paris under James Joyce's direct influence, before the Second World War tested him personally through Resistance work and hiding in Vichy France — formative experiences that would feed into a theatrical revolution belonging to a later period of this book's story. Chapter 7 now turns to the London literary and intellectual circle at the center of so much of this Part's fiction and criticism: the Bloomsbury Group.

## Glossary

- **Fabian Society** — a British socialist organization, founded in 1884, advocating gradual, democratic social reform; George Bernard Shaw was a leading member.
- **Drama of ideas** — Shaw's characteristic dramatic mode, built around structured argument about a genuine social or moral question rather than plot suspense or emotional catharsis.
- **Life Force** — Shaw's philosophy of an evolutionary, creative energy driving living things toward greater self-awareness, developed in *Man and Superman* (1903).
- **Theatre of the Absurd** — a later dramatic movement, previewed here and studied fully in Part IX, Chapter 3, dramatizing existence's fundamental meaninglessness through fragmented plot and language.
