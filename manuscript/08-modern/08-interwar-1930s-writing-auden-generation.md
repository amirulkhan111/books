## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The poets you'll meet in this chapter — W. H. Auden, Stephen Spender, Louis MacNeice, and Cecil Day-Lewis — came of age not amid the First World War's direct trauma, which shaped Chapter 3's soldier-poets, but amid its economic and political aftermath: the Great Depression following the 1929 Wall Street Crash, which produced mass unemployment across industrial Britain, and the rise of fascism in Italy and Germany, which by the mid-1930s had become an urgent, immediate danger rather than a distant political abstraction. All four poets were educated at Oxford in overlapping years, and their shared generation, background, and broadly left-wing political sympathy led contemporary critics to treat them, not always fairly, as a single unified group — sometimes nicknamed **"MacSpaunday,"** a mocking portmanteau of their four surnames coined by the rival poet Roy Campbell, who strongly disagreed with their politics.

Nothing defined this generation's political engagement more completely than the **Spanish Civil War** (1936–1939), in which Spain's democratically elected Republican government fought a losing battle against General Franco's fascist-backed Nationalist forces. The war drew volunteers from across Europe, including British writers who joined the International Brigades to fight for the Republican cause, and it became, for Auden's generation, the single clearest moral test of their generation's political convictions — a test some of them approached from the relative safety of literary reportage, and others, like the young poet John Cornford, paid for with their lives. Where Bloomsbury's leading men had responded to the First World War with principled pacifism (Part VIII, Chapter 7), this younger generation largely responded to fascism's rise with direct, urgent political commitment — even as, you'll see below, some of that commitment proved more durable than the rest.

## Literary Movements

Where Chapter 2's modernist poets pursued formal experimentation largely for its own sake — Pound's Imagism, Eliot's impersonality, Yeats's private mythology — this younger generation turned poetry more directly toward explicit political argument and reportage, treating a poem as a legitimate space for taking a public political stand rather than pursuing art independent of immediate social crisis. This shift shows up concretely in what critics sometimes call **"Pylon poetry,"** after Stephen Spender's 1933 poem "The Pylons," which treats electricity pylons striding across the English countryside as genuinely poetic subject matter — a deliberate embrace of modern industrial imagery that earlier poetic convention, even much of modernism itself, had generally avoided in favor of more traditionally "poetic" subjects.

This generation's political commitment reached its most intense expression in poetry responding directly to the Spanish Civil War, discussed above, and to the deepening European crisis of the later 1930s. Louis MacNeice's book-length "Autumn Journal" (1939), written amid the Munich Crisis's anxious uncertainty, exemplifies a genuinely different note within the same generation: rather than confident ideological commitment, MacNeice's poem moves through personal memory, ordinary daily observation, and open political doubt, refusing the firmer Marxist commitments some of his contemporaries, particularly Cecil Day-Lewis, embraced more directly during the same period.

## Author Profiles

**W. H. Auden** (1907–1973) emerged from Oxford as the most technically brilliant and, in time, most influential voice of this generation, absorbing early influence from Eliot (Chapter 2) and from Freud's psychological ideas (Chapter 1) before developing his own distinctive voice across the 1930s. His 1937 visit to Spain and the resulting poem "Spain," discussed fully below, made him this generation's most prominent political poet, but Auden later grew deeply uncomfortable with his own 1930s political rhetoric, revising and eventually excluding several poems, including "Spain," from his own collected works. In January 1939, Auden emigrated to the United States alongside his close friend and collaborator Christopher Isherwood — a decision some contemporaries criticized as an abandonment of Britain at a moment of national crisis, just months before the Second World War began.

**Stephen Spender** (1909–1995), another Oxford contemporary and close friend of Auden, became closely associated with "Pylon poetry" through "The Pylons" (1933), and, like Auden, traveled to Spain during its civil war, writing directly out of that experience in both poetry and prose.

**Louis MacNeice** (1907–1963), born in Belfast, brings this book's recurring Irish literary thread (Wilde, Yeats, Joyce, Shaw, and Beckett among the writers who share it) into this chapter's generation, though his own poetic temperament proved notably more skeptical and less doctrinaire than his English contemporaries'. "Autumn Journal" (1939), discussed above, remains his most significant achievement from this period, valued precisely for its refusal of the firmer political certainty found elsewhere in the group.

**Cecil Day-Lewis** (1904–1972), the eldest of the four poets, held the most explicitly, doctrinally left-wing politics of the group during the 1930s, briefly joining the Communist Party of Great Britain and writing poetry more directly aligned with orthodox Marxist positions than Auden's, Spender's, or MacNeice's more independent political voices.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Auden, "Spain" (1937).** Written after Auden's own visit to Spain during its civil war, the poem frames the conflict as history's urgent moral demand on the present moment, memorably concluding that history offers the defeated no pardon, only sympathy. A controversial passage referring to "the necessary murder" drew sharp criticism from George Orwell in his 1940 essay "Inside the Whale," which accused Auden of the kind of detached, abstract willingness to accept violence available only to an intellectual who would never have to commit it himself; Auden, evidently persuaded by similar doubts of his own, later revised and ultimately excluded the poem from his collected works.
- **Auden, "September 1, 1939."** Written immediately after the Second World War's outbreak, the poem searches for meaning and human solidarity amid the crisis, closing on a much-quoted call to love one another or perish — a line Auden himself later found too easy and rhetorically false, revising it in later printings and eventually refusing to allow the poem's continued republication in some collections, a striking case of a poet publicly disowning his own famous work.
- **Spender, "The Pylons" (1933).** Treating the stark, modern silhouette of electricity pylons crossing the English countryside as genuinely worthy poetic subject matter, the poem gave this generation's embrace of industrial and technological imagery its lasting critical nickname, "Pylon poetry."
- **MacNeice, "Autumn Journal" (1939).** Written across the autumn of 1938, amid the Munich Crisis's anxious uncertainty, this book-length poem in diary form mixes personal memory, ordinary daily life, and open political doubt, offering a genuinely more skeptical, ambivalent alternative to the firmer ideological commitment found in much of his contemporaries' work from the same period.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Auden's later repudiation of his own political poetry honest self-correction, or evasion?** Auden came to see poems like "Spain" and "September 1, 1939" as rhetorically persuasive in ways he no longer trusted, and revised or excluded them accordingly; some critics admire this as genuine intellectual honesty about his own earlier overconfidence, while others see it as a later, more comfortable Auden disowning the political risks his younger self had actually taken.
- **Was Orwell's critique of "Spain" fair?** Orwell's objection to "the necessary murder" targeted what he saw as an intellectual's abstract, bloodless language for real violence he would likely never personally commit; some critics find this a devastatingly accurate diagnosis of a real weakness in 1930s political poetry, while others argue Orwell judged a single compressed phrase too harshly, out of context from the poem's larger, genuinely serious moral argument.
- **Was Auden's 1939 emigration to America a betrayal or a legitimate choice?** Leaving Britain just before war broke out drew real contemporary criticism, especially given Auden's prior reputation as a politically committed public poet; but Auden himself argued that a poet's obligations were not identical to a citizen-soldier's, a distinction worth weighing directly against the very different choices made by the war poets of Chapter 3 and the conscientious objectors of Chapter 7.
- **Does "MacSpaunday" flatten real differences between these four poets?** Grouping Auden, Spender, MacNeice, and Day-Lewis under a single mocking nickname obscures real distinctions in temperament and conviction — MacNeice's habitual skepticism sits uneasily beside Day-Lewis's more doctrinaire Communism, suggesting the label tells us more about how contemporaries perceived a generation than about any genuine uniformity among these four actual poets.

## Chapter Summary

The Auden generation — Auden, Spender, MacNeice, and Day-Lewis — turned 1930s English poetry decisively toward explicit political engagement, shaped by the Great Depression, the rise of fascism, and above all the Spanish Civil War, which became this generation's defining moral test. Where Chapter 2's modernists pursued formal experimentation largely independent of immediate political crisis, this generation, sometimes mockingly grouped together as "MacSpaunday," embraced industrial imagery through "Pylon poetry" and treated poetry itself as a legitimate vehicle for urgent political argument. Auden's "Spain" and its controversial reception by George Orwell, and Auden's own later discomfort with his most famous political poems, show a generation's confident commitments proving harder to sustain than they first appeared; MacNeice's more skeptical "Autumn Journal" suggests the group's political unity was always more apparent than real. With Britain's entry into the Second World War in September 1939, the historical crisis this entire generation had anticipated finally arrived in full — closing this Part's survey of the Modern Period, which began in Chapter 1 with an earlier war's shattering of Victorian confidence, and ending with a second, even greater war on the horizon. Part IX now turns to the postwar and contemporary literature that followed.

## Glossary

- **"MacSpaunday"** — a mocking portmanteau nickname for Auden, Spender, MacNeice, and Day-Lewis, coined by the rival poet Roy Campbell, treating them as a single homogeneous political poetic generation.
- **Pylon poetry** — 1930s poetry, exemplified by Spender's "The Pylons," embracing modern industrial imagery as legitimate poetic subject matter.
- **Spanish Civil War** — the 1936–1939 conflict between Spain's Republican government and Franco's fascist-backed Nationalist forces, which became this poetic generation's defining political cause.
