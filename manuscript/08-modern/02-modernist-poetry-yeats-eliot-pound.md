## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Modernist poetry in English was never a purely English achievement. As Chapter 1 previewed, much of it took shape among American-born writers who crossed the Atlantic to find each other in London and Paris, alongside Irish and English poets working through their own national crises — which is exactly what the label **Anglo-American Modernism** is meant to capture: a shared literary movement built across, not within, a single national tradition. T. S. Eliot and Ezra Pound were both born in the United States and did much of their defining work as expatriates in London; W. B. Yeats, meanwhile, wrote from inside a very different national crisis altogether — Ireland's own violent path toward independence from British rule.

That Irish context matters enormously for Yeats specifically. The **Easter Rising** of April 1916, an armed uprising against British rule in Dublin, ended in military failure but produced a wave of British executions of its leaders that transformed Irish public opinion and hardened support for full independence — a transformation Yeats, who knew several of the executed leaders personally, wrestled with directly in his poetry. More broadly, all three poets in this chapter wrote through the same First World War and its aftermath that Chapter 1 described: a conflict that, alongside the Russian Revolution of 1917 and the general collapse of several European empires, left many writers of the early 1920s genuinely uncertain whether European civilization itself could hold together — a mood you'll see crystallized directly in this chapter's poetry.

## Literary Movements

Chapter 1 introduced **Imagism**, and this chapter's poets show exactly how central Ezra Pound was to organizing it: Pound didn't just write Imagist poems, he actively recruited, edited, and promoted the writers around him, arranging the young Eliot's first major publication and pushing Yeats — whom he served as personal secretary during several winters in the 1910s — away from his earlier, softer Celtic Revival style toward harder, more concrete modern language. Pound then pushed his own aesthetic further still into **Vorticism**, a short-lived, deliberately aggressive movement (developed with the painter Wyndham Lewis around 1914) that celebrated the dynamic, machine-age energy of modern urban life rather than Imagism's quieter, more contemplative single image.

Eliot's most influential contribution to modernist literary theory came not through a poem but through criticism: his 1919 essay "Tradition and the Individual Talent" argued for **poetic impersonality** — the idea that a poem's power comes not from a poet directly expressing their own personal feelings, but from the poet's mind acting as a kind of catalyst that fuses tradition, language, and experience into something that exists independently of the poet's own biography. This theory also insisted that a genuinely skilled poet needs a strong **historical sense**: an awareness of the entire literary tradition as something existing simultaneously, all at once, rather than a simple chronological sequence a new poet either follows or breaks from — a demanding, densely allusive ideal you'll see put directly into practice in *The Waste Land*, discussed below.

Yeats, working independently of both Pound's Imagism and Eliot's impersonality theory, developed his own strikingly personal mythology of history, most fully worked out in his prose treatise *A Vision* (1925, revised 1937). Yeats proposed that history moves through repeating cycles he visualized as interlocking **gyres** — cone-shaped spirals of expanding and contracting historical energy — with each roughly two-thousand-year cycle giving way violently to its opposite. This private mythological system, however eccentric it might sound in isolation, gave Yeats a genuinely powerful symbolic vocabulary for describing historical collapse and renewal, deployed directly in "The Second Coming," discussed below.

## Author Profiles

**William Butler Yeats** (1865–1939) began his career deeply immersed in the **Celtic Revival**, an Irish cultural movement recovering and celebrating native Irish mythology, folklore, and legend as a foundation for a distinct national literature; his early poetry, dreamy and melancholic, drew heavily on the landscape of County Sligo and Irish fairy lore. He co-founded Dublin's Abbey Theatre in 1904 to give this national literary revival a stage of its own, and pursued a long, largely unrequited love for the Irish revolutionary and actress Maud Gonne, who became a recurring figure across his poetry. Under Pound's influence and his own response to Ireland's revolutionary politics, Yeats's later style grew harder, more direct, and more publicly engaged, producing the poems for which he remains best known, including "Easter, 1916," "The Second Coming" (1919), and "Sailing to Byzantium" (1928). He served as a senator in the newly independent Irish Free State from 1922 to 1928 and won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923, the first Irish writer to do so. He died in France in January 1939.

**Thomas Stearns Eliot** (1888–1965) was born in St. Louis, Missouri, educated at Harvard and briefly at the Sorbonne and Oxford, and settled permanently in England, becoming a British subject and converting to the Anglo-Catholic wing of the Church of England in 1927 — describing himself, in his own later phrase, as conservative in literature, politics, and religion alike. Pound recognized Eliot's talent early and arranged the 1915 publication of "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock," now widely considered the first major modernist poem in English. Eliot's reputation was transformed by *The Waste Land* (1922), discussed fully below, and he went on to write "The Hollow Men" (1925) and, later in his career, the more explicitly religious and philosophical *Four Quartets* (published complete in 1943), a meditative sequence on time, memory, and spiritual renewal written substantially during the Second World War. Alongside his poetry, Eliot edited the influential literary journal *The Criterion* and worked as an editor at the publishing house Faber & Faber, giving him enormous influence over which other modernist writers found an audience.

**Ezra Pound** (1885–1972) did more than perhaps any other single figure to organize Anglo-American Modernism into an actual literary community: moving to London in 1908, he founded and led Imagism, then Vorticism, discovered and promoted Eliot, and served as Yeats's secretary during several formative winters. His most ambitious project, *The Cantos*, begun around 1915 and continued for the rest of his life, is an enormous, deliberately difficult long poem weaving together history, economics, classical and Chinese literature, and Confucian philosophy across more than a hundred individual cantos, left formally unfinished at his death. Pound's biography, though, cannot be told honestly without confronting its darkest turn: settling in Italy during the 1920s, he became an enthusiastic supporter of Mussolini's fascism, and during the Second World War broadcast propaganda on Italian state radio that combined attacks on the American government with explicit antisemitic conspiracy theories. Arrested by American forces in 1945 and indicted for treason, Pound was ultimately judged mentally unfit to stand trial and confined to a psychiatric hospital in Washington, D.C. for over a decade — a genuinely disturbing outcome that responsible criticism has to hold together with his undeniable, foundational influence on the poetry this entire chapter studies, rather than choosing to remember only one half of that record.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Yeats, "Easter, 1916."** Written in direct response to the Easter Rising and its executions, the poem repeats the refrain that a "terrible beauty is born," capturing Yeats's genuinely conflicted response to ordinary, previously unremarkable people transformed by violent political sacrifice into permanent, almost mythic national symbols — admiration and unease held together without resolving into either.
- **Yeats, "The Second Coming" (1919).** Written amid the disorienting aftermath of the First World War and the Russian Revolution, the poem opens with an image of a falcon spinning outward in ever-widening circles until it can no longer hear the falconer who released it — a directly visualized version of Yeats's gyre theory of history spinning out of control. Its declaration that "Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold" has become one of the single most quoted lines in modern English poetry, a vivid shorthand for civilizational collapse invoked far beyond readers who know the poem's own elaborate mythology.
- **Eliot, "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock" (1915).** An extended interior monologue given to an anxious, chronically indecisive man rehearsing and endlessly second-guessing a romantic proposal he seems unable to make, the poem's jarringly unromantic opening image — comparing the evening sky to a patient under anesthesia on an operating table — announced modernist poetry's decisive break from conventional lyrical beauty right from its very first lines.
- **Eliot, *The Waste Land* (1922).** Already previewed in Chapter 1, the poem deserves full attention here as modernism's single most influential long poem: across five fragmented sections, it juxtaposes multiple voices, several languages, and allusions ranging from Shakespeare and Dante to Buddhist scripture, Arthurian Grail legend, and popular song, without ever settling into one stable narrating voice or a single clear meaning. Pound's heavy editorial cutting — reducing Eliot's draft by roughly half — was significant enough that Eliot dedicated the finished poem to Pound as "il miglior fabbro," Dante's own phrase for "the better craftsman," publicly crediting Pound's editorial hand in shaping the poem's final, deliberately difficult form.
- **Pound, *The Cantos* (begun c. 1915).** Rather than a single unified narrative, *The Cantos* accumulates historical fragments, economic theory, translated classical and Chinese poetry, and Pound's own increasingly idiosyncratic political convictions into an open-ended, lifelong project — the most extreme demonstration, among this chapter's three poets, of modernist difficulty and fragmentation pursued almost without compromise toward accessibility.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Can Pound's poetry be separated from his fascism and antisemitism?** This is among the most difficult questions in modernist criticism: Pound's technical innovations shaped Eliot, Yeats, and countless later poets, yet his own late *Cantos* directly incorporate antisemitic material inseparable from the propaganda he broadcast during the war. Most serious critics now insist on holding both facts together, refusing either to cancel his literary importance entirely or to excuse the political content on aesthetic grounds.
- **Is Eliot's "impersonality" theory itself a personal literary strategy?** Eliot argued that great poetry escapes the poet's own personality, yet critics have long noted that *The Waste Land*'s fragmented despair closely tracks Eliot's own severe personal crises around 1921 (including a nervous breakdown and a troubled first marriage) — suggesting that "impersonality" may itself function as a deliberate mask over intensely personal material, rather than its genuine absence.
- **Is Yeats's mythological system in *A Vision* serious philosophy or private eccentricity?** Some critics have treated Yeats's gyres and historical cycles as an embarrassing occult indulgence best set aside in favor of the poems themselves; others argue the system, however strange it seems from outside, gave Yeats a genuinely powerful and coherent symbolic vocabulary that the mature poetry could not have achieved without it.
- **Was Anglo-American Modernism genuinely international, or still centered on London?** Eliot and Pound were American by birth, but both did their major defining work while based in London, and much of their shared literary community and its "little magazines" (Chapter 1) remained concentrated there — a reminder that "transatlantic" collaboration in this period still often ran through a single geographic hub rather than a truly decentralized network.

## Chapter Summary

Modernist poetry in English took shape as a genuinely transatlantic project: Ezra Pound, an American expatriate in London, organized Imagism and Vorticism, discovered and championed T. S. Eliot, and pushed the Irish poet W. B. Yeats toward a harder, more modern style. Yeats's own poetry, shaped directly by Ireland's violent path to independence, developed an elaborate personal mythology of historical cycles, most powerfully expressed in "The Second Coming." Eliot's theory of poetic impersonality and his fragmented, densely allusive masterwork *The Waste Land* — heavily edited by Pound himself — gave modernism its most influential long poem. And Pound's own lifelong *Cantos* pursued modernist difficulty to its furthest extreme, even as his later fascism and antisemitic propaganda broadcasts, and his 1945 arrest for treason, left a genuinely dark stain that criticism cannot responsibly separate from his enormous influence on the poets around him. Chapter 3 now turns to poetry written directly out of the First World War's front-line experience: the Georgian and war poets Brooke, Owen, Sassoon, and Rosenberg.

## Glossary

- **Anglo-American Modernism** — the transatlantic literary movement uniting American-born and British or Irish poets, chiefly based in London, who developed modernist poetic technique together.
- **Vorticism** — a short-lived, aggressive modernist movement, led by Ezra Pound and Wyndham Lewis around 1914, celebrating the dynamic energy of modern machine-age urban life.
- **Poetic impersonality** — T. S. Eliot's theory that a poem's power comes from the poet's mind fusing tradition and experience into something independent of the poet's own personality, rather than from direct personal expression.
- **Gyre** — W. B. Yeats's term, from his mythological system in *A Vision*, for the interlocking, cone-shaped historical cycles he believed governed the rise and fall of civilizations.
