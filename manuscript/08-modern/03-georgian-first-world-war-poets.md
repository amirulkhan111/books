## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Before turning to the war itself, it's worth understanding the poetic climate it interrupted. From 1912, the editor Edward Marsh published a series of anthologies titled *Georgian Poetry* — named after the recently crowned King George V, who had succeeded Edward VII in 1910 — collecting a generation of English poets who wrote in more traditional, accessible forms than the radical experimentation of Yeats, Eliot, and Pound in Chapter 2. Georgian poetry favored clear, often gently pastoral subjects — the English countryside, rural custom, quiet domestic feeling — over modernism's fragmentation and difficulty; Rupert Brooke, discussed below, was among its best-known contributors, and the movement gives this chapter its name alongside the war poetry that followed.

Everything changed with the outbreak of the First World War in August 1914. As Chapter 1 described, the war's new industrial weapons and trench warfare produced casualties on a scale no previous conflict had approached — but it's crucial to understand that this horror wasn't obvious to everyone from the very first day. In 1914, before the full scale of trench slaughter became widely known, patriotic idealism about the war's nobility remained genuinely widespread, and Brooke's poetry, discussed below, captures that early mood exactly. By 1916 and 1917, after the catastrophic casualties of battles like the Somme, soldier-poets who had actually lived through prolonged front-line combat — Sassoon, Owen, and Rosenberg among them — began producing a far darker, more disillusioned poetry that directly challenged the patriotic rhetoric still current at home.

This shift connects directly to the era's growing understanding of psychological trauma. As Chapter 1 described, Freud's theory of an unconscious mind governed by hidden conflict was still a relatively new idea in English culture, but the war gave it urgent, immediate relevance: many soldiers who had witnessed sustained combat developed severe psychological symptoms then called **shell shock**, treated at specialist facilities like Craiglockhart War Hospital in Scotland, where the psychiatrist W. H. R. Rivers pioneered talk-based treatment for traumatized officers. As you'll see below, Craiglockhart is also where two of this chapter's central poets, Sassoon and Owen, actually met.

## Literary Movements

**Georgian poetry**, as described above, offers a useful contrast to modernism's formal break: it represents English poetry's more conservative wing in the years just before and during the war, retaining traditional meter, rhyme, and accessible subject matter even as Yeats, Eliot, and Pound pursued the radical departures of Chapter 2. It's worth being cautious here, though — much as with "Modernism" itself (Part VIII, Chapter 1), "war poetry" isn't a single coherent movement its own writers consciously joined. Brooke died in 1915, before the war's full horror had fully registered even among combatants, and never overlapped with Sassoon, Owen, or Rosenberg; Owen and Sassoon knew each other well and directly shaped one another's work at Craiglockhart, while Rosenberg, serving as an ordinary private rather than an officer, wrote in comparative isolation from the others. What genuinely unites this chapter's poets isn't a shared program, but a shared subject — direct, lived experience of the war — treated with dramatically different tones as the conflict wore on.

Wilfred Owen's poetry also introduced a specific technical innovation worth naming: **pararhyme** (or slant rhyme), in which line endings share consonant sounds but not the matching vowel sounds a full rhyme would require — "grained" and "groined," for instance, or "hall" and "hell." This slightly unsettling near-rhyme, rather than a clean, satisfying full rhyme, mirrors the poems' subject matter directly: a world in which things almost, but never quite, resolve into order or comfort.

## Author Profiles

**Rupert Brooke** (1887–1915) was among Georgian poetry's most celebrated contributors, admired for his good looks and charm as much as his verse, and became, through his early death, the war's most famous symbol of youthful, idealistic sacrifice. His sonnet sequence "1914," written in the war's first months before he had experienced sustained combat, expresses confident, patriotic faith in the nobility of dying for one's country. Brooke never reached the trenches: he died in April 1915 of blood poisoning from an infected mosquito bite, aboard a hospital ship bound for the Gallipoli campaign, and was buried on the Greek island of Skyros — a death that, however accidental, allowed his idealistic reputation to remain permanently untested against the disillusioning reality of prolonged front-line combat.

**Siegfried Sassoon** (1886–1967) served with genuine bravery, earning the Military Cross in 1916, but grew increasingly outraged by what he saw as the war's needless prolongation and the comfortable ignorance of civilians at home. In July 1917, he issued a public "Soldier's Declaration," read in the House of Commons and published in *The Times*, refusing further service and accusing Britain's leadership of deliberately extending a war it could have ended. Rather than face a politically embarrassing court-martial, the army — with the intervention of his friend, the poet Robert Graves — instead had Sassoon declared unfit for duty and sent to Craiglockhart War Hospital, where he met and mentored the young Wilfred Owen before eventually returning to active service. Sassoon's poetry from this period is characterized by bitter, direct satire, sharply contrasting the trenches' brutal reality with the complacent patriotic rhetoric of those who had never seen it.

**Wilfred Owen** (1893–1918) is now widely considered the single greatest poet of the First World War, though only about five of his poems were published in his own lifetime; Sassoon and other friends collected and published his work posthumously in 1920. Meeting Sassoon at Craiglockhart in 1917 transformed Owen's poetic technique, encouraging him toward the direct, unflinching depictions of combat's physical horror for which he is remembered. Owen was killed in action on 4 November 1918, exactly one week before the Armistice; his mother reportedly received the telegram announcing his death on Armistice Day itself, as church bells rang across England celebrating the war's end.

**Isaac Rosenberg** (1890–1918) brought a markedly different social vantage point to the same war: born to a Jewish immigrant family and raised in London's East End, he trained as a visual artist before enlisting as an ordinary private soldier rather than an officer, unlike Brooke, Sassoon, and Owen. This working-class, non-officer perspective gives his poetry a distinctive, less rhetorically polished, often strikingly original imagery, shaped partly by his visual artist's eye for concrete detail. Rosenberg was killed in action in April 1918, and his critical reputation, comparatively modest in his own lifetime, has grown substantially since.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Brooke, "The Soldier" (1915).** Imagining his own death and burial in a foreign land, the poem transforms that death into an act of national consecration, describing the patch of foreign earth where he might be buried as thereby made permanently, spiritually English — a confident, comforting vision of sacrifice utterly untouched by trench warfare's actual conditions.
- **Sassoon, "The General" and "Base Details."** These short satirical poems sharply contrast the callous, complacent incompetence of senior officers safely removed from combat with the ordinary soldiers whose deaths their decisions caused, using plain, colloquial language and bitter irony rather than elevated poetic diction to make their attack land as directly as possible.
- **Owen, "Dulce et Decorum Est."** Taking its title from a phrase by the Roman poet Horace meaning "it is sweet and fitting to die for one's country," the poem describes a horrifying gas attack in unflinching physical detail before directly confronting the reader with the claim that anyone who had witnessed such suffering could never again repeat that old Latin phrase to eager young recruits — a direct, deliberate rebuttal of the patriotic idealism Brooke's "The Soldier" represents.
- **Owen, "Strange Meeting."** Imagining a wounded soldier who encounters, in an eerie underworld, the enemy soldier he killed the day before, the poem uses Owen's characteristic pararhyme to build a hushed, uneasy atmosphere around its central revelation: the dead enemy explains that the two of them shared the same hopes and humanity, undone entirely by the war that has now made them equals in death. Owen's own preface to his planned collection distilled his poetic mission into a single memorable claim: that the poetry is in the pity.
- **Rosenberg, "Break of Day in the Trenches" (1916).** Addressing a rat that moves freely between British and German trenches, indifferent to either side's cause, the poem uses this small, darkly comic detail to meditate on the arbitrary absurdity of the war and the fragile chance governing which soldiers live and die — a distinctly unsentimental, philosophically probing angle on trench experience.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does Brooke's early death protect his reputation from a fair test?** Because Brooke died before experiencing sustained trench combat, his idealistic vision of noble sacrifice was never directly challenged by the same brutal reality Owen and Sassoon lived through — a fact that makes some critics read "The Soldier" as a poignant, if limited, product of the war's earliest and most innocent phase, and others read it as a comforting myth that later, harder-won poetry had to dismantle.
- **Was Sassoon's "Soldier's Declaration" a meaningful act of protest?** Sassoon's public refusal to continue fighting was a genuinely courageous act, but the army's decision to treat it as a medical rather than a political matter — sending him to Craiglockhart instead of court-martialling him — effectively neutralized its protest value, and Sassoon himself eventually returned to active service, complicating any simple reading of the gesture as either fully successful defiance or empty gesture.
- **How does Owen's technical innovation serve his anti-war content?** Owen's pararhyme deliberately withholds the satisfying closure a full rhyme would provide, formally enacting, at the level of sound, the same absence of resolution or comfort his subject matter describes — a case where a poet's technical choice directly reinforces, rather than merely decorates, the meaning of the poem.
- **Should Rosenberg's working-class, non-officer perspective change how "war poetry" is generally read?** Because Brooke, Sassoon, and Owen were all officers, their poetry — however different in tone — shares certain assumptions about command, responsibility, and articulate self-expression that Rosenberg's ordinary-soldier perspective does not share, suggesting that any single account of "what World War I poetry says" risks flattening real differences of class experience within the same war.

## Chapter Summary

Georgian poetry's traditional, accessible verse gave English poetry a conservative counterpart to the modernist experimentation of Chapter 2, and Rupert Brooke's confident, patriotic "The Soldier" captures the war's earliest, still-untested idealism before the full scale of trench slaughter became widely understood. As the war continued, Siegfried Sassoon's bitter satire and Wilfred Owen's unflinching, technically innovative depictions of combat's horror directly challenged that earlier idealism — Owen's "Dulce et Decorum Est" stands as a deliberate rebuttal of exactly the sentiment Brooke's sonnet expresses. Isaac Rosenberg's distinct perspective as an ordinary private soldier, rather than an officer, adds a further, less romanticized angle on the same war. The era's growing understanding of psychological trauma, connected to Freud's ideas from Chapter 1, shaped both Sassoon's and Owen's own treatment for shell shock at Craiglockhart, where their fateful meeting transformed Owen's poetry. Chapter 4 now turns to modernist prose fiction, and the novelists — Conrad, Joyce, and Woolf — who pursued in narrative form many of the same formal departures this chapter's poets and Chapter 2's poets pursued in verse.

## Glossary

- **Georgian poetry** — a body of English poetry, collected in Edward Marsh's anthology series from 1912, favoring traditional form and accessible, often pastoral subject matter over modernist experimentation.
- **Shell shock** — the era's term for the severe psychological trauma many soldiers developed after sustained combat, treated at specialist facilities such as Craiglockhart War Hospital.
- **Pararhyme (slant rhyme)** — a near-rhyme in which line endings share consonant sounds but not matching vowel sounds, a technique associated with Wilfred Owen.
