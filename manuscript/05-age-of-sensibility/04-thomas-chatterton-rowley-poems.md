## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 3 left you with a question worth carrying forward: does it matter, for a work's cultural power, whether its claimed origins are genuine? This chapter answers that question from a second angle, through a case even more extreme than Macpherson's — because where Macpherson at least drew on real fragments of Gaelic oral tradition, Thomas Chatterton's forgeries were, as far as scholars have ever established, invented from nothing at all.

Chatterton was born in Bristol in 1752, a few months after his father's death, into a family with a long-standing connection to St Mary Redcliffe Church: several generations of his family, including his father, had served as sextons there. That connection gave the young Chatterton an unusual privilege — access to the church's **Muniment Room**, a chamber above the north porch where old parish chests held genuine fifteenth-century deeds, documents, and papers relating to Bristol's medieval history. Precociously bookish and largely self-taught, Chatterton spent his childhood absorbing this material, and by around age eleven or twelve, he had begun writing poems and prose that he attributed to an invented fifteenth-century Bristol monk named **Thomas Rowley**, supposedly writing under the patronage of a real historical figure, the wealthy Bristol merchant William Canynges, who had genuinely funded restoration work on St Mary Redcliffe in the fifteenth century. In 1767, at fourteen, Chatterton was apprenticed to a Bristol attorney, John Lambert, as a scrivener — tedious legal copying work that, almost accidentally, gave him further practice with old document forms and handwriting styles he would put to very different use.

## Literary Movements

Chatterton's method of forgery differs from Macpherson's in one crucial respect, worth stating clearly before anything else: where Macpherson claimed to be *collecting* oral poetry passed down through generations, Chatterton claimed to have *discovered* physical written documents — genuine fifteenth-century manuscripts, supposedly preserved among the real historical materials in St Mary Redcliffe's Muniment Room. This documentary approach demanded a different kind of craft. Chatterton invented an elaborate **pseudo-medieval language** for the Rowley poems — an approximation of fifteenth-century English spelling and vocabulary, built from real medieval sources he'd studied but assembled according to patterns no genuine medieval scribe ever actually used. Scholars eventually identified these anachronisms as clear proof of forgery, but in the meantime, the invented antique surface was convincing enough to deceive a considerable number of contemporary readers, and even some expert antiquarians.

The broader cultural desire this satisfied is the same **primitivism** you studied in Chapter 3: a hunger for direct access to an authentic, unspoiled poetic past, imagined as more emotionally sincere than anything sophisticated modern culture could produce. But Chatterton adds something Ossian's story doesn't quite have to the same degree: because he was a real, historically documented English poet who died shockingly young, in poverty, apparently unrecognized by the literary establishment that might have supported him, his own biography became at least as culturally powerful as anything he actually wrote under Rowley's name. You will see, by the end of this chapter, that later Romantic writers responded overwhelmingly to the *story* of Chatterton's short life and death — not primarily to the specific content of the Rowley poems themselves.

## Author Profiles

**Thomas Chatterton** (1752–1770) produced, in roughly six years of writing, a substantial body of poetry and prose under Rowley's invented name, including "An Excelente Balade of Charitie" and "The Bristowe Tragedie" (a poem on the historical execution of Sir Charles Bawdin), alongside fabricated prose "histories" of Bristol and, more troublingly, forged genealogies he apparently hoped to sell to wealthy local families eager for a distinguished ancestry. His single most consequential venture beyond Bristol came in 1769, when he sent Horace Walpole a manuscript titled "The Ryse of Peyncteynge yn Englande," claiming it was genuine fifteenth-century material documenting the history of English painting, in the hope of securing Walpole's patronage. Walpole was initially intrigued, but after consulting other scholars, including Thomas Gray from Chapter 2, about the material's authenticity, he coldly withdrew his support and returned the manuscripts — a rejection Chatterton apparently never got past. Still only seventeen, Chatterton moved to London in April 1770 hoping to build a career as a professional writer across journalism, satire, and verse, but found only unstable, poorly paid work, and died within months, on 24 August 1770, from an overdose of arsenic. Whether this was a deliberate suicide brought on by poverty and rejection, or an accidental death connected to self-medication for an unrelated illness, remains genuinely disputed among modern biographers — but the popular image of a starving genius driven to suicide by an indifferent, philistine world took hold almost immediately, and proved, as this chapter's final section shows, extraordinarily durable.

**Horace Walpole** (1717–1797) is a figure worth knowing beyond his role in Chatterton's story: he was the author of *The Castle of Otranto* (1764), widely considered the first Gothic novel, a genre you'll study in full in Part VI, Chapter 9. Walpole's rejection of Chatterton's manuscripts, discussed above, became one of the era's most debated episodes of literary judgment — a case this chapter's critical perspectives section takes up directly.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **The 1768 Bristol Bridge account.** Chatterton's first public success came at just sixteen, when a local newspaper published his invented description of a medieval ceremony supposedly held at the original opening of Bristol Bridge, drawing on his self-taught command of antiquarian detail. The piece's convincing surface, and the fact that it went essentially unchallenged, likely encouraged Chatterton's subsequent, far more ambitious deceptions.
- **"An Excelente Balade of Charitie" and "The Bristowe Tragedie."** These Rowley poems show real formal skill: controlled, musical verse dressed in Chatterton's invented pseudo-medieval spelling, treating charity, historical violence, and moral judgment with a gravity that reads, once you set the authorship question aside, as genuinely impressive work for a writer barely out of childhood.
- **"The Ryse of Peyncteynge yn Englande" (1769).** The manuscript Chatterton sent Horace Walpole represents his most consequential single act of forgery — not because of its content, but because Walpole's rejection of it, and Chatterton's death less than a year later, fused into a single, powerfully symbolic story of genius spurned by an indifferent establishment.
- **Tyrwhitt's edition of the Rowley poems (1777, revised 1782).** Thomas Tyrwhitt's first edition presented the poems relatively neutrally, but his revised 1782 edition added an appendix acknowledging growing scholarly doubt about their authenticity — an important moment in settling, more decisively than the Ossian debate ever fully was, that Rowley had never existed.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Horace Walpole's rejection of Chatterton fair, or culpably callous?** Contemporary and later critics have often blamed Walpole harshly, treating his cold withdrawal of support as a wealthy, established writer's failure to recognize and nurture a genuine, desperate talent. A more balanced reading notes that Walpole's skepticism about the manuscripts' authenticity was, on the evidence, entirely reasonable — he was right that they were forged — even if his subsequent response to a struggling seventeen-year-old could still be judged as lacking in generosity or curiosity about the real talent behind the deception.
- **Suicide, accident, or something the historical record simply can't settle?** The traditional account of Chatterton's death as a despairing suicide has powerfully shaped his legacy, but modern biographers increasingly note that the arsenic he took was also a contemporary treatment for venereal disease, leaving real uncertainty about his intentions. This uncertainty is itself worth sitting with: sometimes a culturally powerful story outruns what the evidence can actually confirm.
- **How does Rowley's forgery compare with Ossian's?** Macpherson at least drew on genuine, if heavily reshaped, oral fragments; Chatterton, as far as anyone has established, invented Rowley and his poems from nothing beyond his own reading and imagination. Does this make Chatterton's forgery more dishonest, or does it make his achievement, judged purely as an act of poetic invention, more remarkable? Both readings are defensible, and holding them together is more useful than resolving the tension.
- **Did the Chatterton myth eclipse the Chatterton poems?** Wordsworth's "marvellous Boy," Keats's dedication of *Endymion*, Coleridge's "Monody on the Death of Chatterton," and Shelley's inclusion of him among poets who died young in *Adonais* all respond to Chatterton's life and death far more than to the specific content of the Rowley poems. This raises a genuine critical question: has Chatterton's afterlife as a symbol of doomed genius made it harder for readers to assess his actual poetic skill on its own terms?

## Chapter Summary

Growing up beside St Mary Redcliffe Church's genuine medieval archive, Thomas Chatterton invented a fictional fifteenth-century monk, Thomas Rowley, and produced, while still a teenager, an entire body of pseudo-medieval poetry and prose in an invented archaic language. His 1769 approach to Horace Walpole, later the author of the founding Gothic novel *The Castle of Otranto* (Part VI, Chapter 9), ended in cold rejection once Walpole consulted Thomas Gray and others about the manuscripts' authenticity; Chatterton died in London the following year, at seventeen, in circumstances that popular myth quickly and durably transformed into a story of poetic genius destroyed by an uncaring establishment. Unlike Macpherson's Ossian, built on genuinely reshaped oral tradition, Rowley appears to have been invented from nothing at all — a distinction this chapter's comparative table sets directly against Chapter 3's findings. What outlasted the forgery itself was the myth: Wordsworth, Keats, Coleridge, and Shelley all responded to Chatterton's death as a defining image of the doomed young genius, making him, arguably, more influential as a symbol for the Romantic poets of Part VI than as a poet in his own right — a pattern of legacy that closes out this chapter's exploration of eighteenth-century literary forgery before Part V turns, in Chapter 5, to the novel of sensibility.

## Glossary

- **Rowley poems** — the body of poetry and prose Thomas Chatterton wrote under the invented identity of a fifteenth-century monk, Thomas Rowley.
- **Muniment Room** — the chamber at St Mary Redcliffe Church, Bristol, where genuine medieval documents were stored, and to which Chatterton had childhood access.
- **Pseudo-medieval language** — Chatterton's invented approximation of fifteenth-century English spelling and vocabulary, assembled from genuine sources but containing anachronisms that eventually exposed the forgery.
- **The doomed genius myth** — the powerful cultural narrative, consolidated after Chatterton's death, of a brilliant young artist destroyed by poverty and an indifferent literary establishment.
