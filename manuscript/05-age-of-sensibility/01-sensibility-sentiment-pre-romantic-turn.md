## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Part IV closed with Samuel Johnson's commanding critical authority and Eliza Haywood's late turn toward calmer, more reflective fiction. Neither ending was really an ending at all — both point directly toward this Part. You may recall, from Chapter 7 of Part IV, that Richardson's *Clarissa* (1747–1748) already asked readers to feel intensely with its suffering heroine rather than simply judge her, and that Haywood's *The History of Miss Betsy Thoughtless* (1751), discussed in Part IV, Chapter 9, was already moving toward a gentler, more emotionally reflective mode of fiction. This Part picks up exactly that thread and follows it forward: across the second half of the eighteenth century, English culture increasingly prized **feeling** — tender emotion, ready sympathy, an open, visible responsiveness to others' suffering — as a mark of genuine moral worth, sometimes even above the reasoned judgment and witty self-control that Pope and Swift, in Part IV, had made the era's defining literary virtues.

This shift had a genuine philosophical foundation, developed largely by a group of thinkers now often grouped together as the **Scottish Enlightenment**. Where earlier moral philosophy had generally argued that people become good by reasoning their way to virtue, philosophers including Francis Hutcheson, David Hume, and Adam Smith argued instead that morality is grounded in **feeling**: humans possess something like an inborn "moral sense," which responds to kindness and suffering the way the eye responds to color or the ear to music, well before reason has a chance to weigh in. This chapter takes that philosophy seriously as the intellectual backbone of everything else Part V studies, because the literature of sensibility only makes full sense once you understand that, for this period, crying at another person's suffering wasn't seen as weakness or self-indulgence — it was read as direct, visible evidence of a well-formed moral character.

That belief had real social consequences beyond literature. This period saw a marked growth in organized charitable and humanitarian activity: Thomas Coram's Foundling Hospital, chartered in 1739 to care for abandoned infants, is one prominent example, and the same expanding culture of sympathy fed directly into the early **abolitionist movement**, including the Society for Effecting the Abolition of the Slave Trade, founded in 1787. You already met an earlier, more ambivalent literary treatment of enslavement in Aphra Behn's *Oroonoko* (Part IV, Chapter 9); the sensibility this chapter describes gave later writers, including Laurence Sterne, whom you'll study fully in Chapter 5, a new emotional vocabulary — sympathy, tenderness, shared suffering — for engaging with exactly this kind of injustice.

## Literary Movements

Two words anchor this entire Part, and both need careful unpacking before you meet the writers who use them: **sensibility** and **sentiment**. Neither began as a literary term. "Sensibility" was first a word from contemporary medicine and physiology, describing the nervous system's capacity to register and respond to stimulation — physicians of the period studied how "sensible" (responsive) living tissue was to touch, pain, or irritation. Writers and moralists borrowed this physical vocabulary and extended it to the emotions: a person of fine "sensibility" was one whose nerves, so to speak, were finely strung enough to register other people's suffering almost as if it were their own, producing visible physical symptoms — tears, trembling, a quickened pulse, even fainting — as outward proof of inward moral refinement. Keep this physiological root in mind whenever you meet a fainting heroine or a weeping hero later in this Part: eighteenth-century readers didn't see this as melodrama for its own sake, but as the literal, bodily evidence of a genuinely responsive moral nature. "Sentiment," and its adjective "sentimental," describe the same quality from a slightly different angle — refined, tender emotion, particularly moral emotion — and the word "sentimental" itself only became widely fashionable after the title of Laurence Sterne's 1768 novel *A Sentimental Journey*, which you will study fully in Chapter 5.

Out of this vocabulary grew a recognizable literary and social ideal: the **person of feeling** (sometimes, following a novel you'll meet in Chapter 5, called "the man of feeling"). This figure — and it could be male or female, though the culture coded emotional expressiveness rather differently for each — moves through the world responding to suffering wherever encountered, human or animal, stranger or friend, with immediate, visible sympathy. It's worth pausing on the gender point: eighteenth-century sensibility, unlike many later ideals of manliness, treated a man's tears and tender feeling as entirely compatible with, even evidence of, moral seriousness — a real contrast with the more emotionally guarded model of masculinity that later Victorian culture would prefer.

The rest of Part V traces this sensibility across several distinct literary developments, and it's worth having the whole map before you start: Chapter 2 studies the **Graveyard Poets** — Gray, Young, and Blair — whose meditative, melancholy poems on death and mortality gave sensibility one of its most distinctive early moods. Chapter 3 examines James Macpherson's **Ossian** poems and the fierce authenticity debate they triggered. Chapter 4 turns to Thomas Chatterton's **Rowley poems**, a second, tragically consequential episode in literary forgery. Chapter 5 studies the **novel of sensibility** directly, through Sterne, Mackenzie, and Goldsmith. Chapter 6 introduces William Blake's early poetry, and Chapter 7 closes the Part with Robert Burns and the vernacular Scottish tradition — a fitting close, given how many of this chapter's own philosophical figures were themselves part of the Scottish Enlightenment.

## Author Profiles

**Anthony Ashley Cooper, Third Earl of Shaftesbury** (1671–1713), and **Francis Hutcheson** (1694–1746), together laid the philosophical groundwork this whole Part builds on. Shaftesbury's *Characteristicks of Men, Manners, Opinions, Times* (1711) argued that human beings possess a natural, inborn love of virtue and beauty, taking real pleasure in benevolent action for its own sake rather than out of calculated self-interest or religious fear — a striking departure from more pessimistic accounts of human nature. Hutcheson, an Irish-born philosopher who became a central figure of the Scottish Enlightenment through his professorship at the University of Glasgow, developed this idea into a more systematic "moral sense" theory in *An Inquiry into the Original of our Ideas of Beauty and Virtue* (1725), arguing that just as we perceive beauty through an inbuilt aesthetic sense, we perceive moral goodness through an inbuilt moral sense that responds with approval to kindness and benevolence.

**David Hume** (1711–1776) pushed this line of thought toward one of philosophy's most provocative claims. In *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–1740), Hume argued that morality cannot be grounded in reason alone, since reason by itself can tell you facts but never tell you what you *ought* to value — famously concluding that "reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions." For Hume, our moral judgments ultimately rest on feeling and sentiment, not abstract logical deduction — a claim that gave the literary culture of sensibility a serious philosophical foundation, whether or not most readers of sentimental novels had actually read Hume themselves.

**Adam Smith** (1723–1790), Hume's close friend and fellow Scottish Enlightenment figure, gave this chapter its single most important text: *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759). Smith argues that morality begins in **sympathy** — our imaginative capacity to place ourselves in another person's situation and feel, at least partially, what they feel — and that we judge our own conduct by imagining how an **impartial spectator** would view it. Smith is much better remembered today for *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), his foundational work of economic theory, but *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* is arguably just as important for this book's purposes: it gives the entire literary culture of sensibility its clearest, most influential philosophical statement.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Shaftesbury, *Characteristicks of Men, Manners, Opinions, Times* (1711).** Read this as sensibility's philosophical starting point: Shaftesbury's confident claim that virtue is naturally pleasurable, not merely dutiful, gives later writers permission to treat emotional responsiveness itself as a form of moral achievement, rather than something reason must strictly control.
- **Hutcheson, *An Inquiry into the Original of our Ideas of Beauty and Virtue* (1725).** Hutcheson's analogy between aesthetic and moral perception — we simply *see* beauty and goodness, the way we see colors — helps explain why so much sensibility literature treats a tender emotional response as immediate and involuntary, not the product of careful reasoning.
- **Hume, *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–1740).** Hume's claim that reason serves the passions rather than governing them directly challenges the Augustan confidence in reasoned judgment you saw throughout Part IV, and helps explain sensibility's willingness to trust strong feeling as a reliable moral guide.
- **Smith, *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759).** Smith's concept of sympathy — imaginatively entering another person's situation — describes almost exactly what a sentimental novel asks its reader to do with its suffering characters. His "impartial spectator," an imagined, disinterested observer of our own conduct, gives sensibility's culture of self-scrutiny and moral feeling a workable psychological model.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is "sensibility" a coherent movement, or a loose cultural mood?** Unlike, say, heroic tragedy in Part IV, sensibility was never a formal literary school with declared rules. Critics generally treat it instead as a broad cultural sensibility (the pun is unavoidable) that shaped poetry, fiction, philosophy, and social reform alike, expressed differently by each writer Part V studies rather than through any single shared program.
- **What does the "pre-Romantic" label get right, and what does it risk?** Calling this period "pre-Romantic" usefully highlights real continuities — the value placed on emotion, imagination, and individual subjective experience clearly anticipates the Romantic poets you'll meet in Part VI. But the label also risks treating sensibility as merely preparatory, valuable only for what it led to, rather than as a rich, distinctive literary culture worth understanding on its own terms. This book uses the term cautiously, and encourages you to do the same.
- **Why did "sentimental" become an insult?** The word you've just learned to use carefully and historically in this chapter later became, especially from the nineteenth century onward, a term of real critical dismissal — "sentimental" fiction accused of manipulating readers' emotions cheaply, without earning them through genuine artistic skill. Understanding sensibility's serious philosophical foundation, as this chapter has tried to give you, is the best defense against reading this period's literature only through that later, dismissive lens.
- **How did sensibility complicate ideas about gender?** Because tender emotional expression was, in this culture, compatible with masculine virtue rather than opposed to it, sensibility literature often lets male characters weep, faint, and openly display feeling in ways later literary periods would code as exclusively feminine. This makes the period a genuinely interesting test case for how differently "appropriate" masculine emotional expression has been defined across literary history.

## Chapter Summary

Building on the emotional intensity already visible in Richardson's *Clarissa* and Haywood's late fiction from Part IV, English culture in the second half of the eighteenth century increasingly prized sensibility: a refined, visible capacity for sympathetic feeling, grounded philosophically in the Scottish Enlightenment moral theory of Shaftesbury, Hutcheson, Hume, and, above all, Adam Smith's *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759). Rooted originally in the physiological vocabulary of nerves and responsiveness, sensibility produced a new literary and social ideal, the person of feeling, whose tears and trembling were read as genuine evidence of moral worth rather than weakness — and whose culture of sympathy fed directly into real humanitarian movements, including early abolitionism. The label "pre-Romantic," useful but imperfect, marks this period's real continuities with the Romanticism of Part VI without doing justice to its own distinct character. The rest of Part V now traces sensibility's specific literary expressions: the Graveyard Poets' melancholy in Chapter 2, the authenticity controversies of Macpherson's Ossian in Chapter 3 and Chatterton's Rowley poems in Chapter 4, the sentimental novel proper in Chapter 5, Blake's early visionary poetry in Chapter 6, and Robert Burns's vernacular Scottish voice in Chapter 7.

## Glossary

- **Sensibility** — a refined capacity for sympathetic emotional response, understood in this period as rooted in the body's nervous responsiveness as much as in moral character.
- **Sentiment / sentimental** — refined, tender moral emotion; the adjective "sentimental" became widely fashionable following Laurence Sterne's 1768 novel *A Sentimental Journey*.
- **Moral sense** — the theory, developed by Shaftesbury and Hutcheson, that humans possess an innate faculty for perceiving moral goodness, analogous to the perception of beauty.
- **Sympathy** — in Adam Smith's usage, the imaginative capacity to place oneself in another person's situation and share, at least partially, their feeling.
- **Impartial spectator** — Adam Smith's term for an imagined, disinterested observer by whose standards we judge our own conduct.
