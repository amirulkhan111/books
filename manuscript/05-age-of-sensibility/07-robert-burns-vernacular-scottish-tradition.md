## Historical and Socio-Political Context

This Part closes where it has quietly been heading all along: with a poet who achieves, in a completely different language tradition, exactly the kind of authentic vernacular power that Macpherson's Ossian, in Chapter 3, could only fake. Before going further, it's worth clearing up a distinction this Part has left implicit until now. Scottish Gaelic, the language behind the real oral fragments Macpherson drew on and inflated, is a Celtic language, historically associated with the Scottish Highlands. **Lowland Scots**, the language Robert Burns wrote in, is something quite different: a Germanic language closely related to English, historically spoken across Lowland Scotland, with its own long independent literary tradition stretching back centuries. These are two separate linguistic and cultural inheritances, and this chapter's "vernacular Scottish tradition" means Scots, not Gaelic.

That tradition faced real pressure in the decades after the 1707 Act of Union joined Scotland and England under one Parliament. Ambitious Scottish writers and intellectuals — including the very Scottish Enlightenment philosophers you met in Chapter 1 — increasingly wrote in standard English rather than Scots, partly to reach a wider British readership and partly to distance themselves from English prejudices that treated Scots as a rustic, provincial dialect unfit for serious literature. Two poets had already begun pushing back against that trend before Burns: Allan Ramsay (1686–1758) and Robert Fergusson (1750–1774), whose Scots-language poetry Burns read closely and openly credited as his own model — he later paid, out of his own limited means, for a proper headstone at Fergusson's neglected Edinburgh grave. Burns was born in 1759 in Alloway, Ayrshire, the son of a struggling tenant farmer, and grew up doing hard agricultural labor from childhood — work that likely damaged his health permanently and contributed to his early death, but that also gave him direct, lived knowledge of the rural Scottish world his poetry describes so vividly.

## Literary Movements

Burns's achievement sits in a genuinely interesting contrast with the two forgery controversies you've just studied. Macpherson's Ossian (Chapter 3) and Chatterton's Rowley (Chapter 4) both claimed to recover an authentic ancient voice, and both, to different degrees, faked that authenticity. Burns needed no such claim: Scots was a living, spoken, contemporary language, and his poetry's vernacular power is straightforwardly genuine rather than manufactured. Where Macpherson and Chatterton looked backward toward an imagined lost past, Burns simply wrote, brilliantly, in the actual language of the people and place around him — proving, in the process, that a "rustic" vernacular could carry comedy, satire, tenderness, and political conviction with exactly the same seriousness as standard literary English.

Burns's technical range is genuinely wide. He could write sharp social satire, as in "Holy Willie's Prayer," a dramatic monologue that lets a self-righteous Calvinist hypocrite condemn himself out of his own mouth. He could write tender, sentimental domestic poetry very much in the mode of this Part's broader culture of sensibility, as in "The Cotter's Saturday Night," which dignifies pious rural family life with real warmth. And in his later career, he devoted enormous, largely unpaid effort to **song-collecting**: gathering fragments of traditional Scottish tunes and lyrics, and honestly adapting or completing them with his own new verses, for two major published collections. This blending of collection and composition raises an authorship question that echoes Chapter 3's discussion of Macpherson — but with one crucial difference. Burns was always transparent that he was adapting and completing older material, never claiming false ancient authorship the way Macpherson did; his honesty here stands as a useful contrast to the forgery debates that dominated Chapters 3 and 4.

## Author Profiles

**Robert Burns** (1759–1796) was largely self-taught beyond a basic education arranged by his father, reading widely — including Pope's translations of Homer (Part IV, Chapter 6) and popular English song collections — while working as a farm laborer through his youth. His 1786 collection, *Poems, Chiefly in the Scottish Dialect*, known as the Kilmarnock volume after the town where it was printed, was an immediate sensation; Burns had, by his own account, already booked passage to emigrate to Jamaica for economic reasons before the volume's unexpected success changed his prospects entirely. The book contains many of his most enduring poems, including "To a Mouse," whose famous line — "The best laid schemes o' Mice an' Men / Gang aft agley" — later gave John Steinbeck the title of his own novel. Following the volume's success, Burns spent the winter of 1786–1787 in Edinburgh, where he was warmly received by the city's Enlightenment literary establishment, including figures you've already met in this book — and where Henry Mackenzie, from Chapter 5, published his famous, enthusiastic review in *The Lounger*, calling Burns a "Heaven-taught ploughman." That phrase stuck, and became part of Burns's enduring public image, though it somewhat understates the extent of his real reading and technical craft, treating him as an untutored natural wonder rather than a serious, well-read poet who had worked hard at his art. Burns married Jean Armour in 1788 and later supported his family by working as an excise officer, even as his own political sympathies, like William Blake's in Chapter 6, leaned toward the radical, democratic ideals of the French Revolution — sympathies expressed most directly in "A Man's a Man for A' That" (1795), which insists that rank and wealth are no true measure of human worth: "The rank is but the guinea's stamp, / The Man's the gowd for a' that." In his final years, Burns devoted enormous unpaid effort to two major song collections, James Johnson's *The Scots Musical Museum* and George Thomson's *A Select Collection of Original Scottish Airs*, contributing new lyrics to traditional tunes and preserving a huge body of Scottish song that might otherwise have been lost — among them "Auld Lang Syne," now sung around the world at New Year, and "Scots Wha Hae," a patriotic song imagining Robert the Bruce rallying his troops before the Battle of Bannockburn. Burns died in Dumfries in 1796, at thirty-seven, likely from heart disease connected to the hard physical labor of his early life.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Poems, Chiefly in the Scottish Dialect* (1786).** The Kilmarnock volume announced, in a single stroke, that vernacular Scots could carry the full range of serious poetic ambition — comic, satiric, tender, and politically pointed — reversing decades of "polite" Scottish culture's drift toward standard English.
- **"To a Mouse."** Written, according to tradition, after Burns's plough turned up a mouse's nest, the poem moves from that small, specific incident to a genuinely moving meditation on the shared vulnerability of all creatures, human and animal alike, to misfortune and disappointed plans.
- **"Holy Willie's Prayer."** This dramatic monologue, voiced entirely by a self-satisfied religious hypocrite congratulating himself on his own election to salvation while excusing his own sins, is one of the sharpest satires of Calvinist self-righteousness in the whole of Scottish literature.
- **"Auld Lang Syne" (1788).** Built from older traditional fragments and completed by Burns, this song's global popularity — sung at New Year's celebrations far beyond Scotland — is a striking demonstration of how thoroughly Burns's adaptive songwriting reshaped what the whole world now thinks of as traditional Scottish culture.
- **"Scots Wha Hae" (1793).** Composed during Burns's own period of sympathy for revolutionary and radical political causes, this song imagines Robert the Bruce's rallying speech before Bannockburn, fusing patriotic historical memory with the era's live political urgency.
- **"A Man's a Man for A' That" (1795).** Burns's clearest statement of democratic conviction argues that true human worth has nothing to do with rank, title, or wealth — a direct, unmistakable expression of the same radical egalitarian hope that, as Chapter 6 showed you, also animated Blake's response to the French Revolution.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was the "Heaven-taught ploughman" label a compliment or a limitation?** Mackenzie's famous phrase helped make Burns famous, but it also risks flattening him into a simple, romanticized image of untutored rural genius, obscuring how widely he had actually read and how deliberately he crafted his technique. Modern criticism generally insists on recovering the working poet-craftsman behind the convenient myth.
- **Has Burns's status as a beloved national icon sanitized his actual work?** The annual Burns Supper, held on his birthday, celebrates a warm, familiar image of Burns — but his actual poetry includes sharp religious satire, real political radicalism, and a personal life involving several relationships outside marriage that popular commemoration doesn't always foreground. Understanding Burns fully means holding the beloved national poet and the sharper, more complicated historical figure together.
- **How does Burns's honesty about song adaptation compare with Macpherson's deception?** Both Burns and Macpherson took older material and substantially reshaped it with their own composition. The crucial difference is disclosure: Burns never hid his own authorial hand, where Macpherson's entire project depended on hiding his. This comparison is a useful way to think about where legitimate creative adaptation ends and forgery begins.
- **What does Burns's success suggest about vernacular language and literary prestige?** At a moment when ambitious Scottish writers were moving toward standard English for broader acceptance, Burns proved that a supposedly "provincial" vernacular could achieve the highest literary seriousness on its own terms — a lesson with implications well beyond Scotland, relevant to any literary tradition working in a language or dialect treated as less prestigious than a dominant standard.

## Chapter Summary

Working from a genuine, living vernacular tradition rather than a claimed ancient one, Robert Burns proved decisively that Lowland Scots could carry the full range of serious literary achievement, from the sharp religious satire of "Holy Willie's Prayer" to the tender universal sympathy of "To a Mouse" and the democratic conviction of "A Man's a Man for A' That." His 1786 Kilmarnock volume made him famous almost overnight, and Henry Mackenzie's admiring but somewhat mythologizing review, from Chapter 5, helped fix his enduring public image as a "Heaven-taught ploughman" — a label modern criticism has learned to hold at some distance from the widely read, technically deliberate poet it actually describes. Burns's later song-collecting work, honestly blending preservation and original composition, gave the world "Auld Lang Syne" and "Scots Wha Hae," and stands in instructive contrast to Macpherson's dishonest claims of ancient authorship in Chapter 3.

That closes Part V. Across seven chapters, you've traced the culture of sensibility from its Scottish Enlightenment philosophical foundations, through the graveyard poets' meditative sublime, two controversial episodes of literary forgery built on primitivist longing for an authentic past, the novel of sensibility's full flowering, William Blake's visionary early poetry, and now Burns's genuine vernacular achievement — the real, undisputed authenticity this Part has been implicitly measuring its forgers against all along. Both Blake and Burns, in their different ways, greeted the French Revolution with genuine hope for a freer, more equal world. Part VI picks up that story directly, opening with the French Revolution's impact on the Romantic imagination.

## Glossary

- **Lowland Scots** — a Germanic language closely related to English, historically spoken across Lowland Scotland, with its own independent literary tradition distinct from Scottish Gaelic.
- **Vernacular** — the everyday spoken language of a particular place or people, as opposed to a more formal or prestigious standard language.
- **"Heaven-taught ploughman"** — Henry Mackenzie's phrase for Burns, celebrating him as a natural, untutored genius, though modern criticism regards it as understating his actual reading and craft.
