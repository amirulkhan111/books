## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 2 ended with Thomas Gray reaching past English and classical models toward ancient Norse and British poetry — part of a broader mid-eighteenth-century hunger for older, more "primitive" native traditions, imagined as closer to raw feeling and further from Augustan artifice. This chapter studies where that hunger led when it met a real, and recently devastated, national culture: the Scottish Highlands.

In 1746, at the Battle of Culloden, government forces crushed the Jacobite rising that had tried to restore the exiled Stuart monarchy to the British throne. The defeat was catastrophic for Highland Gaelic society specifically: the government followed up with deliberate, sustained cultural suppression, including the Dress Act of 1746, which banned traditional Highland dress and tartan, and a broader dismantling of the clan system that had structured Highland life for centuries. James Macpherson was born in 1736 in Ruthven, in the Highland district of Badenoch — close to the site where retreating Jacobite forces gathered for their final, failed muster after Culloden — and grew up, in other words, inside a culture that had just been politically and militarily broken. That context matters enormously for what he did next: publishing poetry that claimed to recover and preserve an ancient, heroic Gaelic literary inheritance at precisely the moment Highland culture's living present was being deliberately erased.

Macpherson found an eager audience for this project among the literary circles of Edinburgh, including the influential professor Hugh Blair — worth distinguishing clearly and immediately from Robert Blair, the *different* Blair you met writing *The Grave* in Chapter 2 — who encouraged Macpherson to search further after his first small collection appeared, convinced that a genuine ancient Gaelic epic, comparable to Homer, might really be recoverable from Highland oral tradition.

## Literary Movements

This chapter's central term is **primitivism**: the mid-eighteenth-century belief that older, simpler, less "civilized" cultures preserved a kind of raw emotional and poetic power that sophisticated modern society had lost. Where Augustan writers in Part IV looked to Greece and Rome as models of refined civilization, primitivist taste looked instead to Europe's own ancient and "barbaric" past — Norse sagas, ancient British bards, Gaelic oral epic — for exactly the kind of intense, unmediated feeling that Chapter 1's culture of sensibility prized. Macpherson's Ossian poems arrived as the single most spectacular product of this taste.

Beginning with the slim *Fragments of Ancient Poetry* (1760), Macpherson claimed to be translating genuine ancient Gaelic verse, composed by a legendary third-century warrior-poet named Ossian (Oisín in the wider Gaelic tradition), son of the hero Fingal (Fionn mac Cumhaill). Encouraged by Hugh Blair and others, Macpherson expanded this claim dramatically, publishing *Fingal* (1761–1762) and *Temora* (1763) as full-scale ancient epics, on the scale of Homer's *Iliad* and *Odyssey*, and collecting everything as *The Works of Ossian* in 1765. The poems' melancholy tone — noble warriors, misty Highland landscapes, an elegiac sense of a vanished heroic age — proved enormously appealing across Europe, well beyond Britain. Napoleon Bonaparte reportedly carried a copy on campaign; Johann Wolfgang von Goethe had his tragic hero read Ossian aloud in a pivotal emotional scene in *The Sorrows of Young Werther* (1774); the poems fed directly into the German Sturm und Drang movement, the same current that had already embraced Young's *Night Thoughts* from Chapter 2. Few literary works of this century had a wider or more immediate international impact.

Almost as quickly, the poems' authenticity came under sustained attack — a controversy this chapter now studies directly.

## Author Profiles

**James Macpherson** (1736–1796) grew up in the Highland culture Culloden's aftermath had just shattered, and his literary career is inseparable from that fact. After his early *Fragments* (1760) succeeded, he traveled through the Highlands and Hebrides, by his own account collecting oral poetry and manuscript material, and returned to publish *Fingal* (1761–1762) and *Temora* (1763) as translations of a coherent, ancient Gaelic epic tradition. He never produced the original Gaelic manuscripts his critics repeatedly demanded to see, and modern scholarship, discussed below, generally concludes that while Macpherson did draw on genuine fragments of the Gaelic oral tradition surrounding Fionn mac Cumhaill and his warriors — a body of legend known as the **Fenian Cycle** — he heavily reshaped, expanded, and substantially composed the resulting "translations" himself, imposing a continuous, Homer-like epic structure that traditional Gaelic balladry never actually possessed. Macpherson went on to a successful and financially rewarding career, becoming a Member of Parliament in 1780, and died a wealthy man in 1796 — remarkably, given the controversy that surrounded him throughout his life, he was buried in Westminster Abbey.

**Hugh Blair** (1718–1800) was a professor of rhetoric and belles-lettres at the University of Edinburgh, and Ossian's most influential early champion. His *A Critical Dissertation on the Poems of Ossian* (1763) argued at length for the poems' genuine antiquity and defended their literary merit as comparable to Homer's, giving the Ossian phenomenon crucial scholarly and institutional credibility in its earliest, most vulnerable years. It's worth repeating the point made above: Hugh Blair is a different person entirely from Robert Blair, the graveyard poet you met writing *The Grave* in Chapter 2 — a coincidence of surname that has confused many students before you.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Fragments of Ancient Poetry* (1760).** Macpherson's first, modest collection of short prose-poems set the template for everything that followed: melancholy tone, misty Highland settings, and a claimed basis in genuine oral tradition that immediately excited Edinburgh's literary establishment.
- ***Fingal* (1761–1762) and *Temora* (1763).** These two full-length "epics" gave Ossian genuine claim to rival Homer in scale and ambition, structured with formal epic conventions — invocations, extended battle scenes, heroic genealogies — that most scholars now agree owe more to Macpherson's own classical education than to any authentic, continuous Gaelic epic source.
- **Hugh Blair, *A Critical Dissertation on the Poems of Ossian* (1763).** Blair's defense treated the poems as genuine evidence that "primitive" societies could produce poetry of real sublimity, without the benefit of classical learning — an argument that mattered as much for what it said about primitivism generally as for Ossian specifically.
- **Samuel Johnson, *A Journey to the Western Islands of Scotland* (1775).** Johnson's account of his 1773 tour, taken partly to investigate the Ossian controversy firsthand, concluded bluntly that the poems could not be genuine ancient translations. When Macpherson responded with threats of violence, Johnson's famous defiant reply refused to be intimidated — he reportedly kept a large oak cudgel by his door in case Macpherson made good on the threat — a vivid coda to the critical authority you already studied in Part IV, Chapter 8.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Macpherson a forger, or something more complicated?** The word "forgery" implies straightforward, deliberate fraud, but Macpherson's actual practice sits somewhere between forgery, translation, and original composition: he genuinely collected fragments of Gaelic oral tradition, then reshaped, expanded, and substantially invented material to fit a continuous epic form modeled on Homer — passing the whole result off as faithful translation. Most modern scholars treat this as a real, serious deception about the poems' actual origin, while also recognizing that Macpherson's creative achievement, whatever its honesty, was genuinely substantial in its own right.
- **Should Ossian be read as cultural reclamation rather than simple fraud?** Given the historical context this chapter opened with — a Highland culture recently and deliberately suppressed after Culloden — some critics read Macpherson's project more sympathetically, as an attempt to assert dignity and literary greatness for a culture Britain had just tried to erase, even if the specific means he chose involved serious deception about the poems' actual origins.
- **Whose heritage was Ossian, really?** The Fenian Cycle material Macpherson drew on belongs to a shared Gaelic tradition spanning both Scotland and Ireland, and Irish scholars at the time and since have objected that Macpherson effectively claimed shared Gaelic heritage as exclusively Scottish, reshaping figures — Fionn mac Cumhaill above all — more strongly associated with Irish tradition into specifically Scottish national heroes. This dispute anticipates the distinct national literary traditions Part X, Chapters 2 and 3, will study in Irish and Scottish literature respectively.
- **Why did Ossian matter so much, regardless of the authenticity question?** The poems' enormous European influence — on Goethe, on Napoleon, on the wider Sturm und Drang movement — happened almost entirely before the authenticity controversy was settled, and arguably continued regardless of it. This suggests that what readers wanted from Ossian — melancholy sublimity, a vanished heroic past, intense unmediated feeling — mattered to them more than strict historical accuracy, a pattern worth remembering when you meet Thomas Chatterton's similarly controversial, similarly influential forgeries in Chapter 4.

## Chapter Summary

Growing up in a Highland Gaelic culture recently and deliberately suppressed after the 1746 Battle of Culloden, James Macpherson published a sequence of increasingly ambitious poems — the *Fragments* (1760), *Fingal* and *Temora* (1761–1763), and the collected *Works of Ossian* (1765) — claiming to translate a genuine ancient epic tradition, encouraged by Hugh Blair's influential scholarly defense. The poems became one of the eighteenth century's greatest international literary sensations, shaping European Romanticism from Goethe to Napoleon, even as Samuel Johnson, building on the critical authority you studied in Part IV, Chapter 8, publicly and forcefully doubted their authenticity. Macpherson's actual practice — reshaping genuine Gaelic oral fragments into an invented, Homer-like epic structure — sits uneasily between translation, forgery, and original composition, and the dispute over whether the underlying Fenian Cycle material belongs to Scotland or Ireland anticipates the distinct national literary traditions Part X will study. Whatever the final verdict on its authenticity, Ossian's cultural impact was real and enormous — a pattern of controversial influence you'll see repeated, on British soil this time, in Chapter 4's study of Thomas Chatterton.

## Glossary

- **Primitivism** — the eighteenth-century belief that older, less "civilized" cultures preserved a raw emotional and poetic power lost to sophisticated modern society.
- **Ossianic** — describing the melancholy, sublime, Highland-inflected literary style associated with Macpherson's Ossian poems.
- **Fenian Cycle** — the body of Gaelic oral legend concerning the hero Fionn mac Cumhaill (Fingal) and his warriors, shared across Scottish and Irish tradition.
- **Literary forgery** — a work presented as genuinely older, or by a different author, than it actually is; a recurring phenomenon of this period, also discussed in Chapter 4.
