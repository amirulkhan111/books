## Historical and Socio-Political Context

You've now seen William Blake's name twice already in this Part — as the illustrator, in 1808, of Robert Blair's *The Grave* (Chapter 2), and as a poet whose imagery Blair's graveyard meditations anticipated. This chapter finally gives Blake his own full treatment, though with one honest limitation stated up front: Blake's career runs well past 1798, the year this Part ends, into the full Romantic period covered in Part VI, and his later, deliberately difficult mythological "prophetic books" — works like *Milton* and *Jerusalem* — build an entire private symbolic universe that deserves separate, sustained study this chapter does not attempt. What this chapter covers instead is Blake's formative early achievement: his training, his invented printing method, and the two linked collections that remain his most widely read work, *Songs of Innocence* (1789) and *Songs of Experience* (1794).

Blake was born in London in 1757, the son of a hosier, and showed enough early artistic talent that his parents apprenticed him, at fourteen, to the engraver James Basire — training that gave him the technical printmaking skill his entire later career would depend on. He later studied briefly at the Royal Academy of Arts, founded by Sir Joshua Reynolds, the same Reynolds who co-founded The Club with Samuel Johnson in Part IV, Chapter 8; Blake came to despise Reynolds's classical, academic taste, later covering his own copy of Reynolds's *Discourses* with furious marginal objections, convinced that true art came from individual imaginative vision, not inherited rules. Blake spent the 1790s moving in London's radical political circles, connected to the publisher Joseph Johnson's circle, which also included the philosopher and early feminist Mary Wollstonecraft and the radical political writer Thomas Paine. This was the decade of the French Revolution, which many English radicals, Blake among them, greeted with genuine early enthusiasm as the dawn of a new age of liberty — a hope Part VI, Chapter 1, will trace through to its later, more disillusioned outcome. That radical political context runs directly through the *Songs*, which repeatedly turn their attention to institutional religion, child exploitation, and social injustice.

## Literary Movements

Understanding Blake requires understanding **illuminated printing**, the method he claimed his dead brother Robert revealed to him in a vision shortly after Robert's death in 1787. Rather than setting movable type and printing illustrations separately, as conventional publishing did, Blake wrote his poems and drew his accompanying images together, directly onto copper plates, using an acid-resistant varnish; when he applied acid to the plate, everything *except* his varnished text and design was eaten away, leaving text and image standing together in relief, ready to be inked and printed as a single unified page. Blake then hand-colored many copies himself, often with his wife Catherine's help, which means no two surviving copies of a Blake illuminated book are ever quite identical. This matters enormously for how you should read the *Songs*: Blake's poems were never meant to exist as pure text. Image and word work together on the page, sometimes reinforcing each other, sometimes pulling in quietly different directions — a dimension modern printed anthologies, showing you only the words, inevitably lose.

The *Songs*' full original title states Blake's central organizing idea directly: *Songs of Innocence and of Experience, Shewing the Two Contrary States of the Human Soul*. **Contrary states**, for Blake, are not simply "good" and "bad," or an earlier, better condition followed by a later, worse one. Innocence and Experience are two different, equally real ways of perceiving the same world, existing in productive tension rather than a simple developmental sequence. Blake states the wider principle behind this directly in a work composed in the same years, *The Marriage of Heaven and Hell* (1790–1793): "Without Contraries is no progression." Real spiritual and imaginative growth, for Blake, comes from holding opposing forces — reason and energy, restraint and desire, innocence and experience — together, not from simply choosing one side and discarding the other.

## Author Profiles

**William Blake** (1757–1827) worked his entire life as a professional engraver to support himself, since his own poetry and illuminated books sold only in small numbers to a handful of admirers, leaving him in more or less constant financial precarity. His first collection, *Poetical Sketches* (1783), printed conventionally with friends' help, already shows real lyrical talent, but his mature, distinctive voice emerges only with *Songs of Innocence* (1789), his first fully illuminated book. Its companion volume, *Songs of Experience* (1794), presents a darker, harder-edged vision of the same world — institutional religion, poverty, sexual repression, and political tyranny replacing Innocence's pastoral warmth — and Blake combined both under a single title that same year, arranging many poems as explicit, paired "contraries." Blake's visionary temperament was lifelong and well documented: he reported seeing visions from childhood onward, including, famously, a vision of angels in a tree on Peckham Rye, and he treated these experiences as genuine spiritual perception throughout his life, a claim later biographers and critics have handled with varying degrees of literalism and skepticism. Largely unrecognized as a major poet in his own lifetime, and considered eccentric or worse by many contemporaries, Blake died in London in 1827 in comparative obscurity; his reputation was substantially rebuilt afterward, especially following Alexander Gilchrist's 1863 biography, until he came to be recognized as one of the most original imaginative voices of the entire Romantic period.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Poetical Sketches* (1783).** Blake's earliest published poems, conventionally printed rather than illuminated, already show real formal skill, though without yet the distinctive visionary intensity and social critique of his mature work.
- ***Songs of Innocence* (1789).** These poems adopt the simple, hymn-like language of contemporary children's verse — echoing writers like Isaac Watts — but frequently embed genuine social critique beneath their surface simplicity. "The Chimney Sweeper," narrated by a child chimney sweep who dreams of an angel freeing him and his fellow sweeps, ends with the deeply ambiguous line "So if all do their duty, they need not fear harm" — a moral that can be read either as sincere comfort or as bitter irony about how religious language was used to pacify exploited children rather than actually protect them.
- ***The Marriage of Heaven and Hell* (1790–1793).** Composed alongside the *Songs*, this prose-and-verse work states Blake's "contraries" philosophy most directly, arguing that conventional moral categories of good and evil misunderstand a deeper truth: that energy and restraint, desire and reason, are both necessary, and genuine progress comes from their tension rather than the triumph of one over the other.
- **"The Lamb" (Innocence) and "The Tyger" (Experience).** This is the collection's single most famous paired contrary. "The Lamb" asks a child's gentle question — "Little Lamb, who made thee?" — and answers it with tender, nurturing imagery of a gentle Creator. "The Tyger" asks essentially the same question — "Did he who made the Lamb make thee?" — but of a terrifying, sublime, violent creation, and pointedly never fully answers it. Read together, the two poems ask whether the same God, or the same creative force, can really be responsible for both gentleness and terror — a question Blake leaves genuinely open.
- **The two "Chimney Sweeper" poems.** *Experience*'s version of this poem drops *Innocence*'s ambiguity for direct, angry accusation: the child sweep tells us his parents "are gone to praise God and his Priest and King, / Who make up a heaven of our misery" — an unmistakable indictment of religious and social institutions that tolerate child exploitation.
- **The two "Holy Thursday" poems.** *Innocence*'s version describes charity-school children processing into St Paul's Cathedral with apparent warmth and approval; *Experience*'s version bitterly attacks the "cold and usurous hand" of a supposedly charitable society that allows real poverty and suffering to continue alongside its institutional displays of benevolence.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is "Innocence" simply naive, or already quietly critical?** The ambiguous ending of *Innocence*'s "The Chimney Sweeper" is the classic test case: does the poem's final line offer genuine comfort, or does it show a child having already absorbed the exploitative logic of the adults around him? Most modern critics favor the second reading, treating even Blake's "Innocent" poems as carrying real social critique beneath their deceptively simple surface — a reminder that "innocent" and "naive" are not the same thing in Blake's hands.
- **What is lost when Blake's illuminated pages are reduced to plain text?** Because most readers encounter the *Songs* only as printed words in an anthology, it's worth remembering that Blake's original conception was inseparably visual and verbal. Modern criticism increasingly insists on reading image and text together wherever possible, since Blake sometimes uses the relationship between them ironically — an illustration that complicates, rather than simply decorates, the words beside it.
- **How should literary criticism handle Blake's claimed visionary experiences?** Blake's reports of seeing angels and receiving visionary instruction (as with the relief-etching technique) resist easy categorization as either literal supernatural experience, a distinctive mode of poetic perception, or a symptom to be diagnosed. Most careful critics avoid both credulous endorsement and dismissive pathologizing, treating Blake's visionary claims instead as central to how he understood his own imaginative process, whatever their ultimate nature.
- **What does Blake's posthumous rediscovery suggest about literary reputation?** Blake's obscurity during his own lifetime, followed by his later recognition as a major Romantic poet, echoes a pattern you've now seen more than once in this book — Aphra Behn's long marginalization and later feminist recovery (Part IV, Chapter 9), and Chatterton's posthumous mythologization (Chapter 4) — a useful reminder that a writer's reputation at the time of their death is rarely a reliable guide to their lasting significance.

## Chapter Summary

Trained as an engraver and largely unrecognized as a major poet during his own lifetime, William Blake invented a unique method of illuminated printing — text and image etched together onto copper plates and hand-colored individually — that makes his *Songs of Innocence* (1789) and *Songs of Experience* (1794) inseparably visual and verbal works. Organized around Blake's principle of "contrary states," the two collections repeatedly pair poems that view the same subject from opposite angles, most famously "The Lamb" and "The Tyger," while poems like the two versions of "The Chimney Sweeper" and "Holy Thursday" show even Blake's seemingly simple "Innocent" verse carrying real social and religious critique, sharpened by his connections to the radical political circles of 1790s London and the early hopeful reception of the French Revolution that Part VI, Chapter 1, will pick up directly. Blake's obscurity in his own lifetime gave way, after his death in 1827, to a substantial critical rediscovery — closing this Part's recurring pattern of writers whose true significance only became visible well after their own time.

---

## Glossary

- **Illuminated printing (relief etching)** — Blake's invented method of etching text and image together onto a copper plate, so that both are printed as a single, unified design; each hand-colored copy is unique.
- **Contrary states** — Blake's term for innocence and experience as two equally real, coexisting ways of perceiving the world, rather than a simple sequence from a better state to a worse one.
- **Visionary poetry** — poetry that claims to draw on genuine visionary or mystical experience as its source, as Blake claimed throughout his life.
