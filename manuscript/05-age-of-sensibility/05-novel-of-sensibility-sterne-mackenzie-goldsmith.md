## Historical and Socio-Political Context

You've now met sensibility's philosophy (Chapter 1), its poetry of mortality (Chapter 2), and two spectacular controversies over its appetite for "authentic," emotionally intense material from the past (Chapters 3 and 4). This chapter turns to where sensibility found its most natural literary home: the novel. You already have the groundwork for this from Part IV, Chapter 7 — Richardson's *Clarissa* demonstrated how far a novel could push its reader's sympathetic identification with a suffering heroine, and Eliza Haywood's late fiction, discussed in Part IV, Chapter 9, was already moving toward a calmer, more reflective emotional register. This chapter's three writers take that inheritance and build a recognizable new genre from it: the **novel of sensibility**, structured less around tight, causally connected plots than around a sequence of emotionally charged scenes, designed to make the reader feel intensely alongside the characters.

It's worth pausing on a pattern in this chapter's cast of writers: Laurence Sterne and Oliver Goldsmith were both born in Ireland to families of English descent — Anglo-Irish, exactly like Jonathan Swift, whom you studied in Part IV, Chapter 5. Sensibility, as a literary mode, drew heavily on Anglo-Irish writers working within, and sometimes gently against, English literary culture, a pattern worth keeping in mind as you move toward Part X's dedicated study of Irish literature.

## Literary Movements

The **novel of sensibility** favors emotional intensity over plot mechanics. Where a novel like Fielding's *Tom Jones* (Part IV, Chapter 7) builds toward a tightly engineered resolution, sensibility fiction often unfolds as a loosely connected sequence of scenes, each designed to provoke a strong sympathetic response — tears, tenderness, moral reflection — more or less independent of what comes before or after. Two structural techniques recur across this chapter's writers. The first is **digression**: a willingness to interrupt or delay the story itself, trusting that feeling, not forward plot momentum, is the real point. Sterne takes this furthest of anyone in English literature — his narrator Tristram isn't even born until the novel's third volume. The second is the **fragmentary or "found" frame device**: presenting a narrative as an incomplete, damaged, or interrupted document, which lets a sentimental novel skip past ordinary narrative connective tissue and move straight to its most emotionally charged moments. Mackenzie's *The Man of Feeling*, discussed below, uses exactly this device.

This chapter also introduces an important internal tension within sensibility culture itself, through Oliver Goldsmith. Even as sensibility dominated fiction, some writers pushed back specifically against its presence on the *stage*. You may recall from Part IV, Chapter 2, that Jeremy Collier's 1698 attack on Restoration comedy helped push English drama toward a gentler, more moralized "sentimental comedy," further developed by Steele's *The Conscious Lovers* (Part IV, Chapter 4). By the 1770s, Goldsmith judged that this sentimental comic tradition had gone stale — replacing genuine laughter with tearful moral uplift — and argued, in his 1773 essay contrasting "laughing" and "sentimental" comedy, for a return to comedy that actually makes an audience laugh. *She Stoops to Conquer* (1773), discussed below, is his own practical demonstration of that argument. Holding Goldsmith's sentimental novel and his anti-sentimental drama together in your mind is the single best way to understand that this period's culture of feeling was never as uniform or unchallenged as it might first appear.

## Author Profiles

**Laurence Sterne** (1713–1768), an Anglo-Irish Church of England clergyman who held a living in Yorkshire, wrote one of the most formally radical novels in the English language: *The Life and Opinions of Tristram Shandy, Gentleman* (1759–1767), published across nine volumes. The novel constantly interrupts its own attempt to narrate Tristram's life — digression follows digression, a black page mourns a character's death, a marbled page defies straightforward reading, and diagrams stand in for the plot's wandering shape — turning the impossibility of ever telling a life story in a straight line into the book's real subject. Beneath this comic, self-aware playfulness, though, *Tristram Shandy* contains genuinely celebrated moments of sensibility, above all in the character of Tristram's uncle, Toby Shandy, a gentle, wounded former soldier whose compassion extends even to a fly he refuses to kill — "This world surely is wide enough to hold both thee and me." Sterne's second major work, *A Sentimental Journey Through France and Italy* (1768), published shortly before his death, narrates a journey through France almost entirely through its narrator's emotional responses to small encounters — a caged starling repeating "I can't get out," a dying donkey, a chance meeting with a monk — deliberately replacing conventional travel writing's catalogue of sights with a catalogue of feelings, and giving the word "sentimental" itself the popular currency you first met in Chapter 1.

**Henry Mackenzie** (1745–1831) wrote *The Man of Feeling* (1771), often treated as the purest, most concentrated expression of the entire novel of sensibility. Its hero, Harley, is so overwhelmed by tenderness and sympathy for the suffering he encounters that he proves almost incapable of surviving contact with a harsh, self-interested world, and the novel ends with his death, apparently brought on by the sheer intensity of his own feeling — a literal enactment of sensibility's own physiological vocabulary from Chapter 1, where excessive nervous sensitivity was understood as a genuine bodily vulnerability, not just a metaphor. Mackenzie frames the novel as a damaged, incomplete manuscript, supposedly rescued from a curate who had been using pages of it as gun-wadding — a fragmentary device that lets the novel move directly between its most emotionally charged scenes without conventional narrative connection. Mackenzie went on to a long career as an Edinburgh man of letters, and in 1786 used his periodical *The Lounger* to give Robert Burns, whom you will meet in Chapter 7, one of his most important early public endorsements.

**Oliver Goldsmith** (1728–1774), another Anglo-Irish writer and an original member of Samuel Johnson's Club (Part IV, Chapter 8), worked across an unusually wide range of genres. His novel, *The Vicar of Wakefield* (1766), follows the good-hearted, somewhat naive clergyman Dr. Charles Primrose and his family through a sequence of genuine misfortunes — lost fortune, a daughter's seduction, imprisonment, a house fire — toward an eventual, providentially engineered happy resolution, blending real sentimental pathos with gentle comic irony; the novel became hugely popular across Europe, admired by Johann Wolfgang von Goethe, whom you already met responding just as enthusiastically to Ossian in Chapter 3. Goldsmith's long poem *The Deserted Village* (1770) mourns the destruction of a traditional rural community, displaced by agricultural enclosure and the growing wealth of large estates — "Ill fares the land, to hastening ills a prey, / Where wealth accumulates, and men decay" — combining graveyard poetry's melancholy tone from Chapter 2 with genuine social and economic critique. And in *She Stoops to Conquer* (1773), Goldsmith put his own anti-sentimental theory of comedy into practice, writing a genuinely funny play of mistaken identity as a deliberate corrective to what he saw as sentimental comedy's exhausted moralizing.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Tristram Shandy* (1759–1767).** Read this less as a conventional novel than as an extended comic meditation on why no life can ever actually be told in a straight line — every attempt at linear narrative keeps colliding with memory, association, and digression, and Sterne treats that collision as the real, honest shape of human experience rather than a failure of storytelling.
- ***A Sentimental Journey* (1768).** The narrator Yorick's small, intense emotional encounters — with the caged starling above all — deliberately prioritize feeling over factual observation, and the starling's trapped, repeated cry has often been read as an implicit commentary on captivity and, more specifically, the era's growing unease about slavery, discussed in Chapter 1.
- ***The Man of Feeling* (1771).** Harley's fatal excess of sympathy raises a genuine interpretive question, addressed fully below: is Mackenzie holding Harley up as sensibility's ideal, or gently suggesting that feeling this intensely is itself a kind of vulnerability the world will not forgive?
- ***The Vicar of Wakefield* (1766).** The novel's cascading misfortunes, followed by a resolution most readers experience as almost too neatly providential, makes it a useful test case for how far sentimental fiction is willing to let suffering stand unresolved before restoring order and reward.
- ***The Deserted Village* (1770).** Goldsmith's lament for a vanishing rural world combines graveyard poetry's melancholy, from Chapter 2, with a specific economic argument about enclosure and rural displacement — proof that sensibility's emotional vocabulary could serve genuine social critique, not only private feeling.
- ***She Stoops to Conquer* (1773).** Goldsmith's comedy of mistaken identity deliberately avoids sentimental comedy's moral uplift in favor of genuine laughter, putting into dramatic practice the argument of his own 1773 essay and pushing directly back against the sentimental dramatic tradition traced from Part IV, Chapters 2 and 4.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is *Tristram Shandy* sincerely sentimental, or is it quietly parodying sensibility even while using its techniques?** Uncle Toby's tenderness is genuinely moving, and generations of readers have taken it at face value — but Sterne's constant comic self-awareness, and his willingness to mock his own narrator's digressive excesses, leaves real room to read the novel as simultaneously celebrating and gently satirizing sensibility's own conventions. Most modern critics resist choosing one reading over the other, treating the tension itself as the novel's real achievement.
- **Does *The Man of Feeling* endorse or critique its hero's excessive sensitivity?** Harley's death can be read as sensibility's ultimate tribute to itself — a hero too pure and tender to survive a corrupt world — or as a quieter warning that feeling this intensely, without any capacity for practical self-protection, is genuinely dangerous. Mackenzie never fully resolves the question, which is part of why the novel has remained a central, debated text for understanding sensibility's own limits.
- **What does Goldsmith's internal contradiction reveal about sensibility as a cultural value?** A writer who produced one of sensibility's most beloved sentimental novels and, within a few years, one of the era's sharpest arguments against sentimental drama complicates any simple picture of the period as uniformly devoted to feeling. Goldsmith's career suggests that sensibility's value was always genre-specific and contested, not a single, unchallenged cultural consensus.
- **Does *The Vicar of Wakefield*'s happy ending undercut its earlier suffering?** Some critics read the novel's providential resolution as sentimentally satisfying, proof that virtue is eventually rewarded; others find it artistically weaker than the novel's genuinely painful middle chapters, arguing that the neat resolution lets both the characters and the reader off too easily.

## Chapter Summary

Building on Richardson's and Haywood's sentimental fiction from Part IV, this chapter's three writers gave sensibility its fullest novelistic expression, and each did so differently. Sterne's *Tristram Shandy* (1759–1767) turned digression itself into a comic meditation on the impossibility of linear life-narrative, while still finding room for genuinely celebrated sentimental moments in Uncle Toby, and his *A Sentimental Journey* (1768) gave the word "sentimental" its lasting popular meaning. Mackenzie's *The Man of Feeling* (1771) pushed sensibility to its logical, fatal extreme, its hero destroyed by the very tenderness the age prized. Goldsmith's *The Vicar of Wakefield* (1766) and *The Deserted Village* (1770) embraced sensibility's emotional register in fiction and poetry, even as his *She Stoops to Conquer* (1773) and its accompanying essay argued forcefully against sentimental drama, proving that this period's devotion to feeling was neither uniform nor unchallenged. Mackenzie's later championing of Robert Burns points directly toward Chapter 7, which closes Part V with the vernacular Scottish tradition.

## Glossary

- **Novel of sensibility** — a genre of eighteenth-century fiction structured around emotionally charged scenes designed to provoke the reader's sympathy, often at the expense of tight plot construction.
- **Digression** — the technique, used most radically by Sterne, of interrupting or delaying a narrative's forward movement, treating association and memory as more truthful than linear storytelling.
- **Fragmentary (found manuscript) frame** — a narrative device presenting a story as an incomplete or damaged document, allowing a novel to move directly between its most emotionally intense scenes.
- **Laughing comedy** — Goldsmith's term, from his 1773 essay, for comedy designed to provoke genuine laughter, offered as a corrective to sentimental comedy's moralizing.
