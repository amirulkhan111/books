# Master Timeline: All Periods at a Glance

This single chart is deliberately compact — a fold-out overview you can return to at any point while reading, to see where the chapter in front of you sits against the whole eleven-century sweep of English literature. It is not exhaustive: for every date this book actually discusses, in full chronological order and without omission, see Appendix B's Master Chronological Timeline, which merges over 1,300 entries drawn from every chapter's own Timeline table. This chart's job is different — orientation at a single glance, not complete reference.

---

## The Nine Chronological Periods (Parts I–IX)

| Part | Period | Approximate Dates | Defining Historical Backdrop | A Few Names You'll Meet |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | Old English (Anglo-Saxon) | c. 450–1066 | Germanic settlement, conversion to Christianity, Viking invasions, the Norman Conquest | *Beowulf*, Cædmon, Cynewulf, Alfred the Great |
| II | Middle English | 1066–1500 | Norman rule and trilingual culture, the Hundred Years' War, the Black Death, the arrival of print | Chaucer, Langland, Gower, Julian of Norwich, Caxton |
| III | The Renaissance / Early Modern | 1500–1660 | The Reformation, the Tudor and Stuart courts, the rise of the public playhouse, civil war and regicide | Spenser, Marlowe, Shakespeare, Donne, Milton |
| IV | The Restoration and Augustan Age | 1660–1750 | The monarchy restored, the Royal Society and the new science, the rise of the periodical and the novel | Dryden, Swift, Pope, Defoe, Behn |
| V | The Age of Sensibility | 1750–1798 | Enlightenment rationalism giving way to feeling and sympathy as literary values, ahead of Romanticism proper | Gray, Chatterton, Sterne, Blake, Burns |
| VI | The Romantic Period | 1798–1837 | The French Revolution's hope and disillusionment, industrialization's first shocks | Wordsworth, Coleridge, Byron, Shelley, Keats, Austen |
| VII | The Victorian Age | 1837–1901 | Industrial Britain, imperial expansion, the crisis of religious faith after Darwin | Tennyson, the Brontës, George Eliot, Dickens, Hardy, Wilde |
| VIII | The Modern Period | 1901–1945 | The First World War, Freud and Einstein unsettling old certainties, formal experiment | Yeats, Eliot, Woolf, Joyce, Lawrence |
| IX | Postwar, Postmodern, and Contemporary British Literature | 1945–present | The welfare state, decolonization, the Windrush generation, postmodern uncertainty | Larkin, Beckett, Golding, Heaney, Ishiguro |

## Two Parts That Run Alongside This Chronology, Not Within It

**Part X — Postcolonial and Global Anglophone Literatures** does not occupy one slot on this chart because its writers span the entire period the chart covers — Swift belongs to Part IV's Augustan Age and to Part X's Irish tradition at once — while its distinct national literatures (Irish, Scottish, Welsh, Indian, African, Caribbean, Australian, Canadian, and diaspora writing) each have their own internal chronologies, most reaching full flower only in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. Part X's own opening chapter explains this placement choice, and its tensions, directly.

**Part XI — Literary Criticism and Theory** runs even further back and stays open at the front: it begins with Aristotle's *Poetics* (c. 335 BCE), long before Part I's Old English poetry, and continues past Part IX's contemporary literature into critical movements — ecocriticism, the digital humanities — still actively developing today. Criticism is the history of how readers have argued about literature across this entire span, so it cannot be folded into any single chronological slot beside the literature it discusses.

### How to use this chart

Find the period you're currently reading about, glance left to see roughly what else was happening in England (or the wider Anglophone world) at the same time, and glance right for two or three names to anchor the period in memory before you meet them properly. When a chapter mentions a writer or event from a different period — and this book's chapters cross-reference each other constantly — this chart is the fastest way to place that reference in time before you look up the fuller detail in Appendix B or in the chapter itself.
