# Chapter 1: The Norman Conquest and the Trilingual Literary Culture of Medieval England

*Part II: Middle English Literature, 1066–1500*

---

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Explain the immediate linguistic and institutional consequences of the Norman Conquest of 1066 for written culture in England.
2. Describe the "trilingual" division of function among Latin, Anglo-Norman French, and English in medieval England, and how that division shifted over four centuries.
3. Account for the near-disappearance of English from the written literary record in the century or so after the Conquest, and identify the earliest texts marking its gradual return.
4. Analyze the significance of the Statute (Pleading in English Act) of 1362 as a landmark in English's recovery of official and legal status.
5. Situate Anglo-Norman literature (Marie de France, Wace) and its direct role in shaping early Middle English literary works such as Layamon's *Brut*.

---

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The Norman Conquest, following William of Normandy's victory at the Battle of Hastings and Edward the Confessor's death without an undisputed heir (see Chapter 1, Part I), replaced the Anglo-Saxon aristocracy and higher clergy with a French-speaking Norman ruling class within a generation. William's commissioning of the **Domesday Book** (surveyed 1086), a comprehensive land and resource survey compiled in Latin (with occasional untranslated English or Norman terms where no Latin equivalent existed), exemplifies the new administrative order: Latin remained the prestige language of record-keeping and the Church, while Anglo-Norman French became the language of the court, the aristocracy, and increasingly of law and government business, displacing English from the domains of power it had substantially occupied under the Alfredian and later Anglo-Saxon kings (Part I, Chapter 6).

English did not disappear as a spoken language — it remained the majority mother tongue of the common people throughout the period — but its near-total exclusion from prestigious written production for roughly a century and a half after the Conquest means that surviving written evidence of English in this period is comparatively sparse, a genuine "eclipse" in the written record rather than in everyday speech.

## Literary Movements

Medieval England's literary culture from 1066 to roughly 1500 is best understood not as a single national tradition but as a **trilingual system**, in which Latin, Anglo-Norman French, and English coexisted with shifting and overlapping domains of use: Latin for the Church, formal scholarship, and enduring record; Anglo-Norman French for the court, aristocratic literary culture, and (for a time) law; and English as the spoken vernacular of the majority, gradually reclaiming written literary and official status across the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries. Writers, scribes, and audiences frequently moved between all three languages, and single texts (such as the devotional guide *Ancrene Wisse*) were sometimes produced or circulated in more than one language version. This chapter's central narrative arc is English's slow recovery of literary prestige, culminating in the fourteenth-century flowering treated in the remaining chapters of this Part.

## Author Profiles

**William the Conqueror** (r. 1066–1087) — Duke of Normandy who claimed and won the English throne at Hastings; his reign's administrative Latinization (epitomized by the Domesday Book) and installation of a French-speaking aristocracy established the linguistic conditions this chapter describes.

**Geoffrey of Monmouth** (c. 1095–c. 1155) — Cleric, probably of Welsh or Breton background, whose Latin *Historia Regum Britanniae* (c. 1136) constructed an influential, largely legendary history of Britain from its supposed Trojan founding through the reign of King Arthur; though written in Latin, it became the direct or indirect source for the vernacular "Matter of Britain" tradition, via Wace and then Layamon.

**Wace** (c. 1110–after 1174) — Anglo-Norman poet, born in Jersey, whose *Roman de Brut* (c. 1155) versified and expanded Geoffrey of Monmouth's *Historia* in Anglo-Norman French, introducing narrative elements (including the Round Table motif) later carried into English by Layamon.

**Marie de France** (fl. late 12th century) — Poet, probably French-born but active in England, generally regarded as the first known woman to write francophone verse; author of twelve *Lais*, short narrative poems in Anglo-Norman French drawing on (and helping establish) the "Breton lai" genre, probably composed c. 1155–1170 and dedicated to a "noble king," likely Henry II.

**Orm** — Augustinian canon, probably of south Lincolnshire, author of the *Ormulum*, an extensive early Middle English verse commentary on the Gospel readings of the liturgical year; notable for its unusually consistent, phonetically motivated spelling system, valuable to modern scholars for reconstructing early Middle English pronunciation.

**Layamon** (fl. c. 1200) — English priest of Areley Kings, Worcestershire, author of the *Brut*, a roughly 16,000-line Middle English poem largely based on Wace's *Roman de Brut* (itself based on Geoffrey of Monmouth), narrating a legendary history of Britain; written substantially in the older alliterative verse tradition inherited from Old English (Part I, Chapter 2) blended with rhyme, it is generally regarded as the first extended work of history in English since the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*, and the first English-language treatment of the Arthurian "Matter of Britain."

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Domesday Book** (1086) — Not a literary work, but the defining administrative document of the new Latinate, French-governed order; its incidental preservation of untranslated English and Norman terms offers linguistic evidence of the earliest phase of language contact following the Conquest.
- ***Historia Regum Britanniae*** (Geoffrey of Monmouth) — Though composed in Latin and of dubious historical reliability, this text's legendary material (Trojan Brutus as founder of Britain, Lear, Arthur) became foundational for English national mythology across three centuries of subsequent vernacular adaptation.
- ***Lais*** (Marie de France) — Anglo-Norman short verse narratives combining Celtic ("Breton") folkloric material with courtly romance sensibility; though composed in French, they were produced and read within England and are integral to medieval England's literary culture even though not written in English.
- ***Ormulum*** (Orm) — Valuable primarily as linguistic and phonological evidence of very early Middle English rather than for literary accomplishment; its systematic spelling reforms reflect a conscious effort at clarity for oral delivery/preaching.
- ***Brut*** (Layamon) — The pivotal text of this chapter: an English-language adaptation of a French adaptation of a Latin legendary history, Layamon's *Brut* demonstrates both the persistence of Old English alliterative verse technique (Part I, Chapter 2) into the early Middle English period and English's re-emergence as a vehicle for ambitious historical/legendary narrative after roughly a century and a half of comparative written silence.

## Critical Perspectives

- **The "eclipse" of English: absence or invisibility?** Older literary histories sometimes described post-Conquest English literature as having effectively ceased for a century or more. More recent scholarship emphasizes that this reflects the bias of surviving written and elite-patronized evidence rather than the disappearance of English as a living, spoken vernacular; English simply lost access, for a time, to the resources (aristocratic patronage, prestige institutions) that produced durable written literary record.
- **Multilingualism as the norm, not the exception.** Current scholarship (e.g., work under the heading "language and culture in medieval Britain") increasingly resists treating Latin, French, and English as sequential, mutually exclusive phases, emphasizing instead their sustained, overlapping coexistence and the fluid multilingual competence of many medieval English writers, readers, and institutions across three full centuries.
- **The political significance of the 1362 Statute.** The Pleading in English Act's own stated justification — that ordinary litigants could not understand proceedings conducted in French — reflects both declining French fluency among the English-born population over time and growing English linguistic self-assertion, though implementation was gradual and Law French persisted in restricted legal usage for centuries afterward.
- **Layamon's *Brut* as transitional text.** Critics read Layamon's blending of inherited Old English alliterative technique with rhyme, and his adaptation of French-derived Arthurian material into English, as emblematic of this chapter's larger story: English literary culture did not simply resume where Old English left off, but re-emerged transformed by nearly a century and a half of French and Latin literary contact.

## Chapter Summary

The Norman Conquest of 1066 inaugurated nearly four centuries of trilingual literary culture in England, in which Latin, Anglo-Norman French, and English occupied shifting, overlapping domains of prestige and use. English, though it remained the majority spoken language throughout, was largely displaced from written literary and administrative production for a century and a half, re-emerging gradually through texts such as the *Ormulum* and, decisively, Layamon's *Brut* (c. 1200), which adapted Wace's Anglo-Norman verse (itself derived from Geoffrey of Monmouth's Latin *Historia Regum Britanniae*) into English while drawing on the older Old English alliterative tradition. The 1362 Statute requiring English for legal pleading marks a significant institutional milestone in this recovery, setting the stage for the major English-language literary achievements of Chaucer, Langland, and the Gawain-poet studied in the remaining chapters of this Part.

## Glossary

- **Anglo-Norman** — the variety of Old French used in England following the Norman Conquest, the prestige language of the court and aristocracy for several centuries.
- **Breton lai** — a short narrative verse genre, associated with Celtic ("Breton") folkloric material, exemplified by Marie de France.
- **Domesday Book** — the Latin-language land and resource survey of England commissioned by William the Conqueror, completed 1086.
- **"Matter of Britain"** — the legendary historical/Arthurian material descending from Geoffrey of Monmouth's *Historia Regum Britanniae*, transmitted through Wace to Layamon and beyond.
- **Trilingualism (medieval England)** — the coexistence of Latin, Anglo-Norman French, and English as the three literary/administrative languages of medieval England, c. 1066–c. 1500.

## Discussion Questions

1. In what ways does the "eclipse" of written English after 1066 reflect a loss of prestige and patronage rather than a disappearance of the spoken language?
2. How does the transmission chain from Geoffrey of Monmouth (Latin) to Wace (Anglo-Norman) to Layamon (English) illustrate the trilingual literary culture of this period?
3. What does the 1362 Statute's stated rationale reveal about the practical, as well as symbolic, stakes of language choice in medieval law?
4. Why might Marie de France's *Lais*, though written in French, be considered part of medieval England's literary culture?
5. How does Layamon's blending of Old English alliterative technique with post-Conquest French-derived narrative material complicate a simple "before and after" account of the Norman Conquest's literary impact?
