# Chapter 1: The Norman Conquest and the Trilingual Literary Culture of Medieval England

*Part II: Middle English Literature, 1066–1500*

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Explain the immediate linguistic and institutional consequences of the Norman Conquest of 1066 for written culture in England.
2. Describe the "trilingual" division of function among Latin, Anglo-Norman French, and English in medieval England, and how that division shifted over four centuries.
3. Account for the near-disappearance of English from the written literary record in the century or so after the Conquest, and identify the earliest texts marking its gradual return.
4. Analyze the significance of the Statute (Pleading in English Act) of 1362 as a landmark in English's recovery of official and legal status.
5. Situate Anglo-Norman literature (Marie de France, Wace) and its direct role in shaping early Middle English literary works such as Layamon's *Brut*.

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Welcome to Part II. Part I ended, in its final chapter, at the year 1066 — the Norman Conquest. This chapter picks up exactly there, because that one event changes almost everything about how English literature gets written for the next four centuries.

Here's what happened. William of Normandy defeated the English king at the Battle of Hastings (the succession crisis that made this possible was set up by Edward the Confessor's death, which you may remember from Part I, Chapter 1) and, within a generation, replaced England's Anglo-Saxon aristocracy and senior clergy with a French-speaking Norman ruling class. You can see the new order stamped directly onto a real document: the **Domesday Book**, a huge land and resource survey William commissioned in 1086, written in Latin, with only the occasional English or Norman word slipped in where no Latin equivalent existed. That one book tells you the whole new language hierarchy at a glance. Latin stayed the prestige language of record-keeping and the Church. Anglo-Norman French became the language of the royal court, the aristocracy, and, increasingly, of law and government. And English — which had been the language of kings and administration under Alfred and his successors, as you saw in Part I, Chapter 6 — was pushed out of the corridors of power almost overnight.

Don't mistake this for English disappearing. It didn't. Ordinary people kept speaking English as their first language throughout this whole period — this is a story about *written*, prestigious literature, not everyday speech. What genuinely did happen for roughly a century and a half after the Conquest is that English almost stopped being *written down* for serious purposes, which means the surviving written evidence from this stretch is thin. Historians sometimes call this the "eclipse" of English — a useful phrase, as long as you remember it describes the page, not the street.

## Literary Movements

Resist the temptation to think of medieval English literature, from 1066 to about 1500, as one single national tradition — it wasn't. It's better understood as a **trilingual system**: Latin, Anglo-Norman French, and English running alongside each other, each with its own job, and those jobs shifting over time. Latin belonged to the Church, formal scholarship, and permanent record-keeping. Anglo-Norman French belonged to the royal court, aristocratic literary culture, and, for a while, the law. English belonged to ordinary spoken life, and only gradually clawed back written and official status across the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries. Writers and scribes routinely moved between all three languages — some texts, like the devotional guide *Ancrene Wisse*, even circulated in more than one language version. Keep this trilingual picture in mind as the organizing idea of this whole chapter: it's the story of English slowly working its way back to literary prestige, a story that reaches its high point in the fourteenth-century writers covered in the rest of this Part.

## Author Profiles

This chapter's cast spans all three languages you've just read about — a king, a legendary historian, two Anglo-Norman French poets, and two of the earliest writers to bring ambitious literature back into English.

**William the Conqueror** (r. 1066–1087) — Duke of Normandy who claimed and won the English throne at Hastings; his reign's administrative Latinization (epitomized by the Domesday Book) and installation of a French-speaking aristocracy established the linguistic conditions this chapter describes.

**Geoffrey of Monmouth** (c. 1095–c. 1155) — Cleric, probably of Welsh or Breton background, whose Latin *Historia Regum Britanniae* (c. 1136) constructed an influential, largely legendary history of Britain from its supposed Trojan founding through the reign of King Arthur; though written in Latin, it became the direct or indirect source for the vernacular "Matter of Britain" tradition, via Wace and then Layamon.

**Wace** (c. 1110–after 1174) — Anglo-Norman poet, born in Jersey, whose *Roman de Brut* (c. 1155) versified and expanded Geoffrey of Monmouth's *Historia* in Anglo-Norman French, introducing narrative elements (including the Round Table motif) later carried into English by Layamon.

**Marie de France** (fl. late 12th century) — Poet, probably French-born but active in England, generally regarded as the first known woman to write francophone verse; author of twelve *Lais*, short narrative poems in Anglo-Norman French drawing on (and helping establish) the "Breton lai" genre, probably composed c. 1155–1170 and dedicated to a "noble king," likely Henry II.

**Orm** — Augustinian canon, probably of south Lincolnshire, author of the *Ormulum*, an extensive early Middle English verse commentary on the Gospel readings of the liturgical year; notable for its unusually consistent, phonetically motivated spelling system, valuable to modern scholars for reconstructing early Middle English pronunciation.

**Layamon** (fl. c. 1200) — English priest of Areley Kings, Worcestershire, author of the *Brut*, a roughly 16,000-line Middle English poem largely based on Wace's *Roman de Brut* (itself based on Geoffrey of Monmouth), narrating a legendary history of Britain; written substantially in the older alliterative verse tradition inherited from Old English (Part I, Chapter 2) blended with rhyme, it is generally regarded as the first extended work of history in English since the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*, and the first English-language treatment of the Arthurian "Matter of Britain."

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **The Domesday Book (1086)**. Not a literary work — but the single document that most clearly displays the new order this chapter describes. Even its rare, incidental use of untranslated English or Norman words gives linguists a snapshot of the earliest contact between these languages after the Conquest.
- ***Historia Regum Britanniae*** (Geoffrey of Monmouth). Written in Latin, and historically unreliable in most of its detail, this text nonetheless became enormously important, because its legendary material — the Trojan Brutus as Britain's founder, King Lear, King Arthur — went on to shape English national mythology for the next three centuries, as it got adapted and readapted into other languages.
- ***Lais*** (Marie de France). Short verse narratives in Anglo-Norman French, blending Celtic ("Breton") folklore with the sensibility of courtly romance. They weren't written in English, but they were written and read in England, which is exactly why this chapter insists on treating medieval England's literary culture as trilingual rather than simply "English literature plus some foreign imports."
- ***Ormulum*** (Orm). Don't expect literary brilliance here — its value is different. Orm designed a remarkably consistent spelling system to guide oral delivery, and that consistency makes the *Ormulum* an unusually reliable window for modern scholars trying to reconstruct exactly how very early Middle English was pronounced.
- ***Brut*** (Layamon). This is the chapter's pivotal text, so pay close attention to its layered history: an English poem, adapting a French poem, adapting a Latin history. Layamon's *Brut* shows you two things happening at once — the survival of Old English alliterative verse technique (which you learned in Part I, Chapter 2) into this new period, and English re-establishing itself as a language capable of ambitious historical storytelling, after roughly a century and a half of comparative written silence.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Did English literature actually stop, or just go quiet on the page?** Older literary histories sometimes claimed post-Conquest English literature effectively ceased for a century or more. More recent scholarship pushes back on that: what we're really seeing is a bias in which evidence survives and who had the resources to write things down, not the disappearance of English as a living spoken language. English didn't vanish — it simply lost, for a while, access to the aristocratic patronage and prestige institutions that produce durable written record.
- **Multilingualism wasn't the exception — it was the norm.** It's tempting to imagine Latin, French, and English as three neat, sequential phases. Current scholarship resists that picture, and for good reason: for three full centuries, these three languages coexisted, overlapped, and were often handled fluently by the very same writers, readers, and institutions.
- **Why did the 1362 Statute matter?** The Pleading in English Act justified itself on a very practical ground: ordinary people in court couldn't understand proceedings conducted in French. That single justification tells you two things at once — French fluency among English-born people was genuinely declining by this point, and English was reasserting itself with real confidence. Don't overstate the change, though: implementation was gradual, and a specialized version of French, called Law French, lingered in restricted legal use for centuries afterward.
- **Layamon's *Brut* as a hinge between two worlds.** Critics read Layamon's blend of old alliterative technique with rhyme, and his translation of French-derived Arthurian material into English, as a perfect example of this chapter's larger point: English literary culture didn't just pick up where Old English left off. It came back transformed by nearly a century and a half of contact with French and Latin.

## Chapter Summary

The Norman Conquest of 1066 opened nearly four centuries of trilingual literary culture in England, with Latin, Anglo-Norman French, and English each occupying their own shifting share of prestige and use. English stayed the majority spoken language throughout, even while it was pushed out of written literary and administrative production for about a century and a half. Its written comeback happened gradually — through texts like the *Ormulum*, and then decisively through Layamon's *Brut* (c. 1200), which took Wace's Anglo-Norman verse (itself built on Geoffrey of Monmouth's Latin history) and turned it into English, using techniques inherited from Old English poetry along the way. The 1362 Statute requiring English in legal pleading marks a real institutional milestone in that recovery, and it sets up exactly where this Part goes next: the major English-language achievements of Chaucer, Langland, and the Gawain-poet in the chapters ahead.

## Glossary

- **Anglo-Norman** — the variety of Old French used in England following the Norman Conquest, the prestige language of the court and aristocracy for several centuries.
- **Breton lai** — a short narrative verse genre, associated with Celtic ("Breton") folkloric material, exemplified by Marie de France.
- **Domesday Book** — the Latin-language land and resource survey of England commissioned by William the Conqueror, completed 1086.
- **"Matter of Britain"** — the legendary historical/Arthurian material descending from Geoffrey of Monmouth's *Historia Regum Britanniae*, transmitted through Wace to Layamon and beyond.
- **Trilingualism (medieval England)** — the coexistence of Latin, Anglo-Norman French, and English as the three literary/administrative languages of medieval England, c. 1066–c. 1500.
