## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Within the trilingual world Chapter 1 introduced you to, one genre came to dominate secular storytelling more than any other: **romance**, a term that, in the medieval sense, has nothing to do with love stories specifically and everything to do with chivalric adventure. It flourished from the twelfth century onward across French, Anglo-Norman, and eventually Middle English.

A twelfth-century French poet named Jean Bodel gave later scholars a handy way to sort all this material: he divided medieval narrative into three "matters" — "that of France, that of Britain, and that of great Rome." The Matter of France covers the Carolingian legends of Charlemagne and Roland. The Matter of Rome draws on classical antiquity. And the Matter of Britain means Arthurian legend, the subject of this chapter. (Modern scholars have since added a fourth category, the "Matter of England," for native English legendary material that doesn't fit neatly into Bodel's original three.)¹

This chapter follows the Matter of Britain to its outstanding Middle English achievement, *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*. That poem belongs to a specific literary movement called the **Alliterative Revival**: a resurgence of alliterative verse, centred in the West Midlands and North of England, running alongside — but formally quite different from — the rhymed, French-influenced verse tradition you'll meet through Chaucer's London-based poetry in Chapter 3.

## Literary Movements

What does **medieval romance** actually look like on the page? Expect chivalric adventure, courtly love, the marvellous or supernatural, and — very often — a hero whose apparently perfect virtue gets tested until a crack shows. Most romance was written in verse, though by Malory's time, as you'll see below, it could be prose too. Within the Matter of Britain specifically, one French writer set the template everyone else worked from: **Chrétien de Troyes**, in the later twelfth century, established conventions — individual knightly quests, the fellowship of the Round Table, courtly love, the Grail — that English writers would keep adapting for centuries.

The **Alliterative Revival** is the name scholars give to a fourteenth-century resurgence of alliterative verse, centred in northern and western England. Notice the word "revival": this verse form is a direct descendant of the Old English tradition you learned in Part I, Chapter 2, even though its vocabulary, subject matter, and precise metrical rules had all changed a great deal by this point. *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight* is the movement's outstanding achievement, alongside William Langland's *Piers Plowman* (which you'll meet in Chapter 4) and various other alliterative romances and moral poems. Think of the Alliterative Revival as a regional, formally distinct alternative to the rhymed, French-influenced verse — Chaucer's, above all — that would go on to dominate later English poetry.

## Author Profiles

This chapter's profiles open with a French poet, not an English one — because, as you've just seen, nearly everything English in this tradition answers to him first.

**Chrétien de Troyes** (fl. 1170s–1180s) was French, not English, but his influence on the English Arthurian tradition is decisive all the same. His pioneering verse romances established many of the conventions that later Arthurian narrative simply takes for granted — the courtly-love ethic, and, in his unfinished *Perceval*, the earliest known literary treatment of the Grail quest.

**The Gawain-poet**, also called the **Pearl-poet**, is anonymous — we don't know this writer's identity at all. What we do know: this poet wrote *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*, *Pearl*, *Cleanness* (sometimes called *Purity*), and *Patience*, all preserved uniquely in the British Library's Cotton Nero A.x manuscript, produced in the North West Midlands around 1400. Dialect and stylistic evidence link all four poems to a single author — a reasonable inference, though not an external certainty — and nothing else about this poet's life has come down to us.

**Thomas Malory** (c. 1415/18–1471) is traditionally identified as a Warwickshire knight with a chequered legal and political career that included periods of imprisonment, though scholars still dispute exactly which historical Thomas Malory is meant. He compiled *Le Morte d'Arthur*, a lengthy English prose synthesis of French and English Arthurian material, completed around 1469–70, drawing extensively on French prose romance sources as well as English alliterative and stanzaic Arthurian poems.

**William Caxton** (c. 1422–1491), England's first printer, whom you'll meet fully in Chapter 9, printed an edition of Malory's work in 1485 — abridging it, reorganizing it into 21 books and 506 chapters, and adding chapter summaries. That edition became the standard text of *Le Morte d'Arthur* until the twentieth century, when the independent Winchester Manuscript was discovered in 1934, preserving a text closer to what Malory actually wrote.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Sir Gawain and the Green Knight***. Here's the plot, because it's worth following closely. A mysterious Green Knight rides into Arthur's court at Camelot and offers a strange challenge: any knight may strike him with an axe, as long as that knight agrees to receive an equal blow in return, exactly one year later. Gawain accepts, beheads the Green Knight in one stroke — and the Green Knight simply picks up his own severed head, reminds Gawain of their appointment, and rides off. A year later, Gawain travels to find the Green Chapel for his return blow, and along the way he's given hospitality at the castle of a lord named Bertilak. While Bertilak is out hunting each day, his wife repeatedly tries to seduce Gawain, and the household plays an "exchange of winnings" game: whatever anyone gains during the day, they must hand over that evening. Gawain accepts a magical green girdle from the lady, which she claims will protect him from harm — and then hides it from Bertilak, breaking the exchange agreement. At the poem's climax, it turns out the Green Knight was Bertilak in disguise all along. He spares Gawain's life, but marks him, lightly, for that one failure. What makes the poem so admired is how skilfully it weaves these three strands together — the beheading game, the temptation, the hunting — and how honestly it explores the gap between chivalric perfection as an ideal and human fallibility as a reality.
- **The pentangle**. Gawain's shield carries a five-pointed star, traditionally associated with King Solomon. The poem itself explains what it means: five interlocking sets of virtues (the five senses, the five fingers, the five wounds of Christ, the five joys of the Virgin Mary, and five knightly virtues including brotherly love, courtesy, and piety), all drawn as one continuous, unbroken line — a symbol of "endless" truth and integrity. Keep this symbol in mind as you read the poem, because it becomes the exact standard Gawain's one lapse, hiding the girdle, gets measured against.
- ***Le Morte d'Arthur*** (Malory). This is a huge English prose synthesis of the whole Arthurian story: Arthur's conception, his reign, his knights' adventures, the Grail quest, and finally the kingdom's tragic collapse through the Lancelot–Guinevere affair and Mordred's betrayal. When Caxton printed it in 1485, it became the single most accessible and influential compilation of Arthurian legend in English — which is exactly why so much of what people today think they know about King Arthur ultimately traces back to Malory.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Romance as a "test" of the hero.** Critics commonly read *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight* through romance's characteristic testing structure: an apparently perfect hero is placed under pressure until a genuine, humanizing flaw shows through. What sets this poem apart from many of its contemporaries is how much moral and psychological complexity it brings to that familiar structure.
- **Who actually read Alliterative Revival poetry?** Scholars still debate the social world this poetry came from — was it gentry and lesser aristocratic households in the West Midlands and North, or a more clerical or urban audience? And how did that world relate to the London-centred, French-influenced literary culture around Chaucer? You'll see this comparison develop further in Chapter 4, when we turn to Langland.
- **Was Malory an original artist, or mainly a skilled compiler?** Critics disagree on how much credit Malory deserves as an artist in his own right, as opposed to a talented translator and compiler working from French prose cycles and English verse sources. The 1934 discovery of the Winchester Manuscript reshaped this debate significantly, because it revealed that Caxton's 1485 print had imposed its own book and chapter divisions — divisions that weren't part of Malory's original structure at all.
- **Why do modern translations of the poem look so different from each other?** Compare Tolkien's 1975 translation with Simon Armitage's 2007 version, and you'll see two genuinely different translation philosophies at work: Tolkien leans toward preserving the poem's medieval strangeness, Armitage toward making it feel immediate and contemporary. Both remain widely read, which tells you something important — this six-hundred-year-old poem still has real life in it for modern readers, continuing the pattern of medieval texts' ongoing afterlife you first saw in Chapter 1.

## Chapter Summary

Medieval romance, and the Arthurian "Matter of Britain" within it, travelled a long road before reaching the two great achievements this chapter centres on. It ran from Geoffrey of Monmouth's Latin legendary history, through Wace's Anglo-Norman verse and Chrétien de Troyes's hugely influential French romances, into English for the first time in Layamon's *Brut* (Chapter 1). From there it produced two major, and formally very different, works: the anonymous Gawain-poet's *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight* (c. 1375–1400), the finest achievement of the Alliterative Revival, and Thomas Malory's prose *Le Morte d'Arthur* (c. 1469–70), printed by Caxton in 1485 as one of the first books published in England. *Sir Gawain*'s interweaving of the beheading game, the temptation plot, and the pentangle's symbolism gives you a genuinely sophisticated exploration of chivalric idealism under pressure; Malory's sprawling prose synthesis, meanwhile, fixed the Arthurian legend into the form that would dominate its English-language reception for centuries to come.

## Glossary

- **Alliterative Revival** — the fourteenth-century resurgence of alliterative verse composition in Middle English, chiefly in the West Midlands and North, formally descended from Old English poetic tradition.
- **Bob and wheel** — the five-line rhymed unit (a short "bob" line followed by a four-line "wheel") concluding each stanza of *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*, contrasting with the poem's longer unrhymed alliterative lines.
- **Matter of Britain** — the body of legendary material concerning King Arthur and his knights, per Jean Bodel's three-matter classification of medieval narrative.
- **Pentangle** — the five-pointed star symbol on Gawain's shield, representing interlocking sets of chivalric and religious virtues.
- **Romance (medieval)** — a narrative genre of chivalric adventure, courtly love, and the marvellous, dominant in medieval French and English secular literature.
