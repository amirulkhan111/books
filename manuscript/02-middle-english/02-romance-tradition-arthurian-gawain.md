## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Medieval romance emerged as the dominant secular narrative genre of aristocratic and (increasingly) middle-class literary culture across the trilingual world described in Chapter 1, flourishing from the twelfth century onward in French, Anglo-Norman, and eventually Middle English. The twelfth-century French poet Jean Bodel influentially classified medieval narrative material into three "matters" — "that of France, that of Britain, and that of great Rome" — corresponding respectively to the Carolingian *chanson de geste* tradition (Charlemagne and Roland), Arthurian legend, and material drawn from classical antiquity; modern scholars have added a fourth, the "Matter of England," for native English legendary and historical material not easily assigned to the other three.¹ This chapter concentrates on the Matter of Britain and its outstanding Middle English achievement, *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*, produced within the broader **Alliterative Revival** — a resurgence of alliterative verse composition, chiefly in the West Midlands and North of England, running parallel to but formally distinct from the rhymed, syllable-counted verse tradition associated with Chaucer's London-based poetry (Chapter 3).

## Literary Movements

**Medieval romance** as a genre is characterized by narratives of chivalric adventure, courtly love, the marvellous or supernatural, and the testing of an aristocratic hero's virtue, typically in verse (though later, as with Malory, in prose). Within the Matter of Britain, French romance — above all the highly influential works of **Chrétien de Troyes** in the later twelfth century — established narrative and thematic conventions (individual knightly quests, the Round Table fellowship, courtly love, the Grail) that English writers subsequently adapted.

The **Alliterative Revival** denotes the resurgence, chiefly in fourteenth-century northern and western England, of unrhymed (or partly rhymed) alliterative verse composition directly descended in form from the Old English tradition discussed in Part I, Chapter 2, though substantially changed in vocabulary, subject matter, and metrical detail. Major Alliterative Revival works include *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*, William Langland's *Piers Plowman* (Chapter 4), and various other alliterative romances and moral poems; the movement represents a regional and formal alternative to the rhymed, French-influenced verse forms (such as Chaucer's) that came to dominate later English poetic history.

## Author Profiles

**Chrétien de Troyes** (fl. 1170s–1180s) — French poet, not English but decisively influential on the English Arthurian tradition; author of pioneering verse romances that established many enduring conventions of Arthurian narrative, including the courtly-love ethic and (in his unfinished *Perceval*) the earliest known literary treatment of the Grail quest.

**The Gawain-poet (Pearl-poet)** — Anonymous author, identity unknown, of *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*, *Pearl*, *Cleanness* (or *Purity*), and *Patience*, all preserved uniquely in the British Library's Cotton Nero A.x manuscript, produced in the North West Midlands around 1400. Dialect and stylistic evidence link all four poems to a single author, though this remains an inference rather than an external certainty; nothing else is known of the poet's biography.

**Thomas Malory** (c. 1415/18–1471) — English writer, traditionally identified (though not without scholarly dispute over which historical Thomas Malory is meant) as a Warwickshire knight with a chequered legal and political career, including periods of imprisonment; compiled *Le Morte d'Arthur*, a lengthy English prose synthesis of French and English Arthurian material, completed around 1469–70, drawing extensively on French prose romance sources as well as English alliterative and stanzaic Arthurian poems.

**William Caxton** (c. 1422–1491) — England's first printer (treated fully in Chapter 9), whose 1485 edition of Malory's work — abridging, reorganizing into 21 books and 506 chapters, and supplying chapter summaries — became the standard textual basis for *Le Morte d'Arthur* until the twentieth-century discovery of the independent Winchester Manuscript (1934), which preserves a text closer to Malory's original.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*** — Narrates a mysterious Green Knight's challenge at Arthur's Camelot court: he invites any knight to strike him with an axe, on condition of receiving an equal blow a year later. Gawain accepts, beheads the Green Knight (who calmly retrieves his head and reminds Gawain of the appointed return), and a year later travels to find the Green Chapel, along the way accepting hospitality at the castle of Lord Bertilak, whose wife repeatedly tempts him while her husband is hunting — a household in which an "exchange of winnings" game (whatever each gains during the day must be exchanged at night) runs parallel to the hunting scenes. Gawain accepts a magical green girdle from the lady, said to protect against harm, and conceals it from Bertilak, violating the exchange agreement — a moral lapse revealed at the poem's climax when the Green Knight, who is Bertilak in disguise, spares Gawain's life but marks him for this single failure. The poem is widely admired for its sophisticated interweaving of plot strands (the beheading game, the temptation, the hunting scenes), its psychological subtlety, and its exploration of the tension between idealized chivalric perfection and human fallibility.
- **The pentangle** — Gawain's shield bears a five-pointed star, traditionally associated with King Solomon, symbolizing five interlocking sets of virtues (the five senses, five fingers, the five wounds of Christ, the five joys of the Virgin Mary, and five knightly virtues including brotherly love, courtesy, and piety), forming, in the poem's own account, a continuous, unbroken figure signifying "endless" truth and integrity — a symbolic standard against which Gawain's single lapse (concealing the girdle) is measured.
- ***Le Morte d'Arthur*** (Malory) — A comprehensive English prose synthesis narrating Arthur's conception, reign, the adventures of his knights, the Grail quest, and the kingdom's eventual tragic collapse through the Lancelot–Guinevere affair and Mordred's treachery; its 1485 printing by Caxton made it the most widely accessible and influential single compilation of Arthurian legend in English, decisively shaping later English-language treatments of the Matter of Britain.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Genre and the "test" structure of romance.** Critics commonly read *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight* through the lens of the romance genre's characteristic testing structure, in which an ostensibly perfect hero is placed under pressure that exposes a genuine, humanizing flaw — a structure the poem handles with unusual moral and psychological complexity compared to many contemporary romances.
- **The Alliterative Revival's regional and social context.** Scholars debate the precise social milieu that produced and consumed Alliterative Revival poetry — proposals range from gentry and lesser aristocratic households in the West Midlands and North to more broadly clerical or urban audiences — and its relationship to the London-centred, French-influenced literary culture of Chaucer's circle remains a significant point of comparative literary-historical interest (developed further in Chapter 4's discussion of Langland).
- **Malory's sources and originality.** Critics debate the extent to which Malory should be regarded as an original artist versus primarily a skilled compiler and translator of French prose Arthurian cycles and English verse sources; the discovery of the Winchester Manuscript in 1934 substantially reshaped scholarly understanding of Malory's own structuring intentions, since Caxton's 1485 print imposed its own book/chapter divisions not present in that manuscript.
- **Modern translation and reception.** The pairing of J. R. R. Tolkien's 1975 and Simon Armitage's 2007 translations of *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight* is frequently used pedagogically to illustrate contrasting translation philosophies — Tolkien's more "foreignizing," archaizing approach against Armitage's more "domesticating," contemporary idiom — and to demonstrate the poem's continued vitality for modern readers, paralleling this Part's broader theme (from Chapter 1 onward) of medieval English texts' ongoing modern afterlife.

## Chapter Summary

Medieval romance, and specifically the Arthurian "Matter of Britain" within Jean Bodel's three-matter classification, developed from Geoffrey of Monmouth's Latin legendary history through Wace's Anglo-Norman verse and Chrétien de Troyes's highly influential French courtly romances, reaching English first in Layamon's *Brut* (Chapter 1) and culminating, in this chapter, in two major but formally very different achievements: the anonymous Gawain-poet's *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight* (c. 1375–1400), the outstanding product of the fourteenth-century Alliterative Revival, and Thomas Malory's prose *Le Morte d'Arthur* (c. 1469–70), printed by Caxton in 1485 as one of the first books published in England. *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*'s interweaving of the beheading game, the temptation plot, and the pentangle's symbolism produces a psychologically sophisticated exploration of chivalric idealism under pressure, while Malory's comprehensive prose synthesis fixed the Arthurian legend in the form that would dominate its English-language reception for centuries afterward.

## Glossary

- **Alliterative Revival** — the fourteenth-century resurgence of alliterative verse composition in Middle English, chiefly in the West Midlands and North, formally descended from Old English poetic tradition.
- **Bob and wheel** — the five-line rhymed unit (a short "bob" line followed by a four-line "wheel") concluding each stanza of *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*, contrasting with the poem's longer unrhymed alliterative lines.
- **Matter of Britain** — the body of legendary material concerning King Arthur and his knights, per Jean Bodel's three-matter classification of medieval narrative.
- **Pentangle** — the five-pointed star symbol on Gawain's shield, representing interlocking sets of chivalric and religious virtues.
- **Romance (medieval)** — a narrative genre of chivalric adventure, courtly love, and the marvellous, dominant in medieval French and English secular literature.
