# Chapter 9: The Transition to Print — Caxton and the Close of the Medieval Period

*Part II: Middle English Literature, 1066–1500*

---

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Trace William Caxton's career from London mercer to England's first printer, and situate his work within the broader European introduction of movable-type printing.
2. Distinguish the first book printed in English (Bruges, 1473/74) from the first book printed in England and the first dated book printed in England.
3. Explain Caxton's editorial role in publishing earlier Middle English literature (Chaucer, Malory, Gower) and his own translations.
4. Analyze Caxton's Eneydos prologue (1490) as evidence for English dialectal diversity and his own role in linguistic standardization.
5. Assess print technology's significance as a convenient — if partial and retrospectively imposed — marker for the close of the medieval period in English literary history.

---

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

This is the final chapter of Part II, and it closes the whole medieval story with a genuine technological revolution: printing. Movable-type printing was developed by Johannes Gutenberg in Mainz in the 1440s and spread steadily across continental Europe over the following decades, before reaching England through an English merchant named William Caxton, who had spent much of his career doing business in the Low Countries.

Caxton brought printing to England in 1476, and that date happens to sit close to another major turning point, though the two weren't connected by design: in 1485, Henry Tudor won the Battle of Bosworth Field, ending the Wars of the Roses and founding the Tudor dynasty. Historians conventionally use that battle as the political marker closing the English "medieval" period and opening the early modern, Renaissance era you'll study in Part III. Between them, Caxton's press and Bosworth Field give this whole Part its natural closing point: the last quarter of the fifteenth century.

## Literary Movements

Here's an idea worth holding onto for this entire chapter: print didn't invent new literary genres. What it changed was the conditions under which the genres you already know — romance (Malory, Chapter 2), Chaucerian and Gowerian poetry (Chapters 3 and 5), devotional and instructional prose — were produced, circulated, and gradually standardized. Caxton's own substantial output of translations, mostly from French, also continues a tradition you've already met: medieval England's long habit of translating continental sources into English, going back to Chapter 1. What print adds is speed and scale that manuscript copying by hand simply could never match. So hold this chapter's central claim in mind as you read: print didn't create a sharp break in content. It accelerated and extended trends — vernacular literary prestige, dialectal standardization — that had already been building across the two centuries this whole Part covers.

## Author Profiles

**William Caxton** (c. 1422–1491/92) — Kent-born mercer who spent much of his career in Bruges as a successful merchant and, eventually, Governor of the Company of Merchant Adventurers of London, before learning printing (working with the Fleming Colard Mansion) and producing, in 1473/74, his own translation of Raoul Lefèvre's French romance as *The Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye* — the first book printed in the English language. Returning to England, he established the country's first printing press at Westminster in 1476 and, over roughly the last fifteen years of his life, printed or translated some 100-plus books (traditionally cited as around 108, comprising some 87 distinct titles), including Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* (1478; corrected second edition 1483), Gower's *Confessio Amantis*, Malory's *Le Morte d'Arthur* (1485), and the first English *Aesop's Fables* (1484), alongside numerous translations of his own from French sources. His prologues and epilogues, appended to many of his editions, provide valuable direct evidence of his own editorial reasoning, linguistic concerns, and sense of English literary tradition.

**Wynkyn de Worde** (d. 1534) — Continental-born printer, probably brought to England by Caxton around 1481 partly to help meet competition from a rival London press; took over Caxton's workshop following litigation in 1495, relocated it to Fleet Street in 1500 (establishing the area's centuries-long association with the printing trade), and vastly expanded on Caxton's output, ultimately publishing more than 400 books in over 800 editions across a wide range of genres, from religious works (his dominant output) to romance, poetry (including work by John Skelton and Stephen Hawes), and practical household manuals.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***The Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye*** (1473/74). This is Caxton's own translation of a French courtly prose romance by Raoul Lefèvre. It was printed in Bruges, not England, but it still holds a real historical distinction: it's the first book ever printed in the English language, roughly two years before Caxton even set up his Westminster press.
- ***The Dictes or Sayengis of the Philosophres*** (18 November 1477). Translated from French by Anthony Woodville, 2nd Earl Rivers, and printed by Caxton at Westminster, this almost certainly wasn't literally the first thing Caxton printed on English soil — but it is the first English-printed book that carries a definite date, which is exactly why scholars use it as a fixed, reliable point in the early history of English printing.
- **Caxton's editions of Chaucer, Gower, and Malory**. None of these are new literary compositions — but don't underestimate them for that. By choosing which Middle English texts to print, and in Chaucer's case to correct and reprint, Caxton directly shaped which medieval English literature would circulate widely, in stable printed form, for centuries afterward. Measured by long-term influence on which texts survived and how, this may be the single most consequential editorial decision anywhere in this Part's whole literary history.
- **Caxton's prologue to the *Eneydos*** (1490). This prologue preserves one of the most quoted anecdotes in the history of English: a London merchant asks for "eggs" (*egges*), and a Kentish woman doesn't understand him — she only knows the older word "eyren." Caxton uses the story to make a real, practical point: how do you write English intelligibly when readers across the country don't even agree on their words for "eggs"? His own answer, in practice, was to favour the London/Chancery Standard dialect — the same dialect that already carried prestige thanks to Chaucer, as you learned in Chapter 3 — which helped push that dialect further along the road toward becoming standard written English.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Did Caxton really "standardize" English, or just speed something already happening?** Caxton is often popularly credited with single-handedly standardizing English, but most language historians push back on that claim. The London/Chancery dialect was already gaining prestige well before Caxton printed a single book — driven by Chaucer's influence, by royal administration concentrating in London, and by the 1362 Statute of Pleading you met in Chapter 1. Caxton's real contribution was to fix that already-emerging standard in print and spread it at a speed and scale no manuscript scribe could match — not to invent the standard from nothing.
- **Which book was really "first"?** Be precise here, because popular accounts often blur three distinct claims together: the first book printed in the English language (*Recuyell*, Bruges, 1473/74), the first book printed in England at all (uncertain — likely 1476–77, exact identity disputed), and the first *dated* book printed in England (*Dictes or Sayengis*, 1477). This same lesson in precision applies elsewhere in this book too — think back to the Cædmon and *Beowulf* dating questions in Part I.
- **Was Caxton just a technician, or an active shaper of the canon?** Because Caxton exercised genuine editorial judgment — correcting texts, writing prefaces, deciding what to print and reprint — modern scholars increasingly treat him as more than a technological pioneer. He actively shaped which parts of medieval English literature got carried forward into later centuries, a role with consequences comparable to the Winchester and Ellesmere manuscript compilers you met back in Chapters 2 and 3.
- **Is 1485 a real dividing line, or just a convenient one?** Historians generally acknowledge that Bosworth Field (1485) is a convenient marker rather than a truly decisive one. Literary and linguistic change kept moving gradually and unevenly across this transition, and the romance, religious prose, and popular ballads you've studied throughout this Part didn't stop existing in 1485 — they carried on well into, and beyond, the Tudor period you'll meet in Part III.

## Chapter Summary

William Caxton brought printing to England — starting with his 1473/74 Bruges translation *The Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye*, the first book ever printed in English, and continuing through his 1476 Westminster press — and in doing so, he transformed the conditions under which everything you've studied in this Part reached its readers. His most consequential act was editorial: his editions of Chaucer, Gower, and Malory determined which medieval English texts would survive into wide circulation at all. His 1490 *Eneydos* prologue's "eggs/eyren" anecdote captures, vividly and memorably, the dialectal diversity his own editorial choices — building on Chaucer's earlier prestige — helped standardize around London/Chancery English. Caxton's death in 1491/92, and Wynkyn de Worde's expansion of the press that followed, land close on the heels of the 1485 political watershed at Bosworth Field. Together, these two developments give this Part its conventional, if necessarily approximate, closing point: the shift from a manuscript-based, politically fragmented medieval English literary culture into the printed, Tudor-era world you'll meet next, in Part III.

## Glossary

- **Chancery Standard** — the London-based written English dialect used in royal administrative documents, increasingly adopted as a de facto written standard from the fifteenth century, reinforced by Caxton's printing practice.
- **Colophon** — a printer's statement at the end of a book, typically giving details of printing, date, and place.
- **Incunabulum** (pl. *incunabula*) — a book printed in Europe before 1501, the "cradle" period of Western printing.
- **Movable type** — individually cast letter blocks that can be arranged and rearranged to print text, the core technology of Gutenberg's printing method.
