# Chapter 9: The Transition to Print — Caxton and the Close of the Medieval Period

*Part II: Middle English Literature, 1066–1500*

---

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Trace William Caxton's career from London mercer to England's first printer, and situate his work within the broader European introduction of movable-type printing.
2. Distinguish the first book printed in English (Bruges, 1473/74) from the first book printed in England and the first dated book printed in England.
3. Explain Caxton's editorial role in publishing earlier Middle English literature (Chaucer, Malory, Gower) and his own translations.
4. Analyze Caxton's Eneydos prologue (1490) as evidence for English dialectal diversity and his own role in linguistic standardization.
5. Assess print technology's significance as a convenient — if partial and retrospectively imposed — marker for the close of the medieval period in English literary history.

---

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Movable-type printing, developed by Johannes Gutenberg in Mainz in the 1440s, spread across continental Europe over the following decades before reaching England through William Caxton, an English merchant who had spent much of his career in the Low Countries. Caxton's introduction of printing to England in 1476 coincided closely, though not by any deliberate design, with the political convulsions closing the Wars of the Roses — Henry Tudor's victory at Bosworth Field in 1485 and the establishment of the Tudor dynasty is the conventional political marker historians use to close the English "medieval" period and open the "early modern"/Renaissance era treated in Part III. This chapter's technological narrative and that political watershed together form the two most commonly cited reasons for treating the last quarter of the fifteenth century as this Part's natural closing point.

## Literary Movements

Print technology did not introduce new literary genres so much as transform the conditions of literary production, circulation, and standardization for the genres already surveyed in this Part: romance (Malory, Chapter 2), Chaucerian and Gowerian poetry (Chapters 3 and 5), and devotional/instructional prose. Caxton's own substantial output of translations (from French, chiefly) also continued medieval England's long tradition of vernacular translation from continental sources (Chapter 1), now disseminated at a speed and scale impossible for manuscript copying. This chapter's central literary-historical claim is that print did not create a sharp break in content so much as accelerate and extend trends — vernacular literary prestige, dialectal standardization — already underway across the preceding two centuries covered by this Part.

## Author Profiles

**William Caxton** (c. 1422–1491/92) — Kent-born mercer who spent much of his career in Bruges as a successful merchant and, eventually, Governor of the Company of Merchant Adventurers of London, before learning printing (working with the Fleming Colard Mansion) and producing, in 1473/74, his own translation of Raoul Lefèvre's French romance as *The Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye* — the first book printed in the English language. Returning to England, he established the country's first printing press at Westminster in 1476 and, over roughly the last fifteen years of his life, printed or translated some 100-plus books (traditionally cited as around 108, comprising some 87 distinct titles), including Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* (1478; corrected second edition 1483), Gower's *Confessio Amantis*, Malory's *Le Morte d'Arthur* (1485), and the first English *Aesop's Fables* (1484), alongside numerous translations of his own from French sources. His prologues and epilogues, appended to many of his editions, provide valuable direct evidence of his own editorial reasoning, linguistic concerns, and sense of English literary tradition.

**Wynkyn de Worde** (d. 1534) — Continental-born printer, probably brought to England by Caxton around 1481 partly to help meet competition from a rival London press; took over Caxton's workshop following litigation in 1495, relocated it to Fleet Street in 1500 (establishing the area's centuries-long association with the printing trade), and vastly expanded on Caxton's output, ultimately publishing more than 400 books in over 800 editions across a wide range of genres, from religious works (his dominant output) to romance, poetry (including work by John Skelton and Stephen Hawes), and practical household manuals.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***The Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye*** (1473/74) — Caxton's own translation of a French courtly prose romance by Raoul Lefèvre; though printed in Bruges rather than England, it holds the historical distinction of being the first book printed in the English language, predating Caxton's Westminster press by roughly two years.
- ***The Dictes or Sayengis of the Philosophres*** (18 November 1477) — Translated from French by Anthony Woodville, 2nd Earl Rivers, and printed by Caxton at Westminster; though almost certainly not literally the first item Caxton printed in England, it is the first English-printed book bearing a definite date, and is therefore conventionally cited as a fixed point in the early history of English printing.
- **Caxton's editions of Chaucer, Gower, and Malory** — Not new literary compositions but editorially significant acts of canon-formation: by choosing to print and re-print (with corrections, in Chaucer's case) these particular Middle English texts, Caxton played a direct role in determining which medieval English literature would circulate widely, in standardized printed form, into the following centuries — arguably the single most consequential editorial intervention in this Part's entire literary history in terms of long-term textual survival and influence.
- **Caxton's prologue to the *Eneydos*** (1490) — Contains Caxton's celebrated anecdote of a London merchant asking for "eggs" (*egges*) and being misunderstood by a Kentish woman who only recognized the older form "eyren," used by Caxton to illustrate the practical difficulty of writing English intelligibly for readers across dialectal regions ("Loo, what sholde a man in thyse dayes now wryte, egges or eyren?"). The anecdote directly informs Caxton's own editorial practice of favouring the London/Chancery Standard dialect already carrying prestige from Chaucer's popularity (Chapter 3), reinforcing that dialect's trajectory toward eventual standard written English.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Print as accelerant, not sole cause, of standardization.** While Caxton is often popularly credited with "standardizing" English, most historians of the language treat this claim with important qualification: the London/Chancery dialect's rise to prestige status was already well underway (driven by Chaucer's influence, the concentration of royal administration in London, and the 1362 Statute of Pleading discussed in Chapter 1) before Caxton printed a single book; his real contribution was to fix and rapidly disseminate an already-emerging standard at unprecedented scale and speed, rather than to invent that standard unilaterally.
- **Which book was really "first"?** Scholars distinguish carefully among several "firsts" often conflated in popular accounts: the first book printed in the English language (*Recuyell*, Bruges, 1473/74), the first book printed in England (uncertain, likely 1476–77, exact identity disputed), and the first *dated* book printed in England (*Dictes or Sayengis*, 1477) — a useful methodological lesson in precise historical claim-making relevant to several other "first" claims made elsewhere in this Part (e.g., the Cædmon/*Beowulf* dating questions of Part I).
- **Caxton as editor and canon-maker.** Because Caxton exercised real editorial judgment — correcting, prefacing, and selecting which texts to print and reprint — modern scholars increasingly examine him not merely as a technological pioneer but as an active shaper of the medieval English literary canon as it would be transmitted into subsequent centuries, a role comparable in its long-term consequences to that of the Winchester and Ellesmere manuscript compilers discussed in Chapters 2 and 3.
- **1485 as periodization convenience.** Historians and literary historians alike routinely acknowledge that 1485 (Bosworth Field) is a convenient rather than a wholly determinative dividing line: literary and linguistic change proceeded gradually and unevenly across the transition, and writers, genres, and forms discussed throughout this Part (romance, religious prose, popular ballads) persisted well into, and beyond, the Tudor period covered in Part III.

## Chapter Summary

William Caxton's introduction of printing to England — beginning with his 1473/74 Bruges translation *The Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye*, the first book printed in English, and continuing through his 1476 Westminster press — transformed the conditions under which the literature surveyed throughout this Part reached readers, most consequentially through his editions of Chaucer, Gower, and Malory, which shaped which medieval English texts would survive into wide circulation. His 1490 *Eneydos* prologue's "eggs/eyren" anecdote vividly documents the dialectal diversity his own editorial practice, building on Chaucer's earlier prestige, helped to standardize around London/Chancery English. Caxton's death in 1491/92 and Wynkyn de Worde's subsequent expansion of the press closely followed the 1485 political watershed of Bosworth Field, and together these developments provide this Part's conventional, if necessarily approximate, closing point: the transition from a manuscript-based, politically fragmented medieval English literary culture to the printed, Tudor-era literary world of Part III.

## Glossary

- **Chancery Standard** — the London-based written English dialect used in royal administrative documents, increasingly adopted as a de facto written standard from the fifteenth century, reinforced by Caxton's printing practice.
- **Colophon** — a printer's statement at the end of a book, typically giving details of printing, date, and place.
- **Incunabulum** (pl. *incunabula*) — a book printed in Europe before 1501, the "cradle" period of Western printing.
- **Movable type** — individually cast letter blocks that can be arranged and rearranged to print text, the core technology of Gutenberg's printing method.
