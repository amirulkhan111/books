## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Medieval English drama developed initially within, and only gradually separated from, the Church's own liturgy, before becoming a major form of urban civic culture sponsored by trade guilds and closely tied to the feast of **Corpus Christi** (established as a universal Church feast in 1264 and actively promoted from 1311), whose themes of Christ's real presence and redemptive sacrifice suited public dramatization of sacred history. The great mystery cycles thus represent a distinctively urban, communally produced form of religious literature, standing apart from — while sharing subject matter with — the individual clerical and courtly literary production surveyed elsewhere in this Part.

## Literary Movements

Medieval English drama is conventionally divided into three related genres: **mystery (or cycle) plays**, dramatizing biblical history from Creation to Last Judgment, performed as a coordinated sequence ("cycle") of individual pageants, each customarily assigned to and funded by a specific trade guild whose occupation sometimes resonated symbolically with its assigned episode (shipwrights performing Noah's Ark; bakers the loaves and fishes; goldsmiths the Magi's gifts); **miracle plays**, a related but formally distinct genre dramatizing the lives and miracles of saints rather than biblical narrative proper (a distinction not always rigorously maintained in casual modern usage); and **morality plays**, allegorical dramas (not organized as biblical cycles) depicting the struggle between virtue and vice for an individual human soul, of which *Everyman* is the outstanding surviving English example, alongside earlier examples such as *The Castle of Perseverance* and *Mankind*.

---

## Author Profiles

Medieval drama is overwhelmingly anonymous and communally produced, reflecting its guild-based civic origins rather than individual authorial ambition; this chapter therefore profiles institutional/traditional attributions rather than named biographical figures.

**The "Wakefield Master"** — Conventional scholarly name for an anonymous, unusually skilled playwright whose distinctive contributions to the Towneley (Wakefield) cycle are identified through a characteristic nine-line stanza (rhyming aabcccb, with internal rhyme in the first four lines); his plays are generally held to include *Noah*, the *First Shepherds' Play*, the *Second Shepherds' Play*, *Herod the Great*, and *The Buffeting of Christ*, and are widely regarded as the artistic high point of English cycle drama for their combination of theological seriousness with sophisticated comic characterization.

**Peter van Diest** — Traditionally credited (though not without scholarly uncertainty) as the author of the Dutch morality play *Elckerlijc* (c. 1470, printed 1495), generally regarded as the probable source, direct or indirect, for the English *Everyman*.

**The *Everyman*-author** — Anonymous English adapter/translator, working in the late fifteenth or early sixteenth century, responsible for *The Somonyng of Everyman* ("The Summoning of Everyman"), commonly known simply as *Everyman*.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **The York, Chester, Wakefield/Towneley, and N-Town cycles** — Together the major surviving evidence for English cycle drama, these differ in scale (York's 48 pageants against Chester's 24), guild organization (documented for York, Chester, and Wakefield; absent or unclear for N-Town), and staging practice (processional pageant wagons moving through the city for York and Chester; less certain staging for N-Town). Collectively they dramatize the full arc of Christian sacred history from Creation and the Fall through the Nativity, Passion, Resurrection, and Last Judgment, offering a comprehensive, communally authored theological narrative in vernacular dramatic form.
- ***The Second Shepherds' Play*** (Wakefield Master, Towneley cycle) — Combines the biblical Nativity announcement to the shepherds with an extended comic subplot in which the sheep-thief Mak disguises a stolen sheep as a newborn infant in a cradle, is discovered, and is subjected to a mock-punishment (tossing in a blanket) by the shepherds — a farcical episode that then gives way, without jarring tonal rupture, to the shepherds' reverent adoration of the newborn Christ. The play is widely admired for this deft integration of broad comedy and devotional seriousness within a single dramatic structure.
- ***Everyman*** — An allegorical morality play in which God, displeased with humanity's worldliness, sends Death to summon Everyman to a final reckoning. Everyman seeks a companion for his journey and is abandoned in turn by Fellowship, Kindred, Cousin, and Goods (Wealth) — each of whom professes friendship but refuses to accompany him to judgment — until only **Good Deeds**, weakened by Everyman's neglect but not destroyed, proves willing and able to accompany him into the grave, alongside Knowledge (who leads Everyman to Confession and Penance) and other supporting virtues. The play's stark, unsentimental treatment of mortality, its systematic personification allegory, and its concluding doctrine — that only Good Deeds ultimately avails at death — have made it the most frequently performed and studied English morality play from the medieval period into the present.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Liturgical origins and dramatic autonomy.** Scholars trace a developmental line from the embedded *Quem Quaeritis* liturgical trope, through increasingly elaborate church-based Latin liturgical drama, to fully vernacular, guild-produced civic cycle drama performed outside the church building entirely — a trajectory read as a gradual "secularization" of dramatic form even while its subject matter remained overwhelmingly religious.
- **Guild sponsorship and civic identity.** The assignment of specific biblical episodes to trade guilds (often, though not always, with occupational resonance) is read by social historians as evidence of how deeply civic and religious identity were intertwined in late medieval English towns, with cycle performance functioning simultaneously as devotional act, civic pageantry, and inter-guild status display.
- **The Wakefield Master's artistic achievement.** Criticism of the *Second Shepherds' Play* frequently focuses on how its comic Mak subplot, rather than undermining the play's devotional purpose, functions as a structural and thematic parallel to the Nativity itself (a false, mocking "birth" — the disguised sheep — set against the true birth of Christ), demonstrating a sophistication of design not always credited to anonymous cycle drama.
- **Everyman and Elckerlijc: translation or common source?** Scholars have long debated the precise relationship between *Everyman* and *Elckerlijc* — whether the English play directly translates/adapts the Dutch text, or whether both derive independently from a shared, now-lost source; the currently prevailing view treats *Everyman* as substantially derived from or closely related to *Elckerlijc*, though the question has not been definitively closed.
- **Reformation-era suppression.** The cycles' progressive suppression from the 1560s–70s onward, amid Reformation hostility to traditional Catholic devotional practice and imagery, is read as a significant, deliberate rupture in English dramatic tradition — one consequence of which was the redirection of English dramatic energy toward the secular and increasingly commercial theatre that would culminate, a generation later, in the Elizabethan stage (Part III).

## Chapter Summary

Medieval English drama developed from the embedded liturgical dialogue of the *Quem Quaeritis* trope into fully vernacular civic drama, chiefly the great guild-produced mystery cycles of York, Chester, Wakefield/Towneley, and N-Town, performed on movable pageant wagons on the feast of Corpus Christi and dramatizing the full span of Christian sacred history. Within the Towneley cycle, the anonymous "Wakefield Master" achieved particular artistic distinction, notably in the *Second Shepherds' Play*'s integration of broad comedy with devotional seriousness. Alongside cycle drama, the morality play tradition — culminating in the anonymous English *Everyman*, closely related to the Dutch *Elckerlijc* — dramatized the individual soul's confrontation with death and judgment through systematic personification allegory, remaining the most enduringly performed medieval English play into the modern period. The cycles' suppression amid Reformation-era religious change marks a significant rupture, redirecting English dramatic energy toward the secular theatrical tradition that would flourish in the following century (Part III).

## Glossary

- **Corpus Christi** — the Church feast (established 1264, promoted from 1311) whose celebration provided the occasion for English cycle drama performance.
- **Miracle play** — a play dramatizing the life or miracles of a saint, distinct from (though sometimes conflated with) mystery plays.
- **Morality play** — an allegorical drama depicting the struggle between virtue and vice for an individual human soul.
- **Mystery (cycle) play** — a guild-produced play dramatizing an episode of biblical history, performed as part of a coordinated sequence.
- ***Quem Quaeritis*** — Latin, "Whom do you seek?"; the earliest identifiable liturgical dramatic trope, embedded in the Easter liturgy from around the tenth century.
