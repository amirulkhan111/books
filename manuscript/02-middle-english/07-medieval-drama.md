## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Every other chapter in this Part has looked at texts meant to be read, alone, on a page. This chapter is different: it's about performance. Medieval English drama started life inside the Church's own liturgy and only gradually broke free of it, eventually becoming a major form of urban civic culture, funded and staged by trade guilds. It became closely tied to a specific Church feast, **Corpus Christi** — established across the whole Church in 1264, and actively promoted from 1311 — whose themes of Christ's real presence and redemptive sacrifice made it a natural occasion for publicly dramatizing sacred history. That civic, guild-funded origin matters: the great mystery cycles you'll meet in this chapter are a distinctively communal, urban form of religious literature, quite different in production from the individual clerical and courtly writing you've studied elsewhere in this Part, even where the subject matter overlaps.

## Literary Movements

Medieval English drama sorts into three related genres, and it's worth keeping them straight, since exam questions (and this chapter's own MCQs) will expect you to. **Mystery (or cycle) plays** dramatize biblical history from Creation to the Last Judgment, staged as a coordinated sequence — a "cycle" — of individual pageants. Each pageant was customarily assigned to, and funded by, a specific trade guild, and sometimes the assignment had a nice logic to it: shipwrights performed Noah's Ark, bakers the loaves and fishes, goldsmiths the Magi's gifts. **Miracle plays** are a related but distinct genre, dramatizing the lives and miracles of saints rather than biblical narrative itself — though be aware that casual modern usage doesn't always keep this distinction sharp. And **morality plays** are allegorical dramas, not organized into biblical cycles at all, depicting the struggle between virtue and vice for one individual human soul. *Everyman* is the outstanding surviving English example of this third genre, alongside earlier examples like *The Castle of Perseverance* and *Mankind*.

## Author Profiles

Medieval drama is overwhelmingly anonymous, produced by communities rather than individual authors chasing personal reputation — a real contrast with, say, Cynewulf's runic signatures back in Part I, or Chaucer's carefully cultivated poetic identity earlier in this Part. So this chapter's profiles are institutional and traditional attributions, not named biographies.

**The "Wakefield Master"** — Conventional scholarly name for an anonymous, unusually skilled playwright whose distinctive contributions to the Towneley (Wakefield) cycle are identified through a characteristic nine-line stanza (rhyming aabcccb, with internal rhyme in the first four lines); his plays are generally held to include *Noah*, the *First Shepherds' Play*, the *Second Shepherds' Play*, *Herod the Great*, and *The Buffeting of Christ*, and are widely regarded as the artistic high point of English cycle drama for their combination of theological seriousness with sophisticated comic characterization.

**Peter van Diest** — Traditionally credited (though not without scholarly uncertainty) as the author of the Dutch morality play *Elckerlijc* (c. 1470, printed 1495), generally regarded as the probable source, direct or indirect, for the English *Everyman*.

**The *Everyman*-author** — Anonymous English adapter/translator, working in the late fifteenth or early sixteenth century, responsible for *The Somonyng of Everyman* ("The Summoning of Everyman"), commonly known simply as *Everyman*.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **The York, Chester, Wakefield/Towneley, and N-Town cycles**. Together, these four cycles are the major surviving evidence for English cycle drama. They differ in scale — York ran to 48 pageants, Chester to 24 — in how well their guild organization is documented (solid for York, Chester, and Wakefield; unclear for N-Town), and in staging: York and Chester used processional pageant wagons that moved through the city, while N-Town's staging is less certain. Between them, they dramatize the entire arc of Christian sacred history, from Creation and the Fall through the Nativity, Passion, Resurrection, and Last Judgment — a genuinely comprehensive theological narrative, authored communally, in vernacular dramatic form.
- ***The Second Shepherds' Play*** (Wakefield Master, Towneley cycle). This play combines the biblical Nativity announcement to the shepherds with an extended comic subplot: a sheep-thief named Mak disguises a stolen sheep as a newborn baby in a cradle, gets caught, and is punished by being tossed in a blanket. That's broad farce. And yet the play moves from this comedy straight into the shepherds' reverent adoration of the newborn Christ, without the tonal whiplash you might expect. Critics admire exactly this: how deftly the play integrates broad comedy and genuine devotional seriousness within a single dramatic structure.
- ***Everyman***. God, displeased with humanity's worldliness, sends Death to summon Everyman to a final reckoning. Everyman looks for a companion to accompany him, and one by one he's abandoned — by Fellowship, by Kindred, by Cousin, by Goods (his wealth) — each of whom professes friendship but refuses to walk with him to judgment. Only **Good Deeds**, weakened by Everyman's past neglect but not destroyed by it, proves willing and able to go with him into the grave, alongside Knowledge (who leads Everyman to Confession and Penance) and a handful of other supporting virtues. The play's stark, unsentimental treatment of death, its systematic use of personification allegory, and its blunt closing doctrine — that only Good Deeds ultimately counts at the end — have made it the most frequently performed and studied English morality play, from the medieval period right up to today.

## Critical Perspectives

- **How did drama break free of the Church liturgy?** Scholars trace a clear developmental line: from the embedded *Quem Quaeritis* trope, through increasingly elaborate Latin liturgical drama performed inside churches, to fully vernacular, guild-produced civic drama performed outside the church building entirely. Read this as a gradual "secularization" of dramatic form — even though the subject matter stayed overwhelmingly religious the whole way through.
- **What does guild sponsorship tell us about medieval town life?** Social historians read the assignment of specific biblical episodes to particular trade guilds — often, though not always, with a neat occupational logic — as evidence of just how tightly civic and religious identity were bound together in late medieval English towns. Performing a cycle play was devotional act, civic pageantry, and a bit of inter-guild status competition, all at once.
- **What makes the Wakefield Master's work stand out?** Criticism of the *Second Shepherds' Play* often focuses on how the comic Mak subplot isn't just comic relief — it functions as a structural and thematic mirror of the Nativity itself: a false, mocking "birth" (the disguised sheep) set directly against the true birth of Christ. That's a level of deliberate design not always credited to anonymous cycle drama, and it's a large part of why the Wakefield Master is treated as exceptional.
- **Is *Everyman* a translation of *Elckerlijc*, or do both come from somewhere else?** Scholars have long debated the exact relationship between *Everyman* and its Dutch counterpart *Elckerlijc* — direct translation, or two independent works drawing on a shared, now-lost source? The currently prevailing view leans toward *Everyman* being substantially derived from, or very closely related to, *Elckerlijc*, though the question isn't fully settled.
- **What was lost when the Reformation shut the cycles down?** From the 1560s–70s onward, England progressively suppressed its mystery cycles amid Reformation hostility to traditional Catholic devotional practice and imagery. Read this as a genuine rupture in English dramatic tradition — and one with a real consequence: English dramatic energy redirected toward secular, increasingly commercial theatre, which would flower a generation later in the Elizabethan stage you'll meet in Part III.

## Chapter Summary

Medieval English drama grew out of the embedded liturgical dialogue of the *Quem Quaeritis* trope into fully vernacular civic drama — chiefly the great guild-produced mystery cycles of York, Chester, Wakefield/Towneley, and N-Town, staged on movable pageant wagons for the feast of Corpus Christi and dramatizing the entire span of Christian sacred history. Within the Towneley cycle, the anonymous "Wakefield Master" achieved a particular artistic distinction, especially through the *Second Shepherds' Play*'s integration of broad comedy with real devotional seriousness. Alongside cycle drama, the morality play tradition — culminating in the anonymous English *Everyman*, closely related to the Dutch *Elckerlijc* — dramatized one soul's confrontation with death and judgment through systematic personification allegory, and remains the most enduringly performed medieval English play today. The cycles' suppression amid Reformation-era religious change marks a genuine rupture, one that redirected English dramatic energy toward the secular theatrical tradition that would flourish in the following century, in Part III.

## Glossary

- **Corpus Christi** — the Church feast (established 1264, promoted from 1311) whose celebration provided the occasion for English cycle drama performance.
- **Miracle play** — a play dramatizing the life or miracles of a saint, distinct from (though sometimes conflated with) mystery plays.
- **Morality play** — an allegorical drama depicting the struggle between virtue and vice for an individual human soul.
- **Mystery (cycle) play** — a guild-produced play dramatizing an episode of biblical history, performed as part of a coordinated sequence.
- ***Quem Quaeritis*** — Latin, "Whom do you seek?"; the earliest identifiable liturgical dramatic trope, embedded in the Easter liturgy from around the tenth century.
