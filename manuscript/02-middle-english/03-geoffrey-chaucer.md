## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chaucer wrote at the culmination of the trilingual literary culture surveyed in Chapter 1, and his career exemplifies the social mobility and cosmopolitan contact increasingly available to a well-placed member of the emerging London civil-service and mercantile class. Son of a London vintner, Chaucer served as a page in the household of the Countess of Ulster, was captured and ransomed during Edward III's 1359–60 Reims campaign in the Hundred Years' War, married Philippa Roet (whose sister Katherine Swynford later became the mistress, and eventually wife, of the powerful John of Gaunt), and held a succession of significant royal administrative posts — Controller of Customs for the port of London (1374–86), Justice of the Peace for Kent (1385–89), Member of Parliament for Kent (1386), and Clerk of the King's Works (1389–91). His diplomatic missions to France and, decisively, to Italy in the 1370s brought him into direct contact with contemporary Italian literary culture, reshaping his poetic development in ways discussed below.

## Literary Movements

Chaucer's career is traditionally divided into three overlapping phases, defined by his shifting primary literary influences: a **French period**, shaped by courtly love poetry and the *Roman de la Rose* (which he translated in part), producing works such as *The Book of the Duchess*; an **Italian period**, following his 1372–73 and 1378 missions to Italy, during which he engaged closely with Dante, Petrarch, and especially Boccaccio, producing *The House of Fame*, *The Parliament of Fowls*, and *Troilus and Criseyde*; and a mature **English period**, culminating in *The Canterbury Tales* and *The Legend of Good Women*, in which Chaucer's own distinctive voice, London vernacular, and formal innovation (including, in *The Legend of Good Women*, English's first known use of the iambic pentameter couplet) come to full expression. Chaucer's decision to write in the London dialect of Middle English, rather than the French or Latin still dominant among much of the court and clerical elite (Chapter 1), was a significant contribution to establishing English vernacular literature's prestige, and his London English forms an important basis for later standard English.

## Author Profiles

**Geoffrey Chaucer** (c. 1343–1400) — London-born son of a vintner; page, soldier, diplomat, and senior civil servant across the reigns of Edward III and Richard II, as well as a major poet. Biographical highlights include his 1359–60 capture and ransom during the Hundred Years' War, his marriage to Philippa Roet (connecting him by marriage to the powerful Gaunt/Lancaster affinity through her sister Katherine Swynford), his sustained career in customs administration and royal works, and his 1386 parliamentary service for Kent. Traditionally styled the "father of English poetry" for his role in establishing English as a vehicle for sophisticated literary achievement comparable to contemporary French and Italian models.

**Giovanni Boccaccio** (1313–1375) — Italian author whose works, though never directly credited by Chaucer himself, provided the principal narrative sources for two of Chaucer's major works: *Troilus and Criseyde* is based on Boccaccio's *Il Filostrato* (1335–40), and the Knight's Tale (in *The Canterbury Tales*) draws on Boccaccio's *Teseida*. Of the three great Italian influences on Chaucer (Dante, Petrarch, Boccaccio), Boccaccio is generally regarded as the one Chaucer drew on most extensively.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Troilus and Criseyde*** (c. 1380–87) — A lengthy tragic love narrative set during the Trojan War, adapted from Boccaccio's *Il Filostrato* but substantially reworked with deeper psychological characterization, philosophical digression (drawing on Boethius, whom Chaucer also translated), and formal ambition. It is Chaucer's first sustained use of **rhyme royal** — a seven-line iambic pentameter stanza rhyming ababbcc — which he is credited with introducing into English poetry, and which later poets (including the Scottish Chaucerians and, much later, Wordsworth and Auden's *The Age of Anxiety*'s successor-forms) adopted for serious narrative and meditative verse.
- ***The Canterbury Tales*** (from c. 1387, unfinished at Chaucer's death) — A frame narrative in which some thirty pilgrims, journeying from the Tabard Inn in Southwark to the shrine of St Thomas Becket at Canterbury, agree (per the Host Harry Bailey's proposal in the General Prologue) to tell two tales each on the outward journey and two on the return, with the winner earning a free meal — a plan implying some 120 tales in total. Chaucer completed only about twenty-four tales and never wrote the return journey, leaving the work formally unfinished, though its surviving portion (particularly the socially panoramic **General Prologue**, with its estates-satire portraits of pilgrims from knight to plowman, miller to prioress) is nonetheless regarded as a complete and self-sufficient literary achievement. The tales themselves range across genre and register — courtly romance (the Knight's Tale), fabliau (the Miller's and Reeve's Tales), moral exemplum, sermon, and beast fable — constituting a comprehensive survey of late medieval English literary and social types.
- **The Ellesmere manuscript** — The most complete and celebrated early manuscript of *The Canterbury Tales*, produced in the fifteenth century with lavish illumination and pilgrim portraits; it remains the primary textual basis for most modern editions.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Chaucer's originality versus his sources.** As with the broader question raised about Malory in Chapter 2, critics debate how to weigh Chaucer's genuine originality against his extensive, often unacknowledged, borrowing from French and Italian sources; the consensus view credits Chaucer not with inventing his narrative material but with a distinctive psychological deepening, ironic narratorial voice, and structural sophistication that substantially transform his sources — nowhere more evident than in the reworking of Boccaccio's more straightforward *Il Filostrato* into the morally and emotionally complex *Troilus and Criseyde*.
- **The unfinished state of *The Canterbury Tales*.** Scholars differ on how to read the work's incompleteness: some treat the surviving fragments as an intentional, open-ended structure reflecting the pilgrimage's own unpredictability, while others regard the 120-tale plan as a genuine, unrealized ambition cut short by Chaucer's death, with the surviving ordering of tales itself a matter of ongoing manuscript-based scholarly reconstruction (different manuscripts, including Ellesmere versus the Hengwrt manuscript, order the fragments differently).
- **Estates satire and social representation.** The General Prologue's portrait gallery is widely read through the lens of medieval "estates satire" (a literary tradition cataloguing social types according to their moral conformity to ideal roles), while modern criticism increasingly examines the poem's treatment of gender (the Wife of Bath), religious institutions (the Prioress, the Pardoner, the Monk), and social mobility in late fourteenth-century England.
- **Chaucer and the standardization of English.** Because Chaucer's London-dialect verse became so widely copied and admired, his usage is often credited (though not without important caveats about overstating any single author's influence on language change) with contributing significantly to the eventual emergence of a standard written English descended substantially from the London dialect.

## Chapter Summary

Geoffrey Chaucer's career as courtier, soldier, diplomat, and civil servant placed him at the intersection of French, Italian, and English literary cultures, a position reflected in the conventional division of his work into French, Italian, and English periods. His Italian-period masterpiece, *Troilus and Criseyde*, reworked Boccaccio's *Il Filostrato* into a psychologically and philosophically deepened tragedy in the newly introduced rhyme royal stanza, while his mature, unfinished masterpiece *The Canterbury Tales* uses a pilgrimage frame narrative to stage a socially panoramic and generically varied survey of late medieval English life and storytelling, surviving in roughly two dozen of a planned 120 tales. Chaucer's choice to write ambitious, sophisticated literature in the London vernacular, at a cultural moment still dominated in prestige terms by French and Latin (Chapter 1), underlies his traditional — and substantially justified — reputation as the "father of English poetry."

## Glossary

- **Estates satire** — a medieval literary tradition cataloguing social types (estates) according to their conformity or failure to conform to idealized roles, exemplified by the *Canterbury Tales* General Prologue.
- **Fabliau** — a short, often comic and bawdy narrative genre, exemplified in *The Canterbury Tales* by the Miller's and Reeve's Tales.
- **Frame narrative** — a narrative structure in which an outer story (here, the Canterbury pilgrimage) contains and organizes a series of inner, told stories.
- **Rhyme royal** — a seven-line iambic pentameter stanza rhyming ababbcc, introduced into English by Chaucer in *Troilus and Criseyde*.
