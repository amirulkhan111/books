## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Everything Chapter 1 taught you about medieval England's trilingual culture — Latin, French, and English coexisting and competing — reaches a kind of turning point in this chapter's subject: Geoffrey Chaucer. His life shows you exactly how a well-placed member of the rising London civil-service class could move fluently between all three languages and their literary worlds, and his career is worth knowing in some detail, because it explains so much about how his poetry developed.

Chaucer was the son of a London wine merchant. As a young man he served as a page in an aristocratic household, and in 1359–60 he was captured and ransomed during a French campaign of the Hundred Years' War. He married Philippa Roet, whose sister Katherine Swynford later became the mistress, and eventually the wife, of John of Gaunt — one of the most powerful men in England — which connected Chaucer by marriage to real political influence. Alongside his poetry, he held a string of serious administrative posts: Controller of Customs for the port of London (1374–86), Justice of the Peace for Kent (1385–89), Member of Parliament for Kent (1386), and Clerk of the King's Works (1389–91). None of this was incidental to his writing. His diplomatic missions to France, and especially to Italy in the 1370s, put him in direct contact with contemporary Italian literary culture — a contact that reshaped his poetry in ways you'll see clearly below.

## Literary Movements

Scholars traditionally sort Chaucer's career into three overlapping phases, and each one is named for whatever literary influence dominated his writing at the time. First comes a **French period**, shaped by courtly love poetry and by the *Roman de la Rose*, which he partly translated, producing works like *The Book of the Duchess*. Then comes an **Italian period**, following his 1372–73 and 1378 missions to Italy, during which he engaged closely with Dante, Petrarch, and especially Boccaccio, producing *The House of Fame*, *The Parliament of Fowls*, and *Troilus and Criseyde*. Finally comes a mature **English period**, where his own distinctive voice takes over — culminating in *The Canterbury Tales* and *The Legend of Good Women* (the latter containing English's first known use of the iambic pentameter couplet, a form you'll see become hugely important in later centuries of this book). Throughout, one choice matters more than any single influence: Chaucer wrote in the London dialect of Middle English, not in the French or Latin that still dominated prestige literature in his lifetime, as you learned in Chapter 1. That choice did real work — it helped establish English as a language capable of serious literary achievement, and his London English became an important foundation for the standard English that later developed.

## Author Profiles

**Geoffrey Chaucer** (c. 1343–1400) — London-born son of a vintner; page, soldier, diplomat, and senior civil servant across the reigns of Edward III and Richard II, as well as a major poet. Biographical highlights include his 1359–60 capture and ransom during the Hundred Years' War, his marriage to Philippa Roet (connecting him by marriage to the powerful Gaunt/Lancaster affinity through her sister Katherine Swynford), his sustained career in customs administration and royal works, and his 1386 parliamentary service for Kent. Traditionally styled the "father of English poetry" for his role in establishing English as a vehicle for sophisticated literary achievement comparable to contemporary French and Italian models.

**Giovanni Boccaccio** (1313–1375) — Italian author whose works, though never directly credited by Chaucer himself, provided the principal narrative sources for two of Chaucer's major works: *Troilus and Criseyde* is based on Boccaccio's *Il Filostrato* (1335–40), and the Knight's Tale (in *The Canterbury Tales*) draws on Boccaccio's *Teseida*. Of the three great Italian influences on Chaucer (Dante, Petrarch, Boccaccio), Boccaccio is generally regarded as the one Chaucer drew on most extensively.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Troilus and Criseyde*** (c. 1380–87). A long tragic love story set during the Trojan War, adapted from Boccaccio's *Il Filostrato* but reworked into something considerably deeper — richer psychological characterization, philosophical digression (Chaucer drew on Boethius, whom he also translated), and real formal ambition. Here Chaucer introduces **rhyme royal** into English poetry for the first time: a seven-line, iambic-pentameter stanza rhyming ababbcc. Later poets kept coming back to this stanza for serious narrative and meditative verse, right down to Wordsworth and beyond, which tells you how durable a formal innovation this was.
- ***The Canterbury Tales*** (from c. 1387, unfinished at Chaucer's death). Picture the setup: roughly thirty pilgrims travel together from the Tabard Inn in Southwark to the shrine of St Thomas Becket at Canterbury, and their host, Harry Bailey, proposes a storytelling contest — two tales each on the way out, two more on the way back, with a free meal for the winner. Do the arithmetic and that's a plan for around 120 tales. Chaucer actually finished about twenty-four, and never wrote the return journey at all, so the work is formally incomplete. That doesn't make it feel unfinished to read, though — the surviving portion, especially the **General Prologue**'s panoramic gallery of pilgrim portraits (knight to plowman, miller to prioress), stands on its own as a complete literary achievement. The tales themselves range wildly in genre and tone: courtly romance in the Knight's Tale, bawdy comedy (called **fabliau**) in the Miller's and Reeve's Tales, moral exemplum, sermon, beast fable — together adding up to a genuinely comprehensive survey of late medieval English life and storytelling.
- **The Ellesmere manuscript**. This fifteenth-century manuscript, lavishly illustrated with portraits of the pilgrims, is the most complete and celebrated early copy of *The Canterbury Tales* to survive, and it remains the basis most modern editions are built on.

## Critical Perspectives

- **How much of Chaucer's work is really his own?** You met a version of this question already with Malory in Chapter 2, and it applies here too: how should we weigh Chaucer's genuine originality against his extensive, often unacknowledged borrowing from French and Italian sources? The consensus answer isn't that Chaucer invented his narrative material — he mostly didn't — but that he transformed it, through psychological depth, an ironic narratorial voice, and real structural sophistication. Nowhere is this clearer than in *Troilus and Criseyde*, where Boccaccio's relatively straightforward *Il Filostrato* becomes something morally and emotionally far more complex.
- **Is *The Canterbury Tales* unfinished, or open-ended by design?** Scholars split on how to read its incompleteness. Some see the surviving fragments as a deliberate, open-ended structure, mirroring the pilgrimage's own unpredictability. Others see a genuine, ambitious 120-tale plan cut short by Chaucer's death. It doesn't help that even the order of the surviving tales isn't fully settled — different manuscripts, including Ellesmere and another called the Hengwrt manuscript, arrange the fragments differently, and scholars are still reconstructing the most likely intended order.
- **What is "estates satire," and why does it matter here?** The General Prologue's gallery of pilgrim portraits belongs to a medieval literary tradition called estates satire, which catalogues social types according to how well or badly they live up to their idealized roles. Modern criticism has pushed further into this material, examining what the poem does with gender (through the Wife of Bath), with religious institutions (the Prioress, the Pardoner, the Monk), and with social mobility in late fourteenth-century England.
- **Did Chaucer help standardize written English?** Because his London-dialect verse was copied and admired so widely, his usage is often credited with helping the London dialect become the basis of later standard written English. Be a little careful with this claim, though — most historians would caution against crediting any single writer with too much power to shape how a whole language develops.

## Chapter Summary

Chaucer's career as courtier, soldier, diplomat, and civil servant placed him right at the intersection of French, Italian, and English literary cultures, which is exactly why his work divides so naturally into French, Italian, and English periods. His Italian-period masterpiece, *Troilus and Criseyde*, reworked Boccaccio's *Il Filostrato* into a psychologically and philosophically deepened tragedy, written in the newly introduced rhyme royal stanza. His mature, unfinished masterpiece, *The Canterbury Tales*, uses a pilgrimage frame narrative to stage a panoramic, wildly varied survey of late medieval English life and storytelling — surviving today as roughly two dozen tales out of a planned 120. Underneath both achievements sits one deliberate choice: writing ambitious, sophisticated literature in the London vernacular, at a moment when French and Latin still held most of the prestige, as you learned in Chapter 1. That choice is the real foundation of his traditional, and genuinely earned, title: "father of English poetry."

## Glossary

- **Estates satire** — a medieval literary tradition cataloguing social types (estates) according to their conformity or failure to conform to idealized roles, exemplified by the *Canterbury Tales* General Prologue.
- **Fabliau** — a short, often comic and bawdy narrative genre, exemplified in *The Canterbury Tales* by the Miller's and Reeve's Tales.
- **Frame narrative** — a narrative structure in which an outer story (here, the Canterbury pilgrimage) contains and organizes a series of inner, told stories.
- **Rhyme royal** — a seven-line iambic pentameter stanza rhyming ababbcc, introduced into English by Chaucer in *Troilus and Criseyde*.
