## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Every writer you've met so far in this Part — Chaucer, Langland, Gower, Julian, Margery — belonged, in one way or another, to an educated or clerical elite. This chapter looks at something different: the popular narrative verse ordinary people actually sang and told each other, transmitted orally, often with music, among a broad audience with no particular claim to learning. Only occasionally, and usually only partially, did any of it get written down before the sixteenth century.

The most famous medieval example of this tradition is the cycle of outlaw ballads about **Robin Hood**. And here's a striking fact about how we know it existed this early: the earliest certain reference to Robin Hood doesn't come from a ballad collection at all — it comes from William Langland's *Piers Plowman* (Chapter 4), where a character mentions knowing "rymes of Robyn hood" almost as a throwaway line. That's valuable evidence in itself: it tells you Robin Hood material was already familiar enough by the 1370s to work as a passing aside inside a sophisticated allegorical poem.

## Literary Movements

Medieval popular verse comes in several related but distinct forms. **Narrative ballads** tell a compact story — often about outlawry, tragic love, the supernatural, or border conflict — in quatrain stanzas, with musical accompaniment and, frequently, a refrain. **Carols** began as round-dance songs built around a repeated burden or refrain; despite what the word suggests to you today, they weren't originally restricted to Christmas, though many surviving examples happen to be devotional. And popular verse romances, in simpler tail-rhyme stanzas, circulated alongside the courtly romance tradition you met in Chapter 2, but in a noticeably plainer register. This chapter focuses on the ballad tradition, and above all on the **Robin Hood cycle** — the most historically significant and best-documented medieval popular verse tradition we have.

## Author Profiles

Ballads are, by definition, anonymous and communally transmitted, not individually authored — so instead of poets, this chapter profiles the semi-legendary subject at the centre of the tradition, plus the collectors and scholars who are the reason any of it survives for us to read today.

**Robin Hood** — A figure of English outlaw legend, not a documented historical individual (despite extensive antiquarian and modern speculation attempting to identify a "real" Robin Hood), whose earliest certain literary trace is Langland's 1377 allusion; the surviving medieval ballads consistently locate him in and around Sherwood Forest and Barnsdale, in conflict with the Sheriff of Nottingham, and portray him as a skilled archer and outlaw noted for generosity to the poor and hostility to corrupt clergy and officials — themes that resonated with, without being reducible to, the social tensions of the period discussed in Chapter 4.

**Thomas Percy** (1729–1811) — Anglican bishop and antiquarian scholar whose 1765 *Reliques of Ancient English Poetry*, based substantially on the seventeenth-century "Percy Folio" manuscript he rescued from a friend's household, was the first major published anthology to bring medieval and early modern English ballads to a wide literary readership, decisively influencing the later Romantic revival of interest in popular and "primitive" poetry (Part VI).

**Francis James Child** (1825–1896) — American Harvard scholar whose monumental *The English and Scottish Popular Ballads* (published in ten parts, 1882–98, completed after his death by his student George Lyman Kittredge) collected 305 ballads with extensive comparative and international scholarly apparatus, becoming the standard reference collection — ballads are still conventionally identified today by their "Child number" (e.g., Child 117 for *A Gest of Robyn Hode*).

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"Robin Hood and the Monk"**. This is the earliest surviving Robin Hood ballad text, copied after 1450 into a Cambridge manuscript. It already contains many elements you'll recognize from the later legend — the Nottingham setting, the enmity with the Sheriff — which tells you a fully developed narrative tradition existed well before this, its earliest surviving written record.
- **"A Gest of Robyn Hode"**. This longer narrative was compiled, probably around 1450–75, out of several originally separate episodes, stitched into something closer to a continuous outlaw story, and first printed around 1500. No medieval manuscript of the *Gest* survives, but linguistic analysis supports that mid-to-late-fifteenth-century compilation date — making it, alongside "Robin Hood and the Monk," central evidence that the legend genuinely circulated in medieval times, not just later.
- **The ballad stanza**. Picture a four-line quatrain, alternating a four-stress line with a three-stress line, rhyming ABCB — sometimes called "common meter" or "hymn meter." Ballads frequently pair this with a refrain and with **incremental repetition**: a recurring phrase or stanza pattern that advances the story through small, cumulative changes each time it returns. None of this is accidental — these are exactly the features that make a poem easy to compose, remember, and sing aloud, rather than features suited to solitary silent reading.

## Critical Perspectives

- **How do you date something that was sung for generations before anyone wrote it down?** Ballads were passed on orally for generations before being written down, if they ever were — and even material with demonstrably medieval origins usually wasn't recorded in writing until the sixteenth century or later, whether in print broadsides, the Percy Folio, or a later collection still. That gap forces scholars to carefully separate a ballad's probable period of oral origin from the date of its earliest surviving text. This is exactly why Langland's 1377 allusion matters so much: it gives a firm date for Robin Hood material's currency, completely independent of any surviving ballad text.
- **Was Robin Hood a "social bandit," or a later invention dressed up as one?** Historians and folklorists have long debated how much of the medieval Robin Hood ballads reflects genuine popular feeling — resentment of corrupt officials, sympathy for the poor — versus later idealization. One thing is now fairly settled: the "gentrification" of Robin Hood into a dispossessed nobleman (the Earl of Huntingdon tradition) is a later, non-medieval addition. In the earliest ballads, Robin is simply a yeoman, not an aristocrat in disguise.
- **Can you trust Percy's *Reliques* as a window onto the real medieval ballads?** Not entirely. Percy is now known to have significantly edited, "improved," and in places substantially rewritten his source material to suit eighteenth-century literary taste — a practice criticized even in his own lifetime, and one modern scholars treat with real caution. Child's later method, more rigorously comparative and far less interventionist, is generally seen as methodologically stronger, though even Child was working from print and manuscript sources already several steps removed from any medieval oral "original."
- **Why did popular verse take so long to be taken seriously?** The ballad tradition sat lower in status than the courtly, clerical, and civic literature discussed elsewhere in this Part, and serious scholarly attention to it lagged well behind Chaucer or the Gawain-poet. Its eventual "rehabilitation," starting with Percy and reaching full scholarly rigor with Child, is itself an important episode in the history of English literary taste — closely tied to the Romantic era's rediscovered enthusiasm for "primitive" and popular poetry, which you'll meet again in Part VI.

## Chapter Summary

Medieval English popular verse was transmitted chiefly through oral performance, and it survives to us mainly through much later manuscript and print transcription — above all in the Robin Hood ballad cycle, first certainly attested in Langland's 1377 aside in *Piers Plowman*, with the earliest surviving ballad text ("Robin Hood and the Monk") dating from after 1450 and the compiled *Gest of Robyn Hode* reaching print around 1500. This material was composed in the characteristic ballad stanza — a four-line, ABCB-rhymed quatrain built for oral performance, refrain, and incremental repetition — and its modern preservation owes an enormous debt to two collectors: Thomas Percy, whose 1765 *Reliques* was pioneering but editorially heavy-handed, and Francis James Child, whose far more rigorous 1882–98 collection remains the standard scholarly reference today. The gap between this material's likely medieval oral life and its much later textual preservation is a genuine methodological challenge — related to, but distinct from, the manuscript-survival concerns you've seen raised throughout the rest of this Part.

## Glossary

- **Ballad stanza** — a four-line quatrain, typically alternating four-stress and three-stress lines, rhyming ABCB.
- **Broadside** — a single printed sheet, a common early modern medium for disseminating ballad texts.
- **Burden/refrain** — a repeated line or phrase recurring throughout a ballad or carol, often for musical/communal performance.
- **Incremental repetition** — a ballad technique in which a phrase or stanza pattern recurs with small, cumulative variation, advancing the narrative.
- **"Child number"** — the standard reference number assigned to a traditional ballad per Francis James Child's catalogue.
