---

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The final decades of the fourteenth century, already discussed in Chapters 3 and 4 through Chaucer and Langland, produced a wider circle of writers working across the trilingual culture established in Chapter 1 — some, like Gower, moving fluidly between all three languages within a single career; others, like Trevisa, working specifically to expand English prose's capacity for scholarly and encyclopedic material; and still others, like Usk, caught up directly in the volatile London civic politics of the 1380s. Beyond England, this period also saw the emergence of an independent Older Scots literary tradition, represented here by John Barbour, writing under the patronage of the Scottish crown during the ongoing aftermath of the Wars of Scottish Independence.

## Literary Movements

This chapter's figures illustrate three distinct late-fourteenth-century literary tendencies operating alongside Chaucer's and Langland's: (1) **sustained trilingualism within a single career**, exemplified by Gower's deliberate choice to compose major works in French, Latin, and English successively, treating all three as live literary vehicles for shared moral and political themes; (2) **the expansion of English prose for scholarly/encyclopedic translation**, exemplified by Trevisa's rendering of major Latin reference works into English, extending the vernacular-literacy project traced from the Alfredian programme (Part I, Chapter 6) through this Part; and (3) **the emergence of an independent Older Scots literary tradition**, exemplified by Barbour, distinct from, though related to, the Middle English literary developments occurring further south.

## Author Profiles

**John Gower** (c. 1330–1408) — English poet, probably from a Kent or Suffolk landholding family, who practised law in or near London and moved in circles close to the nobility; a close personal friend of Chaucer, who granted him power of attorney during his 1378 Italian mission. Gower is remembered chiefly for three long poems, one in each of medieval England's three literary languages: the *Mirour de l'Omme* ("Mirror of Mankind," French, 1376–79), the *Vox Clamantis* ("The Voice of One Crying," Latin, 1379–82), and the *Confessio Amantis* ("The Lover's Confession," English, begun c. 1386), all united by shared moral and political preoccupations. His elaborate self-designed tomb in Southwark Cathedral depicts his head resting on all three books, a deliberate monument to his trilingual achievement.

**Thomas Usk** (d. 1388) — London civil servant entangled in the factional politics of the city's government in the early 1380s; having switched allegiance from Lord Mayor John Northampton to his rival Nicholas Brembre, Usk was ultimately prosecuted and executed following the ascendancy of the Duke of Gloucester's faction in the Merciless Parliament of 1388. His prose work *The Testament of Love*, written partly in prison, is an allegorical appeal combining Boethian philosophy with personal political pleading; it was long mistakenly attributed to Chaucer, and an acrostic formed by its section-initial letters spells out an appeal to "Margaret," commonly read as encoding the author's own name ("thin Usk").

**John Trevisa** (c. 1342–1402) — Cornish-born cleric and translator, educated at Exeter College, Oxford, later vicar of Berkeley, Gloucestershire; his English prose translations of Ranulf Higden's universal history the *Polychronicon* (completed 1387) and Bartholomaeus Anglicus's encyclopedic *De Proprietatibus Rerum* (completed c. 1398) made major bodies of Latin historical and scientific knowledge accessible in English prose, becoming standard reference works well into the following century.

**John Barbour** (c. 1320–1395) — Scottish cleric and poet, generally regarded as the first major named literary figure to write in (Older) Scots; composed *The Bruce* (*The Brus*), a roughly 14,000-line octosyllabic narrative poem (c. 1375) celebrating Robert the Bruce and Sir James Douglas's roles in the Scottish Wars of Independence, composed while Barbour was attached to the household of King Robert II, whose royal legitimacy the poem implicitly reinforces by celebrating his grandfather's achievements.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Confessio Amantis*** (Gower) — A substantial English poem structured as a confession by "Amans" ("the Lover") to Genius, priest of Venus, organized around the Seven Deadly Sins; within this confessional frame, Genius illustrates each sin (and its remedy) through a wide-ranging collection of exemplary narrative tales, drawn from classical, biblical, and medieval sources — a structural strategy comparable in ambition, though different in organizing principle, to Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* frame. The poem exists in multiple recensions: the first (1390) dedicated to Richard II and including a passage praising Chaucer; a later, revised (1392) recension rededicated to Henry of Lancaster, reflecting the shifting political climate of Richard II's troubled later reign and Gower's apparent recalibration of patronage as Henry's eventual usurpation (1399) approached.
- ***The Testament of Love*** (Usk) — An allegorical prose work modelled partly on Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy* (itself translated by Chaucer), notable for its acrostic self-identification and for its direct entanglement with the violent factional politics that ultimately cost its author his life — a reminder of the real personal stakes literary and political writing could carry in this period.
- ***Polychronicon* translation and *De Proprietatibus Rerum* translation** (Trevisa) — Neither an original literary composition, but together a major expansion of what English prose could carry: comprehensive world history and comprehensive natural-scientific/encyclopedic knowledge, previously accessible in England chiefly through Latin, now rendered into a vernacular prose capable of sustaining complex expository and reference material.
- ***The Bruce*** (Barbour) — Combines chronicle history with the values and conventions of chivalric romance, presenting Robert the Bruce's struggle for Scottish independence in heroic terms; as the earliest major work of Scots literature, it stands as a founding text for a national literary tradition that develops, over the following century, into the fuller flowering of the "Scottish Chaucerians" (Robert Henryson, William Dunbar, Gavin Douglas), writing in the generations after Chaucer's death and extending his formal influence northward.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Gower's trilingual project as a coherent artistic statement.** Rather than treating Gower's shift from French to Latin to English as merely following changing literary fashion, critics increasingly read his trilingual corpus as a deliberate, unified moral-political project — the same underlying concerns (social corruption, right governance, the individual's moral responsibility) explored successively in each of medieval England's major literary languages, making Gower a uniquely direct embodiment of Chapter 1's trilingual literary culture.
- **"Moral Gower" and the Chaucer-Gower relationship.** Chaucer's "moral Gower" dedication in *Troilus and Criseyde*, and Gower's reciprocal praise of Chaucer (via Venus's speech) in the *Confessio Amantis*, are frequently read as evidence of a genuine, mutually respectful literary friendship between the two most significant English poets of the late fourteenth century, though some scholars have also noted subtle tensions or implicit rivalry beneath the mutual courtesy, particularly given the two poets' differing tonal registers (Gower's more consistently didactic manner against Chaucer's greater ironic play).
- **Political recalibration and the *Confessio*'s dedications.** Gower's shift in dedication from Richard II (1390) to Henry of Lancaster (1392, ahead of Henry's 1399 usurpation) has attracted critical attention as a case study in how a major literary work's paratextual framing could be revised to track — or anticipate — shifting political fortunes, a concern also relevant to the C-text revisions of *Piers Plowman* discussed in Chapter 4.
- **The vernacularization of scholarly prose.** Trevisa's translations are read by historians of the English language as significant evidence for English prose's growing capacity, by the late fourteenth century, to carry sustained expository, historical, and scientific content previously restricted to Latin — a development running in parallel with, but distinct from, the poetic achievements of Chaucer, Langland, and Gower.

## Chapter Summary

Alongside Chaucer and Langland, late fourteenth-century England produced a wider circle of significant literary figures: John Gower, whose deliberate trilingual career (French, Latin, English) and close friendship with Chaucer — commemorated in Chaucer's "moral Gower" dedication — make him a direct embodiment of medieval England's trilingual literary culture; Thomas Usk, whose allegorical prose *Testament of Love* is inseparable from the violent London factional politics that led to his 1388 execution; John Trevisa, whose translations expanded English prose's capacity for historical and encyclopedic writing; and, beyond England's borders, John Barbour, whose *Bruce* founded an independent Older Scots literary tradition. Together, this generation's collective achievement — spanning moral allegory, political prose, scholarly translation, and national chivalric narrative — substantially advanced English's standing as a literary language capable of the full range of purposes previously reserved, in whole or part, for French and Latin.

## Glossary

- **Confessio Amantis** — "The Lover's Confession"; the title and organizing frame of Gower's major English poem.
- **Merciless Parliament** — the 1388 Parliament, dominated by the Duke of Gloucester's faction, that prosecuted and executed a number of Richard II's associates and allies, including Thomas Usk.
- **Older Scots** — the medieval form of the Scots language, distinct from but related to Middle English, in which John Barbour composed *The Bruce*.
- **Recension** — a distinct revised edition or version of a text, as with the differently dedicated recensions of Gower's *Confessio Amantis*.
