## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chaucer and Langland were not writing in isolation. This chapter widens the lens to the rest of their generation — writers working across the same trilingual culture you learned about in Chapter 1, but each pulling in a different direction. Gower moved fluidly between all three languages within a single career. Trevisa worked specifically to expand what English prose could carry, translating major scholarly and encyclopedic works. Usk got pulled directly into the volatile, often dangerous civic politics of 1380s London. And beyond England's borders entirely, this same period saw an independent literary tradition emerge in Scotland, represented here by John Barbour, who wrote under royal Scottish patronage in the long aftermath of the Wars of Scottish Independence.

## Literary Movements

Three distinct tendencies run through this chapter, alongside what you've already learned about Chaucer and Langland. The first is **sustained trilingualism within a single career**: Gower deliberately composed major works in French, Latin, and English, one after another, treating all three as equally live vehicles for the same underlying moral and political concerns. The second is **the expansion of English prose into scholarly and encyclopedic territory**: Trevisa translated major Latin reference works into English, continuing a project of vernacular literacy you can trace all the way back to Alfred's programme in Part I, Chapter 6. The third is **the emergence of an independent Older Scots literary tradition**, represented here by Barbour — related to the Middle English literature developing further south, but its own distinct national tradition.

## Author Profiles

**John Gower** (c. 1330–1408) was an English poet, probably from a landholding family in Kent or Suffolk, who practised law in or near London and moved in circles close to the nobility. He was also a close personal friend of Chaucer's — close enough that Chaucer granted him power of attorney during his 1378 Italian mission. Gower is remembered chiefly for three long poems, one in each of medieval England's three literary languages: the *Mirour de l'Omme* ("Mirror of Mankind," French, 1376–79), the *Vox Clamantis* ("The Voice of One Crying," Latin, 1379–82), and the *Confessio Amantis* ("The Lover's Confession," English, begun around 1386) — three languages, but the same underlying moral and political concerns running through all of them. His own elaborate, self-designed tomb in Southwark Cathedral depicts his head resting on all three books at once: a deliberate monument to exactly this trilingual achievement.

**Thomas Usk** (d. 1388) was a London civil servant who got tangled up in the city government's factional politics in the early 1380s. He switched allegiance from Lord Mayor John Northampton to his rival Nicholas Brembre, and when the Duke of Gloucester's faction rose to power, Usk was prosecuted and executed in the Merciless Parliament of 1388. His prose work, *The Testament of Love*, written partly in prison, is an allegorical appeal blending Boethian philosophy with personal political pleading. It was long mistakenly attributed to Chaucer — but an acrostic, formed by the first letters of its sections, spells out an appeal to "Margaret," which most readers take as encoding the author's own name: "thin Usk."

**John Trevisa** (c. 1342–1402) was a Cornish-born cleric and translator, educated at Exeter College, Oxford, and later vicar of Berkeley, Gloucestershire. His English prose translations of two major Latin works — Ranulf Higden's universal history the *Polychronicon* (completed 1387) and Bartholomaeus Anglicus's encyclopedic *De Proprietatibus Rerum* (completed around 1398) — put major bodies of Latin historical and scientific knowledge into English prose for the first time, and both became standard reference works well into the following century.

**John Barbour** (c. 1320–1395) was a Scottish cleric and poet, generally regarded as the first major named literary figure to write in Older Scots. His *The Bruce* is a roughly 14,000-line narrative poem, composed around 1375, celebrating Robert the Bruce and Sir James Douglas's roles in the Scottish Wars of Independence. Barbour wrote it while attached to the household of King Robert II — and by celebrating the achievements of Robert II's grandfather, the poem quietly reinforces the king's own claim to legitimacy.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Confessio Amantis*** (Gower). This substantial English poem is structured as a confession: "Amans" ("the Lover") confesses to Genius, priest of Venus, and the whole poem is organized around the Seven Deadly Sins. Within that frame, Genius illustrates each sin — and its remedy — through a wide-ranging collection of exemplary stories, drawn from classical, biblical, and medieval sources. Notice the family resemblance to Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales*: both poems use a frame to organize a large collection of individual stories, even though their organizing principles are completely different. The poem also survives in more than one version: the first (1390) was dedicated to Richard II and includes a passage praising Chaucer; a later, revised version (1392) was rededicated to Henry of Lancaster, reflecting just how much the political climate of Richard II's troubled later reign was shifting, and how carefully Gower seems to have been recalibrating his own political loyalties as Henry's eventual seizure of the throne (1399) drew closer.
- ***The Testament of Love*** (Usk). This allegorical prose work draws partly on Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy* (a text Chaucer also translated). Two things make it stand out: it hides the author's name in an acrostic — reading down the first letters of its sections spells out an appeal to "Margaret," widely read as encoding "thin Usk" — and it's directly entangled with the violent factional politics that ultimately cost its author his life. That second fact is worth sitting with: literary and political writing in this period could carry real, sometimes fatal, personal stakes.
- **Trevisa's translations of the *Polychronicon* and *De Proprietatibus Rerum***. Neither of these is an original literary composition, but together they mark a major expansion of what English prose could actually carry. Comprehensive world history and comprehensive natural-scientific, encyclopedic knowledge — material previously accessible in England mainly through Latin — could now live in vernacular English prose capable of handling complex, sustained reference material.
- ***The Bruce*** (Barbour). This poem combines chronicle history with the conventions of chivalric romance, telling the story of Robert the Bruce's fight for Scottish independence in openly heroic terms. As the earliest major work of Scots literature, it's a genuine founding text — the tradition it starts develops, over the following century, into the fuller flowering of the "Scottish Chaucerians" (Robert Henryson, William Dunbar, Gavin Douglas), poets writing in the generations after Chaucer's death who carried his formal influence north into Scotland.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Gower's trilingual career a coherent plan, or just following fashion?** It's tempting to read Gower's shift from French to Latin to English as simply tracking changing literary fashion. Critics increasingly resist that reading, though, and see his trilingual body of work instead as a deliberate, unified project: the same underlying concerns — social corruption, right governance, individual moral responsibility — explored successively in each of medieval England's major literary languages. That makes Gower a uniquely direct, personal embodiment of the trilingual culture you learned about in Chapter 1.
- **What does "Moral Gower" tell us about the Chaucer-Gower friendship?** Chaucer calls Gower "moral Gower" in his dedication to *Troilus and Criseyde*, and Gower praises Chaucer in return, through a speech by Venus, in the *Confessio Amantis*. Most critics read this exchange as evidence of a genuine, mutually respectful friendship between the two most significant English poets of the late fourteenth century — though some also detect a subtle tension underneath the courtesy, given how differently the two poets actually write: Gower consistently didactic, Chaucer far more given to irony and play.
- **Why did Gower change the *Confessio*'s dedication?** Gower's shift from dedicating the poem to Richard II (1390) to dedicating it to Henry of Lancaster (1392) — a full seven years before Henry actually took the throne in 1399 — gives scholars a useful case study in how a major literary work's public framing could be revised to track, or even anticipate, shifting political fortunes. You'll notice a similar concern in Chapter 4's discussion of the C-text revisions to *Piers Plowman*.
- **Trevisa and the growing power of English prose.** Historians of the English language treat Trevisa's translations as significant evidence that, by the late fourteenth century, English prose could carry sustained expository, historical, and scientific content that had previously been restricted to Latin — a development running alongside, but genuinely distinct from, the poetic achievements of Chaucer, Langland, and Gower.

## Chapter Summary

Alongside Chaucer and Langland, late fourteenth-century England produced a wider circle of significant writers. John Gower's deliberate trilingual career — French, Latin, English — and his close friendship with Chaucer, commemorated in the "moral Gower" dedication, make him a direct, personal embodiment of medieval England's trilingual literary culture. Thomas Usk's allegorical prose *Testament of Love* is inseparable from the violent London factional politics that led to his 1388 execution. John Trevisa's translations expanded what English prose could carry, into history and encyclopedic knowledge. And beyond England's borders, John Barbour's *Bruce* founded an independent Older Scots literary tradition. Taken together — moral allegory, political prose, scholarly translation, national chivalric narrative — this generation's collective work pushed English a long way further toward becoming a language capable of everything French and Latin had previously done on their own.

## Glossary

- **Confessio Amantis** — "The Lover's Confession"; the title and organizing frame of Gower's major English poem.
- **Merciless Parliament** — the 1388 Parliament, dominated by the Duke of Gloucester's faction, that prosecuted and executed a number of Richard II's associates and allies, including Thomas Usk.
- **Older Scots** — the medieval form of the Scots language, distinct from but related to Middle English, in which John Barbour composed *The Bruce*.
- **Recension** — a distinct revised edition or version of a text, as with the differently dedicated recensions of Gower's *Confessio Amantis*.
