## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Fourteenth- and early fifteenth-century England produced a distinctive body of vernacular and Latin mystical writing, conventionally grouped around four major figures — Richard Rolle, Walter Hilton, the anonymous author of *The Cloud of Unknowing*, and Julian of Norwich — to whom this chapter adds Margery Kempe as a related, if generically distinct, figure of the following generation. This writing developed against a religious landscape increasingly marked by anxiety over unorthodox belief: the Lollard movement (associated with John Wyclif's teachings) provoked official concern over vernacular religious writing and unauthorized claims to direct spiritual experience, a tension directly visible in Margery Kempe's repeated formal examinations for suspected heresy, discussed below. Both Julian and Margery also wrote (or dictated) as women claiming direct spiritual authority and experience in a religious culture that offered women few institutionally sanctioned channels for theological voice — making their surviving texts significant not only devotionally but as rare instances of medieval Englishwomen's authorship.

## Literary Movements

English mystical writing of this period spans a spectrum from the affective, ecstatic devotionalism of Richard Rolle, through the more contemplative, apophatic ("negative theology") tradition of *The Cloud of Unknowing* and Walter Hilton's *Scale of Perfection*, to Julian of Norwich's carefully reasoned theological reflection on a specific set of visionary experiences, and finally to Margery Kempe's distinctive genre-blending combination of visionary devotion, pilgrimage narrative, and what modern readers recognize as proto-autobiographical life-writing. This chapter's two central figures thus represent both the culmination of the English mystical tradition (Julian) and its extension into a wholly new kind of first-person vernacular prose (Margery).

## Author Profiles

**Julian of Norwich** (c. 1342–after 1416) — Little is known of her life before her illness; she took her name from St Julian's Church, Norwich, to which she was attached as an anchoress (a woman enclosed for life in a small cell adjoining a church, devoted to prayer and contemplation) following her recovery. In May 1373, aged about thirty and believing herself close to death, she experienced sixteen visionary "shewings" concerning Christ's Passion and the nature of divine love, which she first recorded in a shorter account and then, some twenty years later, expanded into the substantially longer and more theologically developed "Long Text." Her *Revelations of Divine Love* is the earliest surviving book in English known to have been written by a woman, and the earliest surviving work by an English anchorite or anchoress of either sex.

**Margery Kempe** (c. 1373–after 1438) — Laywoman of Bishop's Lynn, Norfolk, daughter of a prominent local mayor and merchant, married with fourteen children; underwent a period of severe post-partum mental crisis after her first child's birth, followed by a lifelong pattern of intense visionary experience, "holy weeping" (loud, sometimes disruptive public crying fits interpreted by Margery as spiritual gifts and by some contemporaries as suspicious or disruptive behaviour), and extensive pilgrimage, including journeys to Jerusalem, Rome, and Santiago de Compostela. Illiterate, she dictated her *Book* — describing her spiritual life, visions, pilgrimages, and repeated examinations on suspicion of Lollard heresy — to at least two scribes across the 1430s, the second beginning work in 1436. Her *Book* is widely cited as the first autobiography in English.

**Richard Rolle** (c. 1300–1349) — Yorkshire hermit and mystic, author of the *Incendium Amoris* ("Fire of Love") and other works emphasizing intense, affective, sensory experience of divine love (heat, sweetness, song); an important early figure in the English mystical tradition, later criticized by both Hilton and the *Cloud*-author for aspects of his approach.

**Walter Hilton** (d. c. 1396) — Augustinian canon, author of *The Scale of Perfection*, a systematic guide to contemplative spiritual progress, more measured and structurally disciplined than Rolle's affective mysticism.

**The *Cloud of Unknowing*-author** — Anonymous, writing in the second half of the fourteenth century; author of a contemplative guide emphasizing an apophatic ("unknowing") approach to union with God, in which the intellect must be set aside in favour of a "cloud" of loving, non-conceptual attention.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Revelations of Divine Love*** (also known as *Showings*, Julian of Norwich) — Comprising eighty-seven chapters in its Long Text form, the work moves from vivid, concrete visionary description (the Passion, the crown of thorns, Christ's suffering rendered in intensely physical detail) toward extended theological reflection on divine love, human sin, and providence, most famously articulated in the repeated assurance "All shall be well, and all shall be well, and all manner of thing shall be well." The work is also notable for its striking, theologically careful development of the image of **Christ as Mother**, exploring maternal imagery for Christ's nurturing, sustaining relationship to humanity — an unusual and much-discussed theological move for its period.
- ***The Book of Margery Kempe*** — A third-person narrative (Margery refers to herself throughout as "this creature") recounting her spiritual crises, visions, pilgrimages, difficult marriage, and repeated interrogations by ecclesiastical authorities on suspicion of Lollardy — from which she was each time cleared, her orthodoxy affirmed despite her unconventional, emotionally demonstrative piety. The *Book* includes a first-hand account of her meeting with Julian of Norwich (c. 1413–14): Margery describes travelling to Norwich and spending "many days" in conversation with "Dame Jelyan" in her anchorhold, seeking Julian's discernment as to whether Margery's visions and intense weeping were of divine or potentially diabolical origin; Julian's recorded response affirms Margery's experiences as signs of God's spirit and counsels her to endure the world's scorn as a mark of spiritual merit.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Julian's theological originality.** Critics and theologians continue to debate how far Julian's "Christ as Mother" theology and her confident universalist-leaning assurance that "all shall be well" represent an original and daring contribution to medieval theology, given that she wrote as a laywoman (before her enclosure) without formal theological training, working entirely within, and yet subtly extending, orthodox doctrine.
- **Margery Kempe: mystic, hysteric, or author?** Margery's disruptive public weeping and unconventional behaviour attracted hostility and suspicion in her own time and have prompted varied modern critical responses, ranging from straightforwardly devotional readings, to psychological/medical reinterpretation of her experiences, to literary-historical readings that foreground her *Book*'s status as a pioneering, if third-person and dictated, work of English autobiographical self-construction, regardless of how her spiritual claims are ultimately assessed.
- **The status of dictated authorship.** Because Margery was illiterate and her *Book* was produced through dictation to (at least) two scribes, critics debate the extent to which the surviving text represents Margery's own voice versus a mediated, scribally shaped account — a question with implications for how confidently the work can be treated as autobiography in a modern sense, though most scholars still regard Margery as the text's substantive author despite this mediation.
- **The significance of the Julian–Margery meeting.** Their documented encounter is of particular interest to scholars of medieval women's religious authority: Margery's decision to seek out Julian specifically for authoritative "discernment of spirits," and Julian's affirming response, illustrate both the recognized spiritual authority an anchoress like Julian could hold and the practical mechanisms by which a laywoman like Margery sought to validate her own unconventional religious experience within, rather than against, ecclesiastical norms.
- **A remarkable rediscovery.** The 1934 identification of the unique surviving *Book of Margery Kempe* manuscript — found among miscellaneous papers in a Derbyshire country house, according to family anecdote nearly destroyed for want of a ping-pong ball — is frequently cited as one of the most striking manuscript rediscovery stories in English literary history, comparable in its transformative effect on the field to the earlier recovery of Old English manuscripts discussed in Part I.

## Chapter Summary

Julian of Norwich and Margery Kempe represent the two most significant surviving voices of late medieval English women's religious writing, emerging from a broader fourteenth-century mystical tradition that also includes Richard Rolle, Walter Hilton, and the anonymous *Cloud of Unknowing*-author. Julian's *Revelations of Divine Love*, developed from sixteen visions received in 1373 into a theologically sophisticated Long Text some twenty years later, offers a carefully reasoned meditation on divine love, sin, and providence, including her distinctive "Christ as Mother" theology. Margery Kempe's dictated *Book*, widely regarded as the first autobiography in English, recounts her visions, pilgrimages, and repeated (successfully rebutted) accusations of heresy, and includes a valuable first-hand account of her meeting with Julian for spiritual counsel around 1413–14. The *Book*'s extraordinary modern textual history — its unique manuscript lost for centuries and rediscovered only in 1934 — underscores how contingent and fragile the survival of medieval women's writing has been, and how much of this Part's literary-historical record depends on such chance preservation.

## Glossary

- **Anchoress/anchorite** — a woman/man permanently enclosed in a small cell attached to a church, devoted to prayer and contemplation; Julian of Norwich's vocation.
- **Discernment of spirits** — the practice of assessing whether a claimed visionary or spiritual experience is of divine, natural, or diabolical origin, sought by Margery Kempe from Julian of Norwich.
- **Lollardy** — the unorthodox religious movement associated with John Wyclif's teachings, whose suspected adherents (including, repeatedly if unsuccessfully, Margery Kempe) faced ecclesiastical examination in this period.
- **Shewings** — Julian of Norwich's own term for the sixteen visions underlying *Revelations of Divine Love*.
