## Historical and Socio-Political Context

This chapter turns to a genuinely different kind of medieval writing: not romance, not satire, but direct accounts of religious experience, written by people convinced they had encountered God personally. Fourteenth- and early fifteenth-century England produced a distinctive body of this vernacular and Latin mystical writing, usually grouped around four major figures — Richard Rolle, Walter Hilton, the anonymous author of *The Cloud of Unknowing*, and Julian of Norwich — and this chapter adds a fifth, related figure from the following generation: Margery Kempe.

You need one piece of religious context to understand the risk these writers were taking. This period's Church was increasingly anxious about unorthodox belief, especially the Lollard movement associated with John Wyclif's teachings, which questioned Church authority and pushed for vernacular religious writing outside official control. That anxiety meant unauthorized claims to direct spiritual experience — exactly what mystical writers were making — could draw real suspicion. You'll see this tension play out directly in Margery Kempe's repeated formal examinations for suspected heresy, discussed below. There's a second layer worth flagging too: both Julian and Margery wrote, or in Margery's case dictated, as women claiming direct spiritual authority, in a religious culture that gave women very few sanctioned channels for theological voice. That makes their surviving texts doubly significant — as devotional writing, and as rare surviving instances of medieval Englishwomen's own authorship.

## Literary Movements

Think of English mystical writing in this period as a spectrum, running from the most emotional to the most reasoned. At one end sits Richard Rolle's affective, almost ecstatic devotionalism — heat, sweetness, song. Moving along, you reach the more contemplative, "apophatic" (or negative-theology) tradition of *The Cloud of Unknowing* and Walter Hilton's *Scale of Perfection*, which approach God by setting the intellect aside rather than by feeling intensely. Further along still sits Julian of Norwich, whose *Revelations of Divine Love* takes a specific set of visionary experiences and works them into careful, reasoned theological reflection. And at the far end, doing something genuinely new, sits Margery Kempe, who blends visionary devotion, pilgrimage narrative, and what modern readers recognize as an early form of autobiographical life-writing. This chapter's two central figures, then, represent two different endpoints: Julian as the mystical tradition's most theologically developed voice, and Margery as its extension into an entirely new kind of first-person vernacular prose.

## Author Profiles

**Julian of Norwich** (c. 1342–after 1416) — we know almost nothing about her life before her illness. She took her name from St Julian's Church, Norwich, where she lived as an anchoress after her recovery — an anchoress being a woman enclosed for life in a small cell attached to a church, devoted entirely to prayer and contemplation. In May 1373, around thirty years old and believing she was close to death, she experienced sixteen visionary "shewings" concerning Christ's Passion and the nature of divine love. She wrote these down first in a shorter account, then, some twenty years later, expanded them into the much longer, more theologically developed "Long Text." Her *Revelations of Divine Love* holds a double distinction: it's the earliest surviving book in English known to have been written by a woman, and the earliest surviving work by an English anchorite or anchoress of either sex.

**Margery Kempe** (c. 1373–after 1438) was a laywoman of Bishop's Lynn, Norfolk, the daughter of a prominent local mayor and merchant, and a mother of fourteen children. After the birth of her first child, she went through a period of severe post-partum crisis, which gave way to a lifelong pattern of intense visionary experience and "holy weeping" — loud, sometimes disruptive public crying fits that she took as a spiritual gift, and that some of her contemporaries took as deeply suspicious. She also travelled widely on pilgrimage, journeying as far as Jerusalem, Rome, and Santiago de Compostela. Because she was illiterate, she dictated her *Book* — describing her spiritual life, visions, pilgrimages, and repeated examinations on suspicion of Lollard heresy — to at least two scribes across the 1430s, the second of whom began work in 1436. Her *Book* is widely cited as the first autobiography in English.

**Richard Rolle** (c. 1300–1349) was a Yorkshire hermit and mystic, author of the *Incendium Amoris* ("Fire of Love") and other works built around intense, physically felt experience of divine love — heat, sweetness, song. He's an important early figure in the English mystical tradition, though both Hilton and the *Cloud*-author, below, later criticized aspects of his approach.

**Walter Hilton** (d. c. 1396) was an Augustinian canon and the author of *The Scale of Perfection*, a systematic guide to contemplative spiritual progress. Where Rolle's mysticism runs hot and sensory, Hilton's is more measured and structurally disciplined.

**The *Cloud of Unknowing*-author** is anonymous, writing in the second half of the fourteenth century. This author's contemplative guide takes an apophatic — "unknowing" — approach to union with God: rather than reasoning your way toward the divine, you set the intellect aside entirely, in favour of a "cloud" of loving, non-conceptual attention.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Revelations of Divine Love*** (also known as *Showings*, Julian of Norwich). In its Long Text form, this work runs to eighty-seven chapters, and it moves in a clear direction: from vivid, physically intense visionary description — the Passion, the crown of thorns, Christ's suffering rendered in almost bodily detail — toward extended theological reflection on divine love, human sin, and providence. Its most famous line captures that reflective mode perfectly: "All shall be well, and all shall be well, and all manner of thing shall be well." The work is also known for something theologically daring: its careful development of the image of **Christ as Mother**, exploring maternal imagery for Christ's nurturing, sustaining relationship to humanity — a genuinely unusual move for its period, and one still much discussed today.
- ***The Book of Margery Kempe***. This is a third-person narrative — Margery calls herself "this creature" throughout, never "I" — recounting her spiritual crises, visions, pilgrimages, difficult marriage, and repeated interrogation by Church authorities on suspicion of Lollardy. Each time, she was cleared, her orthodoxy affirmed despite how unconventional and emotionally demonstrative her piety looked to outsiders. The *Book* also gives us a first-hand account of her meeting with Julian of Norwich, around 1413–14: Margery describes travelling to Norwich and spending "many days" talking with "Dame Jelyan" in her anchorhold, seeking Julian's judgment on whether Margery's visions and intense weeping came from God or from somewhere more troubling. Julian's recorded answer affirms Margery's experiences as signs of God's spirit, and counsels her to endure the world's scorn as a mark of spiritual merit.

## Critical Perspectives

- **How original was Julian's theology, really?** Critics and theologians still debate how daring Julian's "Christ as Mother" imagery and her confident, almost universalist assurance that "all shall be well" actually were within medieval theology. Remember, she wrote this as a laywoman without formal theological training — which makes it all the more striking that she worked entirely within orthodox doctrine while quietly extending it in genuinely new directions.
- **Mystic, hysteric, or author — how should we read Margery Kempe?** Margery's disruptive public weeping and unconventional behaviour drew real hostility and suspicion in her own lifetime, and modern critics have responded to her in very different ways: some read her devotionally, on her own terms; others reinterpret her experiences through modern psychology or medicine; others focus on her *Book*'s literary-historical importance as a pioneering work of English autobiographical self-construction, regardless of how you assess her spiritual claims. All three approaches are legitimate starting points — just be clear about which one you're using.
- **Whose voice is this, really — Margery's, or her scribe's?** Margery was illiterate, and her *Book* reached us through dictation to at least two different scribes. That raises a genuine question: how much of the surviving text is Margery's own voice, and how much has been shaped by the scribes who wrote it down? Most scholars still treat Margery as the text's substantive author despite this mediation, but it's worth holding onto the question when you're deciding how confidently to call the *Book* an "autobiography" in the modern sense.
- **Why does the Julian–Margery meeting matter so much to scholars?** Their documented encounter interests scholars of medieval women's religious authority for a specific reason: Margery deliberately sought Julian out for authoritative "discernment of spirits," and Julian's affirming response shows both how much spiritual authority an anchoress like Julian could genuinely hold, and how a laywoman like Margery could try to validate her own unconventional religious experience by working within, rather than against, Church norms.
- **A remarkable rediscovery.** The unique manuscript of *The Book of Margery Kempe* was lost for centuries and only identified in 1934, found among miscellaneous papers in a Derbyshire country house — family anecdote even claims it nearly ended up destroyed for want of a ping-pong ball. It's one of the most striking manuscript rediscovery stories in English literary history, on a par with the recovery stories you met with Old English manuscripts back in Part I.

## Chapter Summary

Julian of Norwich and Margery Kempe are the two most significant surviving voices of late medieval English women's religious writing, emerging out of a broader fourteenth-century mystical tradition that also includes Richard Rolle, Walter Hilton, and the anonymous *Cloud of Unknowing*-author. Julian's *Revelations of Divine Love* grew from sixteen visions she received in 1373 into a theologically sophisticated Long Text some twenty years later, offering a carefully reasoned meditation on divine love, sin, and providence, including her distinctive "Christ as Mother" theology. Margery Kempe's dictated *Book*, widely regarded as the first autobiography in English, recounts her visions, pilgrimages, and repeated — and each time unsuccessful — accusations of heresy against her, and it preserves a valuable first-hand account of her meeting with Julian for spiritual counsel around 1413–14. And the *Book*'s own modern history is extraordinary in its own right: its unique manuscript was lost for centuries and rediscovered only in 1934, a reminder of just how fragile and contingent the survival of medieval women's writing has been, and how much of this Part's literary record depends on exactly this kind of chance preservation.

## Glossary

- **Anchoress/anchorite** — a woman/man permanently enclosed in a small cell attached to a church, devoted to prayer and contemplation; Julian of Norwich's vocation.
- **Discernment of spirits** — the practice of assessing whether a claimed visionary or spiritual experience is of divine, natural, or diabolical origin, sought by Margery Kempe from Julian of Norwich.
- **Lollardy** — the unorthodox religious movement associated with John Wyclif's teachings, whose suspected adherents (including, repeatedly if unsuccessfully, Margery Kempe) faced ecclesiastical examination in this period.
- **Shewings** — Julian of Norwich's own term for the sixteen visions underlying *Revelations of Divine Love*.
