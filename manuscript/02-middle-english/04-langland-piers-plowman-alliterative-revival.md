## Historical and Socio-Political Context

To understand why this chapter's poem is so angry, and so urgent, you need to know what England looked like while it was being written. From 1348 onward, England suffered repeated outbreaks of plague — the Black Death — which killed so much of the workforce that wages had to be regulated by law (the Statute of Labourers, 1351). Criticism of a wealthy, sometimes corrupt clergy was growing. The Hundred Years' War kept draining money and manpower. All of this pressure finally broke out into open rebellion in the Peasants' Revolt of 1381. *Piers Plowman* was composed and repeatedly rewritten right through this period of real social strain, and its sustained satire of corrupt clergy, dishonest officials, and social injustice, alongside its search for what genuine Christian living actually looks like, speaks directly to the world around it. As you'll see below, the poem didn't just describe this crisis from a safe distance — it got pulled directly into the events of 1381 itself, through its apparent influence on the rebel leader John Ball.

## Literary Movements

You already met the **Alliterative Revival** in Chapter 2, through *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*. *Piers Plowman* is the movement's other supreme achievement, and putting the two poems side by side teaches you something important about how flexible this old verse form actually was. Both poems use unrhymed alliterative long lines, formally descended from the Old English tradition you learned in Part I, Chapter 2, and both come out of the West Midlands and North of England, running parallel to — rather than in close contact with — the rhymed, French- and Italian-influenced verse of Chaucer's London circle (Chapter 3). But look at what each poet does with the same tool: *Sir Gawain* uses alliterative verse for chivalric romance, while *Piers Plowman* uses it for **dream-vision allegory** combined with sharp social and religious satire. Same form, two entirely different literary purposes.

## Author Profiles

**William Langland** (c. 1330–c. 1400) is almost a ghost in the historical record. Nearly everything we think we know about him comes from inference — from the poem itself, and from a handful of manuscript notes, one in a Trinity College, Dublin manuscript in particular. The main clue to his very name comes from inside the poem: in the C-text, the narrator identifies himself as "Long Wille," a phrase that plays on both his height and his own name. From scattered hints like this, scholars generally believe he was born in the Malvern Hills region, received some clerical education, possibly at Great Malvern Priory, with an eye toward the priesthood, and spent at least part of his life in London — which fits neatly with the poem's own setting, alternating between the Malvern Hills, where the dreamer's visions begin, and London itself. Whether he held minor clerical orders, married, or depended on a patron's support all remain open questions. One question that used to be contested, though, has largely been settled: whether one poet wrote all three textual versions of the poem. Current scholarship favours a single author, revising and expanding his own work over nearly three decades.

**John Ball** (d. 1381) was a radical priest and one of the leading figures of the 1381 Peasants' Revolt, executed once the revolt was crushed. His letters survive, preserved in the chronicles of Thomas Walsingham and Henry Knighton, and they contain coded allusions to *Piers Plowman* — including a direct invocation of "Piers Plowman" by name, and references to "Do Well" and "Do Better." That's genuinely rare, striking evidence: proof the poem was circulating, and carrying real political weight, within a single generation of its being written.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Piers Plowman***. The narrator, Will, falls asleep on the Malvern Hills, and what follows is a sequence of visions about the search for religious and social Truth. The poem's early sections, usually called the "Visio," give you a vivid satirical panorama of English society — ambitious in scope in a way that's genuinely comparable to Chaucer's General Prologue, though very different in tone and form — and introduce Piers the Plowman, an honest labourer who gradually becomes an almost Christ-like guiding figure. Later sections, added substantially in the B-text, turn inward: Will's search grows harder and more abstract, moving through personified figures like Holy Church, Lady Meed (who personifies Reward, or Bribery), Conscience, and Reason, in pursuit of an escalating ethical and spiritual goal called **Do-Well, Do-Bet, and Do-Best**. That search eventually leads to visions of the Crucifixion, the Harrowing of Hell, and the founding — and later corruption — of the Church.
- **Three different versions of the same poem.** *Piers Plowman* doesn't survive as a single fixed text, and it helps to know why. The **A-text** (c. 1368–75, roughly 2,500 lines) is the poem's earliest recoverable form, and it's unfinished. The **B-text** (1377–79, roughly 7,200 lines) substantially expands and completes the A-text's structure, adding the extended Do-Well/Do-Bet/Do-Best material — this is the version most widely read, edited, and translated today. The **C-text** (1382–88), close to B in length but heavily reworked in wording and emphasis, is generally read as Langland's final, more cautious revision — possibly, as you'll see below, a response to the poem's uncomfortable association with the 1381 revolt.

## Critical Perspectives

- **The "Long Will" problem: who actually wrote this?** Because we have almost no external biographical evidence for Langland, critics have debated not just his identity but whether one person really wrote all three versions. The current scholarly consensus favours a single author revising the poem over nearly three decades — but be aware that the evidence behind this consensus is thinner than for almost any other major writer in this Part.
- **Did Langland intend this poem to fuel a rebellion? Almost certainly not.** It's important to separate the poem's own evident purpose — orthodox, if sharply critical, Christian moral and social reform — from how John Ball and the 1381 rebels later used it as a rallying reference point. Langland's own attitude toward the Revolt, which broke out while he may still have been revising the poem, doesn't read as straightforwardly sympathetic to rebellion. Some scholars even read the C-text's revisions as a cautious retreat from material that could be misread as incitement — a sign of just how real the danger of that political entanglement was.
- **How do satire and allegory fit together in this poem?** The poem moves between two very different modes: vivid, concrete social satire (a corrupt friar, a dishonest brewer, Lady Meed's marriage negotiations) and abstract theological allegory (the Do-Well sequence, the Harrowing of Hell). Critics disagree about whether these two modes form one unified structure or a productive, unstable hybrid — worth deciding for yourself as you read.
- **What nearly sixty surviving manuscripts tell us.** *Piers Plowman* survives in close to sixty manuscripts across its three versions — an unusually high number for a Middle English poem, beaten in this Part only by *The Canterbury Tales*. That number matters: it tells us the poem circulated far more widely in its own time than its comparatively lower modern profile, next to Chaucer, might suggest.

## Chapter Summary

*Piers Plowman*, composed and revised across nearly three decades by the little-documented William Langland, stands with *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight* as one of the two major achievements of the fourteenth-century Alliterative Revival — taking the same inherited Old English verse form and pointing it at dream-vision allegory and pointed social and religious satire instead of chivalric romance. It survives in three progressively revised versions (A, B, and C) and in nearly sixty manuscripts, tracing the narrator Will's search for Christian Truth through vivid social panorama and increasingly abstract allegorical stages — Do-Well, Do-Bet, Do-Best. And it earned a documented, almost certainly unintended, place in real politics: John Ball invoked "Piers Plowman" by name during the 1381 Peasants' Revolt, a striking reminder of just how far vernacular English literature's social and political reach had grown by the later fourteenth century.

---

## Glossary

- **Do-Well, Do-Bet, Do-Best** — the escalating ethical/spiritual stages sought by the dreamer in *Piers Plowman*'s later sections.
- **Dream vision** — a medieval narrative genre in which the main action is presented as occurring within the narrator's dream, often for allegorical or didactic purposes.
- **Lady Meed** — an allegorical figure in *Piers Plowman* personifying reward, payment, or bribery, and the corrupting influence of money.
- **Visio** — the term commonly used for the earlier, more socially satirical section of *Piers Plowman*, preceding the Do-Well sequence.
