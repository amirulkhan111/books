## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Part VIII, Chapter 1, already traced how Freud's theory of the unconscious, alongside Einstein's relativity and Bergson's philosophy of time, unsettled Victorian assumptions and helped shape literary modernism's formal experiments. This chapter turns to a related but distinct question: not how Freud's ideas influenced writers generally, but how Freud himself, and later critics working directly from his theories, developed psychoanalysis into a specific method for interpreting literary texts. Freud wrote directly about literature on several occasions, and later critics, above all Jacques Lacan and Harold Bloom, developed his insights in genuinely different directions — Lacan by fusing psychoanalysis with the structuralist linguistics you met in Chapter 6, and Bloom by using Freud's model of psychological conflict to describe how poets relate not to their own unconscious minds, but to the poetic tradition, already discussed through Eliot's very different account of it in Chapter 5.

## Literary Movements

Freud's essay "Creative Writers and Day-Dreaming" (1908) makes his most direct statement on literature itself: he compares the writer's imaginative work to a child's play and an adult's daydream, arguing that a story's events, however elaborated, are disguised fulfillments of the writer's own unconscious wishes, transformed and made acceptable to a reader through aesthetic pleasure — what Freud calls an "incentive bonus" that allows readers to enjoy fantasies they might otherwise find embarrassing or disturbing if presented undisguised. His later essay "The Uncanny" (1919), analyzing E. T. A. Hoffmann's story "The Sandman," introduces the concept of the **uncanny** (*das Unheimliche*): the peculiar dread produced when something long familiar and repressed returns in unfamiliar, unsettling form — a concept with obvious relevance to the Gothic fiction you met in Part VI, Chapter 9, and one that gave psychoanalytic criticism its first sustained, text-specific method, distinct from Freud's broader theory of the unconscious already discussed in Part VIII, Chapter 1.

Freud's **Oedipus complex** — the theory that a child unconsciously desires the parent of the opposite sex and rivals the parent of the same sex — takes its name directly from Sophocles's tragedy *Oedipus Rex*, the very play Aristotle's *Poetics*, already met in Chapter 1, treats as tragedy's finest example. This concept became a template later critics applied directly to literary texts: Ernest Jones's *Hamlet and Oedipus* (1949) argued that Hamlet's paralysis before avenging his father stems from his own unconscious guilt, since Claudius has actually done what Hamlet himself unconsciously desired — killing his father and possessing his mother — a reading that treats a literary character as though he had an actual unconscious mind capable of psychoanalytic diagnosis.

Jacques Lacan, developing what he called a "return to Freud," fused psychoanalysis with the structuralist linguistics of Saussure, already met in Chapter 6, arguing that "the unconscious is structured like a language" — governed by relations and substitutions among signifiers, rather than existing as a repository of fixed, recoverable contents beneath consciousness. His famous 1955 seminar on Edgar Allan Poe's "The Purloined Letter" reads the story's stolen letter as a signifier whose meaning is never fixed by its actual contents (which the story never reveals) but by its shifting position among the characters who possess, seek, or are blackmailed by it — a reading later subjected to sharp critique by Jacques Derrida, already met in Chapter 7, who argued Lacan's own analysis smuggled back in exactly the stable, centered meaning his theory claimed to reject.

Harold Bloom's *The Anxiety of Influence* (1973) applies a psychoanalytic model to literary tradition itself, in direct, productive tension with Eliot's account of tradition in "Tradition and the Individual Talent," already met in Chapter 5. Where Eliot described a poet's relationship to earlier writers as essentially harmonious — a talented new poet joining and subtly reshaping a tradition experienced as existing "simultaneously" — Bloom describes it as an anxious, competitive, almost Oedipal struggle: every strong poet must, consciously or not, misread and defensively revise a powerful poetic "precursor" in order to clear imaginative space for their own original work, a process Bloom analyzes through six "revisionary ratios" describing different strategies of creative misreading.

## Author Profiles

**Sigmund Freud** (1856–1939), already profiled in Part VIII, Chapter 1, for his general influence on modernist thought, appears here for his direct literary criticism: "Creative Writers and Day-Dreaming" (1908) and "The Uncanny" (1919), discussed above.

**Ernest Jones** (1879–1958), a British psychoanalyst and Freud's official biographer, applied Freudian theory directly to Shakespeare's *Hamlet* in *Hamlet and Oedipus* (1949).

**Jacques Lacan** (1901–1981), a French psychoanalyst, developed a structuralist-influenced "return to Freud," discussed above, most famously through his 1955 seminar on Poe's "The Purloined Letter."

**Harold Bloom** (1930–2019), an American literary critic, developed the psychoanalytic theory of poetic influence discussed above in *The Anxiety of Influence* (1973).

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Freud, "Creative Writers and Day-Dreaming" (1908).** Freud's comparison of literary creation to daydreaming argues that a story disguises the writer's own unconscious wishes, made pleasurable to readers through aesthetic form's "incentive bonus" — a founding statement of psychoanalytic literary theory distinct from Freud's broader theory of the unconscious (Part VIII, Chapter 1).
- **Freud, "The Uncanny" (1919).** Analyzing Hoffmann's "The Sandman," Freud defines the uncanny as dread produced when something familiar and repressed returns in unfamiliar form — a concept directly relevant to the Gothic fiction of Part VI, Chapter 9, though developed independently of that literary-historical context.
- **Jones, *Hamlet and Oedipus* (1949).** Jones's reading of Hamlet's paralysis as unconscious guilt over desires Claudius has actually enacted exemplifies psychoanalytic criticism's characteristic move: treating a literary character as though possessing an actual unconscious mind.
- **Lacan's seminar on "The Purloined Letter" (1955).** Lacan's structuralist-psychoanalytic reading treats Poe's stolen letter as a signifier whose meaning depends entirely on its shifting position among the story's characters, directly applying Saussurean principles (Chapter 6) to psychoanalytic interpretation.
- **Bloom, *The Anxiety of Influence* (1973).** Bloom's model of poetic influence as an anxious, competitive struggle with a powerful precursor stands in direct, productive tension with Eliot's more harmonious account of tradition (Chapter 5).

---

## Critical Perspectives

- **Can a literary character coherently be said to have an unconscious mind?** Ernest Jones's reading of Hamlet treats a fictional character as though he possessed the same psychological structure as a real analytic patient — a move some critics find illuminating and others consider a category error, since Hamlet has no actual childhood or repressed memories beyond what the play itself supplies.
- **Did Derrida's critique of Lacan's Poe reading (Chapter 7) expose a genuine contradiction?** Derrida argued that Lacan's claim about the letter's endlessly shifting signification nonetheless smuggled back a stable, "true" meaning through the story's own resolution — a debate that shows psychoanalytic and post-structuralist criticism, despite real overlaps, are not simply the same method in different vocabulary.
- **Is Bloom's "anxiety of influence" a more honest account of literary tradition than Eliot's?** Eliot's impersonal, harmonious tradition (Chapter 5) and Bloom's anxious, competitive one describe the same basic phenomenon — how new poets relate to powerful predecessors — in strikingly different emotional registers, and deciding between them says as much about which model of creative psychology you find more plausible as it does about literary history itself.

## Chapter Summary

This chapter distinguished Freud's general influence on modernist thought, already met in Part VIII, Chapter 1, from his direct contributions to literary criticism: "Creative Writers and Day-Dreaming" (1908), comparing literary creation to daydreaming, and "The Uncanny" (1919), analyzing Hoffmann's "The Sandman" and introducing a concept directly relevant to the Gothic fiction of Part VI, Chapter 9. The Oedipus complex, named for Sophocles's tragedy, treated by Aristotle's *Poetics* (Chapter 1) as tragedy's finest example, became a template for later criticism, exemplified by Ernest Jones's *Hamlet and Oedipus* (1949). Jacques Lacan's structuralist "return to Freud" applied Saussurean principles (Chapter 6) to psychoanalysis, most famously in his seminar on Poe's "The Purloined Letter," later critiqued by Derrida (Chapter 7). Harold Bloom's *The Anxiety of Influence* (1973) offered a psychoanalytic rival to Eliot's harmonious theory of tradition (Chapter 5), describing poetic influence as an anxious, competitive struggle rather than peaceful inheritance. Chapter 10 now turns to feminist criticism, which will bring a comparably searching, if differently grounded, critique to bear on literature's treatment of gender.

## Glossary

- **The uncanny (*das Unheimliche*)** — Freud's term for the dread produced when something familiar and repressed returns in unfamiliar form.
- **Oedipus complex** — Freud's theory of a child's unconscious desire for the opposite-sex parent and rivalry with the same-sex parent, named for Sophocles's tragedy.
- **"The unconscious is structured like a language"** — Lacan's structuralist reformulation of Freud, treating the unconscious as governed by relations among signifiers rather than fixed contents.
- **Anxiety of influence** — Harold Bloom's term for a poet's anxious, competitive struggle with a powerful precursor, requiring creative misreading to clear imaginative space.
