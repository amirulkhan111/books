## Historical and Socio-Political Context

This Part's final chapter turns to two of literary criticism's most recently developed methods, both emerging significantly since the 1990s and both, in genuinely different ways, responses to a changing world: growing ecological crisis on one hand, and the digital transformation of how texts are stored, searched, and analyzed on the other. Neither method arrived from nowhere. Ecocriticism draws directly on the cultural materialism of Chapter 8 and the critique of binary hierarchies traced through feminist criticism in Chapter 10; the digital humanities defines itself in explicit, deliberate contrast to the close reading founded by I. A. Richards in Chapter 5. Studying both together, as this chapter's final act in Part XI's long survey, shows literary criticism continuing to develop new methods in direct, traceable relationship to everything that came before, rather than simply replacing old questions with entirely new ones.

## Literary Movements

Cheryll Glotfelty's introduction to *The Ecocriticism Reader* (1996) gave the emerging field its founding definition: **ecocriticism** is "the study of the relationship between literature and the physical environment," a deliberately broad framing meant to bring ecological concern into literary study as directly as feminist criticism (Chapter 10) had brought gender, or Marxist criticism (Chapter 8) had brought economic class. That connection to Chapter 8 is more than an analogy: Raymond Williams's *The Country and the City* (1973), extending the cultural materialism you met through his challenge to Arnold's definition of culture, examined the **pastoral** literary tradition — a mode this book has traced from Marvell's country-house poems through Wordsworth's nature poetry to Hardy's Wessex — as itself an ideological construction, one that often idealized rural life in ways that obscured the real agricultural labor and economic exploitation actually taking place in the landscapes pastoral literature idealized. Ecocriticism took this same skeptical, historically grounded attention to the pastoral tradition and extended it into a full critical method centered on ecological, rather than only economic, questions.

**Ecofeminism**, developed by theorists including Val Plumwood, connects ecocriticism directly to the critique of binary hierarchies you traced through feminist criticism in Chapter 10: Plumwood argued that Western thought's habitual pairing of culture over nature closely parallels, and reinforces, its pairing of male over female, meaning the domination of nature and the domination of women share a common ideological structure worth analyzing together rather than separately. This broad ecological concern gained further urgency from the concept of the **Anthropocene**, popularized by the chemist Paul Crutzen around 2000 to name a proposed new geological epoch defined by irreversible human impact on the planet — a framing many ecocritics have since used to read literature, including literature written long before the term existed, for what it reveals about humanity's relationship to a natural world it has permanently altered.

The **digital humanities**, this chapter's second subject, emerged from a genuinely different set of concerns: the application of computational methods to literary texts at a scale no individual reader could manage unaided. Franco Moretti's *Graphs, Maps, Trees* (2005) coined the deliberately provocative term **distant reading**, positioned as the direct opposite of the close reading founded by I. A. Richards and the New Criticism (Chapter 5): rather than reading a single text with painstaking attention to its internal detail, distant reading analyzes patterns across hundreds or thousands of texts simultaneously — genre trends, narrative structures, vocabulary shifts — visible only at a scale individual close reading cannot reach. A related computational method, **stylometry**, statistically analyzes an author's characteristic word choices and sentence patterns to help settle disputed questions of authorship — exactly the method, already glimpsed in Part III, Chapter 6, that modern scholars have applied to the "upstart crow" pamphlet attacking the young Shakespeare, using computer-assisted stylistic comparison to suggest the actual author may have been Thomas Nashe rather than Robert Greene.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Williams, *The Country and the City* (1973).** Extending the cultural materialism of Chapter 8, Williams traces the pastoral tradition's long literary history, arguing that its idealized rural landscapes frequently obscured the real labor and exploitation of the actual countryside — a direct bridge between Marxist criticism's attention to material conditions and ecocriticism's attention to the environment those conditions shape.
- **Plumwood, *Feminism and the Mastery of Nature* (1993).** Plumwood's ecofeminism argues that the culture/nature hierarchy parallels and reinforces the male/female hierarchy already examined through feminist criticism (Chapter 10), suggesting environmental and gender domination share a common ideological root.
- **Moretti, *Graphs, Maps, Trees* (2005): distant reading.** Moretti's method analyzes literary patterns across large corpora of texts computationally, deliberately inverting the close reading founded by Richards and the New Criticism (Chapter 5), and asking what literary history looks like at a scale no individual reader could manage.
- **Computational stylometry and the "upstart crow" question.** Already glimpsed in Part III, Chapter 6, stylometric analysis — statistically comparing an author's characteristic word choices and sentence patterns — offers a concrete, practical instance of digital humanities method resolving a genuine scholarly dispute about authorship.

---

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does ecocriticism's broad definition risk making it too diffuse a method?** Glotfelty's deliberately wide framing — "literature and the physical environment" — invites an enormous range of texts and questions, which some critics find genuinely inclusive and others consider too unfocused to constitute a single, coherent critical method.
- **Does distant reading lose something close reading preserves?** Moretti's method reveals large-scale patterns invisible to any individual reader, but by design sacrifices the close attention to a single text's specific language that Chapters 5 through 11 have largely assumed is criticism's proper object — a genuine methodological trade-off rather than a simple improvement or loss.
- **Can computational stylometry settle authorship questions definitively?** The Shakespeare/Nashe "upstart crow" case (Part III, Chapter 6) shows stylometric analysis offering genuine evidence, but statistical pattern-matching provides probability rather than certainty, meaning computational methods supplement rather than replace traditional scholarly judgment.

This Part's final chapter traced two of literary criticism's most recently developed methods. ## Glossary

- **Ecocriticism** — the study of literature's relationship to the physical environment, founded on Cheryll Glotfelty's definition.
- **Pastoral** — the literary tradition idealizing rural life and landscape, critiqued by Raymond Williams as often obscuring real agricultural labor and exploitation.
- **Ecofeminism** — Val Plumwood's critique connecting the domination of nature to the domination of women through a shared culture/nature, male/female binary hierarchy.
- **Anthropocene** — a proposed geological epoch defined by irreversible human impact on the planet, popularized by Paul Crutzen around 2000.
- **Distant reading** — Franco Moretti's method of analyzing literary patterns across large corpora of texts computationally, deliberately opposed to close reading.
- **Stylometry** — computational, statistical analysis of an author's characteristic word choices and sentence patterns, used to help resolve disputed questions of authorship.
