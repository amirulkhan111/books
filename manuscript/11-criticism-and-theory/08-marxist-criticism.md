## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapters 5 through 7 traced a long arc in which criticism increasingly bracketed out historical and material context: the New Criticism excluded authorial intention, structuralism analyzed texts as instances of impersonal systems, and post-structuralism denied that any stable meaning could be recovered at all. Marxist criticism, this chapter's subject, reintroduces exactly the context those methods excluded — but does so from a specific theoretical foundation: Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels's argument, developed across their nineteenth-century writings, that a society's economic **base** (its modes and relations of production) fundamentally shapes its ideological **superstructure** — law, politics, religion, philosophy, and art, literature among them. On this model, literature is never simply an autonomous aesthetic object, as Chapters 5 and 6 largely treated it, but a product of, and a participant in, the social and economic relations of its own historical moment.

Marxist literary criticism developed considerably beyond this basic model across the twentieth century, through Hungarian, Italian, and British critics working in genuinely different national and institutional contexts. This chapter traces that development from Marx's own scattered comments on literature through Georg Lukács's theory of realism, Antonio Gramsci's concept of cultural hegemony, and Raymond Williams's specifically British cultural materialism — the last of which engages directly with Arnold's definition of culture, already met in Chapter 4, showing this Part's critical conversation continuing to build across chapters rather than simply proceeding chronologically.

## Literary Movements

Marx and Engels themselves wrote relatively little sustained literary criticism, but a single 1888 letter from Marx to the novelist Margaret Harkness became Marxist criticism's founding anecdote. Marx praises Honoré de Balzac's fiction as "a triumph of realism," precisely because Balzac's novels, however much their author's own conscious political sympathies leaned toward a nostalgic, aristocratic royalism, nonetheless depicted the actual rising power of the French bourgeoisie with such faithful, concrete detail that the fiction's social truth effectively overrode its author's stated politics. This single observation gave later Marxist criticism its central, recurring insight: a text's ideological significance need not match its author's conscious intentions, a materialist echo of the "death of the author" you met, on entirely different grounds, in Chapter 7.

Georg Lukács developed this insight into a full theory of **realism** and **totality**: the greatest realist novelists — Balzac, Tolstoy, Walter Scott — created concrete, "typical" characters who embodied entire social forces and historical tendencies, thereby revealing the underlying **totality** of their era's social relations, whether or not they intended to. Lukács used this standard to mount a genuinely controversial critique of literary modernism, arguing that writers like Kafka and Joyce, already met in Part VIII, Chapter 4, fragmented and atomized reality rather than revealing its coherent totality — a decadent retreat, in Lukács's judgment, from realism's fuller social vision, even as this book's own earlier chapters have treated *Ulysses* and its modernist contemporaries as major artistic achievements in their own right.

Antonio Gramsci, writing from an Italian Fascist prison in the 1930s, developed a considerably more flexible account of how ruling-class power actually operates: **hegemony**, the process by which a dominant class secures not merely forced compliance but genuine cultural consent, so that its own particular interests come to feel like simple, unquestioned "common sense" to the society it rules. You have already glimpsed Gramsci's influence indirectly: his term "subaltern," describing subordinated groups without access to power or representation, was the direct source for Gayatri Spivak's postcolonial critique, already met in Part X, Chapter 1.

Raymond Williams, writing in Britain in the late 1950s, extended Marxist criticism into what he called **cultural materialism**, arguing in *Culture and Society, 1780–1950* (1958) that "culture" should be understood as an entire, lived "whole way of life," not merely the narrower canon of great works Arnold, already met in Chapter 4, had in mind with his phrase "the best that has been thought and said." Williams's related concept of **structures of feeling**, from *The Long Revolution* (1961), names the lived, still-emerging quality of a generation's actual experience — felt and expressed in literature, often before it hardens into fully articulated ideology or formal belief — giving Marxist criticism a genuinely subtle tool for describing how literature registers social change as it happens, rather than only after the fact.

## Author Profiles

**Karl Marx** (1818–1883) and **Friedrich Engels** (1820–1895) developed the base/superstructure model across their major nineteenth-century writings, and Marx's 1888 letter to Margaret Harkness on Balzac became Marxist literary criticism's founding anecdote.

**Georg Lukács** (1885–1971), a Hungarian Marxist philosopher and critic, developed the theory of realism and totality discussed above, and mounted an influential, controversial critique of literary modernism from this same standard.

**Antonio Gramsci** (1891–1937), an Italian Marxist theorist imprisoned by Mussolini's Fascist government from 1926 until shortly before his death, developed the concept of cultural hegemony in his *Prison Notebooks*, and his term "subaltern" directly shaped Gayatri Spivak's postcolonial theory, already met in Part X, Chapter 1.

**Raymond Williams** (1921–1988), a Welsh-born British critic, founded cultural materialism, extending Marxist criticism into a specifically British idiom that directly engaged Arnold's definition of culture, already met in Chapter 4.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Marx's 1888 letter to Margaret Harkness.** Marx's praise for Balzac's realism, despite Balzac's own royalist politics, establishes Marxist criticism's founding insight: a text's social truth can exceed, or even contradict, its author's conscious intentions.
- **Lukács, theory of realism and totality (1930s).** Lukács's account of the "typical" character as a vehicle for revealing an era's total social relations gave Marxist criticism a positive aesthetic standard, one he used to criticize modernist fragmentation in writers like Kafka and Joyce (Part VIII, Chapter 4) as a retreat from realism's fuller social vision.
- **Gramsci, *Prison Notebooks*: hegemony.** Gramsci's account of cultural consent, rather than pure coercion, as the mechanism of class power gave later criticism, including Spivak's postcolonial theory (Part X, Chapter 1), a vocabulary for describing how domination operates through culture itself.
- **Williams, *Culture and Society, 1780–1950* (1958) and *The Long Revolution* (1961).** Williams's "whole way of life" definition of culture directly challenges Arnold's narrower "best that has been thought and said" (Chapter 4), while "structures of feeling" describes literature's capacity to register a generation's lived experience before it becomes explicit ideology.

---

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Lukács right to prefer realism over modernism?** Lukács's critique of Kafka and Joyce as decadent and fragmenting raises a genuine question this book's own earlier, favorable treatment of modernist fiction (Part VIII, Chapter 4) implicitly answers differently — worth weighing directly rather than assuming either verdict is simply correct.
- **Does the base/superstructure model risk reducing literature to mere economic reflection?** Critics have long worried that treating art as straightforwardly determined by economic base flattens literature's genuine complexity; Gramsci's hegemony and Williams's structures of feeling were both developed partly to correct this risk, allowing literature relative autonomy and a more active, mediating cultural role.
- **Does Williams's "whole way of life" definition of culture improve on Arnold's, or lose something Arnold's narrower standard protected?** Williams's broader definition includes ordinary, lived experience Arnold's canon-focused standard excludes, but critics sympathetic to Arnold might ask whether this breadth loses any standard at all for distinguishing better from worse literary achievement.

## Glossary

- **Base / superstructure** — Marx and Engels's model in which a society's economic base (modes and relations of production) fundamentally shapes its ideological superstructure, including art and literature.
- **Totality** — Georg Lukács's term for the coherent underlying structure of an era's social relations, which he argued great realist fiction reveals through "typical" characters.
- **Hegemony** — Antonio Gramsci's term for a dominant class's ability to secure cultural consent, rather than mere coercion, so its interests come to feel like unquestioned common sense.
- **Cultural materialism** — Raymond Williams's extension of Marxist criticism, treating culture as an entire "whole way of life" rather than a narrower canon of great works.
- **Structures of feeling** — Williams's term for the lived, still-emerging quality of a generation's actual experience, registered in literature before it hardens into explicit ideology.
