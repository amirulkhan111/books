## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 5 ended with the New Criticism's insistence that a poem's meaning resides in its own autonomous text, independent of author or reader. This chapter traces a related but distinct development, occurring largely outside England and America: a movement that treated literature not merely as an autonomous object for close reading, but as a system governed by identifiable, describable structures — as scientific, in ambition, as linguistics itself. This movement began with **Russian Formalism**, a loose group of critics working in St Petersburg and Moscow in the 1910s and 1920s, who focused on literary devices and techniques as objects of systematic analysis in their own right. Formalism's timing proved politically dangerous: by the 1930s, Stalinist cultural authorities had denounced Formalism as politically suspect for its indifference to social content, favoring the officially sanctioned doctrine of Socialist Realism instead, and many Formalist critics fell silent, were forced into other work, or emigrated. Roman Jakobson, one of the movement's leading figures, left the Soviet Union, eventually settling in the United States, where his later collaboration with the French anthropologist Claude Lévi-Strauss helped carry Formalism's central insights into a new movement: **structuralism**, which flourished in France through the 1950s and 1960s and drew its theoretical foundation from the linguist Ferdinand de Saussure, whose own lectures, delivered in Geneva before the First World War and published posthumously by his students in 1916, had already proposed that language itself is best understood as a self-contained system of relations rather than a simple set of labels attached to things.

## Literary Movements

Russian Formalism's central claim, developed in Viktor Shklovsky's 1917 essay "Art as Technique," is that art's essential function is **defamiliarization** (*ostranenie*, sometimes translated "estrangement"): habitual perception dulls our attention to the world, making familiar objects and experiences effectively invisible through overuse, and literary technique's real purpose is to disrupt this habitual perception, making the familiar strange again so it can be genuinely, freshly seen. This is a different emphasis from the New Criticism's close reading (Chapter 5): where American New Critics analyzed a poem's internal paradox and unity, Russian Formalists analyzed the specific devices — unusual word order, unexpected imagery, disrupted narrative sequence — that produce this perceptual jolt, treating "literariness" itself, rather than any single poem's meaning, as their object of study.

Vladimir Propp's *Morphology of the Folktale* (1928) extended this formalist project to narrative structure specifically. Analyzing a large corpus of Russian fairy tales, Propp identified **31 recurring narrative functions** — recurring plot events, such as "the hero leaves home" or "the villain is punished" — that appeared, he argued, in a fixed sequence across the entire genre, regardless of which specific characters or settings any individual tale used. This was a genuinely structuralist insight before structuralism had a name: a story's specific content (a particular hero, a particular villain) could vary enormously while its underlying structure remained constant, suggesting that narrative itself has an describable grammar, much as a language has one.

That linguistic parallel was not incidental. Ferdinand de Saussure's *Course in General Linguistics* (1916) had already argued that language is a self-contained system of **signs**, each an arbitrary union of a **signifier** (a sound or written form) and a **signified** (the concept it evokes) — arbitrary because no natural connection links the word "tree" to actual trees; only social convention within a language system does. Saussure further distinguished **langue**, a language's whole underlying system of rules and relations, from **parole**, any individual act of speech using that system, and argued that a sign's meaning comes not from any inherent connection to reality but from its position of *difference* within the whole system — "tree" means what it means partly because it is not "free" or "three." Roman Jakobson and Claude Lévi-Strauss extended these Saussurean principles well beyond linguistics proper: Lévi-Strauss's *Structural Anthropology* (1958) analyzed myths across cultures as systems of underlying binary oppositions (raw/cooked, nature/culture), and Roland Barthes applied the same structuralist method directly to literary narrative, most influentially in his 1966 essay "Introduction to the Structural Analysis of Narratives," while his earlier *Mythologies* (1957) analyzed contemporary popular culture — advertising, wrestling, food — as a "second-order" system of signs generating cultural myth from ordinary objects.

## Author Profiles

**Viktor Shklovsky** (1893–1984) was a founding member of OPOJAZ, the St Petersburg Formalist circle, whose 1917 essay "Art as Technique" introduced defamiliarization as literary art's essential function.

**Vladimir Propp** (1895–1970) was a Russian folklorist whose *Morphology of the Folktale* (1928) identified 31 recurring narrative functions across Russian fairy tales, an early structuralist analysis of narrative form.

**Ferdinand de Saussure** (1857–1913) was a Swiss linguist whose Geneva lectures, published posthumously by his students as the *Course in General Linguistics* (1916), founded structural linguistics and, indirectly, structuralist criticism generally.

**Roman Jakobson** (1896–1982) bridged Russian Formalism and French structuralism directly, emigrating from the Soviet Union and later collaborating with Lévi-Strauss on structuralist analysis of poetic language.

**Claude Lévi-Strauss** (1908–2009) and **Roland Barthes** (1915–1980), leading French structuralists, extended Saussure's linguistic model to myth and narrative respectively, in *Structural Anthropology* (1958) and *Mythologies* (1957) and later structuralist narrative essays.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Shklovsky, "Art as Technique" (1917).** Shklovsky argues that habitual perception makes experience "automatic" and effectively unseen, and that art's real function is to defamiliarize ordinary objects and experiences through deliberately difficult, unfamiliar technique, forcing renewed, effortful perception rather than passive recognition.
- **Propp, *Morphology of the Folktale* (1928).** By identifying a fixed sequence of 31 narrative functions recurring across an entire genre regardless of specific content, Propp demonstrated that narrative structure could be studied as an object independent of any individual story's particular characters or setting — a founding document of what would later be called **narratology**.
- **Saussure, *Course in General Linguistics* (1916).** Saussure's arbitrary sign, and his argument that meaning arises from a sign's position of difference within a whole system rather than from any inherent connection to reality, gave later structuralist critics their central theoretical tool for analyzing literature and culture as systems of relations.
- **Lévi-Strauss, *Structural Anthropology* (1958), and Barthes, *Mythologies* (1957).** Lévi-Strauss analyzed myths across cultures as systems built on underlying binary oppositions; Barthes analyzed contemporary popular culture as a "second-order" sign system generating cultural myth from ordinary objects — both applying Saussure's linguistic model well beyond language itself.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Was Stalinist suppression of Formalism purely political, or did it identify a genuine limitation?** Soviet authorities denounced Formalism for ignoring social and political content in favor of pure technique, a charge partly motivated by political control but also raising a real question this chapter leaves open: does analyzing literary devices in isolation risk missing what literature is actually *about*?
- **Does Propp's 31-function model flatten individual fairy tales' distinctiveness?** Identifying a fixed underlying structure across an entire genre is a genuine analytical achievement, but critics have asked whether it also erases exactly the specific details — a particular tale's imagery, tone, or cultural context — that make individual stories worth reading rather than merely instances of a shared formula.
- **Does treating literature as a linguistic system risk removing the author and the world from criticism entirely?** Structuralism's Saussurean foundations continue, and intensify, the New Criticism's exclusion of authorial intention (Chapter 5), but do so on a different basis — treating texts as instances of an impersonal system rather than autonomous verbal icons — a distinction later chapters on post-structuralism (Chapter 7) will push considerably further.

## Chapter Summary

Russian Formalism, developed by critics including Viktor Shklovsky and Vladimir Propp in 1910s–1920s St Petersburg and Moscow, treated literary devices and narrative structure as objects of systematic analysis, before Stalinist authorities denounced the movement in the 1930s for its indifference to social content. Shklovsky's concept of defamiliarization held that art's function is to disrupt habitual perception, while Propp's *Morphology of the Folktale* (1928) identified 31 recurring narrative functions across an entire genre, an early structuralist insight. Roman Jakobson carried Formalism's insights into French structuralism, which drew its theoretical foundation from Ferdinand de Saussure's *Course in General Linguistics* (1916): the arbitrary sign, the signifier/signified distinction, and meaning as a function of difference within a system. Claude Lévi-Strauss and Roland Barthes extended this linguistic model to myth and narrative generally, treating literature and culture as systems of relations rather than direct reflections of reality or authorial psychology — this Part's sixth distinct critical method. Chapter 7 now turns to post-structuralism and deconstruction, which will challenge structuralism's own confidence that such systems can be fully and stably described.

## Glossary

- **Defamiliarization (*ostranenie*)** — Viktor Shklovsky's term for art's function of disrupting habitual perception, making the familiar strange again.
- **Narrative function** — Vladimir Propp's term for a recurring plot event (e.g., "the hero leaves home") appearing in a fixed sequence across an entire genre of narrative.
- **Sign** — in Saussure's linguistics, the arbitrary union of a signifier (sound or written form) and a signified (concept), the basic unit of meaning in a language system.
- **Langue / parole** — Saussure's distinction between a language's whole underlying system of rules (langue) and any individual act of speech using that system (parole).
- **Structuralism** — the critical movement, drawing on Saussurean linguistics, that analyzes literature and culture as systems of underlying relations and oppositions rather than direct reflections of reality.
