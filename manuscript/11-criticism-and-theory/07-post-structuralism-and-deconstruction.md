## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 6 left structuralism confident that language, myth, and narrative could be analyzed as stable systems of underlying relations — Saussure's langue, Propp's narrative functions, Lévi-Strauss's binary oppositions. Post-structuralism, this chapter's subject, emerged in France in the mid-to-late 1960s as an internal challenge to exactly that confidence, arguing that the systems structuralism described were never as stable, complete, or self-consistent as structuralist method assumed. The moment is often dated with a genuine historical irony: in 1966, Johns Hopkins University hosted a conference explicitly organized to introduce French structuralism to American academic audiences, and the young philosopher Jacques Derrida delivered a lecture, "Structure, Sign, and Play in the Discourse of the Human Sciences," that instead announced structuralism's own internal limits — effectively launching post-structuralism at the very event meant to celebrate structuralism's arrival. This same period saw France's May 1968 student and worker protests, a broader climate of skepticism toward established institutional authority that post-structuralist thought, with its persistent suspicion of any claim to fixed, total systems of meaning, both reflected and helped articulate. Post-structuralist criticism subsequently exercised enormous influence on American literary criticism through the "Yale School" of the 1970s and 1980s, including critics like Paul de Man, J. Hillis Miller, and Geoffrey Hartman, who applied Derrida's methods directly to English and American literary texts.

## Literary Movements

Derrida's central concept, **différance** (a term he coined, combining the French words for "to differ" and "to defer"), argues that Saussure's own insight — that a sign's meaning arises from its difference from other signs within a system, not from any inherent connection to reality — actually undermines structuralism's confidence in stable meaning rather than supporting it. If a signifier's meaning depends entirely on its difference from other signifiers, Derrida argues, then meaning is never fully, finally present in any single sign; it is endlessly deferred along a chain of further signifiers, each meaningful only in relation to still others, with no final signified ever actually arrived at. This challenges what Derrida calls **logocentrism**: Western philosophy's long-standing assumption that some stable, self-present foundation — a "transcendental signified," an origin, a fixed truth — ultimately grounds and stabilizes meaning. **Deconstruction**, Derrida's practice of reading, examines how a text's own internal logic undermines the very hierarchical oppositions (speech over writing, presence over absence, nature over culture) it appears to rely on, revealing instability and contradiction where structuralist analysis had found stable, describable system.

This same skepticism toward fixed meaning and stable authority extended, in a closely related development, to the author's role in producing a text's meaning. Roland Barthes, whose earlier structuralist narrative analysis you met in Chapter 6, published "The Death of the Author" (1967/1968) at almost exactly this same moment, arguing that a text is not a message transmitted by a single, authoritative author, but "a tissue of quotations" drawn from innumerable prior texts and cultural codes — meaning, Barthes insists, is produced by the reader's own act of reading, not delivered ready-made by the author's intention. This goes considerably further than the intentional fallacy you met in Chapter 5: where Wimsatt and Beardsley argued that authorial intention is simply not legitimate *evidence* for a poem's single, correct meaning (which close reading could still, in their view, recover from the text itself), Barthes denies that any single, correct, recoverable meaning exists at all. Michel Foucault's related essay "What Is an Author?" (1969) pushed further still, arguing that "the author" is not a natural fact about how texts are produced, but a historically specific **author-function** — a category that emerged at a particular moment to regulate, limit, and assign responsibility for a text's proliferating possible meanings, rather than an eternal truth about how writing actually works.

## Author Profiles

**Jacques Derrida** (1930–2004), an Algerian-born French philosopher, founded deconstruction with *Of Grammatology* and *Writing and Difference* (both 1967), following his landmark 1966 Johns Hopkins lecture, discussed above.

**Roland Barthes** (1915–1980), already profiled in Chapter 6 for his structuralist narrative analysis, appears here again for "The Death of the Author" (1967/1968), a text that marks his own transition from structuralist to post-structuralist criticism.

**Michel Foucault** (1926–1984) was a French philosopher and historian whose essay "What Is an Author?" (1969) argued that authorship is a historically constructed function rather than a natural fact about textual production.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Derrida, "Structure, Sign, and Play in the Discourse of the Human Sciences" (1966).** Delivered at a conference meant to introduce structuralism to America, Derrida's lecture argues that every structuralist system requires an organizing "center" that is paradoxically both inside and outside the system it structures — a foundational instability Derrida finds in structuralist thought generally, including Lévi-Strauss's own anthropology.
- **Derrida, *différance* and logocentrism, from *Of Grammatology* (1967).** Différance names meaning's endless deferral along a chain of signifiers, never arriving at a final, stable signified; logocentrism names Western philosophy's assumption that some such stable foundation must nonetheless exist, an assumption deconstruction's readings persistently unsettle.
- **Barthes, "The Death of the Author" (1967/1968).** Barthes's claim that a text is "a tissue of quotations" whose meaning is produced by the reader, not delivered by authorial intention, extends the intentional fallacy of Chapter 5 into a full denial of any single, recoverable, authorially guaranteed meaning.
- **Foucault, "What Is an Author?" (1969).** Foucault's "author-function" reframes authorship itself as a historically specific category regulating discourse — assigning responsibility, limiting proliferating meaning, enabling certain texts to be gathered under a single name — rather than a simple, natural fact about who wrote a given text.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does deconstruction make literary interpretation impossible, or does it describe interpretation's genuine open-endedness more honestly?** Critics unsympathetic to Derrida have argued that if meaning is truly endlessly deferred, criticism itself becomes pointless; defenders respond that deconstruction does not deny texts can be read closely and rigorously, only that any single reading can claim final, stable authority — a discipline, not a nihilism.
- **Is "the death of the author" too extreme a claim?** Denying the author any authority over a text's meaning can seem to ignore the very real fact that authors make deliberate choices; Barthes's defenders respond that the claim targets criticism's *reliance* on inferred authorial intention as a standard of correctness, not the plain fact that texts have historical authors who made choices.
- **How does post-structuralism's rejection of authorial authority differ from the New Criticism's intentional fallacy (Chapter 5)?** Wimsatt and Beardsley excluded authorial intention as evidence while still assuming the autonomous text held one determinate, recoverable meaning; Barthes and Foucault deny that any single determinate meaning is available at all, a difference in degree that amounts to a difference in kind.
- **Was the Johns Hopkins conference's outcome a genuine philosophical event, or an accident of timing?** That Derrida's critique of structuralism was delivered at the very conference meant to introduce structuralism to America has been read both as a striking historical irony and as evidence that structuralism's internal tensions were already visible enough, by 1966, that its own celebratory introduction could double as its first serious challenge.

## Chapter Summary

Post-structuralism emerged as an internal challenge to the structuralism traced in Chapter 6, dated with genuine irony to Jacques Derrida's 1966 Johns Hopkins lecture, delivered at a conference meant to introduce structuralism to America. Derrida's concepts of différance and logocentrism argue that meaning, rather than arising stably from a sign's difference within a system, is endlessly deferred, undermining any claim to a fixed, final foundation of meaning — a critique his practice of deconstruction applies directly to specific texts' own internal logic. Roland Barthes's "The Death of the Author" and Michel Foucault's "What Is an Author?" extended this same skepticism to authorial authority, going considerably further than the intentional fallacy of Chapter 5 by denying that any single, recoverable meaning exists at all, rather than merely excluding intention as evidence for one. This Part's seventh critical method, tested through the American "Yale School" of the 1970s and 1980s, unsettles every stable standard traced in the chapters before it. Chapter 8 now turns to Marxist criticism, which will reintroduce exactly the historical and material context this and the preceding chapters largely excluded.

## Glossary

- **Différance** — Derrida's coined term for meaning's endless deferral along a chain of signifiers, never arriving at a final, stable signified.
- **Logocentrism** — Derrida's term for Western philosophy's assumption that a stable, self-present foundation ultimately grounds and fixes meaning.
- **Deconstruction** — Derrida's practice of reading a text to reveal how its own internal logic undermines the hierarchical oppositions it appears to rely on.
- **Author-function** — Michel Foucault's term for authorship as a historically constructed category regulating discourse, rather than a natural fact about textual production.
