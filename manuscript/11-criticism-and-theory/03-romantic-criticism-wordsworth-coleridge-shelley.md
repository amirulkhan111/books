## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 2 ended by tracing neoclassicism's own internal turn: Johnson's empirical "common reader" standard already loosened Pope's confident rule-based system from within, testing inherited classical authority against actual literary experience rather than simply reaffirming it. Romantic criticism, the subject of this chapter, completes that turn far more decisively — so decisively that the influential twentieth-century critic M. H. Abrams, in *The Mirror and the Lamp* (1953), described it as a genuine change in the basic model of what literature *is*. Neoclassical criticism, in Abrams's account, treated the literary work as a **mirror**: a reflection, however skillfully polished, of an external nature or reality that existed independently of the writer's own mind. Romantic criticism instead treats the literary work as a **lamp**: a light emanating from the poet's own inner mind, illuminating and even partly creating the world it describes, rather than passively reflecting it.

This shift did not happen in a vacuum. All three writers this chapter studies — Wordsworth, Coleridge, and Shelley — wrote against the backdrop of the French Revolution's disillusioning arc and the individualist, feeling-centered philosophy explored across Part VI, and Coleridge in particular imported German Idealist philosophy, especially Kant and Schelling, directly into English criticism for the first time. Where Chapters 1 and 2 traced critics arguing about how closely literature should follow classical, external rules, this chapter's three critics argue instead about where literature's authority actually comes from: ordinary human speech and feeling for Wordsworth, the mind's own creative imagination for Coleridge, and poetry's power to reshape society's moral sympathies for Shelley.

## Literary Movements

Read together rather than separately, Wordsworth's Preface, Coleridge's *Biographia Literaria*, and Shelley's "A Defence of Poetry" locate literary authority in three related but genuinely distinct places, each a variation on Abrams's "lamp." Wordsworth locates it in **feeling and ordinary human experience**: poetry's value comes from plain language recording real emotion, particularly among people whose lives, less distorted by social artifice, express fundamental human nature most clearly. Coleridge locates it instead in **the creative mind's own organizing power**: his primary/secondary imagination distinction, already met in Part VI, Chapter 4, describes a mental faculty that does not simply record experience but actively, almost divinely, creates unified wholes out of it — and his related concept of **organic form** extends Johnson's defense of Shakespeare's rule-breaking, already met in Part XI, Chapter 2, from an empirical observation about readers' actual response into a full philosophical account of how genuinely creative works grow according to their own inner law, like living organisms, rather than external rules. Shelley locates literary authority in a third place again: **poetry's social and moral power**, arguing in "A Defence of Poetry" that imaginative sympathy, cultivated through poetry, is the essential engine of all real moral and political progress — a claim that pushes the "lamp" metaphor outward, from illuminating one mind to reshaping an entire society's capacity for fellow-feeling.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Wordsworth, Preface to *Lyrical Ballads* (1800/1802).** As you saw in Part VI, Chapter 2, Wordsworth's rejection of "poetic diction" in favor of plain language and his definition of poetry as "the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings" locate literary value in genuine emotion, recollected and shaped by ordinary human minds — Abrams's "lamp" in its most democratic form, since Wordsworth insists this creative light burns in every person, not only in poets.
- **Coleridge, *Biographia Literaria* (1817): Fancy and Imagination.** As you saw in Part VI, Chapter 4, Coleridge's primary imagination — "a repetition in the finite mind of the eternal act of creation in the infinite I AM" — makes the "lamp" metaphor almost literal: the mind does not reflect the world so much as actively, continuously create the very perception of it.
- **Coleridge, organic form.** Also met in Part VI, Chapter 4, this concept directly extends Johnson's defense of Shakespeare's disregard for the unities (Part XI, Chapter 2) from an empirical claim about readers' response into a full theory: great art grows into its own proper shape from within, the way a plant grows, rather than being cut to an external, rule-based pattern.
- **Shelley, "A Defence of Poetry" (1821, published 1840).** As you saw in Part VI, Chapter 6, Shelley's claim that "poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the world" extends the Romantic "lamp" outward from the individual mind to society itself: poetry's imaginative sympathy, in his account, is what allows any moral or political progress to happen at all.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does Abrams's "mirror to lamp" narrative oversimplify neoclassical criticism?** Chapter 2 already showed Johnson testing classical rules empirically and grounding literary value in "the common sense of readers" rather than pure external authority — suggesting the shift Abrams describes was less a clean break than an acceleration of a change already underway within neoclassicism's own later development.
- **Do Wordsworth, Coleridge, and Shelley actually agree on where literary authority comes from?** Read side by side rather than in their separate author chapters, their arguments turn out to locate authority in three genuinely different places — ordinary feeling, creative imagination, social sympathy — a real internal diversity easy to miss when "Romantic criticism" is treated as a single, unified position.
- **Is Shelley's claim for poetry's social power a natural extension of Wordsworth's and Coleridge's arguments, or a distinct claim requiring its own defense?** Wordsworth and Coleridge mostly argue about where a poem's value comes from; Shelley argues, more ambitiously, about what that value can *do* to an entire society — a move from literary theory into political and moral theory that this chapter's own critical apparatus leaves open to genuine debate.

## Chapter Summary

M. H. Abrams's *The Mirror and the Lamp* (1953) frames Romantic criticism as a shift from literature-as-mirror, reflecting an external nature, to literature-as-lamp, expressing the poet's own inner mind — a shift this chapter traced across three genuinely distinct locations of literary authority. Wordsworth's Preface to *Lyrical Ballads*, already met in Part VI, Chapter 2, locates it in ordinary feeling and plain speech; Coleridge's *Biographia Literaria*, already met in Part VI, Chapter 4, locates it in the imagination's own quasi-divine creative power, extending Johnson's defense of Shakespeare (Part XI, Chapter 2) into a full theory of organic form; and Shelley's "A Defence of Poetry," already met in Part VI, Chapter 6, locates it in poetry's power to cultivate an entire society's moral sympathy. Read together, these three arguments show Romantic criticism as an internally varied, not a single unified, expressive turn — one that accelerated, rather than simply invented from nothing, a shift already visible in Johnson's own late neoclassical practice. Chapter 4 now turns to Victorian criticism, and Matthew Arnold's attempt to define culture's social role in terms that directly extend Shelley's ambitions for poetry.

## Glossary

- **Mimetic theory** — the view, associated with Aristotle and neoclassicism, that literature's value lies in accurately imitating or reflecting an external reality; Abrams's "mirror."
- **Expressive theory** — the view, associated with Romantic criticism, that literature's value lies in expressing the writer's own inner feeling or imagination; Abrams's "lamp."
- **Organic form** (recap) — Coleridge's concept, first met in Part VI, Chapter 4, of artistic form growing from a work's own inner law rather than being imposed by external rules.
