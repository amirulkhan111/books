## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 3 traced how Wordsworth, Coleridge, and Shelley located literary authority in feeling, imagination, and poetry's power to shape society's moral sympathy. Matthew Arnold, already profiled in Part VII, Chapter 5, for his poetry and his cultural criticism, takes up this same question — where does literature's value and authority come from? — but arrives at a genuinely different answer, one this chapter treats as a fourth distinct critical method alongside the rule-based criticism of Chapter 1, the common-reader empiricism of Chapter 2, and the expressive theories of Chapter 3.

Arnold's own institutional position shaped this difference. Unlike Wordsworth, Coleridge, or Shelley, none of whom held any formal academic post, Arnold became Oxford's Professor of Poetry in 1857 — the first holder of that chair to lecture in English rather than Latin, a small but genuinely significant marker of English literature's slow, uneven progress toward becoming a serious university subject in its own right, rather than merely the vernacular shadow of a classical education. Arnold's later career as a school inspector, already discussed in Part VII, Chapter 5, further grounded his criticism in direct, comparative observation of European culture and education, distinguishing his critical temperament from the more purely literary and philosophical concerns of the Romantic critics who preceded him.

## Literary Movements

Arnold's "The Function of Criticism at the Present Time," already met in Part VII, Chapter 5, for its concept of **disinterestedness**, makes a further argument this chapter treats in full: that criticism is not a secondary activity trailing behind creative genius, but an active, necessary precondition for great creative literature to occur at all. Arnold distinguishes **epochs of concentration**, when a society channels its energy into vigorous action and creation, from **epochs of expansion**, when a "current of ideas," generated largely through criticism's disinterested work of promoting "the best that is known and thought," supplies creative writers with the intellectual material genuinely great literature requires. On this argument, English Romantic poetry, however brilliant — Wordsworth's and Byron's work among it, already met in Part VI — suffered from insufficient critical groundwork: the Romantics, in Arnold's view, wrote with real power but without enough of the "current of ideas" a fuller preceding critical culture would have supplied, leaving their achievement less complete than it might otherwise have been. This is a direct, substantive challenge to Shelley's claim in "A Defence of Poetry" (Chapter 3) that poetic imagination alone, unaided by criticism, drives moral and social progress: Arnold insists criticism must do its own prior work before poetry can fully succeed.

Arnold's later essay "The Study of Poetry" (1880), already met in Part VII, Chapter 5, for its prediction that poetry would increasingly assume religion's cultural function, also proposes a specific critical technique this chapter examines directly: the **touchstone method**. Arnold recommends keeping in mind a small number of short passages from acknowledged great poets — lines of Homer, Dante, Shakespeare, Milton — and testing any other poetry's quality by direct comparison against them, on the theory that genuinely high poetic quality reveals itself, almost physically, against an authentic standard. This same essay supplies Arnold's most compact definition of poetry itself: **"poetry is a criticism of life,"** a formula distinct from, though related to, his definition of *culture* as "the best that has been thought and said" in *Culture and Anarchy* (Part VII, Chapter 5) — the first concerns what poetry actually does, the second what education and society should pursue.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **"The Function of Criticism at the Present Time" (1864/1865): criticism as precondition, not aftermath.** Beyond the concept of disinterestedness already discussed in Part VII, Chapter 5, this essay's fuller argument holds that a society's "current of ideas," generated by criticism's own disinterested pursuit of "the best that is known and thought," is what allows great creative literature to occur — meaning the English Romantics' achievement, brilliant as it was, lacked the critical groundwork Arnold believed genuinely great creative epochs require.
- **"The Study of Poetry" (1880): the touchstone method.** Arnold proposes testing any poem's quality against short, memorized passages from acknowledged masters — Homer, Dante, Shakespeare, Milton — arguing that authentic poetic greatness reveals itself by direct comparison rather than through any rule, reader-response test, or theory of the imagination.
- **"Poetry is a criticism of life" (1880).** This formula, distinct from *Culture and Anarchy*'s definition of culture (Part VII, Chapter 5), holds that poetry's value lies in how truthfully and beautifully it interprets and judges human existence — a claim that gives Arnold's earlier prediction, that poetry would assume religion's cultural function, its clearest theoretical foundation.

---

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does Arnold's critique of the Romantics hold up against Shelley's claims in Chapter 3?** Arnold argues genius alone cannot substitute for a sufficiently developed "current of ideas"; Shelley argues poetic imagination itself is civilization's engine of moral progress. Weighing these against each other requires deciding whether creative power depends on prior critical and intellectual culture, or generates its own conditions as it goes.
- **Is the touchstone method a genuinely objective critical tool, or does it just relocate the problem of taste?** Arnold's touchstones assume that Homer, Dante, Shakespeare, and Milton are settled, uncontroversial standards of greatness — but selecting those particular touchstones is itself a critical judgment, meaning the method may describe rather than escape the difficulty of establishing literary value.
- **How does "poetry is a criticism of life" relate to the "best that has been thought and said"?** The two formulas, from different essays nine years apart, describe complementary rather than identical claims — one about poetry's specific function, one about culture's broader educational ideal — and distinguishing them carefully avoids collapsing Arnold's whole critical project into a single slogan.

## Chapter Summary

Matthew Arnold, already profiled in Part VII, Chapter 5, appears here as a critic whose institutional position as Oxford's first English-language Professor of Poetry (1857) and whose specific arguments mark a fourth distinct critical method in this Part's developing survey. "The Function of Criticism at the Present Time" (1864/1865) argues that criticism actively prepares the "current of ideas" great creative literature requires, faulting even the Romantic poets of Chapter 3 for insufficient critical groundwork — a direct challenge to Shelley's claim that poetic imagination alone drives moral progress. "The Study of Poetry" (1880) proposes the touchstone method, testing any poem against short passages from acknowledged masters, and supplies the formula "poetry is a criticism of life," distinct from *Culture and Anarchy*'s definition of culture as "the best that has been thought and said." Chapter 5 now turns to early twentieth-century criticism, where T. S. Eliot's own theory of tradition will directly engage Arnold's touchstone method and his claims for poetry's cultural authority.

## Glossary

- **Epochs of concentration / epochs of expansion** — Arnold's distinction between periods of vigorous creative action and periods of idea-generating criticism, from "The Function of Criticism at the Present Time."
- **Touchstone method** — Arnold's technique, from "The Study of Poetry," of testing a poem's quality by direct comparison against short passages from acknowledged great poets.
- **"Poetry is a criticism of life"** — Arnold's definition, from "The Study of Poetry" (1880), of poetry's function: truthfully and beautifully interpreting and judging human existence.
