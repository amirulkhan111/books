## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 1 left English criticism at a genuine crossroads: Dryden's *Of Dramatick Poesie* (1668) staged an unresolved argument between defenders of strict classical rules and defenders of English drama's freer practice, without declaring either side the clear winner. That same argument, known to literary historians as the **Ancients versus Moderns** quarrel, dominated European criticism for the following half-century, and this chapter follows it through its most consequential English episode before turning to the two writers, Alexander Pope and Samuel Johnson, who did the most to define what "neoclassical" actually meant in practice.

The quarrel reached England most directly through Sir William Temple, a diplomat and essayist whose 1690 essay defending ancient learning's permanent superiority over modern achievement employed, among its evidence, a set of supposedly ancient letters that later scholarship exposed as forgeries. Temple's secretary at Moor Park during these years was a young Jonathan Swift, and Swift's satire *The Battle of the Books* (1704), already met in Part IV, Chapter 5, dramatizes this same quarrel as a literal battle among books in a library, siding, like his employer, with the Ancients against modern presumption. Pope's *An Essay on Criticism* (1711), already met in Part IV, Chapter 6, arrived only a few years later as English criticism's fullest positive statement of what siding with the Ancients actually meant for practicing critics and poets: not blind imitation, but confidence that classical writers had already discovered nature's own underlying order. Johnson, writing more than half a century after Pope, inherited this same classical framework but applied it with a far more skeptical, empirical temper — genuinely testing inherited rules against actual literary experience rather than simply reaffirming them, a shift this chapter treats as neoclassicism's own internal turning point.

## Literary Movements

**Neoclassicism**, introduced in Chapter 1, reaches its fullest English theoretical statement in Pope's *An Essay on Criticism*: good judgment and good writing both consist in following classical rules, because those rules capture nature's own underlying order rather than restricting it arbitrarily. Central to Pope's argument is his particular concept of **wit** — not novelty or cleverness for its own sake, but apt, memorable expression of truths readers already half-recognize, summed up in his own couplet: "True Wit is Nature to Advantage drest, / What oft was Thought, but ne'er so well Exprest." Johnson inherited this same classical inheritance but practiced criticism more empirically: rather than treating the rules as self-evidently correct, he tested them against how literature actually affects readers over time, a method that let him defend Shakespeare's violations of the unities of time and place — the very "unities" Chapter 1 traced to Castelvetro's Renaissance invention rather than Aristotle's own text — as truer to life than strict rule-following would have permitted. This shift, from Pope's confident system-building to Johnson's rule-testing empiricism, is neoclassicism's own internal movement toward the more historically and psychologically minded criticism that later chapters in this Part will trace through Romanticism and beyond.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Pope, *An Essay on Criticism* (1711).** As you saw in Part IV, Chapter 6, this poem argues that classical rules distill nature itself rather than restricting it. This chapter adds a further concept central to that argument: Pope's definition of **wit** as apt, memorable expression of an already-recognized truth, not novelty invented from nothing — a definition that makes neoclassical criticism's prized "correctness" compatible with genuine literary pleasure, since the pleasure lies precisely in how perfectly a familiar truth gets phrased.
- **Swift, *The Battle of the Books* (1704).** Already met in Part IV, Chapter 5, this satire imagines the books in a library's Ancients and Moderns sections literally fighting for supremacy, siding, like Temple, with the Ancients — evidence that the Ancients-Moderns quarrel Dryden's dialogue (Chapter 1) left genuinely open was still very much alive a generation later, and that English writers took real, opposed sides in it rather than treating it as settled.
- **Johnson, *Preface to Shakespeare* (1765).** Already met in Part IV, Chapter 8, Johnson's defense of Shakespeare's disregard for the unities of time and place, and his mixture of comedy and tragedy, as truer to life than strict neoclassical rule-following, directly answers the question Chapter 1 raised about the unities' dubious classical authority: Johnson does not simply expose the rule's Renaissance origins, as this chapter's Chapter 1 did, but argues on independent critical grounds that even a genuinely Aristotelian unity of time is not worth enforcing if it costs a play its emotional truth.
- **Johnson, *Lives of the Poets*: the *Life of Gray* (1781).** Already met in Part IV, Chapter 8, for its critical judgments on Milton, Dryden, and Pope, the *Lives* also contains Johnson's most explicit statement of his critical method, in his assessment of Thomas Gray's "Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard": literary merit is finally decided "by the common sense of readers, uncorrupted with literary prejudices," rather than by a critic's private rule-book — a genuinely democratic critical standard that sits in real tension with neoclassicism's usual appeal to expert, rule-bound judgment.

---

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is Pope's "wit" a limiting or a liberating concept?** Defining wit as the perfect expression of an already-known truth can sound like a recipe for unoriginality, but Pope's own practice — the *Essay*'s own famously polished couplets enact the very principle they describe — suggests the concept demands a difficult, disciplined craftsmanship rather than an easy shortcut, a case this chapter leaves open for direct debate.
- **Does Johnson's "common reader" standard genuinely break with neoclassical rule-worship, or extend it by other means?** Johnson never abandons classical learning or literary judgment's authority, but grounding final literary value in ordinary readers' sustained response, rather than in a critic's or an ancient authority's rules, points toward a more historical, reception-based criticism this chapter argues genuinely complicates any simple picture of "neoclassicism" as rigid rule-following throughout.
- **Did Johnson's defense of Shakespeare against the unities settle the debate Chapter 1 raised?** Johnson's argument works on its own critical terms — mixed drama serves emotional truth better than pure genres — rather than by exposing the unities' Renaissance origin, which this book's own Chapter 1 did instead; both approaches arrive at the same practical conclusion by genuinely different routes, worth distinguishing carefully.

## Chapter Summary

The Ancients versus Moderns quarrel, left unresolved by Dryden's *Of Dramatick Poesie* (Chapter 1), reached England most directly through Sir William Temple and his secretary Swift's *Battle of the Books* (1704), already met in Part IV, Chapter 5. Pope's *An Essay on Criticism* (1711), already met in Part IV, Chapter 6, gave neoclassicism its fullest confident statement, defining "wit" as the perfect expression of an already-recognized truth rather than mere novelty. Johnson, in the *Preface to Shakespeare* (1765) and *Lives of the Poets* (1779–1781), already met in Part IV, Chapter 8, inherited this same classical framework but tested it empirically, defending Shakespeare's violation of the unities as truer to life and proposing "the common sense of readers, uncorrupted with literary prejudices" as criticism's final standard — a genuinely more historical, reception-based criterion that marks neoclassicism's own internal turn toward the psychologically and historically minded criticism Chapter 3 now traces through the Romantic period.

## Glossary

- **Ancients versus Moderns** — the sustained European quarrel over whether classical literature represented an unsurpassable standard or merely one admirable tradition among others, staged in England through Temple, Swift, and Dryden.
- **Wit** (in Pope's sense) — apt, memorable expression of an already-recognized truth, not novelty invented from nothing; central to neoclassical literary value.
- **The common reader** — Johnson's proposed standard of literary judgment: sustained, uncorrupted general readership response, rather than a critic's private rule-book.
