## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 4 left Arnold testing poetry against touchstone passages from acknowledged masters — an external, comparative standard still rooted in literary history and canonical authority. Early twentieth-century criticism, this chapter's subject, took a further step: rather than judging a poem against classical rules, common readers' response, expressive authenticity, or canonical touchstones, it increasingly insisted that a poem should be judged by its own internal structure alone, independent of the poet's biography, stated intentions, historical circumstances, or a reader's emotional response. This movement had two distinct origins that converged into a single dominant method. In England, T. S. Eliot, already profiled as a poet in Part VIII, Chapter 2, developed an influential body of criticism alongside his poetry. In Cambridge, I. A. Richards approached the same question from an empirical, classroom-based direction, testing how actual student readers responded to poems stripped of all identifying context. American critics of the 1930s and 1940s then combined both strands into the movement that came to be called, self-consciously, the **New Criticism**.

## Literary Movements

Eliot's criticism, beyond the impersonality theory and "dissociation of sensibility" already met in Part VIII, Chapter 2, and Part III, Chapter 8, contributes a further concept central to this chapter: the **objective correlative**, from his 1919 essay "Hamlet and His Problems." Eliot argues that a work of art can only express emotion by finding "a set of objects, a situation, a chain of events" that functions as the precise external formula for that emotion — and, notoriously, that *Hamlet* itself is an "artistic failure" because Hamlet's disgust with his mother exceeds any objective correlative the play actually supplies. Whether or not you accept this specific verdict on Shakespeare's play, the underlying principle — that a poem's emotional power should be traceable to concrete textual detail, not simply asserted or inferred from the poet's life — became foundational for the movement this chapter now traces.

I. A. Richards approached the same principle experimentally rather than theoretically. In *Practical Criticism* (1929), Richards gave Cambridge students poems with all identifying information — author, title, date — deliberately removed, and collected their written responses. The results were sobering: students routinely misread poems through **stock responses** (reflexive, pre-formed reactions triggered by familiar-seeming subject matter rather than genuine attention to the words on the page), through irrelevant technical presuppositions, and through their own doctrinal or emotional biases, rather than through careful attention to the poem's actual language. Richards's experiment effectively demonstrated, empirically, what Eliot had argued theoretically: readers needed a disciplined method of **close reading** — slow, careful attention to a text's actual words, images, and structure — rather than relying on biographical context or their own prior assumptions. Richards's student William Empson extended this method further in *Seven Types of Ambiguity* (1930), showing how a poem's meaning could depend on deliberately sustained multiple, even contradictory, readings of the same words.

American critics of the 1930s and 1940s, above all John Crowe Ransom, who gave the movement its name in his 1941 book *The New Criticism*, and Cleanth Brooks, whose *The Well Wrought Urn* (1947) treated the well-made poem as a self-contained structure built on internal paradox and tension, systematized Richards's classroom method and Eliot's textual focus into a full critical doctrine: the poem as an autonomous "verbal icon," to use W. K. Wimsatt's later phrase, meaningful entirely on its own terms. Wimsatt and Monroe Beardsley gave this doctrine its sharpest formal statement in two influential essays: **"The Intentional Fallacy"** (1946) argued that a poem's meaning cannot be settled by appeal to what its author intended, since the author's intention is neither reliably knowable nor, even if known, a legitimate standard for judging the finished work; **"The Affective Fallacy"** (1949) argued, by the same logic, that a poem's meaning cannot be settled by appeal to its emotional effect on a particular reader, since that effect varies from reader to reader and confuses the poem itself with its subjective results. Together, the two fallacies excluded both the author's mind and the reader's response from legitimate critical evidence, leaving only the text's own words as the proper object of study.

## Author Profiles

**T. S. Eliot** (1888–1965), already profiled as a poet in Part VIII, Chapter 2, appears here for "Hamlet and His Problems" (1919), discussed above, alongside his already-familiar theories of impersonality and dissociation of sensibility.

**I. A. Richards** (1893–1979) taught at Cambridge, where his classroom experiments with unattributed poems, collected in *Practical Criticism* (1929), founded the empirical, text-focused method this chapter traces into the American New Criticism.

**William Empson** (1906–1984), Richards's student at Cambridge, extended his teacher's close-reading method in *Seven Types of Ambiguity* (1930), a landmark demonstration of how deliberately sustained multiple readings could reveal a poem's fullest meaning.

**John Crowe Ransom** (1888–1974) and **Cleanth Brooks** (1906–1994), leading American New Critics, gave the movement its name and its most influential single readings, respectively, in *The New Criticism* (1941) and *The Well Wrought Urn* (1947).

**W. K. Wimsatt** (1907–1975) and **Monroe Beardsley** (1915–1985), writing together, gave the New Criticism's exclusion of authorial intention and reader response its sharpest formal statement in "The Intentional Fallacy" (1946) and "The Affective Fallacy" (1949).

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Eliot, "Hamlet and His Problems" (1919).** Eliot's claim that *Hamlet* fails because its emotion exceeds any adequate objective correlative remains one of the most disputed single verdicts in Shakespeare criticism, but the underlying principle — that emotion in art must be grounded in precise, traceable textual detail — proved far more durable than the specific judgment about Shakespeare's play.
- **Richards, *Practical Criticism* (1929).** By stripping poems of authorial identity and collecting Cambridge students' actual written responses, Richards demonstrated empirically how much ordinary reading depends on stock responses and prior assumptions rather than genuine attention to a text's own language — the founding document of close reading as a teachable discipline.
- **Wimsatt and Beardsley, "The Intentional Fallacy" (1946) and "The Affective Fallacy" (1949).** These two essays together define the New Criticism's central exclusion: neither the author's stated or inferred intention nor a reader's emotional response counts as legitimate evidence for a poem's meaning, leaving the poem's own words as criticism's sole proper object.
- **Brooks, *The Well Wrought Urn* (1947).** Brooks reads canonical poems as tightly organized structures built on internal paradox and tension — language holding contrary meanings in productive balance — treating this structural unity, rather than any external standard, as the source of a poem's value.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Is Eliot's objective correlative a coherent critical principle, or does it just restate a personal taste as an objective rule?** Many critics reject Eliot's specific verdict that *Hamlet* is an "artistic failure," arguing the play's very refusal to supply a fully adequate external cause for Hamlet's disgust is itself the point — a case where the principle survives even as its most famous application does not.
- **Does excluding authorial intention and reader response leave criticism with too little to work with?** The intentional and affective fallacies deliberately narrow criticism's evidence to the text alone, a discipline many critics found genuinely clarifying, but one that later chapters in this Part — on Marxist, psychoanalytic, feminist, and postcolonial criticism — will each, in different ways, argue excludes exactly the historical, psychological, and social context necessary to understand what a text actually means.
- **Did Richards's experiment prove readers need less context, or simply that they need better-trained context?** *Practical Criticism*'s students misread poems partly through ignorance of the poem's actual circumstances, not only through excessive reliance on them — a tension worth holding onto before accepting close reading's implicit claim that context is dispensable rather than merely secondary.

## Chapter Summary

Eliot's "objective correlative," from "Hamlet and His Problems" (1919), joined his already-familiar theories of impersonality and dissociation of sensibility (Part VIII, Chapter 2; Part III, Chapter 8) in grounding a poem's emotional power in precise textual detail rather than biography. I. A. Richards's *Practical Criticism* (1929) demonstrated empirically, through Cambridge classroom experiments with unattributed poems, how much ordinary reading depends on stock responses rather than genuine attention to language — founding close reading as a teachable discipline his student William Empson extended in *Seven Types of Ambiguity* (1930). American critics including John Crowe Ransom and Cleanth Brooks systematized both strands into the **New Criticism**, and Wimsatt and Beardsley's "intentional fallacy" and "affective fallacy" essays gave the movement its sharpest formal statement: neither authorial intention nor reader response counts as legitimate evidence, leaving the autonomous text alone as criticism's proper object — this Part's fifth distinct critical method. Chapter 6 now turns to structuralism and formalism, which will extend this same turn toward the text's internal structure into an even more systematic, linguistically grounded theory.

## Glossary

- **Objective correlative** — Eliot's term for the set of objects, situations, or events that must be present in a work to evoke a specific emotion precisely and reliably.
- **Stock response** — Richards's term for a reflexive, pre-formed reaction to a poem's subject matter, substituting for genuine attention to its actual language.
- **Close reading** — slow, careful attention to a text's own words, images, and structure, founded by Richards's classroom method and central to the New Criticism.
- **New Criticism** — the mid-twentieth-century American critical movement, named by John Crowe Ransom, treating the literary text as an autonomous structure to be studied apart from authorial biography or reader response.
- **Intentional fallacy** — Wimsatt and Beardsley's term for the error of judging a poem's meaning by appeal to its author's intention.
- **Affective fallacy** — Wimsatt and Beardsley's term for the error of judging a poem's meaning by appeal to its emotional effect on a reader.
