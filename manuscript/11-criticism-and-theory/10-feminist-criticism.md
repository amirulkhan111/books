## Historical and Socio-Political Context

This book has traced a single recurring thread since Part IV, Chapter 9: women writers negotiating a literary culture that variously excluded, pseudonymized, or diminished their work — Aphra Behn, Delarivier Manley, and Eliza Haywood publishing under real financial and reputational constraint; Charlotte, Emily, and Anne Brontë publishing as "Currer, Ellis, and Acton Bell"; Mary Ann Evans publishing as "George Eliot"; and Virginia Woolf's "Shakespeare's sister" thought experiment in *A Room of One's Own* (Part VIII, Chapter 7), which asked what would have become of a woman with Shakespeare's own genius, denied his education and freedom. Feminist criticism, this chapter's subject, turns this recurring historical pattern into a systematic critical method, emerging as part of the broader second-wave feminist movement of the 1960s and 1970s. Kate Millett's *Sexual Politics* (1970), already met through her critique of D. H. Lawrence in Part VIII, Chapter 5, gave this movement its founding text of literary analysis, examining how canonical male writers encoded and naturalized patriarchal power through their fiction's sexual politics. The following decade saw feminist criticism develop considerably more systematic theoretical tools, in America through Elaine Showalter and through Sandra Gilbert and Susan Gubar, and in France through Hélène Cixous, working in direct dialogue with the structuralist and post-structuralist theory of Chapters 6 and 7.

## Literary Movements

Elaine Showalter's *A Literature of Their Own* (1977) coined the term **gynocriticism** to describe a shift in feminist critical focus: rather than continuing Millett's project of critiquing how male writers represented women, gynocriticism constructs women's own literary history and tradition on its own terms, as a subject worth studying in its own right rather than only in relation to a dominant male canon. Showalter proposed a three-phase model for this history: a **Feminine** phase, in which women writers largely imitated dominant literary and cultural standards, often obscuring their gender through pseudonyms — precisely the strategy you met through the Brontës and George Eliot in Part VII, Chapters 8 and 9; a **Feminist** phase, in which women writers directly protested against those same dominant standards and women's exclusion from full literary and social participation; and a **Female** phase, in which women's writing turns toward self-discovery and a specifically female literary identity, less defined by reaction against male tradition than by its own independent concerns.

Sandra Gilbert and Susan Gubar's *The Madwoman in the Attic* (1979) — whose reading of Bertha Mason as Jane Eyre's suppressed symbolic double you already met in Part VII, Chapter 8 — develops a broader theoretical concept this chapter now examines directly: **anxiety of authorship**. Where Harold Bloom, already met in Chapter 9, argued that male poets experience an anxious, competitive struggle against a powerful literary precursor, Gilbert and Gubar argue that nineteenth-century women writers faced a different, prior anxiety altogether: not rivalry with a specific predecessor, but a deeper cultural doubt about whether a woman could be an author at all, given a literary tradition that had long assumed authorship itself was a fundamentally male activity. This concept explains, on their reading, why so many nineteenth-century women novelists — the Brontës, George Eliot among them — encoded rage, confinement, and monstrous doubling figures like Bertha Mason directly into their fiction, symbolically working through an anxiety male writers, in Bloom's account, never had to face in the same form.

Hélène Cixous, writing in France, extended feminist criticism into explicitly post-structuralist territory. Her essay "The Laugh of the Medusa" (1975) proposes **écriture féminine** ("feminine writing"): a mode of writing rooted in women's own bodily experience, capable of disrupting what Cixous calls **phallogocentrism** — a term combining "phallocentrism" (the privileging of masculine authority and presence) with the "logocentrism" you met through Derrida in Chapter 7, naming Western thought's tendency to organize meaning through rigid, hierarchical binary oppositions (active/passive, culture/nature, presence/absence) that consistently place the masculine term above the feminine one. Écriture féminine, in Cixous's account, does not simply mean writing by women, but a mode of writing — available in principle to any writer — that disrupts this binary logic from within, refusing stable, unified, hierarchically organized meaning in favor of multiplicity, contradiction, and bodily immediacy.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Millett, *Sexual Politics* (1970).** Beyond her critique of Lawrence (Part VIII, Chapter 5), Millett's broader argument treats sexuality itself as a site of political power, analyzing how canonical fiction naturalizes patriarchal dominance through its representation of gender and desire — the founding methodological move of second-wave feminist literary criticism.
- **Showalter, *A Literature of Their Own* (1977).** Showalter's gynocriticism and three-phase model (Feminine, Feminist, Female) gave feminist criticism a positive project — constructing women's own literary tradition — distinct from Millett's critique of patriarchal representation, and directly illuminate the pseudonym strategies of the Brontës and George Eliot (Part VII, Chapters 8 and 9) as a documented instance of Showalter's "Feminine" phase.
- **Gilbert and Gubar, *The Madwoman in the Attic* (1979): anxiety of authorship.** Beyond their specific reading of Bertha Mason (Part VII, Chapter 8), this concept reframes Bloom's anxiety of influence (Chapter 9) in explicitly gendered terms: women writers' anxiety concerned not rivalry with a precursor, but the more basic cultural question of whether a woman could be an author at all.
- **Cixous, "The Laugh of the Medusa" (1975).** Cixous's *écriture féminine* extends Derrida's critique of logocentrism (Chapter 7) into explicitly feminist terrain, naming the specific gendering — "phallogocentrism" — that Derrida's own analysis had left largely implicit.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does Showalter's three-phase model impose too tidy a structure on women's literary history?** The Feminine-Feminist-Female sequence offers a genuinely useful framework, but critics have asked whether real literary history moves through such phases as cleanly as the model suggests, or whether individual writers and works resist being sorted so neatly.
- **Is "anxiety of authorship" a more accurate account of women writers' experience than Bloom's "anxiety of influence," or simply a different lens on the same phenomenon?** Gilbert and Gubar's concept explains features of nineteenth-century women's fiction, like Bertha Mason's symbolic function, that Bloom's Oedipal model of rivalry between (implicitly male) poets does not obviously address, suggesting the two concepts describe genuinely different creative anxieties rather than merely relabeling one.
- **Does Cixous's *écriture féminine* risk essentializing "feminine" writing as inherently different from "masculine" writing?** Some critics have worried that grounding a mode of writing in female bodily experience risks reinstating the very binary logic Cixous claims to disrupt; her defenders respond that écriture féminine is explicitly available to writers of any gender, making it a mode of writing rather than a biological category.

## Glossary

- **Gynocriticism** — Elaine Showalter's term for feminist criticism focused on constructing women's own literary history and tradition, rather than critiquing male representation of women.
- **Feminine / Feminist / Female phases** — Showalter's three-phase model of women's literary history: imitation of dominant standards, direct protest, and independent self-discovery.
- **Anxiety of authorship** — Sandra Gilbert and Susan Gubar's term for women writers' foundational doubt about whether a woman could be an author at all, distinct from Bloom's "anxiety of influence."
- ***Écriture féminine*** — Hélène Cixous's term for a mode of writing rooted in bodily experience that disrupts phallogocentric binary logic, available in principle to writers of any gender.
- **Phallogocentrism** — Cixous's term combining phallocentrism and Derrida's logocentrism, naming Western thought's tendency to organize meaning through hierarchical binaries privileging the masculine term.
