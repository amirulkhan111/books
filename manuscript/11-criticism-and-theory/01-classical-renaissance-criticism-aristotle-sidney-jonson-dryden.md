## Historical and Socio-Political Context

This chapter begins a genuinely different kind of Part. Every Part before this one asked you to read literature — poems, plays, novels — and to understand the historical circumstances that produced it. Part XI asks a related but distinct question: how have readers, across roughly 2,300 years, tried to explain *what literature is doing*, and *what standards should judge it*? Several figures in this chapter will feel familiar, because you have already met them as poets or playwrights earlier in this book; this time, you meet them specifically as critics, reading and arguing about literature rather than only writing it.

The starting point has to be Aristotle (384–322 BCE), whose *Poetics*, composed around 335 BCE, is the first systematic work of Western literary theory to survive. But the *Poetics*'s own history is nearly as remarkable as its content. The text was effectively lost to Western Europe for centuries, surviving instead through Arabic scholarship — most importantly a twelfth-century commentary by the philosopher Averroes (Ibn Rushd), later translated into Latin — before the original Greek text was recovered by Italian Renaissance humanists in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. It was Italian critics of that recovery, not Aristotle himself, who did much of the work of turning the *Poetics* into the rigid rulebook later ages associated with his name — a genuine historical distortion this chapter asks you to examine directly rather than simply inherit.

England came to formal literary criticism relatively late compared with Italy and France, and it did so for a strikingly specific reason: criticism arrived here largely as *defense*. In 1579, a former playwright named Stephen Gosson published *The School of Abuse*, a pamphlet attacking poetry, plays, and theatre as morally corrupting — and dedicated it, without permission, to the young courtier and poet Philip Sidney. Sidney's response, *The Defence of Poesy* (composed c. 1579–80, published 1595), which you already studied in Part III, Chapter 3, as a landmark of Elizabethan literary culture, was English criticism's true founding act: not a neutral survey of literary theory, but an urgent argument for poetry's moral legitimacy. Ben Jonson's critical writing, by contrast, grew out of a working dramatist's private notebook rather than any public controversy, while John Dryden's *Of Dramatick Poesie* (1668), already met in Part IV, Chapter 3, marks the moment criticism in England became a sustained, ongoing practice — reasoned argument about literature conducted for its own sake, rather than an occasional defensive reflex.

## Literary Movements

This chapter's central movement is **neoclassicism**: the broad, long-running assumption, inherited from (and partly invented on behalf of) Aristotle, that literature should be judged against classical models and rules — decorum (fitting style and content to subject and social rank), the unity of action, and imitation of nature filtered through the achievements of Greek and Roman writers. Neoclassical criticism reached its most rigid form in seventeenth-century France, where critics and playwrights like Corneille treated the "three unities" of action, time, and place as near-sacred law. English criticism, from Sidney through Jonson to Dryden, engaged with this same neoclassical inheritance far more flexibly: Sidney criticized English drama's own disregard for the unities even while England's actual popular stage, including Shakespeare's, cheerfully ignored them; Dryden's *Of Dramatick Poesie* stages a genuine, unresolved argument between defenders of strict classical rules and defenders of English drama's freer, more emotionally varied practice — a debate later criticism calls the **Ancients versus Moderns** controversy, over whether classical literature represented an unsurpassable standard or merely one admirable tradition among others.

## Author Profiles

**Aristotle** (384–322 BCE), a student of Plato and tutor to Alexander the Great, wrote the *Poetics* as (probably) lecture notes rather than a polished public treatise, which helps explain the work's compressed, sometimes cryptic style — and the many later disputes over what, exactly, he meant.

**Philip Sidney** (1554–1586), already profiled in Part III, Chapter 3, for his sonnet sequence *Astrophil and Stella*, appears here specifically as the author of *The Defence of Poesy*, discussed fully below.

**Ben Jonson** (1572–1637), already profiled in Part III, Chapter 7, as the era's leading comic dramatist and, in Part III, Chapter 9, as the "Tribe of Ben" poets' presiding influence, appears here as English literature's first major working critic, through the posthumously published notebook *Timber, or Discoveries*, discussed fully below.

**John Dryden** (1631–1700), already profiled in Part IV, Chapter 3, for his satirical and religious poetry, appears here for *Of Dramatick Poesie* (1668), the work that earned him Samuel Johnson's later title "the father of English criticism," as you saw in that earlier chapter.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Aristotle, *Poetics* (c. 335 BCE).** Aristotle defines poetry generally as **mimesis** — imitation of human action — and analyzes tragedy specifically as the imitation of a serious, complete action, evoking pity and fear in order to accomplish their **catharsis**, a purging or purification whose exact meaning critics still debate. He identifies plot (*mythos*) as tragedy's most important element, more important even than character, and describes the ideal tragic plot as involving **hamartia** (an error or flaw leading to the protagonist's downfall), **peripeteia** (a reversal of fortune), and **anagnorisis** (a recognition or discovery) — vocabulary later criticism has never stopped using.
- **The myth of the "three unities."** Aristotle discusses at length only one unity: the unity of action, meaning a tragedy should represent one complete, connected sequence of events rather than a loosely strung-together chronicle. He mentions, almost in passing, that tragedies tend to occur within roughly one day (a loose observation about common practice, not a strict rule) — the seed of what later became "unity of time." He says nothing at all about unity of place. Italian Renaissance critics, above all Lodovico Castelvetro in his 1570 commentary, hardened these scattered remarks into a rigid three-part rule, which French neoclassical drama then enforced with real severity — meaning the "three unities," as most students encounter the phrase, describe a Renaissance invention wearing Aristotle's name.
- **Sidney, *The Defence of Poesy* (composed c. 1579–80, published 1595).** As you saw in Part III, Chapter 3, Sidney argues that poetry surpasses both philosophy and history as a vehicle for moral instruction: philosophy teaches abstract precepts too dry to move readers to virtuous action, while history is bound to record what actually happened, however morally unsatisfying — whereas poetry can show ideal examples of virtue rewarded and vice punished, a "golden" world improving on nature's merely "brazen" one.
- **Jonson, *Timber, or Discoveries* (published 1641).** A miscellaneous notebook of critical observations Jonson kept and revised over many years, *Discoveries* contains English criticism's most famous piece of double-edged praise: responding to Shakespeare's fellow actors John Heminges and Henry Condell, who had claimed in the 1623 First Folio's preface that Shakespeare "never blotted out line," Jonson wrote that his own answer had been "would he had blotted a thousand" — followed immediately by the insistence that he loved the man and honored his memory "on this side idolatry as much as any." Held together, the two halves of that judgment — genuine admiration and genuine critical reservation about Shakespeare's undisciplined fluency — model a kind of honest, mixed literary assessment this book's own critical apparatus has tried to practice throughout.
- **Dryden, *Of Dramatick Poesie* (1668).** As you saw in Part IV, Chapter 3, this dialogue among four speakers debates ancient versus modern drama and French versus English tragedy without simply declaring a winner, treating criticism as reasoned argument between genuinely competing positions rather than settled doctrine.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Does treating the "three unities" as authentically Aristotelian distort literary history?** Because Castelvetro's Renaissance codification, not Aristotle's actual text, produced the strict three-part rule, centuries of playwrights and critics have judged drama, including Shakespeare's, against a standard its supposed ancient authority never really established — a caution about how critical "tradition" can misattribute its own later inventions to a founding authority.
- **Is Jonson's judgment of Shakespeare an act of professional jealousy, or of genuine critical honesty?** Jonson and Shakespeare were rival dramatists as well as friends, which has led some critics to read "would he had blotted a thousand" as competitive point-scoring; most now read it instead as exactly what it claims to be — real affection paired with a real, specific critical judgment that Shakespeare's fluency sometimes needed a stricter editor, a case study in holding admiration and reservation together rather than letting either cancel the other.
- **Should criticism aim for Aristotle's descriptive precision or Sidney's persuasive advocacy?** Aristotle's *Poetics* mostly describes how tragedy actually works; Sidney's *Defence* argues for what poetry ought to be allowed to do. This chapter's writers show both modes can coexist within the same critical tradition, though later chapters in this Part will show the balance between description and advocacy shifting repeatedly.

## Chapter Summary

Aristotle's *Poetics* (c. 335 BCE) founded Western literary criticism's vocabulary — mimesis, catharsis, hamartia, peripeteia, anagnorisis — but reached Renaissance England only after surviving through Arabic scholarship and Italian recovery, a transmission history that also produced the "three unities," a Renaissance invention of Castelvetro's rather than an authentic Aristotelian rule. English criticism began, with Sidney's *Defence of Poesy*, as a defensive response to Puritan attacks on poetry's morality, argued poetry's superiority to philosophy and history as moral instruction. Jonson's posthumously published *Timber, or Discoveries* modeled honest, double-edged literary judgment through his famous mixed assessment of Shakespeare, while Dryden's *Of Dramatick Poesie* established sustained critical argument, rather than occasional defense, as an ongoing English literary practice. Chapter 2 now turns to the neoclassical criticism of Alexander Pope and Samuel Johnson, both already met as poets in Part IV, who inherited and extended this same classical framework.

## Glossary

- **Mimesis** — Aristotle's term for imitation, the fundamental activity he takes all poetry to perform.
- **Catharsis** — Aristotle's term for tragedy's purging or purification of pity and fear in its audience; the concept's precise meaning remains critically debated.
- **Hamartia** — a tragic protagonist's error or flaw that precipitates their downfall.
- **Peripeteia** — a sudden reversal of fortune within a tragic plot.
- **Anagnorisis** — a moment of critical recognition or discovery within a tragic plot.
- **The three unities** — the rule that drama should observe unity of action, time, and place; only unity of action is substantially Aristotle's own, the other two being largely a Renaissance Italian invention later codified by French neoclassical criticism.
- **Neoclassicism** — the critical assumption that literature should be judged against classical models and rules, including decorum and the unities.
