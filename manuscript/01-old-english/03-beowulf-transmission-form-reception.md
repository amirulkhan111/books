## Historical and Socio-Political Context

*Beowulf* is set not in England but in Scandinavia — chiefly among the Geats (in what is now southern Sweden) and the Danes — and its narrative world draws on shared Germanic legendary material also found in Scandinavian sources (such as the *Grettis saga* analogues) and in other Old English and Old Norse texts. The poem was, however, composed in Old English and preserved solely in an English manuscript context, and its combination of a pre-Christian Scandinavian legendary setting with a pervasive, if unsystematic, Christian narratorial framework is one of its central interpretive puzzles (see Critical Perspectives, below).

## Literary Movements

*Beowulf* is generally regarded as the outstanding achievement of Old English heroic poetry, drawing together the alliterative verse conventions, formulaic diction, and comitatus ethic discussed in Chapter 2. It combines two distinguishable strands present throughout Old English poetry: an inherited Germanic heroic tradition (concerned with kinship obligation, feud, treasure-giving, and reputation after death) and a Christian interpretive overlay (the narrator repeatedly frames events in providential terms and identifies the monsters with the biblical lineage of Cain). Whether this combination reflects a single poet working within a fully Christianized, retrospectively legendary imagination, or the layering of Christian material onto older pagan story matter, remains debated.

## Author Profiles

**The Beowulf-poet** — Entirely unknown; unnamed in the manuscript and unidentifiable from external evidence. Scholarly convention refers to "the poet" or "the *Beowulf*-poet" as a single composing intelligence, though even this assumption cannot be verified.

**Laurence Nowell** (c. 1530–c. 1570) — Elizabethan antiquarian in whose possession the manuscript is first recorded (1563); the codex is now named for him.

**Sir Robert Bruce Cotton** (1571–1631) — Collector whose library, including the *Beowulf* manuscript, later passed to the British nation and gives the manuscript its shelfmark, Cotton Vitellius A.xv.

**Grímur Jónsson Thorkelin** (1752–1829) — Icelandic-Danish scholar and Keeper of the Danish National Archives who commissioned and made transcripts of the manuscript in 1786–87 and published the first printed edition (*editio princeps*) in 1815; his transcripts preserve readings from the manuscript's edges since lost to fire damage.

**J. R. R. Tolkien** (1892–1973) — Oxford philologist and author whose 1936 British Academy lecture, "Beowulf: The Monsters and the Critics," argued for reading the poem's monsters (Grendel, Grendel's mother, the dragon) as central to its meaning rather than as embarrassing folkloric material to be mined for historical data beneath. Widely credited with founding the modern era of *Beowulf* literary criticism.

**Seamus Heaney** (1939–2013) — Irish Nobel laureate whose 1999 verse translation of *Beowulf* became an unusual bestseller among translations of ancient poetry, winning the Whitbread Book of the Year Award and substantially widening the poem's general readership.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

*Beowulf* runs to 3,182 alliterative long lines and falls into two major movements. In the first (set in Denmark), the young Geatish warrior Beowulf travels to the hall of the Danish king Hrothgar, whose great mead-hall Heorot has been terrorized for years by the monster Grendel. Beowulf kills Grendel unarmed, then defeats Grendel's mother in an underwater lair after she attacks Heorot in revenge. In the second movement (set among the Geats, after a fifty-year gap), Beowulf, now an aged king, fights a treasure-guarding dragon roused by a thief; he kills the dragon but is fatally wounded, and the poem closes with his funeral and elegiac reflections on the uncertain future of his people.

The poem is notable for its digressive structure — episodes of legendary or historical material (the Finnsburg episode, the Swedish–Geatish wars, allusions to Sigemund and Heremod) are woven through the main narrative, generally functioning as thematic commentary on kingship, feud, and the transience of glory (a concern closely related to the elegiac mode discussed in Chapter 4).

## Critical Perspectives

- **The dating controversy.** *Beowulf*'s date of composition is one of the most contested questions in Old English studies. Scholarly opinion has ranged across the eighth through early eleventh centuries; for much of the twentieth century an eighth-century date was the dominant assumption, but Colin Chase's 1981 edited collection *The Dating of Beowulf* (sometimes informally called the "Toronto" intervention) reopened the question and shifted opinion toward later dates for a time. No consensus has since been reached; scholars broadly agree only that the poem was composed sometime between the conversion of the Anglo-Saxons and the copying of the surviving manuscript around 1000, with linguistic, metrical, palaeographical, and onomastic arguments all deployed on different sides without decisive result.
- **Tolkien's intervention.** Before 1936, much scholarship treated *Beowulf* chiefly as a quarry for historical, linguistic, and folkloric data, with the poem's monsters regarded as an embarrassment to be explained away or minimized. Tolkien's lecture argued that the monsters are structurally and thematically central — the poem's true subject is mortality and the doomed grandeur of heroism against inevitable defeat — and reoriented subsequent scholarship toward literary-critical reading. Michael D. C. Drout has called it possibly the single most influential essay in twentieth-century literary studies; John D. Niles similarly notes that modern *Beowulf* criticism is generally traced back to 1936.
- **Christian-pagan syncretism.** Because the poem's Scandinavian, pre-Christian legendary material is narrated by a voice that repeatedly invokes Christian cosmology (identifying Grendel's kin with the biblical Cain; attributing events to "Metod," fate/God), critics remain divided over how to characterize the poem's religious outlook — as a Christian poet's elegy for a noble but doomed pagan past, as a syncretic imaginative world, or as evidence of successive layers of composition and revision.
- **Formalist and structuralist readings** have examined the poem's balanced two-part/three-fight structure (Grendel, Grendel's mother, the dragon) and its use of digression and foreshadowing (*flashback* and *flashforward* techniques) as deliberate artistic strategies rather than compositional untidiness.
- **Feminist and gender criticism** has re-examined female figures in the poem — Wealhtheow, Hildeburh, Grendel's mother, Modthryth — as more thematically and structurally significant than earlier, male-hero-centred criticism generally allowed.

## Chapter Summary

*Beowulf*, the longest and most celebrated surviving work of Old English poetry, tells of the Geatish hero's three great fights — against Grendel, Grendel's mother, and, in old age, a dragon — set within a Scandinavian legendary world narrated through a pervasive Christian framework. The poem survives uniquely in the Nowell Codex, copied around 1000 and damaged in the 1731 Ashburnham House fire; Thorkelin's 1786–87 transcripts and 1815 edition preserve readings since lost. Its date of composition remains genuinely unresolved among specialists. Tolkien's 1936 lecture decisively reoriented modern criticism toward reading the poem as deliberate literary art centred on its monsters and on mortality, a shift that underlies most subsequent critical approaches, from Christian-pagan syncretism to formalist structural analysis to feminist re-readings of its female characters.

---

## Glossary

- ***Editio princeps*** — the first printed edition of a text.
- **Flashback/flashforward (digression)** — the poem's technique of interweaving legendary or historical episodes into the main narrative for thematic effect.
- **Nowell Codex** — the manuscript, part of British Library Cotton MS Vitellius A.xv, containing the unique surviving copy of *Beowulf*.
- **Wyrd** — Old English concept often translated "fate," recurrent in the poem's meditation on mortality and heroic reputation.
