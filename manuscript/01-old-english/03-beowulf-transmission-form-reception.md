## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Here's the first surprise about the most famous poem in Old English literature: it isn't set in England at all. *Beowulf* takes place in Scandinavia, chiefly among the Geats (a people of what is now southern Sweden) and the Danes, and it draws on a body of shared Germanic legend that also turns up in later Scandinavian sources, such as the *Grettis saga*. So why does an English book claim this poem? Because of language, not geography: it was composed in Old English, by an English poet, for an English audience, and it survives only inside an English manuscript. The story is Scandinavian; the poem is English.

That mismatch produces the single biggest interpretive puzzle you'll meet in this chapter. The world the poem describes is pre-Christian and Scandinavian, full of feuding tribes, treasure-hoards, and blood-vengeance. But the voice narrating it is unmistakably Christian, and keeps framing events in Christian terms. Holding those two things together — a pagan story world and a Christian storyteller — is something the poem never quite resolves, and it's exactly what makes *Beowulf* so interesting to argue about. We'll come back to this directly in Critical Perspectives, below.

## Literary Movements

If Chapter 2 taught you the toolkit — alliterative verse, formulas, the comitatus ethic — think of *Beowulf* as the poem that shows you every one of those tools used at full stretch. It's widely regarded as the single greatest achievement of Old English heroic poetry, and it works by braiding together two strands you already know how to spot. The first is the inherited Germanic heroic tradition: obligations to kin, the logic of feud, the ritual of a lord giving treasure to his warriors, and the drive to win a lasting reputation, since a warrior's name outliving his death is often the only immortality this world offers him. The second is a Christian interpretive overlay: the narrator keeps stepping back to frame events in providential terms, and even traces the ancestry of the poem's monsters back to the biblical figure of Cain. Scholars still argue about how deliberately the poet combined these two strands — whether one Christian poet imagined the whole pagan past this way from the start, or whether Christian material was layered onto older pagan story matter over time. Neither answer changes what makes the poem work; it only changes how you imagine the poet at work.

## Author Profiles

We don't know who wrote *Beowulf*. Not the name, not the region, not even for certain whether it was one person or several. What follows, then, isn't really a profile of an author — it's a profile of the poem's afterlife: the people who owned the manuscript, rescued it from fire, transcribed it, and eventually made the wider world care about it.

**The Beowulf-poet** — Entirely unknown; unnamed in the manuscript and unidentifiable from external evidence. Scholarly convention refers to "the poet" or "the *Beowulf*-poet" as a single composing intelligence, though even this assumption cannot be verified.

**Laurence Nowell** (c. 1530–c. 1570) — Elizabethan antiquarian in whose possession the manuscript is first recorded (1563); the codex is now named for him.

**Sir Robert Bruce Cotton** (1571–1631) — Collector whose library, including the *Beowulf* manuscript, later passed to the British nation and gives the manuscript its shelfmark, Cotton Vitellius A.xv.

**Grímur Jónsson Thorkelin** (1752–1829) — Icelandic-Danish scholar and Keeper of the Danish National Archives who commissioned and made transcripts of the manuscript in 1786–87 and published the first printed edition (*editio princeps*) in 1815; his transcripts preserve readings from the manuscript's edges since lost to fire damage.

**J. R. R. Tolkien** (1892–1973) — Oxford philologist and author whose 1936 British Academy lecture, "Beowulf: The Monsters and the Critics," argued for reading the poem's monsters (Grendel, Grendel's mother, the dragon) as central to its meaning rather than as embarrassing folkloric material to be mined for historical data beneath. Widely credited with founding the modern era of *Beowulf* literary criticism.

**Seamus Heaney** (1939–2013) — Irish Nobel laureate whose 1999 verse translation of *Beowulf* became an unusual bestseller among translations of ancient poetry, winning the Whitbread Book of the Year Award and substantially widening the poem's general readership.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

At 3,182 alliterative long lines, *Beowulf* is easily the longest poem to survive from Old English, and its plot divides cleanly into two halves separated by a fifty-year jump in time.

The first half is set in Denmark. A young Geatish warrior named Beowulf sails to the hall of the Danish king Hrothgar, whose great mead-hall, Heorot, has been terrorized for years by a monster called Grendel. Beowulf kills Grendel with his bare hands, refusing even to use a weapon, and then has to fight a second time when Grendel's mother attacks Heorot in revenge — this time following her down into an underwater lair to finish the fight.

Fifty years pass between the first half and the second. Beowulf is now an old man, king of the Geats, and a thief has disturbed a dragon guarding a treasure-hoard. Beowulf fights the dragon and kills it, but is fatally wounded doing so. The poem ends with his funeral, and with his people looking anxiously toward a future they suspect will be far less secure without him.

Notice, as you read, how often the poem interrupts itself. Woven through the main story are digressions — the Finnsburg episode, the wars between the Swedes and the Geats, passing references to legendary figures like Sigemund and Heremod — that have nothing directly to do with Grendel or the dragon. These aren't padding. Read as a group, they're the poem's way of commenting on its own central concerns: kingship, feud, and the fact that glory, however hard-won, never lasts. You'll meet that same concern with glory's transience again in Chapter 4, when we turn to the Old English elegies.

## Critical Perspectives

- **When was it written? Nobody actually knows.** This is one of the most stubbornly unresolved questions in the whole field of Old English studies, and it's worth understanding why. Proposals for the poem's date span the eighth century all the way to the early eleventh. For most of the twentieth century, scholars generally assumed an eighth-century date. Then, in 1981, Colin Chase edited a collection called *The Dating of Beowulf* that reopened the whole question and pushed opinion toward later dates for a while. No consensus has emerged since. About the only thing scholars broadly agree on is the outer boundary: the poem must have been composed sometime after the Anglo-Saxons converted to Christianity and before the surviving manuscript was copied around the year 1000. Everything in between — arguments from language, metre, handwriting style, even the spelling of proper names — has been tried, and none of it has settled the question.
- **Tolkien changed how people read the poem.** Before 1936, most scholars treated *Beowulf* mainly as raw material — a quarry to be mined for historical, linguistic, or folkloric data, with the monsters themselves treated almost as an embarrassment, something to explain away rather than take seriously. Tolkien's lecture flipped that completely: he argued the monsters *are* the poem's real subject, and that the poem is fundamentally about mortality and about heroism's doomed grandeur in the face of certain defeat. That argument reoriented the entire field toward reading *Beowulf* as literature, not just as evidence. The scholar Michael D. C. Drout has called it possibly the single most influential essay in twentieth-century literary studies, and John D. Niles notes that modern *Beowulf* criticism is usually traced back to this one lecture.
- **A pagan world, told by a Christian voice.** The poem's Scandinavian, pre-Christian legendary material is narrated by a voice that keeps invoking Christian ideas — identifying Grendel's ancestry with the biblical Cain, attributing events to "Metod" (fate, or God). Critics are still divided on what to make of this. Is it a Christian poet's elegy for a noble but doomed pagan past? A genuinely blended imaginative world where both belief systems coexist? Or evidence that the poem was composed and then reworked in layers, by more than one hand, over time? All three readings remain live in the scholarship.
- **The shape of the poem is deliberate, not accidental.** Formalist and structuralist critics have shown that the poem's balanced structure — two halves, three fights (Grendel, Grendel's mother, the dragon), and its use of digression and foreshadowing — is a designed artistic strategy, not compositional untidiness, as some earlier critics assumed.
- **Look again at the poem's women.** Feminist and gender criticism has returned to figures like Wealhtheow, Hildeburh, Grendel's mother, and Modthryth, and argued that they carry far more thematic and structural weight than older, hero-centred readings of the poem tended to notice.

## Chapter Summary

*Beowulf* is the longest and most celebrated poem to survive from Old English, and it tells of one hero's three great fights — against Grendel, Grendel's mother, and, in old age, a dragon — set inside a pre-Christian Scandinavian legendary world but narrated by a persistently Christian voice. It survives in exactly one manuscript, the Nowell Codex, copied around 1000 and damaged in the 1731 Ashburnham House fire; we know some of what was lost only because Thorkelin transcribed the manuscript in 1786–87, before the worst of the damage, and published the first edition in 1815. Nobody has settled when the poem was actually composed. What we do know is how radically our way of reading it has changed: Tolkien's 1936 lecture turned *Beowulf* from a quarry of historical data into a work of deliberate literary art centred on mortality and the monsters themselves, and that shift underlies nearly everything critics have argued about the poem since — from its Christian-pagan blend, to its formal design, to what its female characters are actually doing in the story.

## Glossary

- ***Editio princeps*** — the first printed edition of a text.
- **Flashback/flashforward (digression)** — the poem's technique of interweaving legendary or historical episodes into the main narrative for thematic effect.
- **Nowell Codex** — the manuscript, part of British Library Cotton MS Vitellius A.xv, containing the unique surviving copy of *Beowulf*.
- **Wyrd** — Old English concept often translated "fate," recurrent in the poem's meditation on mortality and heroic reputation.
