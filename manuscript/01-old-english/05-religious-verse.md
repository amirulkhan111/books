## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Religious verse composition in Anglo-Saxon England developed directly out of the conversion process described in Chapter 1: as Latin Christian learning took root in monastic centres from the later seventh century onward, a vernacular tradition of specifically Christian poetry grew up alongside — and in continual exchange with — the inherited heroic verse tradition discussed in Chapters 2–4. This chapter's three case studies represent three distinct modes of that tradition's emergence: a miraculous popular legend of vernacular inspiration (Cædmon), a self-consciously literate and signed poetic corpus (Cynewulf), and an anonymous but formally daring devotional poem that recasts the Crucifixion in the imaginative terms of heroic verse (*The Dream of the Rood*).

## Literary Movements

Old English religious verse spans a spectrum from biblical paraphrase (the Junius manuscript poems — *Genesis*, *Exodus*, *Daniel* — traditionally, though incorrectly, once attributed wholesale to Cædmon) to devotional dream-vision and saints' lives. Two features recur across this body of work and are central to this chapter: first, the adaptation of heroic-poetic diction and values (lordship, loyalty, the warrior ethic) to specifically Christian subject matter, most vividly in *The Dream of the Rood*'s presentation of Christ as a "young hero" (*geong hæleð*) who mounts the cross as if to battle; second, an emerging, if still limited, sense of individual poetic authorship, exemplified by Cynewulf's runic signatures, in a literary culture otherwise dominated by anonymity.

## Author Profiles

**Cædmon** (fl. before 680) — According to Bede's account (*Historia Ecclesiastica* IV.24), an illiterate lay herdsman at the double monastery of Streonæshalch (Whitby) under Abbess Hild, who, ashamed of his inability to sing when the harp was passed at communal feasts, left one such gathering and fell asleep in the stable. In a dream a figure commanded him to sing "of the beginning of created things," and Cædmon awoke with the ability to compose Christian verse, later formally entering the monastery and, per Bede, becoming a prolific religious poet — though only the nine-line *Hymn* embedded in Bede's narrative can be securely attributed to him. *Cædmon's Hymn* is the oldest surviving poem in Old English, praising God as creator; it survives in both Northumbrian and later West Saxon dialect forms across the twenty-one surviving manuscripts of the Old English *Historia* translation.

**Cynewulf** (fl. probably 9th century, dates disputed) — One of only a handful of Old English poets known to us by name, and the only one whose corpus can be identified with confidence through internal textual evidence: four poems — *Juliana*, *Elene*, *Christ II* (*The Ascension*), and *The Fates of the Apostles* — each conclude with a passage in which runic characters, standing for the letters of "CYNEWULF," are woven into the verse, typically accompanied by a request for prayers on the poet's behalf. Almost nothing external is known of his identity, dates, or milieu; proposals have ranged from the late eighth to the early tenth century, without resolution. His practice of claiming authorship, unusual in a tradition otherwise dominated by anonymous composition, has made him a focal point for discussions of individual authorial identity in Old English verse.

**The *Dream of the Rood*-poet** — Unknown. Composed a 156-line (in its fullest surviving form) dream-vision poem in which the cross ("rood") itself narrates the Crucifixion in the first person, addressing the human dreamer who has seen it in a vision.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Cædmon's Hymn*** — A nine-line alliterative praise-poem addressing God as "heaven-kingdom's Guardian," creator of heaven and earth. Its significance lies less in complexity than in status: it is the earliest datable English poem whose circumstances of composition are recorded by name and narrative, making it a foundational document in histories of English poetry, and it demonstrates that vernacular verse form could be adapted from heroic to explicitly Christian devotional purpose from a very early point in the conversion period.
- ***The Fates of the Apostles*, *Juliana*, *Elene*, *Christ II*** (Cynewulf) — *Elene*, generally regarded as Cynewulf's finest work, narrates the legend of St Helena's discovery of the True Cross; *Juliana* is a saint's-life/martyrdom narrative; *Christ II* treats Christ's Ascension; *The Fates of the Apostles* briefly surveys the deaths of the apostles. Across all four, Cynewulf's runic epilogues function simultaneously as authorial signature, request for intercessory prayer, and a formally virtuosic display of poetic craft.
- ***The Dream of the Rood*** — The dreamer sees a vision of the cross, which then speaks, describing its own experience of being felled as a tree, forced to bear Christ's body, and enduring the Crucifixion alongside him — the poem daringly figures Christ as a "young hero" who "climbed" or "mounted" the cross with warrior resolve, and the cross itself as a loyal retainer forced, in violation of the comitatus ethic explored in Chapter 2, to participate in its lord's death rather than defend him. The poem closes with the dreamer's resolve to seek the cross and heaven's reward. It is widely regarded as one of the most sophisticated and moving achievements of Old English religious poetry.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Cædmon: legend or history?** Bede's account is often read critically as a hagiographic "miracle of inspiration" narrative — a recognizable topos in Christian literature — rather than a literal record; even so, most scholars accept the historical existence of Cædmon and the substantial authenticity of the nine-line *Hymn*, given its early attestation and dialectal variation across manuscript witnesses.
- **Cynewulf and the problem of authorship.** Cynewulf's runic signatures have prompted extensive debate over their function: are they primarily a bid for personal literary reputation (a striking departure from anonymous Anglo-Saxon compositional norms), a request for intercessory prayer typical of pious humility topoi, or — as some recent scholarship argues — meant to function cryptographically/didactically within the poems' eschatological content, encoding a meditation on Judgment alongside the poet's name. Attempts to identify Cynewulf with a specific historical figure (a bishop, an abbot, various named clerics) remain unproven.
- **Heroic Christology in *The Dream of the Rood*.** Critics such as Stanley B. Greenfield have examined how the poem's recasting of Christ as a warrior-hero and the cross as his retainer inverts and reworks the comitatus ethic central to secular heroic poetry (Chapters 2–3): rather than defending its lord, the cross must become the instrument of his death, a paradox the poem dwells on directly.
- **The Ruthwell Cross and textual history.** Because a related but not identical runic text is carved on the early eighth-century Ruthwell Cross, roughly two and a half centuries before the Vercelli Book's copying, *The Dream of the Rood* offers unusually direct, if incomplete and disputed, evidence for the oral/written prehistory of an Old English poem prior to its manuscript form — a rare check on the manuscript-only transmission problem discussed throughout this Part.

## Chapter Summary

Old English religious verse grew directly out of the conversion process, adapting inherited heroic-poetic form and diction to Christian subject matter. Bede's account of Cædmon preserves the earliest datable English poem and a foundational (if legendary-inflected) narrative of vernacular Christian inspiration. Cynewulf, identifiable through his distinctive runic signatures across four poems, represents a rare instance of claimed individual authorship within an overwhelmingly anonymous tradition. *The Dream of the Rood*, anonymous but formally daring, recasts the Crucifixion through the heroic idiom of lordship and retainer-loyalty explored earlier in this Part, and its partial attestation on the early eighth-century Ruthwell Cross offers unusually direct evidence of a pre-manuscript textual history. Together these three case studies illustrate the range of modes — miraculous, learned/authored, and anonymously virtuosic — through which Anglo-Saxon poets brought vernacular verse to bear on Christian devotion.

## Glossary

- ***Geong hæleð*** — "young hero," the epithet applied to Christ in *The Dream of the Rood*.
- **Prosopopoeia** — a rhetorical device in which an inanimate object or abstraction is given a human voice; central to *The Dream of the Rood*, in which the cross narrates its own experience.
- **Rood** — an Old English word for a cross or crucifix.
- **Runic signature** — Cynewulf's practice of encoding his name in runic characters within the closing lines of his poems.
