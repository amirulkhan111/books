## Historical and Socio-Political Context

The Old English elegies belong to the same mead-hall, comitatus-centred world discussed in Chapter 2: their emotional force depends on an implied audience for whom loss of lord, kin, and hall-companionship represented the deepest possible social rupture. All the poems treated in this chapter survive uniquely in the **Exeter Book**, a manuscript copied c. 975 and given to Exeter Cathedral by Bishop Leofric in 1072 (see Chapter 2). None can be dated with confidence beyond this manuscript terminus; authorship, occasion, and even, in the case of *The Wife's Lament*, the precise narrative situation being described, remain genuinely uncertain.

## Literary Movements

"Elegy" as applied to Old English poetry is a modern critical category, not an Anglo-Saxon generic term; scholars group together a cluster of Exeter Book poems (chiefly *The Wanderer*, *The Seafarer*, *The Wife's Lament*, *The Ruin*, *The Husband's Message*, *Wulf and Eadwacer*, and *Deor*, discussed in Chapter 2) that share a first-person meditative voice, a concern with loss and transience, and recurring formal devices, most notably the **ubi sunt** motif (Latin, "where are they?" — a rhetorical lament for vanished people, glory, or hall-joys) and the **exile theme**, in which a speaker is cut off, voluntarily or involuntarily, from lordship, kinship, and community. As with the heroic verse discussed in Chapters 2–3, a Christian didactic or consolatory frame is frequently laid over material whose emotional charge derives from a pre-Christian heroic value system centred on lordship and hall-community.

## Author Profiles

All three poems are anonymous; nothing is known of their authors beyond what can be inferred from the texts themselves. This chapter therefore profiles the poems' internal speakers and the modern critics whose readings have shaped their interpretation.

**The Wanderer-speaker (*eardstapa*, "earth-stepper")** — An exiled retainer who recalls serving a now-dead lord, receiving gifts and companionship in the mead-hall, and being driven by *wyrd* (fate) into solitary wandering after his lord's death in battle; the poem moves from personal lament through *ubi sunt* meditation on the ruin of all earthly things to a closing statement of consolation in "the Father in the heavens."

**The Seafarer-speaker** — A speaker whose voice shifts between recounting the physical hardship of sea-voyaging and (more ambiguously) reflecting on voluntary exile as spiritual discipline; the poem's second half moves into explicitly homiletic reflection on the transience of earthly glory and the primacy of heavenly reward.

**The Wife's Lament-speaker** — A woman (identifiable as female through Old English grammatical gender in the poem's adjectives and pronouns), separated from her husband/lord under obscure circumstances involving feud, exile, or betrayal, speaking from a state of enforced solitude (described as an "earth-cave" beneath an oak tree). She is one of very few clearly female-voiced speakers in the surviving Old English corpus.

**Benjamin Thorpe** (1782–1870) — Editor of the first full edition of the Exeter Book (1842), who gave the poem now called *The Wife's Lament* its initial title, "The Exile's Lament."

**Dorothy Whitelock** (1901–1982) — Influential Anglo-Saxonist whose 1950 essay argued that *The Seafarer*'s speaker should be read literally as a *peregrinus*, a monk undertaking voluntary exile "for the love of God," rather than as a sustained allegory; her reading directly challenged prior allegorical interpretations and remains a central reference point in the poem's critical history.

**Ezra Pound** (1885–1972) — American Modernist poet whose free 1911 translation of *The Seafarer*, prioritizing rhythmic and imagistic effect over philological precision, introduced the poem to a wide literary readership and linked Old English elegy to Modernist concerns with fragmentation and alienation; the translation later echoed in his own *Cantos*.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***The Wanderer*** (115 lines) — Structured in three movements: personal reminiscence of lost lordship and companionship; a formal *ubi sunt* passage lamenting the ruin of hall, horse, and warrior alike ("Hwær cwom mearg? Hwær cwom mago?" — "Where has the horse gone? Where the young warrior?"); and a closing turn toward Christian consolation. Critics debate whether this closing turn is integral to a unified design or a distinct, possibly later, homiletic frame imposed on older elegiac material.
- ***The Seafarer*** (approximately 124 lines) — Opens with vivid, sensorily precise description of the hardships of winter sea-voyaging, then shifts toward reflection on the seafaring life as spiritual discipline and, in its final third, toward explicit homiletic exhortation on mortality and the fleeting nature of earthly glory. The poem's structural and tonal shifts have generated the field's most sustained interpretive debate (see Critical Perspectives).
- ***The Wife's Lament*** (53 lines) — The most interpretively contested of the three: the poem's narrative situation is never made explicit, its ending is genuinely ambiguous (cursing her husband, or expressing continued longing, depending on reading), and its close relation to the riddle genre elsewhere in the Exeter Book has led some critics to read it as generically hybrid rather than a straightforward first-person lament.

---

## Critical Perspectives

- **The allegorical debate over *The Seafarer*.** Earlier twentieth-century criticism often read the poem's sea-voyage as a sustained allegory of the Christian soul's pilgrimage through life. Dorothy Whitelock's 1950 essay argued instead for a literal reading: the speaker is a *peregrinus*, a real ascetic undertaking voluntary exile at sea "for the love of God," a recognizable practice in "the age of Bede and Boniface." John C. Pope, while agreeing that the poem concerns penitential exile, argued for continued allegorical layering rather than Whitelock's literalism. The debate remains unresolved and is frequently used in teaching to demonstrate how differently trained critics can read the same text's controlling logic.
- **The integrity of the Christian ending in *The Wanderer* and *The Seafarer*.** Both poems close with explicitly Christian consolatory or homiletic material following extended secular/heroic lament. Critics divide over whether this represents a single poet's coherent artistic and spiritual design (moving the reader from worldly to heavenly consolation) or the layering of a Christian frame onto pre-existing elegiac material — a debate structurally similar to the Christian-pagan syncretism question raised about *Beowulf* in Chapter 3.
- **Genre instability in *The Wife's Lament*.** Faye Walker-Pelkey and other critics have argued that the poem's fragmented structure, obscure narrative situation, gnomic wisdom-statements, and manuscript placement near the Exeter Book's riddles suggest it may function partly as a riddle, resisting straightforward classification as a personal lament. Other readings have proposed allegorical interpretations (the speaker as the Church separated from Christ) or have suggested the speaker addresses the reader from beyond death — none has achieved consensus.
- **Feminist recovery of the female voice.** *The Wife's Lament* (along with *Wulf and Eadwacer*) is regularly foregrounded in feminist scholarship as rare direct evidence of a female first-person perspective within a corpus otherwise dominated by male heroic and homiletic voices, prompting renewed attention to women's experience of exile, separation, and social precarity in Anglo-Saxon literary culture.

## Chapter Summary

The Old English elegies — a modern critical grouping rather than an Anglo-Saxon generic label — share a first-person meditative voice, the *ubi sunt* motif, and the exile theme rooted in the comitatus culture discussed in Chapter 2. *The Wanderer* moves an exiled retainer's personal lament through formal *ubi sunt* meditation to Christian consolation; *The Seafarer* pairs vivid physical description of sea-hardship with a contested shift into religious reflection, most influentially debated between Whitelock's literal-pilgrimage reading and allegorical alternatives; and *The Wife's Lament* offers Old English poetry's clearest female first-person voice, while remaining the corpus's most genuinely unresolved interpretive puzzle. All three poems survive only in the Exeter Book, and all three raise, in different ways, the recurring Old English critical question — familiar from *Beowulf* — of how a Christian textual frame relates to material whose emotional logic is rooted in a pre-Christian heroic ethic.

## Glossary

- ***Eardstapa*** — "earth-stepper," the term used for the speaker of *The Wanderer*.
- **Elegy (Old English)** — a modern critical term for a cluster of Exeter Book poems sharing a meditative first-person voice and themes of loss, exile, and transience.
- **Exile theme** — the recurring Old English motif of a speaker cut off from lordship, kinship, and hall-community.
- ***Peregrinus*** — Latin, "wanderer/pilgrim"; in Whitelock's reading of *The Seafarer*, a monk undertaking voluntary religious exile.
- **Ubi sunt** — Latin, "where are they?"; a rhetorical formula lamenting the disappearance of past glory, people, or joys.
