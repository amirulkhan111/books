## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 1 gave you the politics. This chapter takes you inside a building: the Anglo-Saxon hall. Once you can picture what actually happened there, most of Old English poetry's strangest features — its formulas, its praise of generosity, its obsession with loyalty — stop looking strange and start looking obvious.

Anglo-Saxon aristocratic life revolved around the hall, a single large timber building that was part royal court, part barracks, part banqueting room. You'll recognize it later as Heorot, the great hall at the centre of *Beowulf*. Inside it, a lord fed, armed, and rewarded the warriors who served him, and in exchange those warriors owed him military service and personal loyalty, even at the cost of their own lives. Scholars have long borrowed a Latin word for this bond: **comitatus**. It comes from the Roman historian Tacitus, who described a very similar warband ethic among Germanic peoples in his book *Germania* (written around AD 98) — more than three centuries before the Anglo-Saxons even reached Britain. Use Tacitus carefully: he never saw Anglo-Saxon England, he was writing about a different (if related) set of Germanic peoples, and he had his own agenda — holding up "Germanic" simplicity as an implicit rebuke to what he saw as Roman decadence back home. Still, the loyalty bond he describes matches, closely enough to be useful, the ethic you'll see dramatized again and again in Old English heroic poetry.¹

Every functioning hall needed someone to keep its stories straight, and that job belonged to the **scop** (say it roughly like "shop"). A scop was a poet-singer, often permanently attached to one lord's court, and the role was far more than entertainment. A scop preserved a family's genealogy, kept historical memory alive, and — perhaps most importantly — reinforced the very heroic and social values the comitatus depended on, simply by praising them in verse. A related, looser Old English term, **gleeman**, describes a more itinerant kind of performer; the sources don't always draw a sharp line between the two. Crucially, none of this poetry started life as writing. It was composed and performed aloud, usually to the accompaniment of a harp or lyre, long before — and often quite apart from — anyone wrote it down.

Two Old English poems give us our best picture of how this culture imagined the scop's role: *Widsith* and *Deor*, both preserved in the same manuscript, the Exeter Book. We'll look at both in detail below.

## Literary Movements

### Oral-formulaic composition

Here's a question worth sitting with for a moment: if a scop composed poetry aloud, on the spot, without a script, how did he keep track of it all — metre, alliteration, and a coherent story, simultaneously, in front of an audience? **Oral-formulaic theory** is the modern scholarly answer to that question. It proposes that poets in an oral tradition aren't reciting from memory the way an actor recites lines. Instead, they compose in real time by drawing on a stock of ready-made building blocks: **formulas** (recurring word-groups that already fit the metre, like fixed descriptive phrases for a hero or a sea-voyage) and **themes** (recurring type-scenes, such as arriving at a hall, arming for battle, or describing a feast). Think of it less like writing an essay from scratch and more like a jazz musician improvising over a set of familiar chord changes.

This theory didn't start out as a theory about Old English at all. It began with the American classicist Milman Parry, who noticed in 1928 that Homer's epic epithets ("swift-footed Achilles," "wine-dark sea") looked suspiciously like exactly this kind of formula. Parry then went looking for a living oral tradition he could actually observe, and in the 1930s he and his student Albert Lord travelled to Yugoslavia to study South Slavic oral epic singers still performing this way. Lord eventually turned their findings into a landmark book, *The Singer of Tales* (1960).²

In 1953, the scholar Francis P. Magoun applied this same framework directly to Old English poetry for the first time. His argument was striking: he claimed that over 70 percent of *Beowulf*'s lines have formulaic parallels elsewhere in surviving Old English poetry, and he took that density as proof the poem was composed orally, the same way the Yugoslav singers composed.³ Other scholars soon extended Magoun's approach across much of the surviving Old English poetic corpus. You'll see in the Critical Perspectives section below that this argument didn't stand unchallenged for long.

### Alliterative verse form

Before you can hear what makes Old English poetry sound the way it does, unlearn one assumption: it doesn't rhyme, and it isn't counted out in syllables the way, say, a Shakespearean sonnet is. Instead, it's built on stress and **alliteration** — the repetition of the same consonant sound at the start of stressed words. Each line splits into two halves, separated by a pause called a **caesura**, and each half normally carries two stressed syllables (called "lifts"). Alliteration on those stressed syllables is what stitches the two halves of the line together. In the late nineteenth century, the German philologist Eduard Sievers studied thousands of these half-lines and sorted their stress patterns into **five basic types, labelled A through E** — a classification scholars still use today, with some refinement, whenever they analyze Old English metre.⁴

Two other stylistic habits are worth learning now, because you'll meet them constantly from here on. A **kenning** is a compressed, often compound metaphor standing in for an ordinary noun — *hronrad*, literally "whale-road," is simply the sea. And **litotes** is ironic understatement, where a poet affirms something by denying its opposite — calling a catastrophe "no small hardship," for instance, when it was in fact enormous.

### From oral performance to written text

Here's a fact that should give you pause: every single poem discussed in this book from the Old English period survives in one of just **four manuscripts** — the **Exeter Book**, the **Vercelli Book**, the **Junius manuscript** (sometimes called the Cædmon manuscript), and the **Nowell Codex**, which happens to hold the only surviving copy of *Beowulf*. All four were copied out in the late tenth century or around the year 1000.⁵ Whatever oral life a given poem may have had before that point, what has actually reached us is a written object, copied by literate, Christian monks working in a scriptorium — the very institution you met in Chapter 1. That gap between oral origin and written survival is not a minor technicality. It sits at the centre of the critical debate below.

## Author Profiles

Here's something that surprises most students meeting Old English poetry for the first time: almost none of it has a named author. Oral composition and anonymous copying by monastic scribes were simply the norm, and the one major exception, Cædmon, gets his own treatment in Chapter 5. So instead of poets, this chapter profiles two things: the fictional scop-narrators the surviving poems invent for themselves, and the modern scholars whose theories shape how we now read them.

**The Widsith-poet** — Unknown; composed (or, more likely, progressively compiled and reworked) a poem cataloguing, in the first person, the travels of an idealized scop named Widsith ("far-traveller") among the courts of Germanic legend and history. The poem's internal chronology ranges across the fourth to sixth centuries, but its date of composition in anything like its surviving form is uncertain, with proposals ranging from the seventh century onward; it survives only in the tenth-century Exeter Book copy.

**The Deor-poet** — Unknown; composed *Deor*, a shorter lament spoken by a court poet named Deor who has lost his position to a rival scop, Heorrenda. The poem consoles itself through a refrain ("that passed away, so may this") recalling a sequence of misfortunes from Germanic legend, implicitly comparing the speaker's own loss to greater historical sufferings.

**Milman Parry** (1902–1935) — American classicist whose analysis of Homeric formulaic epithets and fieldwork on South Slavic oral epic established the foundations of oral-formulaic theory.

**Albert Lord** (1912–1991) — Parry's student and collaborator; formalized oral-formulaic theory in *The Singer of Tales* (1960), introducing the concept of "composition-in-performance."

**Francis P. Magoun** (1895–1979) — Harvard scholar who first applied Parry–Lord theory systematically to Old English poetry (1953), arguing for the oral composition of *Beowulf* on the basis of formulaic density.

**Larry D. Benson** (1929–2016) — Scholar who challenged Magoun's conclusions in his 1966 article, proposing that much surviving Old English "formulaic" verse is better understood as **written-formulaic** — literate poetry composed in a traditional style rather than proof of genuine oral composition.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Widsith*** (Exeter Book). The poem's title means "far-traveller," and that's exactly its shape: it's structured as a long catalogue — Old English poets called this list-form a **thula** — of the tribal and royal names its speaker claims to have visited, broken up by short narrative digressions. Don't read it as a reliable travel record. Read it instead as a compendium of the legendary geography a working scop needed to have at his fingertips — a kind of professional knowledge-base in verse. Its narrator, welcomed and rewarded at every court he mentions, is an idealized advertisement for exactly what a scop was supposed to be worth to a lord.
- ***Deor*** (Exeter Book). This poem is shorter, more personal, and more tightly patterned than *Widsith*. It's built around a refrain — roughly "that passed away, so may this" in the original Old English — repeated after each of several stanzas recalling sorrows drawn from Germanic legend (among them Weland the Smith and the story of Eormanric). What makes *Deor* unusual is its frame: the speaker is a named scop, Deor, who tells us he has lost his position at court to a rival singer, Heorrenda, and is consoling himself by remembering that history is full of griefs worse than his own. It is the only Old English poem spoken, explicitly, by a named, individual scop character.

Read together, the two poems complement each other neatly. *Widsith* shows you the scop's prestige, mobility, and command of legend at its most idealized; *Deor* shows you the precarity sitting underneath that same role — a job that could, in fact, be lost.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Magoun's theory, and the pushback it received.** Magoun's claim — that a high density of formulas proves a poem was composed orally — has been significantly qualified since Benson's 1966 challenge. Benson pointed out something important: literate Anglo-Saxon poets, trained to write in a traditional style and often working from written Latin sources, could produce heavily formulaic verse without ever composing "in performance" the way Parry and Lord's Yugoslav singers did. His term for this alternative was **written-formulaic**. Most scholars today treat formulaic density as evidence of a *traditional style of composition*, not as proof, by itself, that a particular poem was improvised aloud rather than written down from the start.
- **Remember the scribe stands between you and the poem.** Every surviving Old English poem reaches you through a late, Christian, monastic copy. That means you're never looking at a transparent transcript of an oral performance — scribes made errors, and sometimes reshaped what they copied on purpose. Keep this in mind whenever a critical argument leans heavily on the exact wording of a single surviving manuscript.
- ***Widsith*: history or idealized portrait?** Some early twentieth-century scholars read *Widsith*'s catalogue as a genuine (if garbled) record of migration-era Germanic tribal memory. More recent criticism leans the other way, treating the poem as a constructed, idealized picture of what a scop's role was supposed to look like, rather than a reliable historical gazetteer.

## Chapter Summary

Anglo-Saxon poetry grew out of a performance culture centred on the mead-hall and the comitatus bond between lord and retainer, and the scop was that culture's entertainer, memory-keeper, and moral teacher all at once — a role you can see most directly in *Widsith* and *Deor*. Twentieth-century scholars, building on Parry and Lord's fieldwork and applied to Old English by Magoun, argued that this poetry's heavy use of formulas proves it was composed orally; Benson's "written-formulaic" challenge showed that formulaic style on its own can't actually tell oral composition apart from literate composition in a traditional style. And there's a further complication behind all of it: everything that survives reaches us through just four late manuscripts, copied by Christian scribes working well after — and at a real remove from — whatever oral performance may once have produced these poems. Keep that gap in mind as you move into the next chapters, without letting it stop you from taking the poems seriously as literature.

## Glossary

- **Caesura** — the metrical pause dividing an Old English poetic line into two half-lines.
- **Comitatus** — the bond of mutual loyalty and obligation between a lord and his retainers, central to Anglo-Saxon heroic culture.
- **Formula** — a recurring, metrically fitted word-group used by oral or oral-derived poets to meet compositional needs in performance.
- **Gleeman** — an Old English term for an itinerant entertainer/performer, sometimes distinguished from, sometimes overlapping with, the court-attached scop.
- **Kenning** — a compressed, often compound metaphorical expression standing in for a more ordinary noun (e.g., "whale-road" for the sea).
- **Litotes** — ironic understatement, frequent in Old English verse, in which something is affirmed by negating its opposite.
- **Scop** — an Anglo-Saxon poet-singer, typically attached to a lord's court.
- **Thula** — an Old English catalogue-poem or list-poem, as exemplified by much of *Widsith*.
- **Written-formulaic** — Larry Benson's term for literate Old English poetry that employs a traditional formulaic style without necessarily being orally composed.
