## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Anglo-Saxon aristocratic and royal life centred on the hall — the physical and symbolic space (evoked by *Beowulf*'s Heorot) in which a lord fed, armed, and rewarded his retainers in exchange for military service and personal loyalty. This bond has long been described by scholars using the Latin term **comitatus**, borrowed from the Roman historian Tacitus's *Germania* (c. AD 98), which describes a similar warband ethic among first-century Germanic peoples; historians treat Tacitus's account cautiously, since he wrote partly as an outsider and partly to hold up "Germanic" simplicity as an implicit reproach to Roman decadence, but the comitatus bond he describes corresponds closely to the loyalty ethic dramatized throughout surviving Old English heroic poetry.¹

Within this hall culture the **scop** (pronounced roughly "shop") held a distinct and valued position: a poet-singer, often attached to a particular lord's court, whose function combined entertainment, historical memory, genealogical record-keeping, and the moral reinforcement of heroic and social ideals. Anglo-Saxon sources also use the term **gleeman** for a more itinerant kind of performer/entertainer, though the distinction between scop and gleeman is not always sharply drawn in the evidence. Poetry in this culture was composed and transmitted primarily through oral performance, accompanied on a harp or lyre, long before — and, in many cases, in addition to — any written transcription.

Two Old English poems, *Widsith* and *Deor*, both preserved in the Exeter Book, are the primary surviving witnesses to how Anglo-Saxon culture imagined the scop's role, and they are treated in detail below.

## Literary Movements

### Oral-formulaic composition

Oral-formulaic theory holds that poets working in an oral tradition compose not by memorizing fixed texts but by drawing, in real time, on a stock of **formulas** (recurring metrically-fitted word-groups, such as fixed epithets) and **themes** (recurring type-scenes, such as arrival at a hall, arming for battle, or the description of a feast). The theory originates with Milman Parry's analysis of Homeric epithets (1928) and was extended through his and Albert Lord's fieldwork among South Slavic oral epic singers in the 1930s, culminating in Lord's *The Singer of Tales* (1960).²

Francis P. Magoun applied this framework directly to Old English poetry in his influential 1953 article, arguing that the high density of repeated formulaic half-lines in *Beowulf* (he claimed over 70 percent of the poem's lines have formulaic parallels elsewhere in the surviving corpus) demonstrated that the poem was orally composed.³ Magoun's approach was extended by other scholars to much of the surviving Old English poetic corpus.

### Alliterative verse form

Old English poetry is unrhymed and organized not by syllable-count but by stress and alliteration. Each line divides into two half-lines separated by a pause (**caesura**), typically with two stressed syllables (lifts) per half-line, linked across the line by alliteration on stressed syllables. The German philologist Eduard Sievers, working in the late nineteenth century, classified the resulting half-line stress patterns into **five basic types (A–E)**, a taxonomy still used, with modification, in Old English metrical study today.⁴ Poets drew on formulaic vocabulary including compound metaphors known as **kennings** (e.g., *hronrad*, "whale-road," for the sea) and understatement through **litotes** (e.g., describing a fearsome event as "no small hardship").

### From oral performance to written text

Nearly the entire surviving corpus of Old English poetry is preserved in just four manuscript codices, all copied in the late tenth or c. 1000: the **Exeter Book**, the **Vercelli Book**, the **Junius manuscript** (also called the Cædmon manuscript), and the **Nowell Codex** (containing the unique surviving copy of *Beowulf*).⁵ Whatever oral prehistory particular poems may have had, what survives to us is inescapably a written artifact, copied by literate, Christian, monastic scribes — a mediation that is central to the critical debate discussed below.

## Author Profiles

Because oral-formulaic composition and anonymous scribal transmission are the norm for this material, Old English poetry offers almost no securely identified individual poets (the major exception, Cædmon, is treated in Chapter 5). This chapter instead profiles the fictive/idealized scop-personas of the poems themselves, and the modern scholars whose theories govern how we read them.

**The Widsith-poet** — Unknown; composed (or, more likely, progressively compiled and reworked) a poem cataloguing, in the first person, the travels of an idealized scop named Widsith ("far-traveller") among the courts of Germanic legend and history. The poem's internal chronology ranges across the fourth to sixth centuries, but its date of composition in anything like its surviving form is uncertain, with proposals ranging from the seventh century onward; it survives only in the tenth-century Exeter Book copy.

**The Deor-poet** — Unknown; composed *Deor*, a shorter lament spoken by a court poet named Deor who has lost his position to a rival scop, Heorrenda. The poem consoles itself through a refrain ("that passed away, so may this") recalling a sequence of misfortunes from Germanic legend, implicitly comparing the speaker's own loss to greater historical sufferings.

**Milman Parry** (1902–1935) — American classicist whose analysis of Homeric formulaic epithets and fieldwork on South Slavic oral epic established the foundations of oral-formulaic theory.

**Albert Lord** (1912–1991) — Parry's student and collaborator; formalized oral-formulaic theory in *The Singer of Tales* (1960), introducing the concept of "composition-in-performance."

**Francis P. Magoun** (1895–1979) — Harvard scholar who first applied Parry–Lord theory systematically to Old English poetry (1953), arguing for the oral composition of *Beowulf* on the basis of formulaic density.

**Larry D. Benson** (1929–2016) — Scholar who challenged Magoun's conclusions in his 1966 article, proposing that much surviving Old English "formulaic" verse is better understood as **written-formulaic** — literate poetry composed in a traditional style rather than proof of genuine oral composition.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- ***Widsith*** (Exeter Book) — Structured largely as a catalogue (a "thula") of tribal and royal names, interspersed with brief narrative digressions, *Widsith* is best understood not as a straightforward historical record but as a compendium of the shared legendary geography a functioning scop needed to command. Its idealized narrator, welcomed and rewarded at every court he names, dramatizes the scop's social value as a guardian and disseminator of collective memory.
- ***Deor*** (Exeter Book) — A more personal and formally patterned poem, *Deor* is organized around a refrain-like formula (*þæs ofereode, þisses swa mæg*, roughly "that passed, this also may") that recurs after each of several stanzas recalling sorrows from Germanic legend (including Weland the Smith and the story of Eormanric). Its central conceit — a scop specifically named and given a coherent, if fictionalized, situation (loss of place to a rival named Heorrenda) — makes it the only Old English poem explicitly presented as spoken by a named, individualized scop.

Both poems are frequently read together as complementary evidence: *Widsith* idealizes the scop's mobility, prestige, and encyclopedic command of legend, while *Deor* dramatizes the professional precarity underlying that same role.

---

## Critical Perspectives

- **Magoun's oral-formulaic thesis and its critics.** Magoun's claim that high formulaic density proves oral composition has been substantially qualified since 1966. Benson's "written-formulaic" category pointed out that literate Anglo-Saxon poets, trained in a traditional style and working from written Latin sources, could and did produce heavily formulaic verse without composing "in performance" as Parry and Lord's Yugoslav singers did. Contemporary scholarship generally treats formulaic density as evidence of a *traditional compositional style* rather than as proof, on its own, of a poem's specific mode of composition (oral improvisation versus literate writing within an oral-derived tradition).
- **The mediation of the scribe.** Because every surviving Old English poem reaches us through a late, Christian, monastic scribal filter, scholars caution against treating any given text as a transparent transcript of oral performance; scribal intervention, error, and even deliberate reshaping are demonstrable in the manuscript record.
- **Historicity versus idealization in *Widsith*.** Some early twentieth-century scholars read *Widsith*'s catalogue as embedding genuine historical memory of migration-era Germanic tribes; more recent criticism emphasizes its function as a constructed, idealized portrait of the scop's role rather than a reliable historical gazetteer.

## Chapter Summary

Anglo-Saxon poetry emerged from, and continued to be shaped by, an oral performance culture centred on the mead-hall and the comitatus bond between lord and retainer. Within this culture the scop held a distinctive role as entertainer, memorialist, and moral instructor, a role most directly represented in the surviving corpus by *Widsith* and *Deor*. Twentieth-century oral-formulaic theory, developed by Parry and Lord and applied to Old English verse by Magoun, offered an influential account of how such poetry might have been composed, but Benson's "written-formulaic" critique showed that formulaic style alone cannot securely distinguish oral composition from literate composition in a traditional idiom. What survives of this poetry reaches us exclusively through four late manuscript codices, copied by Christian scribes centuries (in effect) removed from any originating oral performance — a mediation that must inform, without foreclosing, how the poems in the following chapters are read.

## Glossary

- **Caesura** — the metrical pause dividing an Old English poetic line into two half-lines.
- **Comitatus** — the bond of mutual loyalty and obligation between a lord and his retainers, central to Anglo-Saxon heroic culture.
- **Formula** — a recurring, metrically fitted word-group used by oral or oral-derived poets to meet compositional needs in performance.
- **Gleeman** — an Old English term for an itinerant entertainer/performer, sometimes distinguished from, sometimes overlapping with, the court-attached scop.
- **Kenning** — a compressed, often compound metaphorical expression standing in for a more ordinary noun (e.g., "whale-road" for the sea).
- **Litotes** — ironic understatement, frequent in Old English verse, in which something is affirmed by negating its opposite.
- **Scop** — an Anglo-Saxon poet-singer, typically attached to a lord's court.
- **Thula** — an Old English catalogue-poem or list-poem, as exemplified by much of *Widsith*.
- **Written-formulaic** — Larry Benson's term for literate Old English poetry that employs a traditional formulaic style without necessarily being orally composed.
