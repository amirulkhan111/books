# Chapter 6: Prose — The Alfredian Translation Programme, Bede, and the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*

*Part I: Old English (Anglo-Saxon) Literature, c. 450–1066*

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Remember from Chapter 1 how thoroughly Viking raids damaged English monastic learning through the ninth century — libraries burned, scriptoria destroyed, a whole generation's worth of scholarship lost along with the buildings that housed it. This chapter picks up the recovery. Once King **Alfred the Great** (r. 871–899) had secured Wessex against the Danes, he didn't stop at military victory. He launched a deliberate programme to translate important books into English, aiming to rebuild the learning the wars had wrecked.

We know his thinking directly, because one of his prefaces survives in his own voice — the only Alfredian preface where the king speaks as author rather than through a translator's hand. In it, prefacing his Old English translation of Pope Gregory the Great's *Pastoral Care*, he laments that learning had declined so badly in England that hardly anyone south of the Humber could even understand the church services they attended in English, let alone translate a Latin letter. His solution: translate "those books which are most necessary for all men to know" into English, and send a copy to every bishop — each one accompanied by a valuable pointer, called an *æstel*, for following the text while reading.¹ This single programme is the central event in Old English prose literature. It's also the direct institutional backdrop for both the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle* and the Old English translation of Bede's *Historia Ecclesiastica* — so keep Alfred's project in mind as this chapter moves through both.

## Literary Movements

You can trace almost everything in Old English prose back to Alfred's programme or to what grew out of it afterward, along three main paths. The first is **translation**: taking authoritative Latin texts and rendering them into English for pastoral and educational use — this is the Alfredian programme in its purest form. The second is **vernacular historiography**: writing history in English rather than Latin, above all in the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*, kept going across two and a half centuries by multiple monasteries. The third is **homiletic and didactic prose** — sermons and teaching texts — which reached its finest form a century after Alfred, in the hands of Ælfric and Wulfstan. Both of those later writers developed a rhythmical, sometimes even alliterative prose style, borrowing sound-effects from the poetry you studied in earlier chapters while still writing, formally, in prose rather than verse.

## Author Profiles

**Alfred the Great** (r. 871–899) — West Saxon king whose military success against the Danes (Chapter 1) enabled a sustained programme of vernacular translation aimed at restoring English learning. Traditionally credited with personally translating Gregory's *Pastoral Care*, Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy*, Augustine's *Soliloquies*, and the first fifty Psalms, alongside sponsoring translations of Orosius's world history and (per older scholarship) Bede's *Historia*. Since Malcolm Godden's influential 2007 article "Did King Alfred Write Anything?", scholars have increasingly questioned how much of this corpus Alfred translated personally versus commissioned from his court circle of scholars — including Bishop Asser, Archbishop Plegmund, and the priests Grimbald and John — with the debate now generally understood as concerning a shared court educational programme rather than settled solely on the question of Alfred's individual pen.²

**The Ohthere/Wulfstan-narrator (Old English *Orosius*)** — An anonymous adapter, working c. 890 and possibly encouraged by Alfred, who expanded Orosius's late-antique Latin world history with an updated northern-European geography, incorporating first-person-derived travel accounts from two seafarers, Ohthere (who sailed from Hålogaland in northern Norway to the White Sea, and separately to Hedeby in southern Jutland) and Wulfstan (who sailed from Hedeby into the Baltic), reportedly related directly to Alfred's court — among the earliest English-language travel/geographical writing.

**The compilers of the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*** — introduced in Chapter 1; a set of related annals begun under Alfredian sponsorship in the late ninth century and continued at multiple monastic centres (the surviving versions are conventionally labelled A through H), with the Peterborough Chronicle (version E) uniquely continuing the record to 1154, nearly a century past the Norman Conquest.

**Ælfric of Eynsham** (c. 955–c. 1010) — Monk, later abbot, educated at Winchester under Bishop Æthelwold; author of the two series of *Catholic Homilies* (*Sermones Catholici*), the *Lives of Saints* (composed for private reading rather than preaching), and the *Colloquy*, a Latin conversational teaching text with an Old English gloss designed to help students learn spoken Latin. Ælfric developed an increasingly rhythmical and alliterative prose style — so regular in the *Lives of Saints* that modern editors have sometimes laid it out as verse — that draws on the sound-patterning resources of Old English poetry while remaining prose in form and function.

**Wulfstan, Archbishop of York** (d. 1023) — Prominent ecclesiastical and legal figure (also a drafter of royal law codes) best known for *Sermo Lupi ad Anglos* ("The Sermon of the Wolf to the English"), composed between 1010 and 1016 and dated in its manuscript rubric to 1014. Delivered amid renewed Viking attacks under Æthelred the Unready, the sermon catalogues English moral failings as the cause of national suffering, combining eschatological urgency with pointed political commentary in a densely rhetorical, incantatory prose style distinct from Ælfric's more measured homiletic manner.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **The preface to the Old English *Pastoral Care***. Read this as two things at once: a practical statement of educational policy, and a founding document in the history of English as a serious written language. Alfred's insistence on translating "those books most necessary for all men to know" had no real precedent in earlier Anglo-Saxon culture — nobody before him had set out to build an English-language education programme on this scale.
- **The Old English *Orosius*, with Ohthere and Wulfstan's voyages**. This text takes a late-antique Latin world history and adapts it for an English audience, but it does something the Latin original never could: it inserts up-to-date reports about the geography and peoples of northern Europe. Those reports come from two named seafarers, Ohthere and Wulfstan, and they matter twice over — as genuine literature, and as a rare near-contemporary window onto Scandinavian seafaring, written down within living memory of the events described.
- **The *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle***, which you already met in Chapter 1. It's a vernacular, year-by-year record kept going, with real gaps and regional differences, from the late ninth century into the twelfth. Its prose ranges from terse, one-line annal entries to passages of real narrative ambition — including, in one famous entry for the year 937, an embedded poem on the Battle of Brunanburh.
- **Ælfric's *Catholic Homilies* and *Lives of Saints***. The *Homilies* were written for preaching aloud; the *Lives of Saints* for private reading. Both share the same goal — doctrinal clarity for a lay audience with no Latin — delivered in an increasingly polished, rhythmical prose style. Many scholars consider Ælfric the most accomplished prose stylist Old English produced.
- **Wulfstan's *Sermo Lupi ad Anglos***. This is a homily written under real political pressure, interpreting the fresh Viking attacks of the early eleventh century as God's punishment for English sin. Its style is unmistakable once you've read it: long catalogues of vice, insistent repetition, an almost incantatory urgency — very different from Ælfric's calmer, more measured doctrinal prose, because Wulfstan is responding to a specific unfolding crisis, not laying out timeless teaching.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Did Alfred actually write any of "his" books?** In 2007, Malcolm Godden published an article bluntly titled "Did King Alfred Write Anything?", and it reopened a question scholars had mostly taken for granted. Godden's answer, in short: probably not much of it personally. The Old English translation of Bede's *Historia Ecclesiastica*, once routinely called "Alfred's Bede," is now generally accepted as not being Alfred's own work at all, and similar doubt now touches other texts in the traditional Alfredian corpus, including the *Boethius*. Most scholars today describe the "Alfredian" programme as a collaborative project run by Alfred's court, rather than a personal literary achievement by the king himself — though nobody has written Alfred out of the picture entirely; he clearly drove the programme even if he didn't hold the pen for all of it.
- **The *Chronicle* as literature, not just record.** Scholars increasingly read the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*'s two-and-a-half-century survival, across multiple monasteries, as evidence of something more deliberate than routine record-keeping — a genuinely cultivated vernacular prose tradition, part of the same Alfredian push toward English-language writing that produced the translation programme in the first place.
- **Two very different prose stylists, a century after Alfred.** It's worth comparing Ælfric and Wulfstan directly. Ælfric favours clarity, doctrinal caution, and a controlled, rhythmical prose. Wulfstan favours urgency, rhetorical pressure, and long catalogues of accusation. Teachers often use this contrast to show just how much range Old English prose had developed, some 150 years after Alfred's original programme made vernacular prose scholarship possible at all.

## Chapter Summary

Alfred the Great's translation programme, launched in the 890s while England was still recovering from Viking devastation, is the central institutional achievement of Old English prose. It produced, or at least sponsored, translations of Gregory's *Pastoral Care*, Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy*, Augustine's *Soliloquies*, and Orosius's world history — the last expanded with Ohthere and Wulfstan's voyage narratives — and it set the stage for the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*'s long run as vernacular history. Godden's 2007 article complicated the old picture of Alfred as personal author of all this, pointing instead to a collaborative court programme. A century later, Ælfric of Eynsham and Wulfstan of York took Old English prose to its most accomplished late form, in two contrasting registers: Ælfric's measured, rhythmical doctrinal clarity against Wulfstan's urgent, rhetorically charged political preaching — bringing Old English prose to its close on the very eve of the Norman Conquest.

## Glossary

- ***Æstel*** — a valuable pointer (for following text while reading) that Alfred reportedly sent with each copy of the *Pastoral Care* distributed to his bishops.
- **Homily** — a sermon or didactic religious prose text intended for instruction, often for oral delivery.
- ***Sermo Lupi ad Anglos*** — Latin, "The Sermon of the Wolf to the English"; Wulfstan's title, playing on his own name (Wulf-stan, "wolf-stone").
