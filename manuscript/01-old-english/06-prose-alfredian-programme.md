# Chapter 6: Prose — The Alfredian Translation Programme, Bede, and the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*

*Part I: Old English (Anglo-Saxon) Literature, c. 450–1066*

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Chapter 1 described the devastation Viking raids inflicted on English monastic learning through the ninth century. It is against this backdrop that King **Alfred the Great** (r. 871–899), having secured Wessex against the Danes (see Chapter 1), initiated a deliberate programme of vernacular translation intended to restore and extend English learning. In the prefatory letter to his (or his court circle's) Old English translation of Pope Gregory the Great's *Pastoral Care* — the only Alfredian preface in which the king speaks in his own voice as author — he laments that "learning had declined so thoroughly in England that there were very few men on this side of the Humber who could understand their divine services in English, or even translate a letter from Latin into English," and sets out a programme to translate into English "those books which are most necessary for all men to know," distributing copies (each accompanied by a valuable pointer, or *æstel*) to his bishops.¹ This royally sponsored programme is the central episode in Old English prose literature and the direct institutional context for both the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle* and the surviving Old English translation of Bede's *Historia Ecclesiastica*.

## Literary Movements

Old English prose develops along three broadly distinguishable strands, all traceable to the Alfredian moment or its aftermath: (1) **translation/adaptation of authoritative Latin texts** into English for pastoral and educational purposes (the Alfredian programme proper); (2) **vernacular historiography**, chiefly the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*, sustained across two and a half centuries by multiple monastic centres; and (3) **homiletic and didactic prose**, reaching its most accomplished form a century after Alfred in the work of Ælfric and Wulfstan, both of whom developed distinctive rhythmical, at times alliterative, prose styles that draw on the resources of Old English verse discussed in earlier chapters while remaining formally prose.

## Author Profiles

**Alfred the Great** (r. 871–899) — West Saxon king whose military success against the Danes (Chapter 1) enabled a sustained programme of vernacular translation aimed at restoring English learning. Traditionally credited with personally translating Gregory's *Pastoral Care*, Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy*, Augustine's *Soliloquies*, and the first fifty Psalms, alongside sponsoring translations of Orosius's world history and (per older scholarship) Bede's *Historia*. Since Malcolm Godden's influential 2007 article "Did King Alfred Write Anything?", scholars have increasingly questioned how much of this corpus Alfred translated personally versus commissioned from his court circle of scholars — including Bishop Asser, Archbishop Plegmund, and the priests Grimbald and John — with the debate now generally understood as concerning a shared court educational programme rather than settled solely on the question of Alfred's individual pen.²

**The Ohthere/Wulfstan-narrator (Old English *Orosius*)** — An anonymous adapter, working c. 890 and possibly encouraged by Alfred, who expanded Orosius's late-antique Latin world history with an updated northern-European geography, incorporating first-person-derived travel accounts from two seafarers, Ohthere (who sailed from Hålogaland in northern Norway to the White Sea, and separately to Hedeby in southern Jutland) and Wulfstan (who sailed from Hedeby into the Baltic), reportedly related directly to Alfred's court — among the earliest English-language travel/geographical writing.

**The compilers of the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*** — introduced in Chapter 1; a set of related annals begun under Alfredian sponsorship in the late ninth century and continued at multiple monastic centres (the surviving versions are conventionally labelled A through H), with the Peterborough Chronicle (version E) uniquely continuing the record to 1154, nearly a century past the Norman Conquest.

**Ælfric of Eynsham** (c. 955–c. 1010) — Monk, later abbot, educated at Winchester under Bishop Æthelwold; author of the two series of *Catholic Homilies* (*Sermones Catholici*), the *Lives of Saints* (composed for private reading rather than preaching), and the *Colloquy*, a Latin conversational teaching text with an Old English gloss designed to help students learn spoken Latin. Ælfric developed an increasingly rhythmical and alliterative prose style — so regular in the *Lives of Saints* that modern editors have sometimes laid it out as verse — that draws on the sound-patterning resources of Old English poetry while remaining prose in form and function.

**Wulfstan, Archbishop of York** (d. 1023) — Prominent ecclesiastical and legal figure (also a drafter of royal law codes) best known for *Sermo Lupi ad Anglos* ("The Sermon of the Wolf to the English"), composed between 1010 and 1016 and dated in its manuscript rubric to 1014. Delivered amid renewed Viking attacks under Æthelred the Unready, the sermon catalogues English moral failings as the cause of national suffering, combining eschatological urgency with pointed political commentary in a densely rhetorical, incantatory prose style distinct from Ælfric's more measured homiletic manner.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Preface to the Old English *Pastoral Care*** — Both a practical statement of educational policy and a foundational document for the history of English as a literary and administrative language; Alfred's (or his circle's) insistence on translating "those books most necessary for all men to know" articulates a vernacular-literacy programme without real precedent in earlier Anglo-Saxon culture.
- **Old English *Orosius* (with Ohthere and Wulfstan's voyages)** — Adapts a late-antique Latin universal history for an Anglo-Saxon audience while inserting up-to-date northern geographical and ethnographic material unavailable in the Latin source, making the embedded voyage narratives valuable both as literature and as a rare near-contemporary account of Scandinavian seafaring and peoples.
- ***Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*** — As discussed in Chapter 1, a vernacular annalistic record sustained, with regional variation and interruption, from the late ninth century into the twelfth; its prose ranges from terse annal entries to passages of considerable narrative and even poetic ambition (e.g., the embedded poem on the Battle of Brunanburh, 937).
- ***Catholic Homilies* and *Lives of Saints*** (Ælfric) — Designed respectively for preaching and private devotional reading, these collections combine doctrinal clarity, aimed at a lay audience without Latin, with an increasingly sophisticated rhythmical prose style; Ælfric is often regarded as the most accomplished Old English prose stylist.
- ***Sermo Lupi ad Anglos*** (Wulfstan) — A politically urgent homily interpreting the Viking attacks of the early eleventh century as divine punishment for English sin, notable for its incantatory rhetorical structure (extended catalogues of vice, insistent repetition) and its direct engagement with a specific contemporary crisis rather than timeless doctrinal exposition.

## Critical Perspectives

- **The Godden authorship debate.** Malcolm Godden's 2007 article "Did King Alfred Write Anything?" substantially reopened questions about which, if any, of the works traditionally attributed to Alfred he translated personally, as opposed to commissioning or merely sponsoring; the Old English translation of Bede's *Historia Ecclesiastica*, once routinely called "Alfred's Bede," is now generally regarded by scholars as not being Alfred's own work, and doubt has extended to varying degrees to the *Boethius* and other texts in the traditional corpus. Current scholarship tends to treat the "Alfredian" programme as a collaborative court project rather than a personal authorial achievement, without wholly abandoning Alfred's own role.
- **Vernacular prose historiography as a literary achievement.** The *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*'s sustained maintenance across more than two centuries and multiple institutions is increasingly read not merely as a historical record but as evidence of a deliberately cultivated vernacular prose tradition, part of the same Alfredian impulse toward English-language textual culture as the translation programme proper.
- **Ælfric versus Wulfstan as prose stylists.** Critics commonly contrast Ælfric's clarity, doctrinal caution, and controlled rhythmical patterning with Wulfstan's more urgent, rhetorically driven, catalogue-heavy style — a comparison frequently used pedagogically to illustrate the stylistic range achieved within late Old English prose, some 150 years after the Alfredian program that made vernacular prose scholarship possible.

## Chapter Summary

Alfred the Great's vernacular translation programme, launched in the 890s amid recovery from Viking devastation, represents the central institutional achievement of Old English prose, producing (or sponsoring) translations of Gregory's *Pastoral Care*, Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy*, Augustine's *Soliloquies*, and Orosius's world history — the last expanded with the Ohthere and Wulfstan voyage narratives — and providing the sponsorship context for the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*'s long vernacular historiographical tradition. Malcolm Godden's 2007 intervention has substantially complicated the traditional picture of Alfred as personal author of this corpus, redirecting attention toward a collaborative court programme. A century later, Ælfric of Eynsham and Wulfstan of York brought Old English prose to its most stylistically accomplished late form, in contrasting registers — Ælfric's measured, rhythmical doctrinal clarity against Wulfstan's urgent, rhetorically driven political homiletics — closing out the prose achievement of the Old English period on the eve of the Norman Conquest.

## Glossary

- ***Æstel*** — a valuable pointer (for following text while reading) that Alfred reportedly sent with each copy of the *Pastoral Care* distributed to his bishops.
- **Homily** — a sermon or didactic religious prose text intended for instruction, often for oral delivery.
- ***Sermo Lupi ad Anglos*** — Latin, "The Sermon of the Wolf to the English"; Wulfstan's title, playing on his own name (Wulf-stan, "wolf-stone").
