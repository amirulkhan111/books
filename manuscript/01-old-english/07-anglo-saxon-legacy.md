# Chapter 7: The Anglo-Saxon Legacy in Later English Literature

*Part I: Old English (Anglo-Saxon) Literature, c. 450–1066*

---

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Trace the major stages by which Old English literature was recovered, studied, and institutionalized after the medieval period: Reformation-era antiquarianism, nineteenth-century philology, and twentieth-century academic and literary revival.
2. Explain Matthew Parker's role in preserving Anglo-Saxon manuscripts and the religious-political motives behind his collecting.
3. Assess J. R. R. Tolkien's dual role as Anglo-Saxon scholar and creative writer in transmitting Old English literary material to a modern popular readership.
4. Analyze how the alliterative verse tradition and other Old English formal and thematic elements persisted or were revived in later English poetry, from the Middle English alliterative revival through W. H. Auden.
5. Critically discuss the contested modern use of the term "Anglo-Saxon" itself, including the 2019 renaming of the field's leading professional society.

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

You've now spent six chapters inside the Old English period itself. This closing chapter asks a different question: how did any of that literature reach us at all, a thousand years later? The honest answer is that its survival owes as much to the political and religious motives of the people who rescued and studied it as to its own literary merit. Its first real post-medieval recovery, in the sixteenth century, was driven by Reformation politics, not literary appreciation. Its nineteenth-century academic consolidation was bound up with the nationalism of that era. And its twentieth- and twenty-first-century afterlife runs from major creative achievements — Tolkien, Auden, Heaney — to a genuinely live, current debate about whether the field's own name has been misused for exclusionary ends. This chapter walks through that whole afterlife, and closes out Part I.

## Literary Movements

Old English literature shaped everything that came after it in at least three distinguishable ways, and it's worth learning to tell them apart. The first is a **linguistic and formal legacy**: Old English is the direct ancestor of the language you're reading right now, and its alliterative metre survived into Middle English — you'll meet it again in Part II as the fourteenth-century "Alliterative Revival," in poems like *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight* and *Piers Plowman* — before twentieth-century poets deliberately revived it once more. The second is an **institutional and scholarly legacy**: the long, ongoing work of recovering, editing, and teaching these texts as an academic subject, starting in the sixteenth century, which is the only reason this Part's earlier chapters could be written at all. The third is a **direct creative legacy**: modern writers, Tolkien above all, drawing straight from Old English language and literature as raw material for new imaginative work.

## Author Profiles

**Matthew Parker** (1504–1575) — Archbishop of Canterbury (1559–75) and the leading English antiquarian of his century. Motivated substantially by a Reformation-era desire to demonstrate the historical existence of an English-speaking Christian church independent of Rome, Parker secured a royal licence in 1568 to search former monastic and cathedral libraries for "ancient records and monuments," assembling the earliest major antiquarian book collection in England — predating those of Robert Cotton (whose collection preserves the *Beowulf* manuscript; see Chapter 3) and Thomas Bodley. His bequest to Corpus Christi College, Cambridge, in 1575 includes the earliest surviving manuscript of the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle* and remains one of the world's most significant collections of Anglo-Saxon manuscripts.

**J. R. R. Tolkien** (1892–1973) — Rawlinson and Bosworth Professor of Anglo-Saxon at Oxford (1925–1945), whose 1936 lecture reshaped *Beowulf* scholarship (Chapter 3). Beyond scholarship, Tolkien drew directly on his professional expertise in his fiction: the language of the Rohirrim in *The Lord of the Rings* is represented by (Mercian-dialect) Old English itself, preserving the same structural relationship to the invented language Westron that historical Old English holds to Modern English, and broader elements of *Beowulf* and Old English heroic and elegiac material (including *The Wanderer*'s *ubi sunt* rhetoric, echoed in Tolkien's "Where now the horse and the rider?" passage) pervade his imaginative world.

**W. H. Auden** (1907–1973) — English poet who, as an Oxford undergraduate, attended Tolkien's lectures on Anglo-Saxon philology and was decisively drawn to Old English alliterative technique; his long poem *The Age of Anxiety* (1947) is written largely in a modern four-stress alliterative line directly modelled on Old English (and, via the "Alliterative Revival," Middle English) metrical practice, demonstrating the form's continued expressive viability for twentieth-century subject matter.

**Seamus Heaney** (1939–2013) — see Chapter 3; his 1999 translation of *Beowulf* is among the most significant recent instances of Old English literature reaching a broad contemporary readership.

**Mary Rambaran-Olm** — Independent scholar of Anglo-Saxon studies whose 2019 resignation from the vice-presidency of the International Society of Anglo-Saxonists, citing discrimination and elitism within the field, became a catalyst for the professional society's subsequent renaming (see Critical Perspectives, below).

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **The Parker Library collection**. This isn't a literary work — it's an archive, but a foundational one. Without Matthew Parker's Reformation-era rescue of manuscripts from monastic libraries the government had just dissolved, a large chunk of what you've studied throughout Part I, including the earliest surviving *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle* manuscript, might simply not exist today.
- **Tolkien's Rohirric and *The Lord of the Rings***. Of all the works discussed in this chapter, this is probably the most extensive transmission of Old English material into modern popular fiction. Tolkien's scholarly command of Old English heroic and elegiac idiom — comitatus loyalty, *ubi sunt* lament, alliterative naming patterns — shows up throughout his depiction of the kingdom of Rohan in particular.
- ***The Age of Anxiety*** (Auden). This poem proves something important: Old English and Middle English alliterative metre wasn't a museum piece. Auden showed it could still carry distinctly modern subject matter — urban life, wartime anxiety — as a live, technically demanding poetic resource for a twentieth-century audience.

## Critical Perspectives

- **Manuscript recovery was never neutral.** Parker's Anglo-Saxon collecting was invaluable for preservation, but don't mistake it for disinterested scholarship — it served a specific Reformation goal, establishing historical precedent for an English church independent of Rome. Keep this in mind as a general principle: the recovery and framing of "Anglo-Saxon" literary heritage has been shaped by the political and religious agendas of the people doing the recovering, right from this earliest post-medieval phase.
- **Was Tolkien's fiction an extension of his scholarship, or something else?** Critics disagree. Some read his novels as a direct, popularizing extension of his academic arguments — especially his 1936 insistence that pre-Christian Northern European heroic imagination deserved to be taken seriously on its own terms. Others caution against collapsing the two projects too neatly, pointing out that Tolkien's fiction builds an idealized, harmonized version of Anglo-Saxon culture — Rohan's horse-culture, for instance, has no clear historical Anglo-Saxon equivalent — rather than an accurate reconstruction.
- **The word "Anglo-Saxon" itself is now genuinely contested.** Since the later twentieth century, and especially since the 2010s, scholars have scrutinized this term closely, because it has been picked up outside academia — including by white nationalist and white supremacist movements, mainly in North America — as a racialized label implying a single, homogeneous white English origin. That usage has no basis in the actual history: as you saw as early as Chapter 1, and again with the Scandinavian material underlying *Beowulf* in Chapter 3, early medieval England was linguistically and culturally mixed from the start — British, Latin, and Scandinavian influences all through it. In 2019, following the scholar Mary Rambaran-Olm's public resignation from a leadership role in the field's main professional body, citing discrimination and gatekeeping, that society voted (on 19 November 2019) to rename itself from the International Society of Anglo-Saxonists to the International Society for the Study of Early Medieval England (ISSEME). The vote was genuinely contested: some scholars welcomed it as overdue recognition of real harm and a step toward a more welcoming field; others argued for keeping "Anglo-Saxon" as a precise historical term, distinct from its misuse elsewhere, and worried about losing established vocabulary. This book generally sticks with the traditional "Anglo-Saxon" and "Old English" terms, matching most of the standard histories it's benchmarked against — but it flags this live debate deliberately, because the substantive point behind it is one this whole Part has already shown you to be true: the early medieval English-speaking world was never ethnically or culturally homogeneous.

## Chapter Summary

Old English literature survived, and still matters today, largely because of the people who chose to recover and carry it forward. Matthew Parker's Reformation-driven sixteenth-century collecting preserved much of the manuscript record this whole Part has relied on; nineteenth- and twentieth-century academic institutions, like Oxford's Rawlinson and Bosworth Professorship, kept it under serious scholarly study; and writers like Tolkien and Auden carried its language and form directly into major twentieth-century literature, a transmission still continuing today through translators like Seamus Heaney. None of this recovery has ever been politically neutral — from Parker's own religious motives all the way to the field's current, contested reckoning with the term "Anglo-Saxon" itself. That reckoning brings this Part full circle, back to something Chapter 1 told you at the very start: the early medieval English-speaking world was mixed and layered from the beginning, never a single, homogeneous point of racial or national origin.

## Glossary

- **Alliterative Revival** — the fourteenth-century Middle English resurgence of alliterative verse composition (treated in Part II), continuing the formal tradition discussed in Chapter 2.
- **ISSEME** — International Society for the Study of Early Medieval England, the renamed (2019) successor to the International Society of Anglo-Saxonists (ISAS).
- **Philology** — the historical and comparative study of language, the primary nineteenth- and twentieth-century academic discipline through which Old English literature was edited and studied.
