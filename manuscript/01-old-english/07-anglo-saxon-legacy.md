# Chapter 7: The Anglo-Saxon Legacy in Later English Literature

*Part I: Old English (Anglo-Saxon) Literature, c. 450–1066*

---

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Trace the major stages by which Old English literature was recovered, studied, and institutionalized after the medieval period: Reformation-era antiquarianism, nineteenth-century philology, and twentieth-century academic and literary revival.
2. Explain Matthew Parker's role in preserving Anglo-Saxon manuscripts and the religious-political motives behind his collecting.
3. Assess J. R. R. Tolkien's dual role as Anglo-Saxon scholar and creative writer in transmitting Old English literary material to a modern popular readership.
4. Analyze how the alliterative verse tradition and other Old English formal and thematic elements persisted or were revived in later English poetry, from the Middle English alliterative revival through W. H. Auden.
5. Critically discuss the contested modern use of the term "Anglo-Saxon" itself, including the 2019 renaming of the field's leading professional society.

---

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Old English literature's afterlife has been shaped as much by the political and religious purposes of those who recovered it as by its intrinsic literary qualities. Its first systematic post-medieval recovery, in the sixteenth century, was driven by Reformation polemics; its nineteenth-century academic consolidation was entangled with the philological nationalism of that era; and its twentieth- and twenty-first-century reception has ranged from major creative achievements (Tolkien, Auden, Heaney) to renewed critical scrutiny of how the field's own name and self-image have, at times, been appropriated for exclusionary or racialized purposes. This chapter surveys that afterlife as the closing movement of Part I.

## Literary Movements

Old English literature's influence on later English writing operates on at least three distinguishable levels: (1) a **linguistic and formal legacy** — Old English is the direct ancestor of Modern English, and its alliterative metrical tradition persisted into Middle English (the fourteenth-century "Alliterative Revival," including *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight* and *Piers Plowman*, treated in Part II) and was later deliberately revived by twentieth-century poets; (2) an **institutional and scholarly legacy** — the recovery, editing, and teaching of Old English texts as an academic discipline from the sixteenth century onward, which made this Part's chapters possible; and (3) a **direct creative legacy** — modern authors, most prominently Tolkien, drawing on Old English language, literature, and mythic material as source and inspiration for new imaginative work.

## Author Profiles

**Matthew Parker** (1504–1575) — Archbishop of Canterbury (1559–75) and the leading English antiquarian of his century. Motivated substantially by a Reformation-era desire to demonstrate the historical existence of an English-speaking Christian church independent of Rome, Parker secured a royal licence in 1568 to search former monastic and cathedral libraries for "ancient records and monuments," assembling the earliest major antiquarian book collection in England — predating those of Robert Cotton (whose collection preserves the *Beowulf* manuscript; see Chapter 3) and Thomas Bodley. His bequest to Corpus Christi College, Cambridge, in 1575 includes the earliest surviving manuscript of the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle* and remains one of the world's most significant collections of Anglo-Saxon manuscripts.

**J. R. R. Tolkien** (1892–1973) — Rawlinson and Bosworth Professor of Anglo-Saxon at Oxford (1925–1945), whose 1936 lecture reshaped *Beowulf* scholarship (Chapter 3). Beyond scholarship, Tolkien drew directly on his professional expertise in his fiction: the language of the Rohirrim in *The Lord of the Rings* is represented by (Mercian-dialect) Old English itself, preserving the same structural relationship to the invented language Westron that historical Old English holds to Modern English, and broader elements of *Beowulf* and Old English heroic and elegiac material (including *The Wanderer*'s *ubi sunt* rhetoric, echoed in Tolkien's "Where now the horse and the rider?" passage) pervade his imaginative world.

**W. H. Auden** (1907–1973) — English poet who, as an Oxford undergraduate, attended Tolkien's lectures on Anglo-Saxon philology and was decisively drawn to Old English alliterative technique; his long poem *The Age of Anxiety* (1947) is written largely in a modern four-stress alliterative line directly modelled on Old English (and, via the "Alliterative Revival," Middle English) metrical practice, demonstrating the form's continued expressive viability for twentieth-century subject matter.

**Seamus Heaney** (1939–2013) — see Chapter 3; his 1999 translation of *Beowulf* is among the most significant recent instances of Old English literature reaching a broad contemporary readership.

**Mary Rambaran-Olm** — Independent scholar of Anglo-Saxon studies whose 2019 resignation from the vice-presidency of the International Society of Anglo-Saxonists, citing discrimination and elitism within the field, became a catalyst for the professional society's subsequent renaming (see Critical Perspectives, below).

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **The Parker Library collection** — Not a literary work but a foundational archive: without Parker's Reformation-era rescue of manuscripts from dissolved monastic libraries, a substantial portion of the Old English corpus discussed throughout Part I, including the earliest *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle* manuscript, might not have survived at all.
- **Tolkien's Rohirric and *The Lord of the Rings*** — Represents perhaps the most extensive direct transmission of Old English literary and linguistic material into a work of modern popular fiction; Tolkien's scholarly command of Old English heroic-elegiac idiom (comitatus loyalty, *ubi sunt* lament, alliterative naming) is deployed throughout the depiction of Rohan specifically.
- ***The Age of Anxiety*** (Auden) — Demonstrates that Old English/Middle English alliterative metre, far from being a historical curiosity, remained a live and technically demanding resource for major twentieth-century poetry engaging distinctly modern (urban, wartime, anxious) subject matter.

## Critical Perspectives

- **The politics of antiquarian recovery.** Parker's Anglo-Saxon collecting, though invaluable for preservation, was not disinterested scholarship: it served a specific Reformation polemical purpose (establishing precedent for an English church independent of Rome), a reminder that the recovery and framing of "Anglo-Saxon" literary heritage has been shaped by contemporary political and religious agendas since its earliest post-medieval phase.
- **Tolkien as scholar-creator.** Critics debate the relationship between Tolkien's philological scholarship and his fiction: some read his creative work as a direct extension and popularization of his academic arguments (particularly his insistence, per the 1936 lecture, on taking pre-Christian Northern European heroic imagination seriously on its own terms), while others caution against collapsing the two projects, noting that Tolkien's fiction constructs an idealized, harmonized version of Anglo-Saxon culture (Rohan's cavalry culture, for instance, has no clear historical Anglo-Saxon equivalent) rather than a strictly accurate representation.
- **The contested term "Anglo-Saxon."** Since the later twentieth century, and with particular intensity from the 2010s, scholars have scrutinized the term "Anglo-Saxon" itself, noting that it has been appropriated outside the academic field — including by white nationalist and white supremacist movements, chiefly in North America — as a racialized label implying a homogeneous white English ethnic origin, a usage with no basis in the actual linguistic and cultural diversity of early medieval England (which included British, Latin, and Scandinavian populations and influences throughout the period surveyed in this Part). Following Mary Rambaran-Olm's public 2019 resignation from her position in the field's leading professional body, citing discrimination and gatekeeping, the International Society of Anglo-Saxonists voted (19 November 2019) to rename itself the International Society for the Study of Early Medieval England (ISSEME). The vote and its surrounding debate were contentious — some scholars supported the change as overdue recognition of the term's real-world harms and as part of a broader push for a more diverse and welcoming field; others argued for retaining "Anglo-Saxon" as a precise historical-linguistic term, distinct from its misuse, and expressed concern about discarding established terminology. This book generally follows the more traditional "Anglo-Saxon" and "Old English" terminology, consistent with the majority of the standard histories benchmarked in the Preface, while noting this live and unresolved terminological debate and its underlying substantive point: that the early medieval English-speaking world was never ethnically or culturally homogeneous, a point substantiated throughout this Part (from the British-Germanic settlement questions of Chapter 1 to the Scandinavian legendary material at the heart of *Beowulf* in Chapter 3).

## Chapter Summary

Old English literature's survival and modern significance owe as much to the successive agents of its recovery and transmission as to its original composition. Matthew Parker's Reformation-driven sixteenth-century collecting preserved much of the manuscript record examined throughout this Part; nineteenth- and twentieth-century academic institutionalization, exemplified by Oxford's Rawlinson and Bosworth Professorship, sustained its scholarly study; and figures such as J. R. R. Tolkien and W. H. Auden extended its language, form, and imaginative material directly into major twentieth-century literary works, a transmission continued into the present by translators such as Seamus Heaney. This history of recovery has never been politically neutral, from Parker's ecclesiastical polemics to the field's own recent, contested reckoning with the term "Anglo-Saxon" and its modern misappropriation — a reckoning that returns this Part to a point established in its opening chapter: that the early medieval English-speaking world was linguistically and culturally mixed from the outset, never a homogeneous point of racial or national origin.

## Glossary

- **Alliterative Revival** — the fourteenth-century Middle English resurgence of alliterative verse composition (treated in Part II), continuing the formal tradition discussed in Chapter 2.
- **ISSEME** — International Society for the Study of Early Medieval England, the renamed (2019) successor to the International Society of Anglo-Saxonists (ISAS).
- **Philology** — the historical and comparative study of language, the primary nineteenth- and twentieth-century academic discipline through which Old English literature was edited and studied.
