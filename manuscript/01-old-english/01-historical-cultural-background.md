# Chapter 1: Historical and Cultural Background — Settlement, Conversion, and the Viking Incursions

*Part I: Old English (Anglo-Saxon) Literature, c. 450–1066*

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

### The end of Roman Britain

Roman administration in Britain unravelled in the early fifth century amid the empire's wider crisis on the continent. In 410, with Emperor Honorius unable to spare troops or resources — he was simultaneously contending with the loss of Gaul and Spain and the sack of Rome itself by Alaric's Goths — he is recorded as having replied to British appeals for aid by telling the civitates to see to their own defence (the so-called Rescript of Honorius). This response is conventionally, though not uncontroversially, taken as marking the formal end of Roman rule in Britain.¹

### Settlement: the *adventus Saxonum*

The withdrawal of Roman authority left sub-Roman British polities exposed to raiding from the Picts in the north, the Scoti (Irish) in the west, and Germanic peoples from across the North Sea. The near-contemporary British cleric Gildas, writing probably in the mid-sixth century, describes a British ruler (unnamed by Gildas, later identified in tradition with Vortigern) inviting Germanic warbands to Britain as mercenaries against these raiders — an arrangement that, in Gildas's account, ended in the newcomers turning against their hosts.²

The later tradition, recorded by Bede in the eighth century and by the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle* in the ninth, names the leaders of this first Germanic force as the brothers Hengist and Horsa and dates their arrival in Kent to 449.³ Modern scholarship treats this precise date and the figures of Hengist and Horsa with considerable caution: their alliterative, horse-derived names and the schematic quality of their genealogies suggest a legendary rather than strictly historical account, and archaeological evidence indicates Germanic settlement in eastern Britain both before and continuing well after 449.⁴ What is not in serious doubt is the broad process: over the fifth and sixth centuries, Angles, Saxons, and Jutes settled and established control over increasing areas of what became England, eventually consolidating into a number of competing kingdoms — conventionally, if imprecisely, described as the "Heptarchy" — including Kent, Northumbria, Mercia, East Anglia, Essex, Sussex, and Wessex.

### Conversion to Christianity

Anglo-Saxon England was substantially pagan at the point of settlement. Christianity returned to the English kingdoms through two overlapping streams: a Roman mission dispatched by Pope Gregory I, and Irish (Celtic) Christianity spreading from Iona and Ireland into the north.

Pope Gregory sent the Benedictine prior Augustine to England, and his mission landed in Kent in 597. King Æthelberht of Kent, whose queen Bertha was already a practising Frankish Christian, permitted the mission to preach and settle at Canterbury. The precise date of Æthelberht's own baptism is uncertain — a late medieval tradition gives Whit Sunday (2 June) 597, but the contemporary evidence only confirms that he had converted by 601 — yet his conversion secured Kent as the foothold from which Roman Christianity spread.⁵ Augustine became the first Archbishop of Canterbury.

Christianity's Irish strand entered Northumbria from Iona, notably through the mission of Aidan, invited by King Oswald to found the monastery at Lindisfarne. For several decades Roman and Irish Christianity coexisted with differing practices — most visibly, different methods of calculating the date of Easter. The dispute was resolved at the **Synod of Whitby in 664**, where King Oswiu of Northumbria ruled in favour of Roman practice, decisively orienting the English Church toward Rome rather than Iona.⁶ Conversion of the English kingdoms was substantially complete by the later seventh century, though pockets of paganism persisted longer in some regions (notably Sussex, converted under Bishop Wilfrid in the 680s).

### The Viking incursions

The first recorded Viking raid on England struck the monastery of **Lindisfarne on 8 June 793** — an act that shocked contemporary Christian Europe and is conventionally treated as marking the opening of the Viking Age.⁷ Further raids on monastic sites followed (Jarrow in 794; Iona repeatedly from 795), but for several decades Viking activity in England remained sporadic coastal raiding rather than conquest or settlement.

This changed with the arrival of the **Great Heathen Army**, a large coalition force that landed in East Anglia in the autumn of 865 and wintered at Thetford. Over the following decade the army campaigned across England, conquering Northumbria (866–67), East Anglia (869, where King Edmund was killed), and large parts of Mercia, before turning on Wessex.⁸ Wessex alone among the major kingdoms held out, and under **Alfred the Great** (r. 871–899) achieved a decisive victory over the Danish leader Guthrum at the **Battle of Edington in May 878**. The subsequent peace, formalized in the Treaty of Wedmore and a further Alfred–Guthrum treaty, required Guthrum's baptism and defined a boundary between Alfred's territory and the **Danelaw** — the areas of eastern and northern England under Danish law and settlement.⁹

Alfred's successors — his son Edward the Elder, grandson Æthelstan, and later descendants — gradually reconquered the Danelaw over the tenth century, and Æthelstan is generally regarded as the first king to rule a genuinely unified kingdom of England (from 927). Viking pressure resumed in the late tenth century under renewed Scandinavian attacks, culminating in the Danish prince **Cnut**'s successful invasion; following his victory over Edmund Ironside at the Battle of Assandun (18 October 1016) and Edmund's death shortly afterward, Cnut became king of all England in November 1016, ruling a North Sea empire that also encompassed Denmark and Norway.¹⁰ Anglo-Saxon rule was restored under Edward the Confessor (1042–1066), whose death without an undisputed heir precipitated the succession crisis resolved by the **Norman Conquest of 1066** — the conventional end point of the Old English period.

### Why this history matters for literary study

Nearly all surviving Old English literature is preserved in manuscripts copied in the tenth and eleventh centuries, generally later than the events or even the original composition of the works they contain. The political history above matters to the literary historian for three reasons: (a) it explains the near-total loss of earlier written literature (most manuscripts perished in Viking raids on monastic scriptoria, the very institutions that produced and preserved texts); (b) the Alfredian court's programme of vernacular translation, discussed in Chapter 6, was a direct response to the cultural damage inflicted by the Viking wars; and (c) the mixed, layered political history — British, Anglo-Saxon, Latin-Christian, and Scandinavian — is embedded in the language and subject matter of the literature itself, from the Latinate learning of Bede and Cynewulf to the Scandinavian legendary material underlying *Beowulf*.

## Literary Movements

There is no "literary movement" in the modern sense in this period; rather, this chapter establishes the conditions under which two literary strands developed side by side:

- **Vernacular oral-formulaic poetry**, rooted in a shared Germanic heritage, transmitted orally by scops (poets/singers) before (and alongside) later manuscript transcription. This tradition is the subject of Chapter 2.
- **Latin Christian literate culture**, introduced and sustained by the Church from 597 onward, centred on monastic scriptoria (notably Wearmouth-Jarrow, home to Bede, and later Winchester under Alfred's patronage), which produced historiography, biblical translation and paraphrase, and hagiography, and which — crucially — is also the means by which vernacular poetry such as *Beowulf* was eventually written down.

The interaction of these two strands — a pagan Germanic heroic ethos and a Christian didactic framework, frequently coexisting within single texts — is the defining literary-historical feature of the Old English period and recurs throughout Part I.

## Author Profiles: The Chroniclers

*(This chapter treats the period's chroniclers as historical sources rather than "authors" in a literary sense; author profiles of poets are given in the chapters that follow.)*

**Gildas** (fl. mid-sixth century) — British monk and author of *De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae* ("On the Ruin and Conquest of Britain"), a polemical sermon condemning British rulers and clergy that incidentally provides the earliest surviving connected narrative of the end of Roman Britain and the Anglo-Saxon settlement. Written from a British Christian viewpoint, hostile to the Saxon newcomers.

**Bede** ("the Venerable Bede," c. 673–735) — monk of the twin monastery of Wearmouth-Jarrow in Northumbria; author of the *Historia Ecclesiastica Gentis Anglorum* ("Ecclesiastical History of the English People"), completed in 731. Writing in Latin, Bede combined documentary research, correspondence with informants across England, and providential historical interpretation to produce the single most important narrative source for early Anglo-Saxon history, including the Gregorian mission and the Synod of Whitby.

**The compilers of the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*** — a set of related Old English annals begun in Alfred's Wessex in the late ninth century (drawing on earlier material) and continued at various monastic centres, in one version (the Peterborough Chronicle), until 1154. The Chronicle is both a historical record and, in places, a literary text: several entries incorporate verse, most famously *The Battle of Brunanburh* (937) and *The Battle of Maldon*-adjacent material, discussed in later chapters.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Gildas, *De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae*** — Not a neutral chronicle but a jeremiad: Gildas's purpose is moral condemnation, and the Anglo-Saxon settlement is presented as divine punishment for British sin. Historians therefore use Gildas cautiously as a source of fact while valuing him as the only substantially contemporary witness to the period.
- **Bede, *Historia Ecclesiastica Gentis Anglorum*** — Structured around the providential progress of English Christianity, Bede's history is nonetheless remarkable for its comparative use of sources and its care to distinguish reported from verified information. It is also a foundational literary text: Bede's account of Cædmon (Book IV) is the earliest documented moment of English vernacular religious poetry (treated in Chapter 5).
- ***The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*** — Uniquely valuable as a vernacular prose record maintained (with interruptions and regional variation) across more than two centuries, and as evidence that Old English prose historiography, not only poetry, was a living literary form. Its annalistic form and occasional embedded verse make it a hybrid historical-literary document central to Chapter 6's discussion of the Alfredian programme.

## Critical Perspectives

- **The "migration" versus "elite transfer" debate.** Traditional historiography (following Bede and the *Chronicle*) presents the Anglo-Saxon settlement as a mass migration and displacement of the British population. Since the later twentieth century, archaeologists and geneticists have proposed alternative models — ranging from large-scale migration to smaller elite groups whose culture and language were adopted by a substantially surviving British population — and the question remains actively contested rather than settled.
- **Historicity of Hengist and Horsa.** Scholarly consensus regards the brothers as at least partly legendary figures rather than strictly verifiable historical actors, an important caution against treating Bede and the Chronicle as disinterested reportage.
- **The problem of retrospective sources.** Both Bede (writing c. 731 of events from 449 onward) and the *Chronicle* (compiled from the 890s of events from the fifth century onward) are working at a considerable remove from the events of the settlement period; the chapter's chronology should be read as the received traditional narrative refined by, but not immune to revision from, archaeological evidence.
- **"Dark Ages" as a contested label.** Older scholarship termed the fifth to seventh centuries the "Dark Ages," implying cultural void; this terminology is now widely rejected by historians as both factually misleading (the period produced Sutton Hoo, sophisticated metalwork, and eventually Bede's scholarship) and value-laden, and it is avoided in this book.

## Chapter Summary

The Old English literary period unfolds against three overlapping historical processes: the collapse of Roman authority and the Germanic settlement of Britain from the fifth century; the conversion of the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms to Christianity between 597 and the later seventh century, which introduced Latin literacy and monastic scriptoria; and two centuries of Viking incursion, conquest, and eventual assimilation, from the Lindisfarne raid of 793 through the Danelaw to Cnut's Anglo-Danish kingship after 1016. This history is known to us chiefly through three retrospective, non-neutral sources — Gildas, Bede, and the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle* — whose limitations as evidence must inform, but need not disqualify, their use in literary-historical study. The chapter closes the pre-Conquest political frame within which the poetry and prose treated in the remaining chapters of Part I were composed, transmitted, and, in most cases, first written down.

## Glossary

- **Adventus Saxonum** — Latin, "the coming of the Saxons"; conventional term for the traditional Germanic settlement of Britain.
- **Danelaw** — the area of eastern and northern England under Scandinavian settlement and legal custom following the Alfred–Guthrum treaty.
- **Heptarchy** — a traditional (and imprecise) term for the seven principal Anglo-Saxon kingdoms: Kent, Northumbria, Mercia, East Anglia, Essex, Sussex, Wessex.
- **Scop** — an Old English poet-singer, oral performer of vernacular verse.
- **Scriptorium** — a monastic room dedicated to the copying and production of manuscripts.
- **Witan / Witenagemot** — the council of an Anglo-Saxon king's leading nobles and clergy.
