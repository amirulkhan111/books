# Chapter 1: Historical and Cultural Background — Settlement, Conversion, and the Viking Incursions

## Historical and Socio-Political Context

Before you read a single Old English poem, you need to know why this period has any literature at all — and why so little of it has come down to us. This chapter contains no poems. What it gives you instead is the political and religious history that the rest of Part I leans on constantly. Read it the way you'd sit through the background lecture before a set text: it won't feel like "literature" yet, but nothing in Chapters 2 through 7 will quite make sense without it.

Here is the shape of the story in one sentence, before we get into the detail: Roman Britain collapsed, Germanic settlers moved in and eventually became "the English," those English converted to Christianity (which is what gave them writing), and then, just as a literate culture was taking root, Viking raiders arrived and very nearly destroyed it. Everything below fills in that sentence.

### The end of Roman Britain

For nearly four centuries, Britain was a Roman province. That stopped, in effect, in the year **410**. The Western Roman Empire was collapsing under pressure on multiple fronts at once — it had just lost Gaul and Spain, and the city of Rome itself was about to be sacked by the Gothic king Alaric. With no soldiers or money to spare, the emperor Honorius told the British communities (*civitates*) to look after their own defence. Historians refer to this reply as the **Rescript of Honorius**, and they conventionally treat it as the moment Roman rule in Britain ended — though it's worth remembering this was less a single dramatic break than the last sentence of a process that had already been under way for years.¹

Why does a Roman administrative letter belong in a book about literature? Because it is the hinge the whole period turns on. Once Rome withdrew, Britain had no central authority left to defend it, and that vacuum is exactly what let the Anglo-Saxons in.

### Settlement: the *adventus Saxonum*

With Roman protection gone, the local British rulers found themselves exposed on three sides: Picts raiding from the north, the Irish (called Scoti at the time) from the west, and Germanic seafarers from across the North Sea. A British monk named **Gildas**, writing probably in the mid-sixth century — so, roughly a century after the events he describes — tells us that a British ruler (later tradition names him Vortigern, though Gildas himself does not) invited Germanic warbands over as hired soldiers to help fight off these raiders. According to Gildas, the newcomers eventually turned on the very people who had invited them.²

Later writers filled in names and a date for this story. Bede, writing in the eighth century, and the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*, compiled in the ninth, both name the leaders of this first Germanic force as the brothers **Hengist and Horsa** and give their arrival in Kent as the year **449**.³ Take that date with some caution. Modern historians are skeptical of Hengist and Horsa as real, individually verifiable people — their names both mean roughly "horse" in Old English, which looks less like a historical record and more like the kind of pattern that legends fall into. Archaeology backs up the skepticism: it shows Germanic settlers already living in eastern Britain before 449, and still arriving well after it.⁴ So hold onto the general picture rather than the precise date: over the fifth and sixth centuries, three related Germanic peoples — the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes — settled across what would become England, gradually carving it into a patchwork of small, competing kingdoms. Later historians gave this patchwork a tidy label, the "**Heptarchy**" (seven kingdoms), though the real picture was messier and less stable than seven neat boxes suggests. The kingdoms usually named are Kent, Northumbria, Mercia, East Anglia, Essex, Sussex, and Wessex — names that will keep recurring throughout Part I, so it's worth fixing them in your mind now.

### Conversion to Christianity

At the point of settlement, the Anglo-Saxons were pagan. Christianity came back to England through two separate missionary streams that eventually met and, for a while, competed.

The first stream was Roman. Pope Gregory I sent a monk named **Augustine** to England, and his mission landed in Kent in **597**. He had a head start: King Æthelberht of Kent was already married to Bertha, a Christian princess from Frankish Gaul, so the ground had been prepared. Æthelberht allowed Augustine's mission to preach and to settle at Canterbury. We don't know exactly when Æthelberht himself converted — a later medieval tradition names Whit Sunday, 2 June 597, but the only thing the surviving contemporary evidence actually confirms is that he had converted by 601 — but his conversion gave Roman Christianity its first secure foothold in England.⁵ Augustine became the first Archbishop of Canterbury, a title that still exists today.

The second stream was Irish, or "Celtic." It reached Northumbria from the island monastery of Iona, carried chiefly by a monk named **Aidan**, whom King Oswald invited to found a new monastery at **Lindisfarne**. For several decades, Roman and Irish Christianity coexisted in England with genuinely different practices — the most visible disagreement was over how to calculate the date of Easter each year, which meant the two traditions could literally be celebrating Easter and Lent on different weeks in the same kingdom. That dispute was settled at the **Synod of Whitby in 664**, where King Oswiu of Northumbria ruled in favour of Roman practice. From that point on, the English Church looked to Rome, not Iona.⁶ Most of England had converted by the later seventh century, though a few pockets of paganism held out longer — Sussex, for instance, was not converted until Bishop Wilfrid arrived there in the 680s.

Why should a literary historian care about any of this? Because conversion is what gave the Anglo-Saxons *writing*. Before Christianity arrived, Old English existed only as a spoken language, occasionally scratched in runes. The Church brought the Latin alphabet, the technology of the book, and — crucially — monasteries: institutions whose entire purpose included copying and preserving texts. Nearly everything discussed in the rest of Part I exists because of this change.

### The Viking incursions

Just as that literate monastic culture was maturing, it was struck by a new threat. The first recorded Viking raid on England hit the monastery of **Lindisfarne on 8 June 793** — the same Lindisfarne founded by Aidan a century and a half earlier. Contemporary Christian writers across Europe were horrified, and this raid is conventionally treated as the opening event of the Viking Age in England.⁷ More monastic raids followed — Jarrow in 794, Iona repeatedly from 795 — but for several decades Viking activity in England stayed at the level of sporadic coastal raiding. It was not yet conquest.

That changed in 865. A large coalition of Scandinavian forces, which English sources call the **Great Heathen Army**, landed in East Anglia that autumn and wintered at Thetford. Over the following decade this army fought its way across England: it conquered Northumbria (866–67), conquered East Anglia (869, killing King Edmund in the process), and took large parts of Mercia, before finally turning on Wessex.⁸ Wessex was the one major kingdom that did not fall. Under its king, **Alfred the Great** (r. 871–899), Wessex won a decisive victory over the Danish leader Guthrum at the **Battle of Edington in May 878**. The peace that followed — formalized in the Treaty of Wedmore and a further Alfred–Guthrum treaty — required Guthrum to be baptized, and it drew a boundary between Alfred's territory and the **Danelaw**: the parts of eastern and northern England now living under Danish law and Danish settlers.⁹

Over the following century, Alfred's descendants — his son Edward the Elder, his grandson Æthelstan, and later kings — slowly won the Danelaw back. Æthelstan is usually credited as the first king to rule a genuinely unified kingdom of England, from 927. Viking pressure returned in the later tenth century, and this second wave ended with the Danish prince **Cnut** conquering the whole country: after defeating Edmund Ironside at the Battle of Assandun on 18 October 1016, and Edmund's death shortly afterward, Cnut became king of all England that November, ruling it as part of a larger North Sea empire that also included Denmark and Norway.¹⁰ English kingship was restored under Edward the Confessor (1042–1066). When Edward died without a clear heir, the resulting succession crisis led directly to the **Norman Conquest of 1066** — the event that, by convention, closes the Old English period and opens the one covered in Part II.

### Why this history matters for literary study

Hold on to three consequences of everything above, because they will keep resurfacing through Part I.

First, almost every surviving piece of Old English literature exists only in manuscripts copied in the tenth or eleventh centuries — usually well after the poem itself was first composed, sometimes centuries after. That gap exists largely *because of* the history above: Viking raids fell specifically on monasteries, the very institutions that were producing and storing texts, so most earlier written literature was simply burned or lost.

Second, when King Alfred later launched a programme of translating important Latin texts into English (the subject of Chapter 6), he did so explicitly as a response to the cultural damage the Viking wars had done to English learning. That programme doesn't make sense without the history of this chapter behind it.

Third, and more subtly: the layered political history you've just read — British, then Anglo-Saxon, then Latin-Christian, then Scandinavian — is not just background. It is baked into the language and subject matter of the literature itself. You'll meet it again in the Latinate learning of Bede and the poet Cynewulf, and in the Scandinavian legendary material that sits underneath *Beowulf*.

## Literary Movements

It's worth pausing on the word "movement," because this chapter is going to disappoint you slightly if you're expecting one. There is no "literary movement" in the modern sense here — no manifesto, no self-conscious group of writers deciding to do something new together, the way you might find in later periods of this book. What this period gives you instead is two separate literary traditions developing side by side, largely independently, before eventually meeting on the manuscript page. Understanding these two strands now will save you confusion in every chapter that follows.

- **Vernacular oral-formulaic poetry.** This is poetry in the spoken English of the day (what we call Old English), composed and performed by heart rather than written down, by professional poet-performers called **scops**. It draws on a shared Germanic heroic culture. Chapter 2 is entirely devoted to how this oral tradition worked and how it eventually reached the page.
- **Latin Christian literate culture.** This is the culture that arrived with the Church from 597 onward — centred on monastic **scriptoria** (a scriptorium is simply a room where monks copied manuscripts by hand), most famously at Wearmouth-Jarrow, Bede's home monastery, and later at Winchester under Alfred's patronage. This literate culture produced history-writing, translations and retellings of the Bible, and saints' lives (hagiography). And it did something else that matters enormously for this book: it is the *only* reason the oral poetry of the first strand — including *Beowulf* — was ever written down at all.

The real story of Old English literature is what happens where these two strands overlap: a pagan Germanic warrior code sitting inside a Christian moral framework, often within the very same poem. Keep an eye out for that combination — it is the single most distinctive feature of the literature in Part I, and you will meet it again and again.

## Author Profiles: The Chroniclers

The three figures below are not "authors" in the sense you'll meet from Chapter 2 onward — they are historians, writing prose accounts rather than poetry. But they matter enormously to literary history, because they are almost the only near-contemporary witnesses we have to the events above, and because two of them also preserve genuine literary material within their histories.

**Gildas** (fl. mid-sixth century) was a British monk, and the author of *De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae* ("On the Ruin and Conquest of Britain"). His real purpose was not history-writing at all — the book is a furious sermon condemning British rulers and clergy for their sins — but along the way it happens to give us the earliest surviving connected account of the end of Roman Britain and the Anglo-Saxon settlement. He writes as a hostile, aggrieved British Christian, not a neutral observer, and that bias is worth remembering every time he's cited.

**Bede**, often called "the Venerable Bede" (c. 673–735), was a monk at the twin monastery of Wearmouth-Jarrow in Northumbria. His major work, the *Historia Ecclesiastica Gentis Anglorum* ("Ecclesiastical History of the English People"), completed in 731, is the single most important narrative source we have for early Anglo-Saxon history — it is where most of what you read above about the Gregorian mission and the Synod of Whitby ultimately comes from. Writing in Latin, Bede combined real documentary research and correspondence with informants across England with a providential outlook: he believed English history was unfolding according to God's plan, and he shapes his narrative accordingly.

**The compilers of the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*** are, collectively, our third source: a set of related annals (year-by-year records) begun in Alfred's Wessex in the late ninth century, drawing on earlier material, and then continued at various monastic centres — one version, the Peterborough Chronicle, was kept going as late as 1154. The *Chronicle* is not only a historical record; in places it is genuinely a literary text. Several entries slip into verse, most famously the poem on *The Battle of Brunanburh* (937) — you'll meet this material again in later chapters.

## Key Works and Critical Analysis

- **Gildas, *De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae***. Don't read this as a neutral chronicle — it isn't one. Gildas's purpose is moral condemnation: he presents the Anglo-Saxon settlement itself as God's punishment for British sin. Historians therefore use him cautiously as a source of fact, while still valuing him highly as the only substantially contemporary witness to the period.
- **Bede, *Historia Ecclesiastica Gentis Anglorum***. Bede organizes his history around the providential progress of English Christianity — the story of a plan unfolding, not a neutral timeline. And yet he is genuinely careful by the standards of his time: he compares sources against each other and is often explicit about what is reported versus verified. For literary history specifically, one passage matters enormously: Bede's account of the poet Cædmon in Book IV is the earliest documented moment of English vernacular religious poetry, and you'll study it properly in Chapter 5.
- ***The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle***. This is a vernacular (English-language) prose record kept up, with gaps and regional variation, across more than two centuries — proof that Old English prose historiography, not just poetry, was a living literary form. Its annalistic structure (entry by entry, year by year) occasionally opens out into embedded verse, which makes it a genuine hybrid of historical record and literary text. Chapter 6 returns to it in detail as part of the Alfredian programme.

## Critical Perspectives

Historians don't agree on everything in the story above, and it's worth knowing where the real disagreements sit — both so you can think critically about the "traditional narrative" this chapter has just given you, and because these debates come up often in exam and essay contexts.

- **Migration versus elite transfer.** The traditional account — following Bede and the *Chronicle* — pictures the Anglo-Saxon settlement as a mass migration that displaced the existing British population. Since the later twentieth century, archaeologists and geneticists have proposed alternatives: some argue for genuine large-scale migration, others for a smaller ruling elite whose language and culture were gradually adopted by a British population that mostly stayed put. This is a live, unresolved debate, not a settled question.
- **Were Hengist and Horsa real people?** Most scholars today treat the brothers as at least partly legendary rather than reliably historical — a useful reminder not to treat Bede or the *Chronicle* as neutral reporting simply because they give names and dates.
- **The problem of writing about the past from a distance.** Bede was writing around 731 about events from 449 onward; the *Chronicle* was compiled from the 890s about events going back to the fifth century. Both sources are working at a real remove from what they describe. Treat the chronology in this chapter as the received traditional story, refined — but not overturned — by archaeology.
- **"Dark Ages" is a label this book avoids.** Older scholarship called the fifth to seventh centuries the "Dark Ages," implying a cultural void. Historians today mostly reject that label: it's factually misleading (this "dark" period produced the Sutton Hoo treasure, sophisticated metalwork, and eventually Bede's own scholarship) and it carries a value judgment that doesn't hold up. You won't see the term used approvingly anywhere else in this book.

## Chapter Summary

Three overlapping historical processes shape everything that follows in Part I. First, the collapse of Roman authority let Germanic peoples settle Britain from the fifth century onward. Second, the conversion of the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms to Christianity, between 597 and the later seventh century, brought Latin literacy and monastic scriptoria with it — the technology that makes English literature possible at all. Third, two centuries of Viking incursion, conquest, and eventual assimilation — from the Lindisfarne raid of 793, through the Danelaw, to Cnut's Anglo-Danish kingship after 1016 — nearly destroyed that literate culture and shaped what did and didn't survive. We know this history mainly through three sources, Gildas, Bede, and the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*, none of them neutral and all of them writing at some distance from the events they describe. Keep their limitations in mind, but don't discount them — they are what we have. With this political frame in place, you're ready for Chapter 2, which turns to the poetry itself: how it was composed, performed, and eventually written down.

## Glossary

- **Adventus Saxonum** — Latin, "the coming of the Saxons"; conventional term for the traditional Germanic settlement of Britain.
- **Danelaw** — the area of eastern and northern England under Scandinavian settlement and legal custom following the Alfred–Guthrum treaty.
- **Heptarchy** — a traditional (and imprecise) term for the seven principal Anglo-Saxon kingdoms: Kent, Northumbria, Mercia, East Anglia, Essex, Sussex, Wessex.
- **Scop** — an Old English poet-singer, oral performer of vernacular verse.
- **Scriptorium** — a monastic room dedicated to the copying and production of manuscripts.
