# Style Guide & Editorial Standards
### *English Literature Through the Ages: A Historical and Critical Companion to English and Anglophone Literature*

This document governs every chapter added to `manuscript/`. It exists so the book stays internally consistent, verifiable, and usable for exam preparation as it is written incrementally.

**Section 2 (Writing Philosophy) is the governing standard for this entire project.** Every other section — template, tone, citation style, quality check — implements it. Where any instruction elsewhere seems to conflict with Section 2, Section 2 wins.

## 1. Verification protocol

- **No date, quotation, or biographical claim is entered without a check against an authoritative source** (standard literary histories, peer-reviewed scholarship, primary texts, reputable reference works such as the *Oxford DNB*, the *Norton Anthology* editorial apparatus, or equivalent).
- Direct quotations from primary works are reproduced only when the source edition is identified; quotations from copyrighted secondary criticism are kept short and attributed.
- If a fact cannot be verified, it is flagged inline as `[VERIFY: ...]` rather than asserted — never silently guessed.
- Chapters are drafted **one at a time**, not generated in bulk, so each can be individually checked before merging.
- **Simplifying the language never means simplifying the scholarship.** Every fact-checking requirement above still applies at full strength — the writing philosophy in Section 2 changes how a verified fact is *explained*, never whether it was verified.

## 2. Writing philosophy (governs everything below)

This book is written for a reader who may have **no prior knowledge of English literature at all** — a first-year undergraduate meeting the subject for the first time. That reader is assumed to be intelligent, curious, and capable of real scholarly depth. They are *not* assumed to know:

- literary periods or movements
- historical events (political, religious, or philosophical)
- literary terminology or critical vocabulary
- authors, genres, or critical theory

Every new concept — a term, a historical event, a movement, a theoretical idea — must be **introduced gently before it is developed**. Never deploy a term the chapter hasn't already explained, even a term a specialist would consider basic (e.g., "blank verse," "hegemony," "the Reformation").

**Primary objective:** the book should read like an outstanding university teacher explaining literature inside a classroom — never like an encyclopedia, and never like AI-generated prose. A diligent student should understand every chapter completely after a single careful reading. Clarity before sophistication. Simplicity before complexity. Teaching before displaying knowledge.

**Direction of explanation**, always:

- known → unknown
- simple → complex
- concrete → abstract

**Voice:** a consistent, human authorial voice throughout — a passionate teacher with years of classroom experience, guiding the reader rather than lecturing at them, and inspiring curiosity rather than merely transmitting information. Write naturally and conversationally without becoming informal. Avoid robotic transitions, repetitive AI-style sentence rhythms, unnecessary verbosity, ornamental academic language, and unexplained jargon. Prefer short and medium-length sentences; use a longer sentence only when it genuinely improves the explanation. Every paragraph explains **one** idea clearly.

This philosophy **overrides all previous stylistic preferences** in this project, including the older "formal academic register, third person" instruction. It applies to every existing chapter and every future chapter.

## 3. Teaching principles

### 3.1 For every important concept

1. Introduce it.
2. Explain why it matters.
3. Explain it in simple language.
4. Give historical context.
5. Give an example.
6. Connect it to previous chapters.
7. Explain its influence on later literature.

Equivalently, every important concept must answer four questions somewhere in its treatment: **What is it? Why did it emerge? Why does it matter? How did it influence later literature?**

### 3.2 Golden teaching principles (non-negotiable)

1. Every page must answer the student's unspoken question: *"Why should I care about this?"*
2. Never present information without explaining its significance.
3. Never introduce a new idea before preparing the reader for it.
4. Every chapter should feel like attending an outstanding university lecture, not reading an encyclopedia.
5. The reader should never feel lost. Every explanation should reduce confusion, not increase it.
6. If a first-year undergraduate might struggle with a paragraph after one careful reading, rewrite it.
7. Never write to impress scholars — write to educate students.
8. Scholarly depth must always be preserved, but complexity should come from the ideas themselves, never from difficult language.
9. Every chapter should build the reader's confidence. A student should finish a chapter thinking, "I understand this now."
10. The writing must sound unmistakably human, never like AI-generated prose.

## 4. Fixed chapter template

Every chapter in Parts I–XI follows this order:

1. **Learning Outcomes** (5–8 outcome statements, action-verb phrased per outcome-based education / NEP 2020 conventions)
2. **Historical & Socio-Political Context**
3. **Timeline** (key dates for the period/chapter topic)
4. **Literary Movements**
5. **Author Profiles** (life, major works, critical standing)
6. **Key Works & Critical Analysis**
7. **Critical Perspectives** (how the period/author has been read across critical schools)
8. **Comparative Tables** (e.g., contrasting authors, genres, or movements)
9. **Figure/Illustration Suggestions** (described, not embedded — e.g., "portrait of X," "facsimile of Y manuscript page," with a placeholder for a production editor to source rights-cleared images)
10. **Chapter Summary**
11. **Glossary** (terms introduced in the chapter)
12. **Discussion Questions** (essay-style, for seminar use)
13. **MCQs** (minimum 10 per chapter, with answer key in Appendix E)
14. **Research Topics** (for term papers/dissertations)
15. **Suggested Readings** (primary texts + secondary criticism, full citations)

The template is a scaffold, not a license to lecture in dense blocks — every section above is written under the Section 2 philosophy. "Historical & Socio-Political Context," for instance, still opens by explaining *why the student needs this background before the literature will make sense*, not by reciting events.

## 5. Inclusivity standard

Women writers, Irish/Scottish/Welsh literatures, and postcolonial/global Anglophone writing are integrated into the main chronological narrative of Parts I–IX wherever historically present (e.g., Aphra Behn in Part IV, not deferred to a supplementary note), in addition to receiving dedicated treatment in Part X.

## 6. Citation style

- **MHRA** referencing style (standard for English literature scholarship in UK/Commonwealth and widely accepted in Indian universities) for footnotes and bibliography, unless a specific course adopts MLA — noted per-chapter if so.
- Primary-text citations give edition used (e.g., "Chaucer, *The Canterbury Tales*, ed. Jill Mann, Penguin Classics, 2005").

## 7. Pedagogical alignment

- Learning outcomes and MCQs are cross-checked against current **UGC-NET English Literature** and **CUET-PG English** syllabi, and mapped in Appendix C.
- Chapter structure supports **NEP 2020** outcome-based, multidisciplinary learning (explicit outcomes, research-topic prompts for independent inquiry, comparative/critical rather than rote framing).

## 8. Tone and register

- A teacher's voice, not a reference work's: conversational without being informal, warm without being imprecise, confident without being ornamental.
- Third person for the historical/critical narrative, but direct address to the reader ("you," "notice that," "you'll see this again in Chapter X") is welcome where it aids teaching — this is a deliberate change from the earlier "no direct address" convention.
- Balanced critical presentation is preserved: where scholarly opinion is divided (e.g., authorship questions, dating disputes), the chapter presents the range of views rather than asserting one as settled fact — but it does so in plain language, explaining *why* the disagreement exists rather than only cataloguing positions.
- No unsupported editorializing about literary quality, but genuine enthusiasm for the material is welcome and encouraged — a flat, affectless register is itself a departure from "sounding human."

## 9. Editorial quality check (required before any chapter is marked complete)

Before a chapter is marked ✅ verified & final, in addition to the fact-verification pass in Section 1, run this reader-experience check against the whole chapter:

- Could this be explained more clearly?
- Is any prior knowledge assumed anywhere?
- Is every technical term explained before it is first used?
- Does the chapter read naturally, the way a person talks?
- Does it sound like a real teacher — or like a generic reference summary?
- Would a first-year student actually enjoy reading this?

If any answer is "no," revise before considering the chapter complete. This check applies retroactively: every existing chapter is re-passed through it as part of the project's ongoing revision to the new standard, not just chapters drafted from here on.

## 10. File and naming conventions

- One Markdown file per chapter: `manuscript/<part-folder>/<chapter-number>-<slug>.md`
- Each part folder carries a `README.md` stub tracking that part's chapter checklist and status until fully drafted.
