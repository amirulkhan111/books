# English Literature Through the Ages
### *A Historical and Critical Companion*

A definitive, publication-grade history of English literature — written for advanced undergraduate and postgraduate study, aligned with **NEP 2020**, **UGC-NET**, **CUET-PG**, and major Indian and international university syllabi, and benchmarked against (without imitating) the standard histories in the field.

This repository is the working manuscript, editorial apparatus, and production scaffold for the book. It is a **work in progress**, built chapter by chapter under an explicit verification standard — see [Editorial Standards](#editorial-standards) below.

---

## Why another history of English literature?

Every existing standard history has real strengths and real limits. This project starts from an honest audit of the field before writing a single chapter, so the resulting structure earns its place rather than repeating inherited habits.

| Work | Strengths | Limitations for a 21st-century course companion |
|---|---|---|
| **W. J. Long**, *English Literature: Its History and Significance* (1909) | Accessible narrative prose; good entry point for beginners | Over a century old; moralizing Victorian tone; factually superseded in places; almost no attention to women writers or non-English-national literatures |
| **E. Legouis & L. Cazamian**, *A History of English Literature* (1926, rev.) | Thorough, comparative (French) critical vantage; strong on medieval and Renaissance detail | Dated critical assumptions; Eurocentric framing; no pedagogical apparatus; pre-dates most 20th-century literature |
| **A. Compton-Rickett**, *A History of English Literature* (1912) | Clear period-by-period manual structure; useful as a study skeleton | Very dated scholarship; minimal critical depth; silent on class, gender, empire |
| **David Daiches**, *A Critical History of English Literature* (2 vols., 1960) | Genuinely sophisticated close reading; strong critical voice | Dense, exam-unfriendly prose; weak past mid-century; almost no postcolonial or global Anglophone material |
| **Edward Albert**, *History of English Literature* (rev. Rea & Dobrée) | The default exam-prep manual across South Asian universities; clear, compact, well-organized for revision | No pedagogical scaffolding beyond prose (no MCQs/outcomes/glossaries); thin on inclusivity; largely silent on literary theory and postcolonial writing |
| **Andrew Sanders**, *The Short Oxford History of English Literature* (3rd ed., 2004) | Modern scholarship, reliable dating and context, OUP rigor | Anglocentric (light on Irish/Scottish/Welsh/Commonwealth writing); not built for competitive-exam use |
| **Michael Alexander**, *A History of English Literature* (Palgrave, 2nd ed., 2007) | Readable, reasonably current, some postcolonial gesture | No exam-mapped pedagogical apparatus; limited engagement with critical theory as a taught subject |

**The gap this book fills:** no single standard history combines (a) literary-historical rigor comparable to Daiches/Sanders, (b) the compact exam-utility of Albert, (c) deliberate inclusion of women writers, Irish/Scottish/Welsh literatures, and postcolonial/global Anglophone writing as integral rather than appended, (d) a taught strand on literary criticism and theory, and (e) full outcome-based pedagogical apparatus (learning outcomes, MCQs, discussion questions, comparative tables) mapped to NEP 2020 and UGC-NET/CUET-PG requirements.

## Structure of the book

Full chapter-level detail lives in [`TABLE_OF_CONTENTS.md`](TABLE_OF_CONTENTS.md). At the top level:

1. **Old English (Anglo-Saxon) Literature** — c. 450–1066
2. **Middle English Literature** — 1066–1500
3. **The Renaissance / Early Modern Period** — 1500–1660
4. **The Restoration and Augustan Age** — 1660–1750
5. **The Age of Sensibility** — 1750–1798
6. **The Romantic Period** — 1798–1837
7. **The Victorian Age** — 1837–1901
8. **The Modern Period** — 1901–1945
9. **Postwar, Postmodern, and Contemporary British Literature** — 1945–present
10. **Postcolonial and Global Anglophone Literatures** — Indian Writing in English, African, Caribbean, Irish, Scottish, Welsh, Australian, Canadian
11. **Literary Criticism and Theory** — from classical criticism to contemporary theory
12. **Appendices** — master timeline, glossary of literary terms, UGC-NET/CUET-PG syllabus mapping, consolidated bibliography

Every period chapter follows a fixed pedagogical template:

> Learning Outcomes → Historical & Socio-Political Context → Timeline → Literary Movements → Author Profiles → Key Works & Critical Analysis → Critical Perspectives → Comparative Tables → Figure/Illustration Suggestions → Chapter Summary → Glossary → Discussion Questions → MCQs → Research Topics → Suggested Readings

## Editorial standards

- **No fabricated facts, dates, or quotations.** Every date, quotation, and biographical claim is checked against authoritative sources before it enters the manuscript. Chapters are written and verified one at a time rather than generated in bulk, precisely to protect this standard.
- **Balanced coverage.** Deliberate inclusion of women writers, Irish/Scottish/Welsh literatures, and postcolonial/global Anglophone writing within the main chronological narrative, not as an afterword.
- **Pedagogical alignment.** Learning outcomes and question banks are mapped to NEP 2020 outcome-based learning and to UGC-NET/CUET-PG English Literature syllabi.
- **Full detail:** see [`STYLE_GUIDE.md`](STYLE_GUIDE.md).
